<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Mathematical study of algebraic structures</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg"><img alt="Picture of a Rubik's Cube" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg/220px-Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg/330px-Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg/440px-Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rubik%27s_cube_v2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Permutation" title="Permutation">permutations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube" title="Rubik's Cube">Rubik's Cube</a> form a <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group</a>, a fundamental concept within abstract algebra.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, more specifically <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a>, <b>abstract algebra</b> or <b>modern algebra</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Algebraic structures include <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Module_(mathematics)" title="Module (mathematics)">modules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vector spaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lattice_(order)" title="Lattice (order)">lattices</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field" title="Algebra over a field">algebras over a field</a>. The term <i>abstract algebra</i> was coined in the early 20th century to distinguish this area of study from older parts of algebra, and more specifically from <a href="/wiki/Elementary_algebra" title="Elementary algebra">elementary algebra</a>, the use of <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> to represent numbers in computation and reasoning.
</p><p>Algebraic structures, with their associated <a href="/wiki/Homomorphism" title="Homomorphism">homomorphisms</a>, form <a href="/wiki/Category_(mathematics)" title="Category (mathematics)">mathematical categories</a>. <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">Category theory</a> is a formalism that allows a unified way for expressing properties and constructions that are similar for various structures.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Universal_algebra" title="Universal algebra">Universal algebra</a> is a related subject that studies types of algebraic structures as single objects. For example, the structure of groups is a single object in universal algebra, which is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(universal_algebra)" title="Variety (universal algebra)">variety</a> of groups</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the nineteenth century, <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a> meant the study of the solution of polynomial equations. Abstract algebra came into existence during the nineteenth century as more complex problems and solution methods developed. Concrete problems and examples came from number theory, geometry, analysis, and the solutions of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_equation" title="Algebraic equation">algebraic equations</a>. Most theories that are now recognized as parts of abstract algebra started as collections of disparate facts from various branches of mathematics, acquired a common theme that served as a core around which various results were grouped, and finally became unified on a basis of a common set of concepts. This unification occurred in the early decades of the 20th century and resulted in the formal <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axiomatic</a> definitions of various <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> such as groups, rings, and fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner2007xi–xii_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner2007xi–xii-2">[2]</a></sup> This historical development is almost the opposite of the treatment found in popular textbooks, such as van der Waerden's <a href="/wiki/Moderne_Algebra" title="Moderne Algebra">Moderne Algebra</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which start each chapter with a formal definition of a structure and then follow it with concrete examples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200741_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200741-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elementary_algebra">Elementary algebra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elementary algebra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_algebra" title="History of algebra">History of algebra</a></div>
<p>The study of polynomial equations or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algebraic_equations" title="Algebraic equations">algebraic equations</a> has a long history. Circa 1700 BC, the Babylonians were able to solve quadratic equations specified as word problems. This word problem stage is classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhetorical_algebra" title="Rhetorical algebra">rhetorical algebra</a> and was the dominant approach up to the 16th century. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB" title="Muhammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī">Muhammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī</a> originated the word "algebra" in 830 AD, but his work was entirely rhetorical algebra. Fully symbolic algebra did not appear until <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te" title="François Viète">François Viète</a>'s 1591 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Algebra" title="New Algebra">New Algebra</a>, and even this had some spelled out words that were given symbols in Descartes's 1637 <a href="/wiki/La_G%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie" title="La Géométrie">La Géométrie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner20071–13_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner20071–13-5">[5]</a></sup> The formal study of solving symbolic equations led <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> to accept what were then considered "nonsense" roots such as <a href="/wiki/Negative_number" title="Negative number">negative numbers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number">imaginary numbers</a>, in the late 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, European mathematicians, for the most part, resisted these concepts until the middle of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/George_Peacock" title="George Peacock">George Peacock</a>'s 1830 <i>Treatise of Algebra</i> was the first attempt to place algebra on a strictly symbolic basis. He distinguished a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolical_algebra" title="Symbolical algebra">symbolical algebra</a>, distinct from the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetical_algebra" title="Arithmetical algebra">arithmetical algebra</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_group_theory">Early group theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early group theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_group_theory" title="History of group theory">History of group theory</a></div>
<p>Several areas of mathematics led to the study of groups. Lagrange's 1770 study of the solutions of the quintic led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galois_group_of_a_polynomial" title="Galois group of a polynomial">Galois group of a polynomial</a>. Gauss's 1801 study of <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_little_theorem" title="Fermat's little theorem">Fermat's little theorem</a> led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring_of_integers_modulo_n" title="Ring of integers modulo n">ring of integers modulo n</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_group_of_integers_modulo_n" title="Multiplicative group of integers modulo n">multiplicative group of integers modulo n</a>, and the more general concepts of <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_group" title="Cyclic group">cyclic groups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian groups</a>. Klein's 1872 <a href="/wiki/Erlangen_program" title="Erlangen program">Erlangen program</a> studied geometry and led to <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry groups</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_group" title="Euclidean group">Euclidean group</a> and the group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projective_transformation" title="Projective transformation">projective transformations</a>. In 1874 Lie introduced the theory of <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">Lie groups</a>, aiming for "the Galois theory of differential equations". In 1976 Poincaré and Klein introduced the group of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation" title="Möbius transformation">Möbius transformations</a>, and its subgroups such as the <a href="/wiki/Modular_group" title="Modular group">modular group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuchsian_group" title="Fuchsian group">Fuchsian group</a>, based on work on automorphic functions in analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200717–22_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200717–22-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The abstract concept of group emerged slowly over the middle of the nineteenth century. Galois in 1832 was the first to use the term “group”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbsGroup_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AbsGroup-10">[10]</a></sup> signifying a collection of permutations closed under composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200723_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200723-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Cayley" title="Arthur Cayley">Arthur Cayley</a>'s 1854 paper <i>On the theory of groups</i> defined a group as a set with an associative composition operation and the identity 1, today called a <a href="/wiki/Monoid" title="Monoid">monoid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1870 Kronecker defined an abstract binary operation that was closed, commutative, associative, and had the left <a href="/wiki/Cancellation_property" title="Cancellation property">cancellation property</a> <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle b\neq c\to a\cdot b\neq a\cdot c}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</p><p>Once this abstract group concept emerged, results were reformulated in this abstract setting. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sylow%27s_theorem" title="Sylow's theorem">Sylow's theorem</a> was reproven by Frobenius in 1887 directly from the laws of a finite group, although Frobenius remarked that the theorem followed from Cauchy's theorem on permutation groups and the fact that every finite group is a subgroup of a permutation group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200734_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200734-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Otto_H%C3%B6lder" title="Otto Hölder">Otto Hölder</a> was particularly prolific in this area, defining quotient groups in 1889, group automorphisms in 1893, as well as simple groups. He also completed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jordan%E2%80%93H%C3%B6lder_theorem" title="Jordan–Hölder theorem">Jordan–Hölder theorem</a>. Dedekind and Miller independently characterized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_group" title="Hamiltonian group">Hamiltonian groups</a> and introduced the notion of the <a href="/wiki/Commutator" title="Commutator">commutator</a> of two elements. Burnside, Frobenius, and Molien created the <a href="/wiki/Representation_theory" title="Representation theory">representation theory</a> of finite groups at the end of the nineteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200734_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200734-17">[17]</a></sup> J. A. de Séguier's 1904 monograph <i>Elements of the Theory of Abstract Groups</i> presented many of these results in an abstract, general form, relegating "concrete" groups to an appendix, although it was limited to finite groups. The first monograph on both finite and infinite abstract groups was O. K. Schmidt's 1916 <i>Abstract Theory of Groups</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200735_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200735-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_ring_theory">Early ring theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early ring theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory#History" title="Ring theory">Ring theory § History</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)#History" title="Ring (mathematics)">Ring (mathematics) § History</a></div>
<p>Noncommutative ring theory began with extensions of the complex numbers to <a href="/wiki/Hypercomplex_number" title="Hypercomplex number">hypercomplex numbers</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton" title="William Rowan Hamilton">William Rowan Hamilton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Quaternion" title="Quaternion">quaternions</a> in 1843. Many other number systems followed shortly. In 1844, Hamilton presented <a href="/wiki/Biquaternion" title="Biquaternion">biquaternions</a>, Cayley introduced <a href="/wiki/Octonion" title="Octonion">octonions</a>, and Grassman introduced <a href="/wiki/Exterior_algebra" title="Exterior algebra">exterior algebras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200742–43_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200742–43-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Cockle_(lawyer)" title="James Cockle (lawyer)">James Cockle</a> presented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tessarine" title="Tessarine">tessarines</a> in 1848<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coquaternion" title="Coquaternion">coquaternions</a> in 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford" title="William Kingdon Clifford">William Kingdon Clifford</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Split-biquaternion" title="Split-biquaternion">split-biquaternions</a> in 1873. In addition Cayley introduced <a href="/wiki/Group_ring" title="Group ring">group algebras</a> over the real and complex numbers in 1854 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_matrices" title="Square matrices">square matrices</a> in two papers of 1855 and 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200743_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200743-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Once there were sufficient examples, it remained to classify them. In an 1870 monograph, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Peirce" title="Benjamin Peirce">Benjamin Peirce</a> classified the more than 150 hypercomplex number systems of dimension below 6, and gave an explicit definition of an <a href="/wiki/Associative_algebra" title="Associative algebra">associative algebra</a>. He defined nilpotent and idempotent elements and proved that any algebra contains one or the other. He also defined the <a href="/wiki/Peirce_decomposition" title="Peirce decomposition">Peirce decomposition</a>. Frobenius in 1878 and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Charles Sanders Peirce</a> in 1881 independently proved that the only finite-dimensional division algebras over <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</p><p>In the 1850s, Riemann introduced the fundamental concept of a <a href="/wiki/Riemann_surface" title="Riemann surface">Riemann surface</a>. Riemann's methods relied on an assumption he called <a href="/wiki/Dirichlet%27s_principle" title="Dirichlet's principle">Dirichlet's principle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200754_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200754-29">[29]</a></sup> which in 1870 was questioned by Weierstrass. Much later, in 1900, Hilbert justified Riemann's approach by developing the <a href="/wiki/Direct_method_in_the_calculus_of_variations" title="Direct method in the calculus of variations">direct method in the calculus of variations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In the 1860s and 1870s, Clebsch, Gordan, Brill, and especially <a href="/wiki/Max_Noether" title="Max Noether">M. Noether</a> studied <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_function" title="Algebraic function">algebraic functions</a> and curves. In particular, Noether studied what conditions were required for a polynomial to be an element of the ideal generated by two algebraic curves in the polynomial ring <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} [x,y]}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>

</math></span><img alt="{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} [x,y]}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de78aea249c4a765d912587db125dfdbacc3d432" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:6.491ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, although Noether did not use this modern language. In 1882 Dedekind and Weber, in analogy with Dedekind's earlier work on algebraic number theory, created a theory of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_function_field" title="Algebraic function field">algebraic function fields</a> which allowed the first rigorous definition of a Riemann surface and a rigorous proof of the <a href="/wiki/Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Riemann–Roch theorem">Riemann–Roch theorem</a>. Kronecker in the 1880s, Hilbert in 1890, Lasker in 1905, and Macauley in 1913 further investigated the ideals of polynomial rings implicit in <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Noether" title="Emmy Noether">E. Noether</a>'s work. Lasker proved a special case of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lasker-Noether_theorem" title="Lasker-Noether theorem">Lasker-Noether theorem</a>, namely that every ideal in a polynomial ring is a finite intersection of <a href="/wiki/Primary_ideal" title="Primary ideal">primary ideals</a>. Macauley proved the uniqueness of this decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200754–57_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200754–57-31">[31]</a></sup> Overall, this work led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200742_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200742-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1801 Gauss introduced <a href="/wiki/Binary_quadratic_form" title="Binary quadratic form">binary quadratic forms</a> over the integers and defined their <a href="/wiki/Binary_quadratic_form#Equivalence" title="Binary quadratic form">equivalence</a>. He further defined the <a href="/wiki/Discriminant" title="Discriminant">discriminant</a> of these forms, which is an <a href="/wiki/Invariant_of_a_binary_form" title="Invariant of a binary form">invariant of a binary form</a>. Between the 1860s and 1890s <a href="/wiki/Invariant_theory" title="Invariant theory">invariant theory</a> developed and became a major field of algebra. Cayley, Sylvester, Gordan and others found the <a href="/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant" title="Jacobian matrix and determinant">Jacobian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hessian_matrix" title="Hessian matrix">Hessian</a> for binary quartic forms and cubic forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200757–58_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200757–58-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1868 Gordan proved that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graded_algebra" title="Graded algebra">graded algebra</a> of invariants of a binary form over the complex numbers was finitely generated, i.e., has a basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Hilbert wrote a thesis on invariants in 1885 and in 1890 showed that any form of any degree or number of variables has a basis. He extended this further in 1890 to <a href="/wiki/Hilbert%27s_basis_theorem" title="Hilbert's basis theorem">Hilbert's basis theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200758_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200758-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Once these theories had been developed, it was still several decades until an abstract ring concept emerged. The first axiomatic definition was given by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Fraenkel" title="Abraham Fraenkel">Abraham Fraenkel</a> in 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200758_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200758-34">[34]</a></sup> His definition was mainly the standard axioms: a set with two operations addition, which forms a group (not necessarily commutative), and multiplication, which is associative, distributes over addition, and has an identity element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In addition, he had two axioms on "regular elements" inspired by work on the <a href="/wiki/P-adic_number" title="P-adic number">p-adic numbers</a>, which excluded now-common rings such as the ring of integers. These allowed Fraenkel to prove that addition was commutative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Fraenkel's work aimed to transfer Steinitz's 1910 definition of fields over to rings, but it was not connected with the existing work on concrete systems. Masazo Sono's 1917 definition was the first equivalent to the present one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200758–59_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200758–59-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1920, <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Noether" title="Emmy Noether">Emmy Noether</a>, in collaboration with W. Schmeidler, published a paper about the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_theory" title="Ideal theory">theory of ideals</a> in which they defined <a href="/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory)" title="Ideal (ring theory)">left and right ideals</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">ring</a>. The following year she published a landmark paper called <i>Idealtheorie in Ringbereichen</i> (<i>Ideal theory in rings'</i>), analyzing <a href="/wiki/Ascending_chain_condition" title="Ascending chain condition">ascending chain conditions</a> with regard to (mathematical) ideals. The publication gave rise to the term "<a href="/wiki/Noetherian_ring" title="Noetherian ring">Noetherian ring</a>", and several other mathematical objects being called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noetherian_(disambiguation)" title="Noetherian (disambiguation)">Noetherian</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKimberling198118_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKimberling198118-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Noted algebraist <a href="/wiki/Irving_Kaplansky" title="Irving Kaplansky">Irving Kaplansky</a> called this work "revolutionary";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKimberling198118_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKimberling198118-38">[38]</a></sup> results which seemed inextricably connected to properties of polynomial rings were shown to follow from a single axiom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200759_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200759-40">[40]</a></sup> Artin, inspired by Noether’s work, came up with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descending_chain_condition" title="Descending chain condition">descending chain condition</a>. These definitions marked the birth of abstract ring theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleiner200760_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleiner200760-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_algebra">Modern algebra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern algebra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The end of the 19th and the beginning of the 20th century saw a shift in the methodology of mathematics. Abstract algebra emerged around the start of the 20th century, under the name <i>modern algebra</i>. Its study was part of the drive for  more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intellectual_rigor" title="Intellectual rigor">intellectual rigor</a> in mathematics. Initially, the assumptions in classical <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a>, on which the whole of mathematics (and major parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a>) depend, took the form of <a href="/wiki/Axiomatic_system" title="Axiomatic system">axiomatic systems</a>. No longer satisfied with establishing properties of concrete objects, mathematicians started to turn their attention to general theory. Formal definitions of certain <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> began to emerge in the 19th century. For example, results about various groups of permutations came to be seen as instances of general theorems that concern a general notion of an <i>abstract group</i>. Questions of structure and classification of various mathematical objects came to forefront.
</p><p>These processes were occurring throughout all of mathematics, but became especially pronounced in algebra. Formal definition through primitive operations and axioms were proposed for many basic algebraic structures, such as <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a>. Hence such things as <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">ring theory</a> took their places in <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>. The algebraic investigations of general fields by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Steinitz" title="Ernst Steinitz">Ernst Steinitz</a> and of commutative and then general rings by <a href="/wiki/David_Hilbert" title="David Hilbert">David Hilbert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emil_Artin" title="Emil Artin">Emil Artin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Noether" title="Emmy Noether">Emmy Noether</a>, building up on the work of <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Kummer" title="Ernst Kummer">Ernst Kummer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Kronecker" title="Leopold Kronecker">Leopold Kronecker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dedekind" title="Richard Dedekind">Richard Dedekind</a>, who had considered ideals in commutative rings, and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Frobenius" title="Georg Frobenius">Georg Frobenius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Issai_Schur" title="Issai Schur">Issai Schur</a>, concerning <a href="/wiki/Representation_theory" title="Representation theory">representation theory</a> of groups, came to define abstract algebra. These developments of the last quarter of the 19th century and the first quarter of 20th century were systematically exposed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartel_van_der_Waerden" title="Bartel van der Waerden">Bartel van der Waerden</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Moderne_Algebra" title="Moderne Algebra">Moderne Algebra</a></i>, the two-volume <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monograph</a> published in 1930–1931 that forever changed for the mathematical world the meaning of the word <i>algebra</i> from <i>the theory of equations</i> to the <i>theory of algebraic structures</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_concepts">Basic concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Basic concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">Algebraic structure</a></div>
<p>By abstracting away various amounts of detail, mathematicians have defined various algebraic structures that are used in many areas of mathematics. For instance, almost all systems studied are <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">sets</a>, to which the theorems of <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a> apply. Those sets that have a certain binary operation defined on them form <a href="/wiki/Magma_(algebra)" title="Magma (algebra)">magmas</a>, to which the concepts concerning magmas, as well those concerning sets, apply. We can add additional constraints on the  algebraic structure, such as associativity (to form <a href="/wiki/Semigroup" title="Semigroup">semigroups</a>); identity, and inverses (to form <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>); and other more complex structures. With additional structure, more theorems could be proved, but the generality is reduced. The "hierarchy" of algebraic objects (in terms of generality) creates a hierarchy of the corresponding theories: for instance, the theorems of <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> may be used when studying <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a> (algebraic objects that have two binary operations with certain axioms) since a ring is a group over one of its operations. In general there is a balance between the amount of generality and the richness of the theory: more general structures have usually fewer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nontrivial" title="Nontrivial">nontrivial</a> theorems and fewer applications.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of its generality, abstract algebra is used in many fields of mathematics and science. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a> uses algebraic objects to study topologies. The <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture" title="Poincaré conjecture">Poincaré conjecture</a>, proved in 2003, asserts that the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_group" title="Fundamental group">fundamental group</a> of a manifold, which encodes information about connectedness, can be used to determine whether a manifold is a sphere or not. <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory" title="Algebraic number theory">Algebraic number theory</a> studies various number <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a> that generalize the set of integers. Using tools of algebraic number theory, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Wiles" title="Andrew Wiles">Andrew Wiles</a> proved <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Fermat's Last Theorem">Fermat's Last Theorem</a>.
</p><p>In physics, groups are used to represent symmetry operations, and the usage of group theory could simplify differential equations. In <a href="/wiki/Gauge_theory" title="Gauge theory">gauge theory</a>, the requirement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_symmetry" title="Local symmetry">local symmetry</a> can be used to deduce the equations describing a system. The groups that describe those symmetries are <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">Lie groups</a>, and the study of Lie groups and Lie algebras reveals much about the physical system; for instance, the number of <a href="/wiki/Force_carrier" title="Force carrier">force carriers</a> in a theory is equal to the dimension of the Lie algebra, and these <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a> interact with the force they mediate if the Lie algebra is nonabelian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
