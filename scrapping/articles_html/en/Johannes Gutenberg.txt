<p><b>Johannes Gensfleisch zur Laden zum Gutenberg</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1400</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> – 3 February 1468) was a German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printer_(publisher)" title="Printer (publisher)">printer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publisher" title="Publisher">publisher</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Goldsmith" title="Goldsmith">goldsmith</a> who introduced <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> to Europe with his mechanical <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable-type</a> <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>. His work started the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printing_Revolution" title="Printing Revolution">Printing Revolution</a> in Europe and is regarded as a milestone of the second millennium, ushering in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_history" title="Modern history">modern period</a> of human history. It played a key role in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Revolution" title="Scientific Revolution">Scientific Revolution</a>, as well as laying the material basis for the modern <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_economy" title="Knowledge economy">knowledge-based economy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Democratization_of_knowledge" title="Democratization of knowledge">spread of learning to the masses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcLuhan1962_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcLuhan1962-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEisenstein1980_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEisenstein1980-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFebvreMartin1984_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFebvreMartin1984-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMan2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMan2002-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>While not the first to use movable type in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[a]</a></sup> Gutenberg was the first European to do so. His many contributions to printing include the invention of a process for mass-producing movable type; the use of oil-based <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a> for printing books;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESivulka19985_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESivulka19985-8">[7]</a></sup> adjustable molds;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> mechanical movable type; and the use of a wooden <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> similar to the agricultural <a href="/wiki/Screw_press" title="Screw press">screw presses</a> of the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> His truly epochal invention was the combination of these elements into a practical system that allowed the mass production of printed books and was economically viable for printers and readers alike. Gutenberg's method for making type is traditionally considered to have included a <a href="/wiki/Type_metal" title="Type metal">type metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Hand_mould#Printing" title="Hand mould">hand mould</a> for casting type. The alloy was a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> that melted at a relatively low temperature for faster and more economical casting, cast well, and created a durable type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELyons201156_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELyons201156-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of movable type was a marked improvement on the handwritten manuscript, which was the existing method of book production in Europe, and upon <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock printing</a>, and revolutionized European book-making. Gutenberg's <a href="/wiki/Global_spread_of_the_printing_press" title="Global spread of the printing press">printing technology spread</a> rapidly throughout Europe and later the world. His major work, the <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible" title="Gutenberg Bible">Gutenberg Bible</a> (also known as the 42-line Bible), was the first printed version of the Bible and has been acclaimed for its high aesthetic and technical quality. In Renaissance Europe, the arrival of mechanical movable type printing introduced the era of <a href="/wiki/Mass_communication" title="Mass communication">mass communication</a> which permanently altered the structure of society. The relatively unrestricted circulation of information—including revolutionary ideas—transcended borders, captured the masses in the Reformation, and threatened the power of political and religious authorities; the sharp increase in <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">literacy</a> broke the monopoly of the literate elite on education and learning and bolstered the emerging <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a>. Across Europe, the increasing cultural self-awareness of its people led to the rise of proto-<a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>, accelerated by the flowering of the European <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular languages</a> to the detriment of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>'s status as <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i>. In the 19th century, the replacement of the hand-operated Gutenberg-style press by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam-powered" title="Steam-powered">steam-powered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotary_press" title="Rotary press">rotary presses</a> allowed printing on an industrial scale, while Western-style printing was adopted all over the world, becoming practically the sole medium for modern bulk printing.
</p><p>An overview of the wide acclaim of Gutenberg's accomplishments is found in several sources:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_of_the_Millennium_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Man_of_the_Millennium-12">[11]</a></sup> In 1999, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%26E_Network" title="A&amp;E Network">A&amp;E Network</a> ranked Gutenberg no. 1 on their "People of the Millennium" countdown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> In 1997, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time%E2%80%93Life" title="Time–Life">Time–Life</a> magazine picked Gutenberg's invention as the most important of the second millennium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Four prominent U.S. journalists did the same in their 1998 resume, ranking his impact high in shaping the millennium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">Catholic Encyclopedia</a></i> describes Gutenberg's invention as having made a practically unparalleled cultural impact in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_era" title="Christian era">Christian era</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1708" data-file-width="1384" decoding="async" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg/220px-Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg/330px-Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg/440px-Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Gutenberg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gutenberg in a 16th-century copper engraving</div></div></div>
<p>Gutenberg was born in the German city of <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhine-Main_area" title="Rhine-Main area">Rhine-Main area</a>, the youngest son of the wealthy <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)#German_cities_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">patrician</a> merchant Friele Gensfleisch zur Laden, and his second wife, Else Wyrich, who was the daughter of a shopkeeper. It is assumed that he was baptized in the area close to his birthplace of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Christoph,_Mainz" title="St. Christoph, Mainz">St. Christoph</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> According to some accounts, Friele was a goldsmith for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Mainz" title="Archbishop of Mainz">bishop at Mainz</a>, but most likely, he was involved in the cloth trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benz_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-benz-18">[17]</a></sup> Gutenberg's year of birth is not precisely known, but it was sometime between the years of 1394 and 1404. In the 1890s the city of Mainz declared his official and symbolic date of birth to be 24 June 1400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childress_2008_62_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Childress_2008_62-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>John Lienhard, technology historian, says "Most of Gutenberg's early life is a mystery. His father worked with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecclesiastic" title="Ecclesiastic">ecclesiastic</a> mint. Gutenberg grew up knowing the trade of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldsmithing" title="Goldsmithing">goldsmithing</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lienhard_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lienhard-20">[19]</a></sup> This is supported by historian Heinrich Wallau, who adds, "In the 14th and 15th centuries his [ancestors] claimed a hereditary position as ... retainers of the household of the master of the archiepiscopal mint. In this capacity, they doubtless acquired considerable knowledge and technical skill in metal working. They supplied the mint with the metal to be coined, changed the various species of coins, and had a seat at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assize_Court" title="Assize Court">assizes</a> in forgery cases."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallau1910_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallau1910-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wallau adds, "His surname was derived from the house inhabited by his father and his paternal ancestors 'zu Laden, zu Gutenberg'. The house of Gänsfleisch was one of the patrician families of the town, tracing its lineage back to the thirteenth century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallau1910_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallau1910-21">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patricianship" title="Patricianship">Patricians</a> (the wealthy and political elite) in Mainz were often named after houses they owned. Around 1427, the name <i>zu Gutenberg</i>, after the family house in Mainz, is documented to have been used for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benz_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-benz-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1411, there was an uprising in Mainz against the patricians, and more than a hundred families were forced to leave. As a result, the Gutenbergs are thought to have moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eltville_am_Rhein" title="Eltville am Rhein">Eltville am Rhein</a> (Alta Villa), where his mother had an inherited estate. According to historian Heinrich Wallau, "All that is known of his youth is that he was not in Mainz in 1430. It is presumed that he migrated for political reasons to <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, where the family probably had connections."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallau1910_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallau1910-21">[20]</a></sup> He is assumed to have studied at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Erfurt" title="University of Erfurt">University of Erfurt</a>, where there is a record of the enrolment of a student called Johannes de Altavilla in 1418—Altavilla is the Latin form of Eltville am Rhein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartin1995217_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartin1995217-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDudley200878_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDudley200878-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nothing is now known of Gutenberg's life for the next fifteen years, but in March 1434, a letter by him indicates that he was living in Strasbourg, where he had some relatives on his mother's side. He also appears to have been a goldsmith member enrolled in the Strasbourg <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>. In 1437, there is evidence that he was instructing a wealthy tradesman on polishing gems, but where he had acquired this knowledge is unknown. In 1436/37 his name also comes up in court in connection with a broken promise of marriage to a woman from Strasbourg, Ennelin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-museum-24">[23]</a></sup> Whether the marriage actually took place is not recorded. Following his father's death in 1419, he is mentioned in the inheritance proceedings.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printing_press">Printing press</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Printer_in_1568-ce.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="972" data-file-width="754" decoding="async" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Printer_in_1568-ce.png/220px-Printer_in_1568-ce.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Printer_in_1568-ce.png/330px-Printer_in_1568-ce.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Printer_in_1568-ce.png/440px-Printer_in_1568-ce.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Printer_in_1568-ce.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Early wooden printing press, depicted in 1568. Such presses could produce up to 240 impressions per hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolf_1974,_67f._25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf_1974,_67f.-25">[24]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">Printing press</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_spread_of_the_printing_press" title="Global spread of the printing press">Global spread of the printing press</a></div>
<p>Around 1439, Gutenberg was involved in a financial misadventure making polished metal mirrors (which were believed to capture holy light from religious relics) for sale to pilgrims to <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>: in 1439 the city was planning to exhibit its collection of relics from <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Emperor Charlemagne</a> but the event was delayed by one year due to a severe flood and the capital already spent could not be repaid. 
</p><p>Until at least 1444 Gutenberg lived in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, most likely in the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Arbogast" title="Saint Arbogast">St. Arbogast</a> parish. It was in Strasbourg in 1440 that he is said to have perfected and unveiled the secret of printing based on his research, mysteriously entitled <i>Aventur und Kunst</i> (enterprise and art). It is not clear what work he was engaged in, or whether some early trials with printing from movable type were conducted there. After this, there is a gap of four years in the record. In 1448, he was back in Mainz, where he took out a loan from his brother-in-law <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Gelthus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arnold Gelthus (page does not exist)">Arnold Gelthus</a>, quite possibly for a printing press or related paraphernalia. By this date, Gutenberg may have been familiar with <a href="/wiki/Intaglio_(printmaking)" title="Intaglio (printmaking)">intaglio</a> printing; it is claimed that he had worked on <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engravings</a> with an artist known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_of_Playing_Cards" title="Master of Playing Cards">Master of Playing Cards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1062260506">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:220px; ; background-color: #c6dbf7;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>"All that has been written to me about that marvelous man seen at Frankfurt  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] is true. </p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">Future pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeneas_Sylvius_Piccolomini" title="Aeneas Sylvius Piccolomini">Pius II</a> in a letter to Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carvajal_(cardinal)" title="Juan Carvajal (cardinal)">Carvajal</a>, March 1455<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childress_2008_62_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Childress_2008_62-19">[18]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>By 1450, the press was in operation, and a <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">poem</a> had been printed, possibly the first item to be printed there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Gutenberg was able to convince the wealthy moneylender <a href="/wiki/Johann_Fust" title="Johann Fust">Johann Fust</a> for a loan of 800 <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">guilders</a>. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sch%C3%B6ffer" title="Peter Schöffer">Peter Schöffer</a>, who became Fust's son-in-law, also joined the enterprise. Schöffer had worked as a <a href="/wiki/Scribe" title="Scribe">scribe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and is believed to have designed some of the first <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a>.
</p><p>Gutenberg's workshop was set up at <a href="/wiki/Humbrechthof" title="Humbrechthof">Humbrechthof</a>, a property belonging to a distant relative. It is not clear when Gutenberg conceived the Bible project, but for this he borrowed another 800 guilders from Fust, and work commenced in 1452. At the same time, the press was also printing other, more lucrative texts (possibly <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> grammars). There is also some speculation that there were two presses: one for the pedestrian texts and one for the Bible. One of the profit-making enterprises of the new press was the printing of thousands of <a href="/wiki/Indulgence" title="Indulgence">indulgences</a> for the church, documented from 1454 to 1455.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1455 Gutenberg completed his <i>42-line Bible</i>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible" title="Gutenberg Bible">Gutenberg Bible</a>. About 180 copies were printed, most on paper and some on <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a>.
</p>

<p>Some time in 1456, there was a dispute between Gutenberg and Fust, and Fust demanded his money back, accusing Gutenberg of misusing the funds. Gutenberg's two rounds of financing from Fust, a total of 1,600 guilders at 6% interest, now amounted to 2,026 guilders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHessels1911_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHessels1911-29">[28]</a></sup> Fust sued at the archbishop's court. A November 1455 legal document records that there was a partnership for a "project of the books," the funds for which Gutenberg had used for other purposes, according to Fust. The court decided in favor of Fust, giving him control over the Bible printing workshop and half of all printed Bibles.
</p><p>Thus Gutenberg was effectively bankrupt, but it appears he retained (or restarted) a small printing shop, and participated in the printing of a Bible in the town of <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> around 1459, for which he seems at least to have supplied the type. But since his printed books never carry his name or a date, it is difficult to be certain, and there is consequently a considerable scholarly debate on this subject. It is also possible that the large <a href="/wiki/Catholicon_(trilingual_dictionary)" title="Catholicon (trilingual dictionary)"><i>Catholicon</i> dictionary</a>, 300 copies of 754 pages, printed in Mainz in 1460, was executed in his workshop.
</p><p>Meanwhile, the Fust–Schöffer shop was the first in Europe to bring out a book with the printer's name and date, the <i><a href="/wiki/Mainz_Psalter" title="Mainz Psalter">Mainz Psalter</a></i> of August 1457, and while proudly proclaiming the mechanical process by which it had been produced, it made no mention of Gutenberg.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440,_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1234" data-file-width="992" decoding="async" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440%2C_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG/220px-Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440%2C_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440%2C_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG/330px-Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440%2C_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440%2C_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG/440px-Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440%2C_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anonymous_portrait_of_Johannes_Gutenberg_dated_1440,_Gutenberg_Museum.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Anonymous portrait of Gutenberg dated 1440, <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg_Museum" title="Gutenberg Museum">Gutenberg Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1462, during the devastating <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Diocesan_Feud" title="Mainz Diocesan Feud">Mainz Diocesan Feud</a>, Mainz was sacked by <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a> <a href="/wiki/Adolph_II_of_Nassau" title="Adolph II of Nassau">Adolph von Nassau</a>. On 18 January 1465, Gutenberg's achievements were recognized by Archbishop von Nassau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallau1910_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallau1910-21">[20]</a></sup>  He was given the title <i>Hofmann</i> (gentleman of the court). This honor included a <a href="/wiki/Stipend" title="Stipend">stipend</a> and an annual court outfit, as well as 2,180 litres of grain and 2,000 litres of wine tax-free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESumner2009_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESumner2009-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gutenberg died in 1468 and was buried likely as a <a href="/wiki/Third_order" title="Third order">tertiary</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Franciscans" title="Franciscans">Franciscan</a> church at Mainz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallau1910_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallau1910-21">[20]</a></sup> This church and the cemetery were later destroyed, and Gutenberg's grave is now lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESumner2009_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESumner2009-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1504, he was mentioned as the inventor of <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">typography</a> in a book by Professor Ivo Wittig. It was not until 1567 that the first portrait of Gutenberg, almost certainly an imaginary reconstruction, appeared in Heinrich Pantaleon's biography of famous Germans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESumner2009_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESumner2009-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printed_books">Printed books</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible" title="Gutenberg Bible">Gutenberg Bible</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gutenberg_Bible.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="925" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Gutenberg_Bible.jpg/220px-Gutenberg_Bible.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Gutenberg_Bible.jpg/330px-Gutenberg_Bible.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Gutenberg_Bible.jpg/440px-Gutenberg_Bible.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gutenberg_Bible.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Gutenberg Bible</i>, <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>, Washington, D.C.</div></div></div>
<p>Between 1450 and 1455, Gutenberg printed several texts, some of which remain unidentified; his texts did not bear the printer's name or date, so attribution is possible only from typographical evidence and external references. Certainly several church documents including a papal letter and two <a href="/wiki/31-line_Indulgence" title="31-line Indulgence">indulgences</a> were printed, one of which was issued in Mainz. In view of the value of printing in quantity, seven editions in two styles were ordered, resulting in several thousand copies being printed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeggsPurvis2016435–436,_442–443_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeggsPurvis2016435–436,_442–443-31">[30]</a></sup> Some printed editions of <i>Ars Minor</i>, a schoolbook on Latin grammar by <a href="/wiki/Aelius_Donatus" title="Aelius Donatus">Aelius Donatus</a>, may have been printed by Gutenberg; these have been dated either 1451–52 or 1455.
</p><p>In 1455, Gutenberg completed copies of a beautifully executed folio <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> (<i>Biblia Sacra</i>), with 42 lines on each page. Copies sold for 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florin_(Italian_coin)" title="Florin (Italian coin)">florins</a> each,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECormackEde2004_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECormackEde2004-32">[31]</a></sup> which was roughly three years' wages for an average clerk. Nonetheless, it was much cheaper than a manuscript Bible that could take a single scribe over a year to prepare. After printing, some copies were <a href="/wiki/Rubrication" title="Rubrication">rubricated</a> or <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">hand-illuminated</a> in the same elegant way as manuscript Bibles from the same period.
</p><p>48 substantially complete copies are known to survive, including two at the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> that can be viewed and compared online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britLibrary_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-britLibrary-33">[32]</a></sup> The text lacks modern features such as page numbers, <a href="/wiki/Indentation_(typesetting)" title="Indentation (typesetting)">indentations</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paragraph_break" title="Paragraph break">paragraph breaks</a>.
</p><p>An undated <a href="/wiki/36-line_Bible" title="36-line Bible">36-line edition of the Bible</a> was printed, probably in <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> in 1458–60, possibly by Gutenberg. A large part of it was shown to have been set from a copy of Gutenberg's Bible, thus disproving earlier speculation that it was the earlier of the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKapr1996322_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKapr1996322-34">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printing_method_with_movable_type">Printing method with movable type</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metal_movable_type.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1520" data-file-width="2288" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Metal_movable_type.jpg/220px-Metal_movable_type.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Metal_movable_type.jpg/330px-Metal_movable_type.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Metal_movable_type.jpg/440px-Metal_movable_type.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metal_movable_type.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Movable metal type, and composing stick, descended from Gutenberg's press.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:95Thesen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="654" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/95Thesen.jpg/220px-95Thesen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/95Thesen.jpg/330px-95Thesen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/95Thesen.jpg/440px-95Thesen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:95Thesen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/95_Theses" title="95 Theses">95 Theses</a></i> which sparked off the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> in a print edition from 1522. Within the span of only two years, Luther's tracts were distributed in 300,000 printed copies throughout Germany and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="953" data-file-width="1121" decoding="async" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png/220px-European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png/330px-European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png/440px-European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:European_Output_of_Printed_Books_ca._1450%E2%80%931800.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>European output of books printed with movable types from Gutenberg to 1800<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBuringhvan_Zanden2009417Table_2_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuringhvan_Zanden2009417Table_2-36">[35]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Gutenberg's early printing process, and what texts he printed with <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a>, are not known in great detail. His later Bibles were printed in such a way as to have required large quantities of type, some estimates suggesting as many as 100,000 individual sorts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Setting each page would take, perhaps, half a day, and considering all the work in loading the press, inking the type, pulling the impressions, hanging up the sheets, distributing the type, etc., it is thought that the Gutenberg–Fust shop might have employed as many as 25 craftsmen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Where does this number come from? (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Gutenberg's technique of making movable type remains unclear. In the following decades, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punch_(typography)" title="Punch (typography)">punches</a> and copper matrices became standardized in the rapidly disseminating printing presses across Europe. Whether Gutenberg used this sophisticated technique or a somewhat primitive version has been the subject of considerable debate.
</p><p>In the standard process of making type, a hard metal punch (made by <a href="/wiki/Punchcutting" title="Punchcutting">punchcutting</a>, with the letter carved back to front) is hammered into a softer copper bar, creating a <i><a href="/wiki/Matrix_(printing)" title="Matrix (printing)">matrix</a></i>. This is then placed into a <a href="/wiki/Hand_mould" title="Hand mould">hand-held mould</a> and a piece of type, or "sort", is cast by filling the mould with molten type-metal; this cools almost at once, and the resulting piece of type can be removed from the mould. The matrix can be reused to create hundreds, or thousands, of identical sorts so that the same character appearing anywhere within the book will appear very uniform, giving rise, over time, to the development of distinct styles of typefaces or <a href="/wiki/Font" title="Font">fonts</a>. After casting, the sorts are arranged into type cases, and used to make up pages which are inked and printed, a procedure which can be repeated hundreds, or thousands, of times. The sorts can be reused in any combination, earning the process the name of "movable type". (For details, see <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">Typography</a>.)
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="540" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG/220px-Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG/330px-Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG/440px-Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Printing3_Walk_of_Ideas_Berlin.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Walk_of_Ideas" title="Walk of Ideas">"Modern Book Printing"</a> − a Berlin sculpture commemorating its inventor Gutenberg</div></div></div>
<p>The invention of the making of types with punch, matrix and mold has been widely attributed to Gutenberg. However, recent evidence suggests that Gutenberg's process was somewhat different. If he used the punch and matrix approach, all his letters should have been nearly identical, with some variation due to miscasting and inking. However, the type used in Gutenberg's earliest work shows other variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, the physicist <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Ag%C3%BCera_y_Arcas" title="Blaise Agüera y Arcas">Blaise Agüera y Arcas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> librarian Paul Needham, used digital scans of a <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">Papal bull</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scheide_Library" title="Scheide Library">Scheide Library</a>, Princeton, to carefully compare the same letters (types) appearing in different parts of the printed text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> The irregularities in Gutenberg's type, particularly in simple characters such as the hyphen, suggested that the variations could not have come either from ink smear or from wear and damage on the pieces of metal on the types themselves. Although some identical types are clearly used on other pages, other variations, subjected to detailed image analysis, suggested that they could not have been produced from the same matrix. Transmitted light pictures of the page also appeared to reveal substructures in the type that could not arise from traditional <a href="/wiki/Punchcutting" title="Punchcutting">punchcutting</a> techniques. They hypothesized that the method involved impressing simple shapes to create alphabets in "<a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a>" style in a matrix made of some soft material, perhaps sand. Casting the type would destroy the mould, and the matrix would need to be recreated to make each additional sort. This could explain the variations in the type, as well as the substructures observed in the printed images.
</p><p>Thus, they speculated that "the decisive factor for the birth of typography", the use of reusable moulds for casting type, was a more progressive process than was previously thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> They suggested that the additional step of using the punch to create a mould that could be reused many times was not taken until twenty years later, in the 1470s. Others have not accepted some or all of their suggestions, and have interpreted the evidence in other ways, and the truth of the matter remains uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENash200486–96_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENash200486–96-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_(1).tiff"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2771" data-file-width="4056" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_%281%29.tiff/lossy-page1-220px-Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_%281%29.tiff.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_%281%29.tiff/lossy-page1-330px-Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_%281%29.tiff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_%281%29.tiff/lossy-page1-440px-Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_%281%29.tiff.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mikl%C3%B3s_Andor_in_the_page-setting_room_of_Athenaeum_Printing_House_-_cca._1920_(1).tiff" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Page-setting room – c. 1920</div></div></div>
<p>A 1568 book <i>Batavia</i> by <a href="/wiki/Hadrianus_Junius" title="Hadrianus Junius">Hadrianus Junius</a> from <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">Holland</a> claims that the idea of the movable type came to Gutenberg from <a href="/wiki/Laurens_Janszoon_Coster" title="Laurens Janszoon Coster">Laurens Janszoon Coster</a> via Fust, who was apprenticed to Coster in the 1430s and may have brought some of his equipment from <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a> to Mainz. While Coster appears to have experimented with moulds and castable metal type, there is no evidence that he had actually printed anything with this technology. He was an inventor and a goldsmith. However, there is one indirect supporter of the claim that Coster might be the inventor. The author of the <i>Cologne Chronicle of 1499</i> quotes <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_Zell" title="Ulrich Zell">Ulrich Zell</a>, the first printer of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, that printing was performed in <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> in 1450, but that some type of printing of lower quality had previously occurred in the Netherlands. However, the chronicle does not mention the name of Coster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKapr1996322_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKapr1996322-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJuchhoff1950131f._42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJuchhoff1950131f.-42">[41]</a></sup> while it actually credits Gutenberg as the "first inventor of printing" in the very same passage (fol. 312). The first securely dated book by Dutch printers is from 1471,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJuchhoff1950131f._42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJuchhoff1950131f.-42">[41]</a></sup> and the Coster connection is today regarded as a mere legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 19th-century printer and typefounder <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fournier_Le_Jeune" title="Fournier Le Jeune">Fournier Le Jeune</a> suggested that Gutenberg was not using type cast with a reusable matrix, but wooden types that were carved individually. A similar suggestion was made by Nash in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENash200486–96_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENash200486–96-41">[40]</a></sup> This remains possible, albeit entirely unproven.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1062260506" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:230px; ; background-color: #c6dbf7;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>"What the world is today, good and bad, it owes to Gutenberg. Everything can be traced to this source, but we are bound to bring him homage, … for the bad that his colossal invention has brought about is overshadowed a thousand times by the good with which mankind has been favored."
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">American writer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> (1835–1910)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>

<p>Although Gutenberg was financially unsuccessful in his lifetime, the printing technologies spread quickly, and news and books began to travel across Europe much faster than before. It fed the growing <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, and since it greatly facilitated scientific publishing, it was a major catalyst for the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" title="Scientific revolution">scientific revolution</a>.
</p><p>The capital of printing in Europe shifted to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, where visionary printers like <a href="/wiki/Aldus_Manutius" title="Aldus Manutius">Aldus Manutius</a> ensured widespread availability of the major <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and Latin texts. The claims of an Italian origin for movable type have also focused on this rapid rise of Italy in movable-type printing. This may perhaps be explained by the prior eminence of Italy in the paper and printing trade. Additionally, Italy's economy was growing rapidly at the time, facilitating the spread of literacy. <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> had a geography book (printed with movable type) bought by his father. That book is in a Spanish museum, the <a href="/wiki/Instituci%C3%B3n_Colombina#Biblioteca_Colombina" title="Institución Colombina">Biblioteca Colombina</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>. Finally, the city of Mainz was sacked in 1462, driving many (including a number of printers and punch cutters) into exile.
</p><p>Printing was also a factor in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s  <i><a href="/wiki/Ninety-five_Theses" title="Ninety-five Theses">Ninety-five Theses</a></i> were printed and circulated widely; subsequently he issued <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheets</a> outlining his anti-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indulgences" title="Indulgences">indulgences</a> position (certificates of indulgences were one of the first items Gutenberg had printed). The broadsheet contributed to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a>.
</p><p>In the decades after Gutenberg, many conservative patrons looked down on cheap printed books; books produced by hand were considered more desirable.
</p><p>Today there is a large <a href="/wiki/Antique" title="Antique">antique</a> market for the earliest printed objects. Books printed prior to 1500 are known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incunabula" title="Incunabula">incunabula</a></i>.
</p><p>There are many statues of Gutenberg in Germany, including the famous one by <a href="/wiki/Bertel_Thorvaldsen" title="Bertel Thorvaldsen">Bertel Thorvaldsen</a> (1837) at <a href="/wiki/Gutenbergplatz_(Mainz)" title="Gutenbergplatz (Mainz)">Gutenbergplatz in Mainz</a>, home to the eponymous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg_University_of_Mainz" title="Johannes Gutenberg University of Mainz">Johannes Gutenberg University of Mainz</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg_Museum" title="Gutenberg Museum">Gutenberg Museum</a> on the history of early printing. The latter publishes the <i><a href="/wiki/Gutenberg-Jahrbuch" title="Gutenberg-Jahrbuch">Gutenberg-Jahrbuch</a></i>, the leading periodical in the field.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="987" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg/220px-Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg/330px-Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg/440px-Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gutenberg_Bible_1952_Issue-3c.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> stamp issued in 1952 commemorating the 500th anniversary of Gutenberg's first printed Bible</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Project_Gutenberg" title="Project Gutenberg">Project Gutenberg</a>, the oldest <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">digital library</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> commemorates Gutenberg's name. The <a href="/wiki/Johannisnacht,_Mainz" title="Johannisnacht, Mainz">Mainzer Johannisnacht</a> commemorates the person Johannes Gutenberg in his native city since 1968.
</p><p>In 1952, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> issued a five hundredth anniversary stamp commemorating Johannes Gutenberg invention of the movable-type printing press.
</p><p>In 1961 the Canadian philosopher and scholar <a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan" title="Marshall McLuhan">Marshall McLuhan</a> entitled his pioneering study in the fields of print culture, cultural studies, and media ecology, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gutenberg_Galaxy" title="The Gutenberg Galaxy">The Gutenberg Galaxy</a>: The Making of Typographic Man</i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="681" data-file-width="932" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg/220px-Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg/330px-Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg/440px-Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Featherbed_Alley_Printshop_Bermuda.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Gutenberg press replica at the <a href="/wiki/Featherbed_Alley_Printshop" title="Featherbed Alley Printshop">Featherbed Alley Printshop</a> Museum, in <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a></div></div></div>
<p>Regarded as one of the most influential people in human history, Gutenberg remains a towering figure in the popular image. In a 1978 book by a historian that purports to rank the 100 most influential persons in history, Gutenberg comes in at number 8, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%27sai_Lun" title="T'sai Lun">T'sai Lun</a> and before Christopher Columbus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> In 1999, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%26E_Network" title="A&amp;E Network">A&amp;E Network</a> ranked Gutenberg the No. 1 most influential person of the second millennium on their "Biographies of the Millennium" countdown. In 1997, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time%E2%80%93Life" title="Time–Life">Time–Life</a></i> magazine picked Gutenberg's invention as the most important of the second millennium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Man_of_the_Millennium_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Man_of_the_Millennium-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In space, he is commemorated in the name of the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> <a href="/wiki/777_Gutemberga" title="777 Gutemberga">777 Gutemberga</a>.
</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a> based on Gutenberg are <i>G, Being the Confession and Last Testament of Johannes Gensfleisch, also known as Gutenberg, Master Printer, formerly of Strasbourg and Mainz</i>, from 2001, with music by <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Bryars" title="Gavin Bryars">Gavin Bryars</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> and <i>La Nuit de Gutenberg</i>, with music by <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Manoury" title="Philippe Manoury">Philippe Manoury</a>, premiered in 2011 in Strasbourg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, <a href="/wiki/WordPress" title="WordPress">WordPress</a> named its new editing system Gutenberg in tribute to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
