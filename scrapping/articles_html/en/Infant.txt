<p>An <b>infant</b> (from the Latin word <i>infans</i>, meaning 'unable to speak' or 'speechless') is a formal or specialised synonym for the common term <i><b>baby</b></i>, meaning the very young <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_beings" title="Human beings">human beings</a>. The term may also be used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">juveniles</a> of other organisms. A <b>newborn</b> is, in colloquial use, an infant who is only hours, days, or up to one month old. In medical contexts, newborn or <b>neonate</b> (from Latin, <i>neonatus</i>, newborn) refers to an infant in the first 28 days after birth;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwn-1">[1]</a></sup> the term applies to <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">premature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy#Term" title="Pregnancy">full term</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Postterm_pregnancy" title="Postterm pregnancy">postmature</a> infants.
</p><p>Before birth, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a></i> is used. The term <i>infant</i> is typically applied to very young children under one year of age; however, definitions may vary and may include children up to two years of age. When a human child learns to walk, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Toddler" title="Toddler">toddler</a></i> may be used instead.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, an <i><a href="/wiki/Infant_school" title="Infant school">infant school</a></i> is for children aged between four and seven.
</p><p>As a legal term, <i>infancy</i> is more like being a <a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">minor</a>, and continues until a person reaches 18 years of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infancy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-infancy-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A newborn's shoulders and hips are wide, the abdomen protrudes slightly, and the arms and legs are relatively long with respect to the rest of their body.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Head">Head</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baby_Face.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2764" data-file-width="3809" decoding="async" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Baby_Face.JPG/220px-Baby_Face.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Baby_Face.JPG/330px-Baby_Face.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Baby_Face.JPG/440px-Baby_Face.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baby_Face.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eight-month-old infant; as a common feature, eyes are usually large in relation to the face.</div></div></div><p>A newborn's head is very large in proportion to the body, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_cranium" title="Human cranium">cranium</a> is enormous relative to his or her face. While the adult human skull is about one seventh of the total body length, the newborn's is about <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020198016">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">1</span>⁄<span class="den">4</span></span>. Normal head circumference for a full-term infant is 33–36 cm at birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At birth, many regions of the newborn's skull have not yet been converted to bone, leaving "soft spots" known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fontanel" title="Fontanel">fontanels</a>. The two largest are the diamond-shaped anterior fontanel, located at the top front portion of the head, and the smaller triangular-shaped posterior fontanel, which lies at the back of the head. Later in the child's life, these bones will fuse together in a natural process. A protein called <a href="/wiki/Noggin_(protein)" title="Noggin (protein)">noggin</a> is responsible for the delay in an infant's skull fusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">labour</a> and birth, the infant's skull changes shape to fit through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_canal" title="Birth canal">birth canal</a>, sometimes causing the child to be born with a misshapen or elongated head. It will usually return to normal on its own within a few days or weeks. Special exercises sometimes advised by <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physicians</a> may assist the process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hair">Hair</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg/220px-Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg/330px-Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg/440px-Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baby_hairy_head_DSCN2483.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brown thin hair of an one-year-old infant girl</div></div></div>
<p>Some newborns have a fine, downy body hair called <a href="/wiki/Lanugo" title="Lanugo">lanugo</a>. It may be particularly noticeable on the back, shoulders, forehead, ears and face of premature infants. Lanugo disappears within a few weeks. Infants may be born with full heads of hair; others, particularly <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">caucasian</a> infants, may have very fine hair or may even be bald. Amongst fair-skinned parents, this fine hair may be blonde, even if the parents are not. An infant's hair color and texture can change: red can give way to blond, curly can go straight, and thick, dark hair could reappear a lot sparser and lighter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scalp" title="Scalp">scalp</a> may also be temporarily <a href="/wiki/Bruise" title="Bruise">bruised</a> or swollen, especially in hairless newborns, and the area around the eyes may be puffy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Length">Length</span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a>, the average total body length of a newborn is 35.6–50.8 cm (14.0–20.0 in), although premature newborns may be much smaller.
</p><p>The way to measure a baby's length is to lay the baby down and stretch a measuring tape from the top of the head to the bottom of the heel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight">Weight</span></h3>
<p>In developed countries, the average <a href="/wiki/Birth_weight" title="Birth weight">birth weight</a> of a full-term newborn is approximately <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1020198016" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>3.4 kg (<span class="frac" role="math">7<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="num">1</span>⁄<span class="den">2</span></span> lb), and is typically in the range of 2.7–4.6 kg (6.0–10.1 lb).
</p><p>Over the first 5–7 days following birth, the body weight of a term neonate decreases by 3–7%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohamed2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mohamed2012-5">[5]</a></sup> and is largely a result of the resorption and urination of the fluid that initially fills the lungs, in addition to a delay of often a few days before <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a> becomes effective. After the first week, healthy term neonates should gain 10–20 grams/day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohamed2012_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mohamed2012-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skin">Skin</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HumanNewborn.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1011" data-file-width="1485" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/HumanNewborn.JPG/220px-HumanNewborn.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/HumanNewborn.JPG/330px-HumanNewborn.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/HumanNewborn.JPG/440px-HumanNewborn.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HumanNewborn.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A newborn infant, seconds after <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">delivery</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amniotic_fluid" title="Amniotic fluid">Amniotic fluid</a> glistens on her skin, and the <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cord" title="Umbilical cord">umbilical cord</a> is still attached.</div></div></div>
<p>Immediately after birth, a newborn's skin is often grayish to dusky blue in color. As soon as the newborn begins to breathe, usually within a minute or two, the skin's color reaches its normal tone. Newborns are wet, covered in streaks of blood, and coated with a white substance known as <a href="/wiki/Vernix_caseosa" title="Vernix caseosa">vernix caseosa</a>, which is hypothesised to act as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibacterial</a> barrier. The newborn may also have <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_spot" title="Mongolian spot">Mongolian spots</a>, various other <a href="/wiki/Birthmark" title="Birthmark">birthmarks</a>, or peeling skin, particularly on the wrists, hands, ankles, and feet.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Umbilical_cord">Umbilical cord</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_extrauterine_life" title="Adaptation to extrauterine life">Adaptation to extrauterine life</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cord" title="Umbilical cord">umbilical cord</a> of a newborn is bluish-white in color. After birth, the umbilical cord is normally cut, leaving a 1–2 inch stub. The umbilical stub will dry out, shrivel, darken, and spontaneously fall off within about 3 weeks. This will later become a belly-button after it heals.
</p><p>The umbilical cord contains three vessels: two arteries and one vein. The two arteries carry blood from the baby to the placenta while one vein carries blood back to the baby.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genitals">Genitals</span></h3>
<p>A newborn's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitals" title="Genitals">genitals</a> are enlarged and reddened, with male infants having an unusually large <a href="/wiki/Scrotum" title="Scrotum">scrotum</a>. The breasts may also be enlarged, even in male infants. This is caused by naturally occurring maternal hormones and is a temporary condition. Females (and even males) may actually discharge milk from their nipples (sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Witch%27s_milk" title="Witch's milk">witch's milk</a>), or a bloody or milky-like substance from the vagina. In either case, this is considered normal and will disappear with time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Care_and_feeding">Care and feeding</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Newborn_care_and_safety" title="Newborn care and safety">Newborn care and safety</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infant_bathing" title="Infant bathing">Infant bathing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="734" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif/220px-%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif/330px-%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif/440px-%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%E5%88%9D%E5%9C%9F%E4%BF%B5%E5%85%A52.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A crying infant in Japan in the <a href="/wiki/Naki_Sumo_Crying_Baby_Festival" title="Naki Sumo Crying Baby Festival">Naki Sumo Crying Baby Festival</a></div></div></div>
<p>Infants <a href="/wiki/Infant_crying" title="Infant crying">cry</a> as a form of basic instinctive communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A crying infant may be trying to express a variety of feelings including hunger, discomfort, overstimulation, boredom, wanting something, or loneliness.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">Breastfeeding</a> is the recommended method of feeding by all major infant health organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAP_Policy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAP_Policy-7">[7]</a></sup> If breastfeeding is not possible or desired, bottle feeding is done with expressed breast-milk or with <a href="/wiki/Infant_formula" title="Infant formula">infant formula</a>. Infants are born with a sucking reflex allowing them to extract the milk from the <a href="/wiki/Nipple" title="Nipple">nipples</a> of the breasts or the nipple of the <a href="/wiki/Baby_bottle" title="Baby bottle">baby bottle</a>, as well as an instinctive behavior known as <i>rooting</i> with which they seek out the nipple. Sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Wet_nurse" title="Wet nurse">wet nurse</a> is hired to feed the infant, although this is rare, especially in developed countries.
</p><p>Adequate food consumption at an early age is vital for an infant's development. The foundations of optimum health, growth, and neurodevelopment across the lifespan are established in the <a href="/wiki/First_1000_days" title="First 1000 days">first 1000 days</a> of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> From birth to six months, infants should consume only breast milk or an unmodified milk substitute. As an infant's diet matures, finger foods may be introduced as well as fruit, vegetables and small amounts of meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>As infants grow, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_supplement" title="Food supplement">food supplements</a> can be added. Many parents choose commercial, ready-made <a href="/wiki/Baby_food" title="Baby food">baby foods</a> to supplement breast milk or formula for the child, while others adapt their usual meals for the dietary needs of their child. Whole cow's <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> can be used at one year, but lower-fat milk is not recommended until the child is 2 to 3 years old. Weaning is the process through which breast milk is eliminated from the infant's diet through the introduction of solid foods in exchange for milk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Until they are toilet-trained, infants in industrialized countries wear <a href="/wiki/Diaper" title="Diaper">diapers</a>. The transition from diapers to training pants is an important transition in the development of an infant/baby to that of a toddler. Children need more sleep than adults—up to 18 hours for newborn babies, with a declining rate as the child ages. Until babies learn to walk, they are carried in the arms, held in slings or baby carriers, or transported in baby carriages or strollers. Most industrialized countries have laws requiring <a href="/wiki/Child_safety_seat" title="Child safety seat">child safety seats</a> for babies in motor vehicles.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_touch">Benefits of touch</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Georgia_National_Guard_(38100696265).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3989" data-file-width="5595" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Georgia_National_Guard_%2838100696265%29.jpg/220px-Georgia_National_Guard_%2838100696265%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Georgia_National_Guard_%2838100696265%29.jpg/330px-Georgia_National_Guard_%2838100696265%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Georgia_National_Guard_%2838100696265%29.jpg/440px-Georgia_National_Guard_%2838100696265%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Georgia_National_Guard_(38100696265).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A baby being hugged by a <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldier</a></div></div></div>
<p>Experiments have been done with infants up to four months of age using both positive touch (stroking or <a href="/wiki/Hug" title="Hug">cuddling</a>) and negative touch (poking, pinching, or tickling). The infants who received the positive touch cried less often and vocalized and smiled more than the infants who were touched negatively.  Infants who were the recipients of negative touch have also been linked with emotional and behavioral problems later in life. A lower amount of physical violence in adults has been discovered in cultures with greater levels of positive physical touching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_development">Language development</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_(50879866102).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3180" data-file-width="2970" decoding="async" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_%2850879866102%29.jpg/220px-A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_%2850879866102%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_%2850879866102%29.jpg/330px-A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_%2850879866102%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_%2850879866102%29.jpg/440px-A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_%2850879866102%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Proper_Space_Book_for_Babies_(50879866102).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An infant holding a space-themed <a href="/wiki/Baby_book" title="Baby book">baby book</a></div></div></div>
<p>Caregivers of an infant are advised to pick up on the infant's facial expressions and mirror them. Reproducing and empathizing with their facial expressions enables infants to experience effectiveness and to recognize their own actions more easily (see <a href="/wiki/Mirror_neuron" title="Mirror neuron">mirror neurons</a>). Exaggeratedly reproduced facial expressions and gestures are recommended, as they are clearer forms of expression. The baby's babbling should also be picked up and repeated. By imitating each other's sounds the first simple dialogues are initiated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasten_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kasten-12">[12]</a></sup> Accentuated pronunciation and melodic <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">intonation</a> make it easier to recognize individual words in a sentence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, it is not advisable to use simplified "<a href="/wiki/Baby_talk" title="Baby talk">baby talk</a>" (e.g. "Did you 'ouch'?" instead of, "Did you hurt yourself?").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Even if parents cannot yet understand infants' babbling, a timely response by parents to babbling leads to faster language acquisition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup> This was confirmed by researchers who first studied mothers' behavior towards 8-month-old infants and later tested the infants' vocabulary when they were 15 months old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> A first important development of infants is the discovery that they can influence their parents through babbling (development of intentional communication).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> Parents can encourage this by engaging with their infants in babbling. This in turn promotes further language development, as infants then turn to their parents more often.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Previous studies have shown that the infant's speech is encouraged when parents, for example, smile in the infant's direction or touch the infant every time the infant looks at them and babbles. It also helps if parents respond to what they think their baby is saying (for example, giving a ball or commenting when the baby looks at the ball and babbles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup> Responding to sounds produced when the baby looks at an object (object-directed vocalizations) thus provide an opportunity to learn the name of the object. In this way, babies also learn that sounds are associated with objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> However, language development is only achieved if parents react positively (e.g. smile) in response to the infant's babbling. A high response rate without a connection to the infant's utterances does not lead to language promotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> It is detrimental to language development if a mother instead tries to divert the infant's attention to something else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid30387273_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid30387273-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep">Sleep</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/SIDS" title="SIDS">SIDS</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sleeping_baby_(6410829477).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Sleeping_baby_%286410829477%29.jpg/220px-Sleeping_baby_%286410829477%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Sleeping_baby_%286410829477%29.jpg/330px-Sleeping_baby_%286410829477%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Sleeping_baby_%286410829477%29.jpg/440px-Sleeping_baby_%286410829477%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sleeping_baby_(6410829477).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A sleeping infant</div></div></div>
<p>A 2018 review analysed 146 studies on infant sleep behavior and listed several factors that show an effect on sleep duration and the number of night wakings. For example, stimulating daytime activities, reading aloud before sleeping, early bedtime, a sleep routine, and avoiding television and media exposure before bedtime are associated with longer sleep duration and fewer nighttime awakenings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: S. 24">: S. 24 </span></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, strong parental involvement at falling asleep is associated with shorter sleep duration, slower falling asleep and more frequent night-time awakenings in the studies analysed. Strong parental involvement is understood to include parental presence, cradling, or breastfeeding at bedtime, as well as carrying the infant to sleep and then putting the infant down. Strong parental involvement has a negative effect on infant sleep because the infant cannot develop the ability to self-soothe. On the other hand, low parental involvement at bedtime gives the infant room to learn self-soothing and self-regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: S. 24">: S. 24 </span></sup>
</p><p>In 2020, a Finnish study established (according to the research leader) for the first time a reference value for infant sleep quality based on a large <a href="/wiki/Data_set" title="Data set">data set</a> (about 5,700 babies).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid32087408_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid32087408-20">[20]</a></sup> Almost 40% of the participating parents with eight-month-old babies said they were worried about their sleep. In fact, sleep problems were common; however, children fall asleep faster, wake up less often during the night and stay awake less late at night the older they get. At the same time, total sleep time decreases.
</p><p>The study was also able to determine reference values for normal sleep (see table). Children who sleep significantly less than average would usually benefit from supportive measures, for which a number of methods would be available (a discussion with the pediatrician or see, for example, the article on <a href="/wiki/Infant_sleep_training" title="Infant sleep training">sleep training</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maternal_sensitivity">Maternal sensitivity</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maternal_sensitivity" title="Maternal sensitivity">Maternal sensitivity</a> plays a particular role in the relationship with the infant and for favorable emotional development. This means being attentive to the infant's behavioral expressions, not misinterpreting the infant's expressions because of one's own moods, reacting immediately to the situation and finding a response that is appropriate to the context and the expressed needs. A secure <a href="/wiki/Attachment_theory" title="Attachment theory">attachment</a> is promoted through empathetic and adequate as well as prompt responses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KS2010_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-KS2010-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson2006_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson2006-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasten_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kasten-12">[12]</a></sup> In accordance with their basic needs, infants show an inborn behavior of seeking closeness to the mother – or to another primary caregiver – and thus in turn foster an attachment. When separated from the mother, infants protest by crying and by body movements.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Umwotsi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3840" data-file-width="5760" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Umwotsi.jpg/220px-Umwotsi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Umwotsi.jpg/330px-Umwotsi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Umwotsi.jpg/440px-Umwotsi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Umwotsi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An infant from Uganda playing with stoves</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Babywearing" title="Babywearing">Wearing</a> has a calming effect on infants. A 2013 study showed that infants placed in a <a href="/wiki/Cradle_(bed)" title="Cradle (bed)">cradle</a> cried and kicked more often and had an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Increased_heart_rate" title="Increased heart rate">increased heart rate</a> (so the infants were stressed), while those picked up and carried by the mother while walking around calmed down significantly. The effect of being held motionless in the arm was intermediate between that of being carried around and that of being put down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23602481_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23602481-23">[23]</a></sup> That carrying (e.g., in a <a href="/wiki/Baby_sling" title="Baby sling">baby sling</a>) makes infants more content and makes them cry less had already been shown in a randomized study in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>For infant feeding, <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a> is recommended by all major infant health organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAP_Policy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-AAP_Policy-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airlines" title="Airlines">airlines</a> refuse boarding for all babies aged under 7 days (for domestic flights) or 14 days for international flights. <a href="/wiki/Asiana_Airlines" title="Asiana Airlines">Asiana Airlines</a> allows babies to board international flights at 7 days of age. <a href="/wiki/Garuda_Indonesia" title="Garuda Indonesia">Garuda Indonesia</a> disallows all babies under the age of 14 days to board any flights.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> allows infants to travel when they are less than 7 days old when they present a physician travel approval letter. Skywest will not allow an infant less than 8 days old on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emotional_development">Emotional development</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:224px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg/222px-Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg/333px-Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg/444px-Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg 2x" width="222"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Being_a_twin_means_you_always_have_a_pillow_or_blanket_handy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eight-month-old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternal_twin" title="Fraternal twin">sororal twin</a> sisters</div></div></div>
<p>Attachment theory is primarily an <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">evolutionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethological</a> theory whereby the infant or child <i>seeks proximity</i> to a <i>specified attachment figure</i> in situations of alarm or distress for the purpose of survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tronick_et_al._26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tronick_et_al.-26">[26]</a></sup>  The forming of attachments is considered to be the foundation of the infant/child's capacity to form and conduct relationships throughout life. Attachment is not the same as love or affection although they often go together. Attachment and attachment <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behaviors</a> tend to develop between the age of 6 months and 3 years. Infants become <a href="/wiki/Attachment_in_children" title="Attachment in children">attached</a> to adults who are sensitive and responsive in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_interaction" title="Social interaction">social interactions</a> with the infant, and who remain as consistent caregivers for some time. Parental responses lead to the development of patterns of attachment, which in turn lead to 'internal working models' which will guide the individual's feelings, thoughts, and expectations in later relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bretherton_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bretherton-27">[27]</a></sup> There are a number of <a href="/wiki/Attachment_in_children" title="Attachment in children">attachment 'styles'</a> namely 'secure', 'anxious-ambivalent', 'anxious-avoidant', (all 'organized') and 'disorganized', some of which are more problematic than others. A lack of attachment or a seriously disrupted capacity for attachment could potentially amount to serious disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Infants develop distinct relationships to their mothers, fathers, siblings, and non-familial caregivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Beside the dyadic attachment relationships also a good quality of the triadic relationships (mother – father – infant) is important for infant mental health development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10216817_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10216817-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid28682511_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid28682511-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_to_sounds">Response to sounds</span></h3>
<p>Infants respond to the sound of snake hissing, angry voices of adults, the crackling sound of a fire, thunder, and the cries of other infants. They have a drop in heart rate, their eyes blinking, increased turning toward the speakers or parent, all of these indicating that they were paying more attention. This is believed to be an evolutionary response to danger. Babies' ability to accurately locate sounds is refined during their first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality">Mortality</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">Infant mortality</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:218px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Babyimmunization.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="341" data-file-width="216" decoding="async" height="341" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Babyimmunization.jpg" width="216"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Babyimmunization.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An infant being immunized in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a></div></div></div>
<p>Infant mortality is the death of an infant in the first year of life, often expressed as the number of deaths per 1000 live births (infant mortality rate). Major causes of infant mortality include <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital malformation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudden_infant_death_syndrome" title="Sudden infant death syndrome">SIDS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiological</a> indicator is recognized as a very important measure of the level of health care in a country because it is directly linked with the <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> status of infants, children, and pregnant women as well as access to medical care, socioeconomic conditions, and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a positive relationship between national wealth and good health. The rich and industrialized countries of the world, prominently <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the United Kingdom, the United States, and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, spend a large proportion of their wealthy budget on the health care system. As, a result, their health care systems are very sophisticated, with many physicians, nurses, and other health care experts servicing the population. Thus, infant mortality is low. On the other hand, a country such as Mexico, which spends disproportionately less of its budget on healthcare, suffers from high mortality rates. This is because the general population is likely to be less healthy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In the U.S., infant mortality rates are especially high in minority groups. For instance, non-Hispanic black women have an infant mortality rate of 13.63 per 1000 live births whereas in non-Hispanic white women it was much lower at a rate of 5.76 per 1000 live births.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The average infant mortality rate in the U.S. is 6.8 per 1000 live births.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Babyhood">Babyhood</span></h2>
<p>Babyhood is a critical period in <a href="/wiki/Personality_development" title="Personality development">personality development</a> when the foundations of adult personality are laid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In contrast <a href="/wiki/Toddler" title="Toddler">toddler</a> is used to denote a baby that has achieved relative independence, in moving about, and feeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>

Supporters of the CCC model generally recognize that the infant and caregiver are able to adjust to a range of conditions, but they consider the adjustments observed to reflect biological variation.
