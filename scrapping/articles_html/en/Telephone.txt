<p>A <b>telephone</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a> device that permits two or more users to conduct a <a href="/wiki/Conversation" title="Conversation">conversation</a> when they are too far apart to be easily heard directly. A telephone converts <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a>, typically and most efficiently the <a href="/wiki/Human_voice" title="Human voice">human voice</a>, into electronic <a href="/wiki/Signal" title="Signal">signals</a> that are transmitted via <a href="/wiki/Electrical_cable" title="Electrical cable">cables</a> and other communication channels to another telephone which reproduces the sound to the receiving user. The term is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">τῆλε</span> (<i>tēle</i>, <i>far</i>) and φωνή (<i>phōnē</i>, <i>voice</i>), together meaning <i>distant voice</i>. A common short form of the term is <i><b>phone</b></i>, which came into use early in the telephone's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1876, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a> was the first to be granted a United States <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> for a device that produced clearly intelligible replication of the human voice at a second device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This instrument was further developed by many others, and became rapidly indispensable in <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">households</a>.
</p><p>The essential elements of a telephone are a <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> (<i>transmitter</i>) to speak into and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earphone" title="Earphone">earphone</a> (<i>receiver</i>) which reproduces the voice at a distant location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The receiver and transmitter are usually built into a <a href="/wiki/Handset" title="Handset">handset</a> which is held up to the ear and mouth during conversation. The transmitter converts the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound waves</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_(information_theory)" title="Signal (information theory)">electrical signals</a> which are sent through  the telecommunication system to the receiving telephone, which converts the signals into audible sound in the receiver or sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeaker</a>. Telephones permit transmission in both directions simultaneously.
</p><p>Most telephones also contain an alerting feature, such as a <i>ringer</i> or a visual indicator, to announce an incoming telephone call. Telephone calls are initiated most commonly with a keypad or dial, affixed to the telephone, to enter a <a href="/wiki/Telephone_number" title="Telephone number">telephone number</a>, which is the address of the call recipient's telephone in the telecommunication system, but other methods existed in the early history of the telephone.
</p><p>The first telephones were directly connected to each other from one customer's office or residence to another customer's location. Being impractical beyond just a few customers, these systems were quickly replaced by manually operated centrally located <a href="/wiki/Telephone_switchboard" title="Telephone switchboard">switchboards</a>. These exchanges were soon connected together, eventually forming an automated, worldwide <a href="/wiki/Public_switched_telephone_network" title="Public switched telephone network">public switched telephone network</a>. For greater mobility, various radio systems were developed for transmission between mobile stations on ships and automobiles in the mid-20th century. Hand-held <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a> were introduced for personal service starting in 1973. In later decades, their analog cellular system evolved into digital networks with greater capability and lower cost.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Technological_convergence#Cell_phone_convergence" title="Technological convergence">Convergence in communication services</a> has provided a broad spectrum of capabilities in cell phones, including mobile computing, giving rise to the <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a>, the dominant type of telephone in the world today.
</p>

<p>Before the development of the electric telephone, the term <i>telephone</i> was applied to other inventions, and not all early researchers of the electrical device used the term. Perhaps the earliest use of the word for a communications system was the <i>telephon</i> created by Gottfried Huth in 1796. Huth proposed an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Optical_telegraph" title="Optical telegraph">optical telegraph</a> of <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chappe" title="Claude Chappe">Claude Chappe</a> in which the operators in the signaling towers would shout to each other by means of what he called "speaking tubes", but would now be called giant <a href="/wiki/Megaphone" title="Megaphone">megaphones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A communication device for sailing vessels, called <i>telephone</i>, was invented by Captain John Taylor in 1844. This instrument used four <a href="/wiki/Air_horn" title="Air horn">air horns</a> to communicate with vessels in foggy weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_Reis" title="Johann Philipp Reis">Johann Philipp Reis</a> used the term in reference to his invention, commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Reis_telephone" title="Reis telephone">Reis telephone</a>, in c. 1860. His device appears to be the first device based on the conversion of sound into electrical impulses.
</p><p>The term <i>telephone</i> was adopted into the vocabulary of many languages. It is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">τῆλε</span>, <i>tēle</i>, "far" and φωνή, <i>phōnē</i>, "voice", together meaning "distant voice".
</p><p>Credit for the invention of the electric telephone is frequently disputed. As with other influential <a href="/wiki/Invention" title="Invention">inventions</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Invention_of_radio" title="Invention of radio">radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_television" title="History of television">television</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb#History" title="Incandescent light bulb">light bulb</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_computing_hardware" title="History of computing hardware">computer</a>, several inventors pioneered experimental work on <i>voice transmission over a wire</i> and improved on each other's ideas. New controversies over the issue still arise from time to time. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bourseul" title="Charles Bourseul">Charles Bourseul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Meucci" title="Antonio Meucci">Antonio Meucci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_Reis" title="Johann Philipp Reis">Johann Philipp Reis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elisha_Gray" title="Elisha Gray">Elisha Gray</a>, amongst others, have all been credited with the invention of the telephone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alexander Graham Bell was the first to be awarded a patent for the electric telephone by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">United States Patent and Trademark Office</a> (USPTO) in March 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-10">[10]</a></sup> Before Bell's patent, the telephone transmitted sound in a way that was similar to the telegraph. This method used vibrations and circuits to send electrical pulses, but was missing key features. Bell found that this method produced a sound through intermittent currents, but in order for the telephone to work a fluctuating current reproduced sounds the best. The fluctuating currents became the basis for the working telephone, creating Bell's patent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> That first patent by Bell was the <i>master patent</i> of the telephone, from which other patents for electric telephone devices and features flowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Bell patents were forensically victorious and commercially decisive.
</p><p>In 1876, shortly after Bell's patent application, Hungarian engineer <a href="/wiki/Tivadar_Pusk%C3%A1s" title="Tivadar Puskás">Tivadar Puskás</a> proposed the telephone switch, which allowed for the formation of <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchanges</a>, and eventually networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, <i>the blower</i> is used as a slang term for a telephone. The term came from navy slang for a <a href="/wiki/Speaking_tube" title="Speaking tube">speaking tube</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the U.S., a somewhat dated slang term refers to the telephone as "the horn," as in "I couldn't get him on the horn," or "I'll be off the horn in a moment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<ul><li>1844: <a href="/wiki/Innocenzo_Manzetti" title="Innocenzo Manzetti">Innocenzo Manzetti</a> first mooted the idea of a "speaking telegraph" or telephone. Use of the "speaking telegraph" and "sound telegraph" monikers would eventually be replaced by the newer, distinct name, "telephone".</li>
<li>26 August 1854: <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bourseul" title="Charles Bourseul">Charles Bourseul</a> published an article in the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Illustration" title="L'Illustration">L'Illustration</a></i> (Paris): "Transmission électrique de la parole" (electric transmission of speech), describing a "make-and-break" type telephone transmitter later created by Johann Reis.</li>
<li>26 October 1861: <a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_Reis" title="Johann Philipp Reis">Johann Philipp Reis</a> (1834–1874) publicly demonstrated the <a href="/wiki/Reis_telephone" title="Reis telephone">Reis telephone</a> before the Physical Society of Frankfurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-9">[9]</a></sup> Reis's telephone was not limited to musical sounds. Reis also used his telephone to transmit the phrase "Das Pferd frisst keinen Gurkensalat" ("The horse does not eat cucumber salad").</li>
<li>22 August 1865, La Feuille d'Aoste reported "It is rumored that English technicians to whom Mr. <a href="/wiki/Innocenzo_Manzetti" title="Innocenzo Manzetti">Manzetti</a> illustrated his method for transmitting spoken words on the telegraph wire intend to apply said invention in England on several private telegraph lines". However, telephones would not be demonstrated there until 1876, with a set of telephones from Bell.</li>
<li>28 December 1871: <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Meucci" title="Antonio Meucci">Antonio Meucci</a> files <a href="/wiki/Patent_caveat" title="Patent caveat">patent caveat</a> <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Meucci#Patent_caveat" title="Antonio Meucci">No. 3335</a> in the U.S. Patent Office titled "Sound Telegraph", describing communication of voice between two people by wire. A 'patent caveat' was not an invention <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> award, but only an unverified notice filed by an individual that he or she intends to file a regular patent application in the future.</li>
<li>1874: Meucci, after having renewed the caveat for two years does not renew it again, and the caveat lapses.</li>
<li>6 April 1875: Bell's U.S. Patent 161,739 "Transmitters and Receivers for Electric Telegraphs" is granted. This uses multiple vibrating steel reeds in make-break circuits.</li>
<li>11 February 1876: Elisha Gray invents a liquid transmitter for use with the telephone but does not build one.</li>
<li>14 February 1876: Gray files a <a href="/wiki/Patent_caveat" title="Patent caveat">patent caveat</a> for transmitting the human voice through a telegraphic circuit.</li>
<li>14 February 1876: <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a> applies for the patent "Improvements in Telegraphy", for electromagnetic telephones using what is now called amplitude modulation (oscillating current and voltage) but which he referred to as "undulating current".</li>
<li>19 February 1876: Gray is notified by the U.S. Patent Office of an interference between his caveat and Bell's patent application. Gray decides to abandon his caveat.</li>
<li>7 March 1876: Bell's U.S. patent 174,465 "Improvement in Telegraphy" is granted, covering "the method of, and apparatus for, transmitting vocal or other sounds telegraphically…by causing electrical undulations, similar in form to the vibrations of the air accompanying the said vocal or other sound."</li>
<li>10 March 1876: The first successful telephone transmission of clear speech using a liquid transmitter when Bell spoke into his device, "Mr. Watson, come here, I want to see you." and Watson heard each word distinctly.</li>
<li>30 January 1877: Bell's U.S. patent 186,787 is granted for an electromagnetic telephone using permanent magnets, iron diaphragms, and a call bell.</li>
<li>27 April 1877: Edison files for a patent on a carbon (graphite) transmitter. The patent 474,230 was granted 3 May 1892, after a 15-year delay because of litigation. Edison was granted patent 222,390 for a carbon granules transmitter in 1879.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_commercial_instruments">Early commercial instruments</span></h2>
<p>Early telephones were technically diverse. Some used a <a href="/wiki/Water_microphone" title="Water microphone">water microphone</a>, some had a metal diaphragm that induced current in an electromagnet wound around a permanent magnet, and some were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_microphone" title="Dynamic microphone">dynamic</a> – their diaphragm vibrated a coil of wire in the field of a permanent magnet or the coil vibrated the diaphragm. The sound-powered dynamic variants survived in small numbers through the 20th century in military and maritime applications, where its ability to create its own electrical power was crucial. Most, however, used the Edison/Berliner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_transmitter" title="Carbon transmitter">carbon transmitter</a>, which was much louder than the other kinds, even though it required an <a href="/wiki/Induction_coil" title="Induction coil">induction coil</a> which was an <a href="/wiki/Impedance_matching" title="Impedance matching">impedance matching</a> transformer to make it compatible with the impedance of the line. The Edison patents kept the Bell monopoly viable into the 20th century, by which time the network was more important than the instrument.
</p><p>Early telephones were locally powered, using either a dynamic transmitter or by the powering of a transmitter with a local battery. One of the jobs of <a href="/wiki/Outside_plant" title="Outside plant">outside plant</a> personnel was to visit each telephone periodically to inspect the battery. During the 20th century, telephones powered from the telephone exchange over the same wires that carried the voice signals became common.
</p><p>Early telephones used a single wire for the subscriber's line, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_wire_earth_return" title="Single wire earth return">ground return</a> used to complete the circuit (as used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraphs</a>). The earliest dynamic telephones also had only one port opening for sound, with the user alternately listening and speaking (or rather, shouting) into the same hole. Sometimes the instruments were operated in pairs at each end, making conversation more convenient but also more expensive.
</p><p>At first, the benefits of a telephone exchange were not exploited. Instead, telephones were leased in pairs to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscriber" title="Subscriber">subscriber</a>, who had to arrange for a telegraph contractor to construct a line between them, for example, between a home and a shop. Users who wanted the ability to speak to several different locations would need to obtain and set up three or four pairs of telephones. <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a>, already using telegraph exchanges, quickly extended the principle to its telephones in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, and Bell was not slow in appreciating the potential.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Signaling_(telecommunications)" title="Signaling (telecommunications)">Signalling</a> began in an appropriately primitive manner. The user alerted the other end, or the exchange <a href="/wiki/Switchboard_operator" title="Switchboard operator">operator</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Whistling" title="Whistling">whistling</a> into the transmitter. Exchange operation soon resulted in telephones being equipped with a bell in a <a href="/wiki/Ringer_box" title="Ringer box">ringer box</a>, first operated over a second wire, and later over the same wire, but with a condenser (<a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitor</a>) in series with the bell coil to allow the <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> ringer signal through while still blocking <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a> (keeping the phone "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On_hook" title="On hook">on hook</a>"). Telephones connected to the earliest <a href="/wiki/Strowger_switch" title="Strowger switch">Strowger switch</a> automatic exchanges had seven wires, one for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changeover_switch" title="Changeover switch">knife switch</a>, one for each <a href="/wiki/Telegraph_key" title="Telegraph key">telegraph key</a>, one for the bell, one for the <a href="/wiki/Push-button" title="Push-button">push-button</a> and two for speaking. Large wall telephones in the early 20th century usually incorporated the bell, and separate <a href="/wiki/Bell_box" title="Bell box">bell boxes</a> for desk phones dwindled away in the middle of the century.
</p><p>Rural and other telephones that were not on a common battery exchange had a <a href="/wiki/Telephone_magneto" title="Telephone magneto">magneto</a> hand-cranked generator to produce a high voltage alternating signal to ring the bells of other telephones on the line and to alert the operator. Some local farming communities that were not connected to the main networks set up <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbed_wire_telephone_lines" title="Barbed wire telephone lines">barbed wire telephone lines</a> that exploited the existing system of field fences to transmit the signal.
</p><p>In the 1890s a new smaller style of telephone was introduced, packaged in three parts. The transmitter stood on a stand, known as a "<a href="/wiki/Candlestick_telephone" title="Candlestick telephone">candlestick</a>" for its shape. When not in use, the receiver hung on a hook with a switch in it, known as a "switchhook". Previous telephones required the user to operate a separate switch to connect either the voice or the bell. With the new kind, the user was less likely to leave the phone "off the hook". In phones connected to magneto exchanges, the bell, induction coil, battery and magneto were in a separate bell box or "<a href="/wiki/Ringer_box" title="Ringer box">ringer box</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In phones connected to common battery exchanges, the ringer box was installed under a desk, or other out-of-the-way place, since it did not need a battery or magneto.
</p><p>Cradle designs were also used at this time, having a handle with the receiver and transmitter attached, now called a <a href="/wiki/Handset" title="Handset">handset</a>, separate from the cradle base that housed the magneto crank and other parts. They were larger than the "candlestick" and more popular.
</p><p>Disadvantages of single-wire operation such as crosstalk and <a href="/wiki/Mains_hum" title="Mains hum">hum from nearby AC power wires</a> had already led to the use of twisted pairs and, for long-distance telephones, <a href="/wiki/Four-wire_circuit" title="Four-wire circuit">four-wire circuits</a>. Users at the beginning of the 20th century did not place <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-distance_call" title="Long-distance call">long-distance calls</a> from their own telephones but made an appointment and were connected with the assistance of a telephone operator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>What turned out to be the most popular and longest-lasting physical style of telephone was introduced in the early 20th century, including Bell's <a href="/wiki/Model_202_telephone" title="Model 202 telephone">202-type</a> desk set. A carbon granule transmitter and electromagnetic receiver were united in a single molded plastic handle, which when not in use sat in a cradle in the base unit. The circuit diagram of the model 202 shows the direct connection of the transmitter to the line, while the receiver was induction coupled. In local battery configurations, when the local loop was too long to provide sufficient current from the exchange, the transmitter was powered by a local battery and inductively coupled, while the receiver was included in the local loop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The coupling transformer and the ringer were mounted in a separate enclosure, called the subscriber set. The dial switch in the base interrupted the line current by repeatedly but very briefly disconnecting the line 1 to 10 times for each digit, and the hook switch (in the center of the circuit diagram) disconnected the line and the transmitter battery while the handset was on the cradle.
</p><p>In the 1930s, telephone sets were developed that combined the bell and induction coil with the desk set, obviating a separate ringer box. The <a href="/wiki/Rotary_dial" title="Rotary dial">rotary dial</a> becoming commonplace in the 1930s in many areas enabled customer-dialed service, but some magneto systems remained even into the 1960s. After World War II, the telephone networks saw rapid expansion and more efficient telephone sets, such as the <a href="/wiki/Model_500_telephone" title="Model 500 telephone">model 500 telephone</a> in the United States, were developed that permitted larger local networks centered around central offices. A breakthrough new technology was the introduction of Touch-Tone signaling using <a href="/wiki/Push-button_telephone" title="Push-button telephone">push-button telephones</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Telephone_%26_Telegraph_Company" title="American Telephone &amp; Telegraph Company">American Telephone &amp; Telegraph Company</a> (AT&amp;T) in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_telephones_and_voice_over_IP">Digital telephones and voice over IP</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_telephony" title="Digital telephony">Digital telephony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voice_over_IP" title="Voice over IP">Voice over IP</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="4608" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg/220px-CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg/330px-CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg/440px-CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CiscoIPPhone7941Series.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An IP desktop telephone attached to a computer network</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="810" data-file-width="956" decoding="async" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png/300px-Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png/450px-Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png/600px-Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fixed_telephone_lines_per_100_inhabitants_1997-2007_ITU.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fixed telephone lines per 100 inhabitants 1997–2007</div></div></div>
<p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a> in 1947 dramatically changed the technology used in telephone systems and in the long-distance transmission networks, over the next several decades. With the development of <a href="/wiki/Stored_program_control" title="Stored program control">stored program control</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOS_integrated_circuit" title="MOS integrated circuit">MOS integrated circuits</a> for <a href="/wiki/Electronic_switching_system" title="Electronic switching system">electronic switching systems</a>, and new transmission technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Pulse-code_modulation" title="Pulse-code modulation">pulse-code modulation</a> (PCM), <a href="/wiki/Telephony" title="Telephony">telephony</a> gradually evolved towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_telephony" title="Digital telephony">digital telephony</a>, which improved the capacity, quality, and cost of the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allstot_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allstot-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Integrated_Services_Digital_Network" title="Integrated Services Digital Network">Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN)</a> was launched in the 1980's, providing businesses and consumers with access to digital telephony services such as data, voice, <a href="/wiki/Videotelephony" title="Videotelephony">video</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a> services. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The development of digital data communications methods made it possible to digitize voice and transmit it as real-time data across <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networks</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, giving rise to the field of Internet Protocol (IP) telephony, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_over_Internet_Protocol" title="Voice over Internet Protocol">voice over Internet Protocol</a> (VoIP). VoIP has proven to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disruptive_technology" title="Disruptive technology">disruptive technology</a> that is rapidly replacing traditional telephone network infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>By January 2005, up to 10% of telephone subscribers in Japan and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> had switched to this digital telephone service. A January 2005 <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a> article suggested that Internet telephony may be "the next big thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The technology has spawned a new industry comprising many VoIP companies that offer services to <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a>. The reported global VoIP market in October 2021 was $85.2 billion with a projection of $102.5 billion by 2026.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>IP telephony uses high-bandwidth Internet connections and specialized customer premises equipment to transmit telephone calls via the Internet, or any modern private data network. The customer equipment may be an <a href="/wiki/Analog_telephone_adapter" title="Analog telephone adapter">analog telephone adapter</a> (ATA) which translates the signals of a conventional analog telephone; an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IP_Phone" title="IP Phone">IP Phone</a>, a dedicated standalone device; or a computer <a href="/wiki/Softphone" title="Softphone">softphone</a> application, utilizing the microphone and headset devices of a personal computer or smartphone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>While traditional analog telephones are typically powered from the central office through the telephone line, digital telephones require a local power supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Internet-based digital service also requires special provisions to provide the service location to the emergency services when an <a href="/wiki/Emergency_telephone_number" title="Emergency telephone number">emergency telephone number</a> is called.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cordless_telephones">Cordless telephones</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cordless_telephone" title="Cordless telephone">Cordless telephone</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Motorola_4050a-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="533" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Motorola_4050a-2.jpg/220px-Motorola_4050a-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Motorola_4050a-2.jpg/330px-Motorola_4050a-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Motorola_4050a-2.jpg/440px-Motorola_4050a-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Motorola_4050a-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cordless telephone system consisting of a handset resting on a base station (left) and a second handset resting on a battery charger unit (right)</div></div></div>
<p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Cordless_telephone" title="Cordless telephone">cordless telephone</a></b> or <b>portable telephone</b> is a telephone consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Base_station" title="Base station">base station</a> unit, which connects with the telephone network through a standard telephone line, or via a VoIP connection, plus one or more portable cordless <a href="/wiki/Handset" title="Handset">handsets</a>, which communicate with the base station via <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> signals. A handset's operational range is limited, usually to within the same building or within a short distance from the base station.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Base_station">Base station</span></h3>
<p>Base stations include a radio transceiver and a pair of frequencies enabling it to simultaneously exchange outgoing and incoming signals with the handsets.  The base station often includes its own microphone, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_amplifier" title="Audio amplifier">audio amplifier</a> and a loudspeaker to enable the user to have hands-free <a href="/wiki/Speakerphone" title="Speakerphone">speakerphone</a> conversations, without needing to use the handset.  This also enables other nearby persons to participate in the conversation.  The base station sometimes has its own numeric keypad, enabling the user to make calls as per a standard landline phone, without using the handset.  Some base stations also have an LCD digital display for <a href="/wiki/Caller_ID" title="Caller ID">caller ID</a>.  Base units also often have an answering machine function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freudenrich4_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freudenrich4-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cordless handset contains a rechargeable <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">battery</a>, which the base station recharges when the handset rests in its cradle.  Muilt-handset systems generally also have additional charger units to provide battery charging for each additional unit.  Unlike a corded telephone, a cordless telephone requires a constant <a href="/wiki/Mains_electricity" title="Mains electricity">electricity supply</a> to power the base station and charger units, may have a DC transformer pack which plugs into a wall AC power outlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freudenrich4_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freudenrich4-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> or cellphone or hand phone is a handheld telephone which connects via radio transmissions to a <a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">cellular telephone network</a>.  The cellular network consists of a network of ground based antennas, usually located on towers or on buildings, with infrastructure connecting the cellular network to land-based telephone lines.  Analog cellular networks first appeared in 1979, with the first digital cellular networks appearing in the early 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galazzo_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galazzo-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mobile phones require a <a href="/wiki/SIM_card" title="SIM card">SIM card</a> to be inserted into the phone.  The SIM card is a small PVC card containing a small <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a> which stores the user's <a href="/wiki/International_mobile_subscriber_identity" title="International mobile subscriber identity">international mobile subscriber identity</a> (IMSI) number and its related key, which are used to identify and authenticate subscribers to the cellular network.
</p><p>Mobile phones generally incorporate an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCD_display" title="LCD display">LCD</a> or <a href="/wiki/OLED" title="OLED">OLED</a> display, with some types, such as smartphones, having touch screens.  Since the 1990s, mobile phones have gained other features which are not directly related to their primary function as telephones.  These include text messaging, calendars, alarm clocks, personal schedulers, cameras, music players, games and later, internet access and <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> functionality.  Nearly all mobile phones have the ability to send text messages to other users via the <a href="/wiki/SMS" title="SMS">SMS</a> (Short Message Service) protocol.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multimedia_messaging_service" title="Multimedia messaging service">multimedia messaging service</a> (MMS) protocol enables users to send and receive multimedia content, such as photos, audio files and video files.  As their functionality has increased over the years, many types of mobile phone, notably smartphones, require an <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> to run.  Popular mobile phone operating systems in the past have included <a href="/wiki/Symbian" title="Symbian">Symbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_OS" title="Palm OS">Palm OS</a>, <a href="/wiki/BlackBerry_OS" title="BlackBerry OS">BlackBerry OS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windows_Mobile" title="Windows Mobile"> mobile phone versions of Windows</a>.  As of 2022, the most used operating systems are Google's <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> and Apple's <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MordorIntelligence_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-MordorIntelligence-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the era of smartphones, mobile phones were generally manufactured by companies specializing in telecommunications equipment, such as <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ericsson" title="Ericsson">Ericsson</a>.  Since the advent of smartphones, mobile phone manufacturers have also included consumer electronics companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xiaomi" title="Xiaomi">Xiaomi</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smartphones">Smartphones</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">Smartphone</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Livraria_do_Senado_(22622160063).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2832" data-file-width="4256" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Livraria_do_Senado_%2822622160063%29.jpg/220px-Livraria_do_Senado_%2822622160063%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Livraria_do_Senado_%2822622160063%29.jpg/330px-Livraria_do_Senado_%2822622160063%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Livraria_do_Senado_%2822622160063%29.jpg/440px-Livraria_do_Senado_%2822622160063%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Livraria_do_Senado_(22622160063).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A smartphone with a touchscreen user interface, held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_orientation" title="Landscape orientation">landscape orientation</a></div></div></div>
<p>As of 2022, most mobile phones are smartphones, being a combination of a mobile phone and a personal computing device in the same unit.  Most smartphones are primarily operated using a <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">graphical user interface</a> and a touch screen.  Many phones have a secondary voice user interface, such as <a href="/wiki/Siri" title="Siri">Siri</a> on Apple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPhones" title="IPhones">iPhones</a>, which can operate many of the device's functions, as well as enabling users to use spoken commands to interact with the internet.  Typically alphanumeric text input is accomplished via an on-screen virtual keyboard, although some smartphones have a small physical keyboard.  Smartphones offer the ability to access internet data through the cellular network and via wi-fi, and usually allow direct connectivity to other devices via <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> or a wired interface, such as <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USB</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lightning_(connector)" title="Lightning (connector)">Lightning</a> connectors.  Smartphones, being able to run <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">apps</a> have vastly expanded functionality compared to previous mobile phones.  Having internet access and built in cameras, smartphones have made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_calling" title="Video calling">video calling</a> readily accessible via IP connections.  Smartphones also have access to a large number of web services and web apps, giving them functionality similar to traditional computers, although smartphones are often limited by their relatively small screen size and the size of their keyboards.  Typically, smartphones feature such tools as cameras, media players, web browsers, email clients, interactive maps, satellite navigation and a variety of sensors, such as a <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerometers" title="Accelerometers">accelerometers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS_receivers" title="GPS receivers">GPS receivers</a>.  In addition to voice calls, smartphone users commonly communicate using a wide variety of messaging formats, including SMS, MMS, email, and various proprietary messaging services, such as <a href="/wiki/IMessage" title="IMessage">iMessage</a> and various social media platforms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_phone_usage">Mobile phone usage</span></h3>
<p>In 2002, only 10% of the world's population used <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a> and by 2005 that percentage had risen to 46%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> By the end of 2009, there were a total of nearly 6 billion mobile and fixed-line telephone subscribers worldwide. This included 1.26 billion fixed-line subscribers and 4.6 billion mobile subscribers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
