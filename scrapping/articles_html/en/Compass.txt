<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Compass</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Instrument used for navigation and orientation</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the direction finding instrument used in navigation. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Compass_(disambiguation)" title="Compass (disambiguation)">Compass (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2016_Busola.JPG"><img alt="compass with a slit at cover and looking hole" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2262" data-file-width="2513" decoding="async" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/2016_Busola.JPG/330px-2016_Busola.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/2016_Busola.JPG/495px-2016_Busola.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/2016_Busola.JPG/660px-2016_Busola.JPG 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2016_Busola.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A modern military compass, with included <a href="/wiki/Sight_(device)" title="Sight (device)">sight device</a> for aligning</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>compass</b> is a device that shows the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_direction" title="Cardinal direction">cardinal directions</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a> and geographic orientation. It commonly consists of a magnetized needle or other element, such as a compass card or <a href="/wiki/Compass_rose" title="Compass rose">compass rose</a>, which can pivot to align itself with <a href="/wiki/North_magnetic_pole" title="North magnetic pole">magnetic north</a>. Other methods may be used, including gyroscopes, <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometers</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a> receivers.
</p><p>Compasses often show angles in degrees: north corresponds to 0°, and the angles increase <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">clockwise</a>, so east is 90°, south is 180°, and west is 270°. These numbers allow the compass to show <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuths</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(angle)" title="Bearing (angle)">bearings</a> which are commonly stated in degrees. If local <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">variation</a> between magnetic north and <a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">true north</a> is known, then direction of magnetic north also gives direction of true north.
</p><p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Four_Great_Inventions" title="Four Great Inventions">Four Great Inventions</a>, the magnetic compass was first invented as a device for <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a> as early as the <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology_in_China" title="History of science and technology in China">Chinese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> (since c. 206 BC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_Shu-hua,_p._176_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Li_Shu-hua,_p._176-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge1-2">[2]</a></sup> and later adopted for navigation by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> Chinese during the 11th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barbara_M._Kreutz_367_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barbara_M._Kreutz_367-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_1_252_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_1_252-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_Shu-hua,_p._182f_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Li_Shu-hua,_p._182f-5">[5]</a></sup> The first usage of a compass recorded in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_world" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a> occurred around 1190.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barbara_M._Kreutz_370_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barbara_M._Kreutz_370-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OEPST_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OEPST-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_compass"><span class="anchor" id="The_magnetic_compass"></span>Magnetic compass</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1067" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg/170px-Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg/255px-Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg/340px-Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Military_Compass_of_J._Lindsay_Brough.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A military compass that was used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a></div></div></div>
<p>The magnetic compass is the most familiar compass type. It functions as a pointer to "<a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">magnetic north</a>", the local magnetic meridian, because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetize" title="Magnetize">magnetized</a> needle at its heart aligns itself with the horizontal component of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> exerts a <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> on the needle, pulling the North end or <i>pole</i> of the needle approximately toward the Earth's <a href="/wiki/North_magnetic_pole" title="North magnetic pole">North magnetic pole</a>, and pulling the other toward the Earth's <a href="/wiki/South_magnetic_pole" title="South magnetic pole">South magnetic pole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The needle is mounted on a low-friction pivot point, in better compasses a <a href="/wiki/Jewel_bearing" title="Jewel bearing">jewel bearing</a>, so it can turn easily. When the compass is held level, the needle turns until, after a few seconds to allow oscillations to die out, it settles into its equilibrium orientation.
</p><p>In navigation, directions on maps are usually expressed with reference to geographical or <a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">true north</a>, the direction toward the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographical_North_Pole" title="Geographical North Pole">Geographical North Pole</a>, the rotation axis of the Earth. Depending on where the compass is located on the surface of the Earth the angle between <a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">true north</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_north" title="Magnetic north">magnetic north</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">magnetic declination</a> can vary widely with geographic location. The local magnetic declination is given on most maps, to allow the map to be oriented with a compass parallel to true north. The locations of the Earth's magnetic poles slowly change with time, which is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_secular_variation" title="Geomagnetic secular variation">geomagnetic secular variation</a>. The effect of this means a map with the latest declination information should be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some magnetic compasses include means to manually compensate for the magnetic declination, so that the compass shows true directions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-magnetic_compasses">Non-magnetic compasses</span></h2>
<p>There are other ways to find north than the use of magnetism, and from a navigational point of view a total of seven possible ways exist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JournalOfNavigation2016_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-JournalOfNavigation2016-10">[10]</a></sup> (where magnetism is one of the seven). Two sensors that utilize two of the remaining six principles are often also called compasses, i.e. the gyrocompass and GPS-compass.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gyrocompass">Gyrocompass</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gyrocompass" title="Gyrocompass">Gyrocompass</a></div>
<p>A <i>gyrocompass</i> is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscope</a>. It is a non-magnetic compass that finds <a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">true north</a> by using an (electrically powered) fast-spinning wheel and friction forces in order to exploit the rotation of the Earth. Gyrocompasses are widely used on <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a>. They have two main advantages over magnetic compasses:
</p>
<ul><li>they find <i>true north</i>, i.e., the direction of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s rotational axis, as opposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_North_Pole#Magnetic_north_and_magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic North Pole">magnetic north</a>,</li>
<li>they are not affected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> metal (including iron, steel, cobalt, nickel, and various alloys) in a ship's hull. (No compass is affected by nonferromagnetic metal, although a magnetic compass will be affected by any kind of wires with <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> passing through them.)</li></ul>
<p>Large ships typically rely on a gyrocompass, using the magnetic compass only as a backup. Increasingly, electronic <a href="/wiki/Fluxgate_compass" title="Fluxgate compass">fluxgate compasses</a> are used on smaller vessels. However, magnetic compasses are still widely in use as they can be small, use simple reliable technology, are comparatively cheap, are often easier to use than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>, require no energy supply, and unlike GPS, are not affected by objects, e.g. trees, that can block the reception of electronic signals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GPS_receivers_used_as_compasses">GPS receivers used as compasses</span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS_receiver" title="GPS receiver">GPS receivers</a> using two or more antennae mounted separately and blending the data with an inertial motion unit (IMU) can now achieve 0.02° in heading accuracy and have startup times in seconds rather than hours for gyrocompass systems. The devices accurately determine the positions (latitudes, longitudes and altitude) of the antennae on the Earth, from which the cardinal directions can be calculated. Manufactured primarily for maritime and aviation applications, they can also detect pitch and roll of ships. Small, portable GPS receivers with only a single antenna can also determine directions if they are being moved, even if only at walking pace. By accurately determining its position on the Earth at times a few seconds apart, the device can calculate its speed and the true bearing (relative to <i><a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">true north</a></i>) of its direction of motion. Frequently, it is preferable to measure the direction in which a vehicle is actually moving, rather than its heading, i.e. the direction in which its nose is pointing. These directions may be different if there is a crosswind or tidal current.
</p><p>GPS compasses share the main advantages of gyrocompasses. They determine true North,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JournalOfNavigation2016_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-JournalOfNavigation2016-10">[10]</a></sup> as opposed to magnetic North, and they are unaffected by perturbations of the Earth's magnetic field. Additionally, compared with gyrocompasses, they are much cheaper, they work better in polar regions, they are less prone to be affected by mechanical vibration, and they can be initialized far more quickly. However, they depend on the functioning of, and communication with, the GPS satellites, which might be disrupted by an electronic attack or by the effects of a severe solar storm. Gyrocompasses remain in use for military purposes (especially in submarines, where magnetic and GPS compasses are useless), but have been largely superseded by GPS compasses, with magnetic backups, in civilian contexts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_(9872744925).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3888" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_%289872744925%29.jpg/220px-Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_%289872744925%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_%289872744925%29.jpg/330px-Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_%289872744925%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_%289872744925%29.jpg/440px-Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_%289872744925%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Song_Figure_Holding_Land_Compass_(9872744925).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figurine of a man holding a compass, <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_compass" title="History of the compass">History of the compass</a></div>
<p>The first compasses in ancient <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> China were made of <a href="/wiki/Lodestone" title="Lodestone">lodestone</a>, a naturally magnetized ore of iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guarnieri_7-1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-guarnieri_7-1-11">[11]</a></sup> Later compasses were made of iron needles, magnetized by striking them with a lodestone, which appeared in China by 1088 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dream_Pool_Essays" title="Dream Pool Essays">as described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Shen_Kuo" title="Shen Kuo">Shen Kuo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merrill_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-merrill-12">[12]</a></sup> Dry compasses began to appear around 1300 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">Medieval Europe</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_world" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane,_p._615_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lane,_p._615-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OEPST_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-OEPST-7">[7]</a></sup> This was supplanted in the early 20th century by the liquid-filled magnetic compass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W._H._Creak_238-239_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-W._H._Creak_238-239-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_compasses">Modern compasses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Walkers_compass_arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1785" data-file-width="1238" decoding="async" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Walkers_compass_arp.jpg/170px-Walkers_compass_arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Walkers_compass_arp.jpg/255px-Walkers_compass_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Walkers_compass_arp.jpg/340px-Walkers_compass_arp.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Walkers_compass_arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A liquid-filled protractor or orienteering compass with lanyard</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_compass_2">Magnetic compass</span></h3>
<p>Modern compasses usually use a magnetized needle or dial inside a capsule completely filled with a liquid (lamp oil, mineral oil, white spirits, purified kerosene, or ethyl alcohol are common). While older designs commonly incorporated a flexible rubber diaphragm or airspace inside the capsule to allow for volume changes caused by temperature or altitude, some modern liquid compasses utilize smaller housings and/or flexible capsule materials to accomplish the same result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The liquid inside the capsule serves to damp the movement of the needle, reducing oscillation time and increasing stability. Key points on the compass, including the north end of the needle are often marked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphorescent_paint" title="Phosphorescent paint">phosphorescent</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoluminescent" title="Photoluminescent">photoluminescent</a>, or self-luminous materials<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> to enable the compass to be read at night or in poor light. As the compass fill liquid is noncompressible under pressure, many ordinary liquid-filled compasses will operate accurately underwater to considerable depths.
</p><p>Many modern compasses incorporate a baseplate and <a href="/wiki/Protractor" title="Protractor">protractor</a> tool, and are referred to variously as "<a href="/wiki/Orienteering" title="Orienteering">orienteering</a>", "baseplate", "map compass" or "protractor" designs. This type of compass uses a separate magnetized needle inside a rotating capsule, an orienting "box" or gate for aligning the needle with magnetic north, a transparent base containing map orienting lines, and a bezel (outer dial) marked in degrees or other units of angular measurement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des110_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-des110-17">[17]</a></sup> The capsule is mounted in a transparent baseplate containing a <i>direction-of-travel</i> (DOT) indicator for use in taking bearings directly from a map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des110_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-des110-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1137" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg/220px-Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg/330px-Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg/440px-Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cammenga-lensatic-compass-model-27.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cammenga air filled lensatic compass</div></div></div>
<p>Other features found on modern orienteering compasses are map and <a href="/wiki/Romer" title="Romer">romer</a> scales for measuring distances and plotting positions on maps, luminous markings on the face or bezels, various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sighting_compass" title="Sighting compass">sighting mechanisms</a> (mirror, prism, etc.) for taking bearings of distant objects with greater precision, gimbal-mounted, "global" needles for use in differing hemispheres, special rare-earth magnets to stabilize compass needles, adjustable declination for obtaining instant true bearings without resorting to arithmetic, and devices such as <a href="/wiki/Inclinometer" title="Inclinometer">inclinometers</a> for measuring gradients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des110111_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-des110111-18">[18]</a></sup> The sport of orienteering has also resulted in the development of models with extremely fast-settling and stable needles utilizing rare-earth magnets for optimal use with a <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">topographic map</a>, a land navigation technique known as <i>terrain association</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Many marine compasses designed for use on boats with constantly shifting angles use dampening fluids such as <a href="/wiki/Isopar_M" title="Isopar M">isopar M</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isopar_L&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isopar L (page does not exist)">isopar L</a> to limit the rapid fluctuation and direction of the needle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The military forces of a few nations, notably the United States Army, continue to issue field compasses with magnetized compass dials or cards instead of needles. A magnetic card compass is usually equipped with an optical, lensatic, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prismatic_sight" title="Prismatic sight">prismatic sight</a>, which allows the user to read the bearing or azimuth off the compass card while simultaneously aligning the compass with the objective (see photo). Magnetic card compass designs normally require a separate protractor tool in order to take bearings directly from a map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des112_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-des112-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The U.S. M-1950 military lensatic compass does not use a liquid-filled capsule as a damping mechanism, but rather <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">electromagnetic induction</a> to control oscillation of its magnetized card. A "deep-well" design is used to allow the compass to be used globally with a card tilt of up to 8 degrees without impairing accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> As induction forces provide less damping than fluid-filled designs, a needle lock is fitted to the compass to reduce wear, operated by the folding action of the rear sight/lens holder. The use of air-filled induction compasses has declined over the years, as they may become inoperative or inaccurate in freezing temperatures or extremely humid environments due to condensation or water ingress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some military compasses, like the U.S. M-1950 (<a href="/wiki/Cammenga" title="Cammenga">Cammenga</a> 3H) military lensatic compass, the <a href="/wiki/Silva_compass" title="Silva compass">Silva 4b <i>Militaire</i></a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Suunto" title="Suunto">Suunto</a> M-5N(T) contain the radioactive material <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">1</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) and a combination of phosphors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The U.S. M-1950 equipped with self-luminous lighting contains 120 mCi (millicuries) of tritium. The purpose of the tritium and phosphors is to provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illumination_(lighting)" title="Illumination (lighting)">illumination</a> for the compass, via <a href="/wiki/Radioluminescence" title="Radioluminescence">radioluminescent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tritium_illumination" title="Tritium illumination">tritium illumination</a>, which does not require the compass to be "recharged" by sunlight or artificial light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> However, tritium has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of only about 12 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> so a compass that contains 120 mCi of tritium when new will contain only 60 when it is 12 years old, 30 when it is 24 years old, and so on. Consequently, the illumination of the display will fade.
</p><p>Mariners' compasses can have two or more magnets permanently attached to a compass card, which moves freely on a pivot. A <i>lubber line</i>, which can be a marking on the compass bowl or a small fixed needle, indicates the ship's heading on the compass card. Traditionally the card is divided into thirty-two points (known as <i>rhumbs</i>), although modern compasses are marked in degrees rather than cardinal points. The glass-covered box (or bowl) contains a suspended <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">gimbal</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Binnacle" title="Binnacle">binnacle</a>. This preserves the horizontal position.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thumb_compass">Thumb compass</span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thumb_compass" title="Thumb compass">Thumb compass</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compasses_orienteering.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Compasses_orienteering.jpg/220px-Compasses_orienteering.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Compasses_orienteering.jpg/330px-Compasses_orienteering.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Compasses_orienteering.jpg/440px-Compasses_orienteering.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compasses_orienteering.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thumb compass on left</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>thumb compass</b> is a type of compass commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Orienteering" title="Orienteering">orienteering</a>, a sport in which map reading and terrain association are paramount. Consequently, most thumb compasses have minimal or no degree markings at all, and are normally used only to orient the map to magnetic north. An oversized rectangular needle or north indicator aids visibility. Thumb compasses are also often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)">transparent</a> so that an orienteer can hold a <a href="/wiki/Orienteering_map" title="Orienteering map">map</a> in the hand with the compass and see the map through the compass. The best models use rare-earth magnets to reduce needle settling time to 1 second or less.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_state_compasses">Solid state compasses</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">Magnetometer</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1019" data-file-width="1359" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg/220px-Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg/330px-Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg/440px-Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Motorola_Xoom_-_AKM_Semiconductor_AKM8975-1693.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3-axis electronic magnetometer AKM8975 by <a href="/wiki/AKM_Semiconductor,_Inc." title="AKM Semiconductor, Inc.">AKM Semiconductor</a></div></div></div>
<p>Small compasses found in clocks, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, and other electronic devices are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-state_(electronics)" title="Solid-state (electronics)">solid-state</a> <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">microelectromechanical systems</a> (MEMS) compasses, usually built out of two or three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_field_sensors" title="Magnetic field sensors">magnetic field sensors</a> that provide data for a microprocessor. Often, the device is a discrete component which outputs either a digital or analog signal proportional to its orientation. This signal is interpreted by a <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">controller</a> or <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> and either used internally, or sent to a display unit. The sensor uses highly calibrated internal electronics to measure the response of the device to the Earth's magnetic field.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialty_compasses">Specialty compasses</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brunton.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2593" data-file-width="3648" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Brunton.JPG/220px-Brunton.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Brunton.JPG/330px-Brunton.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Brunton.JPG/440px-Brunton.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brunton.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A standard Brunton Geo, used commonly by geologists</div></div></div>
<p>Apart from navigational compasses, other specialty compasses have also been designed to accommodate specific uses. These include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Qibla_compass" title="Qibla compass">Qibla compass</a>, which is used by Muslims to show the direction to Mecca for prayers.</li>
<li>Optical or <a href="/wiki/Prismatic_compass" title="Prismatic compass">prismatic compass</a>, most often used by surveyors, but also by cave explorers, foresters, and geologists. These compasses generally use a liquid-damped capsule<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and magnetized floating compass dial with an integral optical sight, often fitted with built-in photoluminescent or battery-powered illumination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des113114_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-des113114-29">[29]</a></sup> Using the optical sight, such compasses can be read with extreme accuracy when taking bearings to an object, often to fractions of a degree. Most of these compasses are designed for heavy-duty use, with high-quality needles and jeweled bearings, and many are fitted for tripod mounting for additional accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des113114_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-des113114-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trough_compasses&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trough compasses (page does not exist)">Trough compasses</a>, mounted in a rectangular box whose length was often several times its width, date back several centuries. They were used for land surveying, particularly with plane tables.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_the_magnetic_compass">Limitations of the magnetic compass</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg"><img alt="A close up photo of a geological compass" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg/220px-%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg/330px-%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg/440px-%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%D7%9E%D7%A6%D7%A4%D7%9F.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A close up photo of a geological compass</div></div></div>
<p>The magnetic compass is very reliable at moderate latitudes, but in geographic regions near the Earth's magnetic poles it becomes unusable. As the compass is moved closer to one of the magnetic poles, the magnetic declination, the difference between the direction to geographical north and magnetic north, becomes greater and greater. At some point close to the magnetic pole the compass will not indicate any particular direction but will begin to drift. Also, the needle starts to point up or down when getting closer to the poles, because of the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_inclination" title="Magnetic inclination">magnetic inclination</a>. Cheap compasses with bad <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a> may get stuck because of this and therefore indicate a wrong direction.
</p><p>Magnetic compasses are influenced by any fields other than Earth's. Local environments may contain magnetic mineral deposits and artificial sources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">MRIs</a>, large iron or steel bodies, electrical engines or strong permanent magnets. Any electrically conductive body produces its own magnetic field when it is carrying an electric current. Magnetic compasses are prone to errors in the neighborhood of such bodies. Some compasses include magnets which can be adjusted to compensate for external magnetic fields, making the compass more reliable and accurate.
</p><p>A compass is also subject to errors when the compass is accelerated or decelerated in an airplane or automobile. Depending on which of the Earth's hemispheres the compass is located and if the force is acceleration or deceleration the compass will increase or decrease the indicated heading. Compasses that include compensating magnets are especially prone to these errors, since accelerations tilt the needle, bringing it closer or further from the magnets.
</p><p>Another error of the mechanical compass is turning error. When one turns from a heading of east or west the compass will lag behind the turn or lead ahead of the turn. Magnetometers, and substitutes such as gyrocompasses, are more stable in such situations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_of_a_magnetic_compass">Construction of a magnetic compass</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_needle">Magnetic needle</span></h3>
<p>A magnetic rod is required when constructing a compass. This can be created by aligning an iron or steel rod with Earth's magnetic field and then tempering or striking it. However, this method produces only a weak magnet so other methods are preferred. For example, a magnetised rod can be created by repeatedly rubbing an iron rod with a magnetic <a href="/wiki/Lodestone" title="Lodestone">lodestone</a>. This magnetised rod (or magnetic needle) is then placed on a low friction surface to allow it to freely pivot to align itself with the magnetic field. It is then labeled so the user can distinguish the north-pointing from the south-pointing end; in modern convention the north end is typically marked in some way.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Needle-and-bowl_device">Needle-and-bowl device</span></h3>
<p>If a needle is rubbed on a <a href="/wiki/Lodestone" title="Lodestone">lodestone</a> or other magnet, the needle becomes magnetized. When it is inserted in a cork or piece of wood, and placed in a bowl of water it becomes a compass. Such devices were universally used as compass until the invention of the box-like compass with a 'dry' pivoting needle sometime around 1300.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Points_of_the_compass">Points of the compass</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Points_of_the_compass" title="Points of the compass">Points of the compass</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg/220px-Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg/330px-Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg/440px-Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boussole_fantassin_russe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wrist compass of the Soviet Army with counterclockwise double graduation: 60° (like a watch) and 360°</div></div></div>
<p>Originally, many compasses were marked only as to the direction of magnetic north, or to the four cardinal points (north, south, east, west). Later, these were divided, in China into 24, and in Europe into 32 equally spaced points around the compass card. For a table of the thirty-two points, see <a href="/wiki/Points_of_the_compass" title="Points of the compass">compass points</a>.
</p><p>In the modern era, the 360-degree system took hold. This system is still in use today for civilian navigators. The degree system spaces 360 equidistant points located clockwise around the compass dial. In the 19th century some European nations adopted the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grad_(angle)" title="Grad (angle)">grad</a>" (also called grade or gon) system instead, where a right angle is 100 grads to give a circle of 400 grads. Dividing grads into tenths to give a circle of 4000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decigrade" title="Decigrade">decigrades</a> has also been used in armies.
</p><p>Most military forces have adopted the French "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millieme_(angle)" title="Millieme (angle)">millieme</a>" system. This is an approximation of a milli-radian (6283 per circle), in which the compass dial is spaced into 6400 units or "mils" for additional precision when measuring angles, laying artillery, etc. The value to the military is that one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_mil" title="Angular mil">angular mil</a> subtends approximately one metre at a distance of one kilometer. Imperial Russia used a system derived by dividing the circumference of a circle into chords of the same length as the radius. Each of these was divided into 100 spaces, giving a circle of 600. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> divided these into tenths to give a circle of 6000 units, usually translated as "mils". This system was adopted by the former <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> countries (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>), often counterclockwise (see picture of wrist compass). This is still in use in Russia.
</p>
<h3><span id="Compass_balancing_.28magnetic_dip.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Compass_balancing_(magnetic_dip)">Compass balancing (magnetic dip)</span></h3>
<p>Because the Earth's magnetic field's inclination and intensity vary at different latitudes, compasses are often balanced during manufacture so that the dial or needle will be level, eliminating needle drag which can give inaccurate readings. Most manufacturers balance their compass needles for one of five zones, ranging from zone 1, covering most of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, to zone 5 covering <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and the southern oceans. This individual zone balancing prevents excessive dipping of one end of the needle which can cause the compass card to stick and give false readings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalcompass_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-globalcompass-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some compasses feature a special needle balancing system that will accurately indicate magnetic north regardless of the particular magnetic zone. Other magnetic compasses have a small sliding counterweight installed on the needle itself. This sliding counterweight, called a 'rider', can be used for counterbalancing the needle against the dip caused by inclination if the compass is taken to a zone with a higher or lower dip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalcompass_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-globalcompass-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compass_correction">Compass correction</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_deviation" title="Magnetic deviation">Magnetic deviation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2560" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg/220px-MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg/330px-MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg/440px-MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MuseeMarine-compas-p1000468.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Binnacle" title="Binnacle">binnacle</a> containing a ship's standard compass, with the two iron balls which correct the effects of <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic</a> materials. This unit is on display in a museum.</div></div></div>
<p>Like any magnetic device, compasses are affected by nearby ferrous materials, as well as by strong local electromagnetic forces. Compasses used for wilderness land navigation should not be used in proximity to ferrous metal objects or electromagnetic fields (car electrical systems, automobile engines, steel <a href="/wiki/Piton" title="Piton">pitons</a>, etc.) as that can affect their accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des122_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-des122-31">[31]</a></sup> Compasses are particularly difficult to use accurately in or near trucks, cars or other mechanized vehicles even when corrected for deviation by the use of built-in magnets or other devices. Large amounts of ferrous metal combined with the on-and-off electrical fields caused by the vehicle's ignition and charging systems generally result in significant compass errors.
</p><p>At sea, a ship's compass must also be corrected for errors, called <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_deviation" title="Magnetic deviation">deviation</a>, caused by iron and steel in its structure and equipment. The ship is <i>swung</i>, that is rotated about a fixed point while its heading is noted by alignment with fixed points on the shore. A compass deviation card is prepared so that the navigator can convert between compass and magnetic headings. The compass can be corrected in three ways. First the <a href="/wiki/Lubber_line" title="Lubber line">lubber line</a> can be adjusted so that it is aligned with the direction in which the ship travels, then the effects of permanent magnets can be corrected for by small magnets fitted within the case of the compass. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Using_a_magnetic_compass">Using a magnetic compass</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CompassUseMapMarked.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="2412" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/CompassUseMapMarked.jpg/220px-CompassUseMapMarked.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/CompassUseMapMarked.jpg/330px-CompassUseMapMarked.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/CompassUseMapMarked.jpg/440px-CompassUseMapMarked.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CompassUseMapMarked.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Turning the compass scale on the map (D – the local magnetic declination)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2060" data-file-width="2140" decoding="async" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg/220px-CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg/330px-CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg/440px-CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CompassUseTargetMarked.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>When the needle is aligned with and superimposed over the outlined orienting arrow on the bottom of the capsule, the degree figure on the compass ring at the direction-of-travel (DOT) indicator gives the magnetic bearing to the target (mountain).</div></div></div>
<p>A magnetic compass points to magnetic north pole, which is approximately 1,000 miles from the true geographic North Pole. A magnetic compass's user can determine true North by finding the magnetic north and then correcting for variation and deviation. <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">Variation</a> is defined as the angle between the direction of true (geographic) north and the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> between the magnetic poles. Variation values for most of the oceans had been calculated and published by 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_deviation" title="Magnetic deviation">Deviation</a> refers to the response of the compass to local magnetic fields caused by the presence of iron and electric currents; one can partly compensate for these by careful location of the compass and the placement of compensating magnets under the compass itself. Mariners have long known that these measures do not completely cancel deviation; hence, they performed an additional step by measuring the compass bearing of a landmark with a known magnetic bearing. They then pointed their ship to the next compass point and measured again, graphing their results. In this way, correction tables could be created, which would be consulted when compasses were used when traveling in those locations.
</p><p>Mariners are concerned about very accurate measurements; however, casual users need not be concerned with differences between magnetic and true North. Except in areas of extreme magnetic declination variance (20 degrees or more), this is enough to protect from walking in a substantially different direction than expected over short distances, provided the terrain is fairly flat and visibility is not impaired. By carefully recording distances (time or paces) and magnetic bearings traveled, one can plot a course and return to one's starting point using the compass alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des149_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-des149-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="690" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png/220px-Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png/330px-Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png/440px-Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Measuring_azimuth_with_a_compass.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soldier using a <a href="/wiki/Prismatic_compass" title="Prismatic compass">prismatic compass</a> to get an azimuth</div></div></div>
<p>Compass navigation in conjunction with a map (<i>terrain association</i>) requires a different method. To take a map bearing or <i>true bearing</i> (a bearing taken in reference to true, not magnetic north) to a destination with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protractor_compass" title="Protractor compass">protractor compass</a>, the edge of the compass is placed on the map so that it connects the current location with the desired destination (some sources recommend physically drawing a line). The orienting lines in the base of the compass dial are then rotated to align with actual or true north by aligning them with a marked line of longitude (or the vertical margin of the map), ignoring the compass needle entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des134135_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-des134135-36">[36]</a></sup> The resulting <i>true bearing</i> or map bearing may then be read at the degree indicator or direction-of-travel (DOT) line, which may be followed as an <i><a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a></i> (course) to the destination. If a <i>magnetic</i> north bearing or <i>compass bearing</i> is desired, the compass must be adjusted by the amount of magnetic declination before using the bearing so that both map and compass are in agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-des134135_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-des134135-36">[36]</a></sup> In the given example, the large mountain in the second photo was selected as the target destination on the map. Some compasses allow the scale to be adjusted to compensate for the local magnetic declination; if adjusted correctly, the compass will give the true bearing instead of the magnetic bearing.
</p><p>The modern hand-held <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protractor_compass" title="Protractor compass">protractor compass</a> always has an additional direction-of-travel (DOT) arrow or indicator inscribed on the baseplate. To check one's progress along a course or azimuth, or to ensure that the object in view is indeed the destination, a new compass reading may be taken to the target if visible (here, the large mountain). After pointing the DOT arrow on the baseplate at the target, the compass is oriented so that the needle is superimposed over the orienting arrow in the capsule. The resulting bearing indicated is the magnetic bearing to the target. Again, if one is using "true" or map bearings, and the compass does not have preset, pre-adjusted declination, one must additionally add or subtract <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">magnetic declination</a> to convert the <i>magnetic bearing</i> into a <i>true bearing</i>. The exact value of the magnetic declination is place-dependent and varies over time, though declination is frequently given on the map itself or obtainable on-line from various sites. If the hiker has been following the correct path, the compass' corrected (true) indicated bearing should closely correspond to the true bearing previously obtained from the map.
</p><p>A compass should be laid down on a level surface so that the needle only rests or hangs on the bearing fused to the compass casing – if used at a tilt, the needle might touch the casing on the compass and not move freely, hence not pointing to the magnetic north accurately, giving a faulty reading. To see if the needle is well leveled, look closely at the needle, and tilt it slightly to see if the needle is swaying side to side freely and the needle is not contacting the casing of the compass. If the needle tilts to one direction, tilt the compass slightly and gently to the opposing direction until the compass needle is horizontal, lengthwise. Items to avoid around compasses are magnets of any kind and any electronics. Magnetic fields from electronics can easily disrupt the needle, preventing it from aligning with the Earth's magnetic fields, causing inaccurate readings. The Earth's natural magnetic forces are considerably weak, measuring at 0.5 <a href="/wiki/Gauss_(unit)" title="Gauss (unit)">gauss</a> and magnetic fields from household electronics can easily exceed it, overpowering the compass needle. Exposure to strong magnets, or magnetic interference can sometimes cause the magnetic poles of the compass needle to differ or even reverse. Avoid iron rich deposits when using a compass, for example, certain rocks which contain magnetic minerals, like <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">Magnetite</a>. This is often indicated by a rock with a surface which is dark and has a metallic luster, not all magnetic mineral bearing rocks have this indication. To see if a rock or an area is causing interference on a compass, get out of the area, and see if the needle on the compass moves. If it does, it means that the area or rock the compass was previously at is causing interference and should be avoided.
</p>
