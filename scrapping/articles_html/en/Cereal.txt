<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_cereal" title="Breakfast cereal">Breakfast cereal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">Grain</a>, and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cereal_(disambiguation)" title="Cereal (disambiguation)">Cereal (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>cereal</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grass</a> cultivated for the edible components of its <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a> (botanically, a type of <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Caryopsis" title="Caryopsis">caryopsis</a>), composed of the <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cereal_germ" title="Cereal germ">germ</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bran" title="Bran">bran</a>. Cereal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_grain" title="Food grain">grain crops</a> are grown in greater quantities and provide more food energy worldwide than any other type of crop<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and are therefore <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staple crops</a>. Edible grains from other plant families, such as <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salvia_hispanica" title="Salvia hispanica">chia</a>, are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Pseudocereal" title="Pseudocereal">pseudocereals</a>.
</p><p>In their unprocessed <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">whole grain</a> form, cereals are a rich source of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral">minerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, oils, and <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a>. When processed by the removal of the bran and germ the remaining endosperm is mostly carbohydrate. In some <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, grain in the form of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> constitutes a majority of daily sustenance. In <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>, cereal consumption is moderate and varied but still substantial, primarily in the form of refined and processed grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Because of this dietary importance, the <a href="/wiki/Grain_trade" title="Grain trade">cereal trade</a> is often at the heart of food trade -- with many cereals sold as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commodity_crops" title="Commodity crops">commodities</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> allowed for the support of an increased population, leading to larger societies and eventually the development of cities. It also created the need for greater organization of political power (and the creation of <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">social stratification</a>), as decisions had to be made regarding labor and <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a> allocation and access rights to water and land. Agriculture bred immobility, as populations settled down for long periods of time, which led to the accumulation of material goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> villages show evidence of the development of processing grain. The <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> is the ancient home of the ancestors of wheat, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>, in which many of these villages were based. There is evidence of the cultivation of cereals in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> approximately 9,000 years ago. Wheat, Barley, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">Rye</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oats" title="Oats">Oats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">Flaxseeds</a> were all domesticated in the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a> during the early Neolithic. During the same period, farmers in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> began to farm rice and millet, using human-made floods and fires as part of their cultivation regimen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatGeo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo-4">[4]</a></sup> Fiber crops were domesticated as early as food crops, with China domesticating <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> being developed independently in Africa and South America, and <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a> domesticating flax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_amendment" title="Soil amendment">soil amendments</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>, fish, <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wood_ash" title="Wood ash">ashes</a>, appears to have begun early, and developed independently in several areas of the world, including <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" title="Nile Valley">Nile Valley</a> and Eastern Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FertMan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FertMan-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first cereal grains were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated" title="Domesticated">domesticated</a> by early primitive humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> About 8,000 years ago, they were domesticated by ancient farming communities in the Fertile Crescent region. <a href="/wiki/Emmer" title="Emmer">Emmer wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Einkorn_wheat" title="Einkorn wheat">einkorn wheat</a>, and barley were three of the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_founder_crops" title="Neolithic founder crops">Neolithic founder crops</a> in the development of agriculture. Around the same time, millets and kinds of rice were starting to become domesticated in East Asia. <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">Sorghum</a> and millets were also being domesticated in sub-Saharan West Africa, which were both used primarily as feed for livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Cereals were the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">human civilization</a>. Cereal frontiers coincided with civilizational frontiers. The term <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a> implies the spatial dependence of civilization on cereals. <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">The Great Wall of China</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_limes" title="Roman limes">Roman limes</a> demarcated the same northern limit of cereal cultivation. The <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> stretched along the cereal belt of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. Numerous Chinese imperial edicts stated: “Agriculture is the foundation of this empire,”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 86">: 86 </span></sup> while the foundation of agriculture were the <a href="/wiki/Five_Grains" title="Five Grains">Five Grains</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The word “cereal” is derived from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceres_(Roman_mythology)" title="Ceres (Roman mythology)">Ceres</a></i>, the Roman goddess of harvest and agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaeth_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-spaeth-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cereals determined how large and for how long an army could be mobilized. For this reason, <a href="/wiki/Shang_Yang" title="Shang Yang">Shang Yang</a> called agriculture and war “the One.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 62">: 62 </span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guan_Zhong" title="Guan Zhong">Guan Zhong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chanakya" title="Chanakya">Chanakya</a> (the author of <i><a href="/wiki/Arthashastra" title="Arthashastra">Arthashastra</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 63–64">: 63–64 </span></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 85">: 85 </span></sup> expressed similar concepts. At the dawn of history, the Sumerians believed that if the agriculture of a state declines, <a href="/wiki/Inanna" title="Inanna">Inanna</a>, the goddess of war, leaves this state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 68">: 68 </span></sup> Several gods of antiquity combined the functions of what Shang Yang called “the One” – agriculture and war: the Hittite <a href="/wiki/Sun_goddess_of_Arinna" title="Sun goddess of Arinna">Sun goddess of Arinna</a>, the Canaanite <a href="/wiki/Lahmu" title="Lahmu">Lahmu</a> and the Roman <a href="/wiki/Janus" title="Janus">Janus</a>. These were highly important gods in their time leaving their legacy until today. We still begin the year with the month of Janus (January). The Jews believe that Messiah's family will originate in the town of Lahmu (<a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a>); in Hebrew, <i>beit lehem</i> literally means "house of bread". Christians believe that <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus Christ</a>, who is said to have been born in Bethlehem, is the messiah. In Hebrew, <i>bread</i> (<i>lehem</i>) and <i>warfare</i> (<i>milhama</i>) are of the same root.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jhom-bread_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-jhom-bread-11">[11]</a></sup> In fact, most persistent and flourishing empires throughout history in both hemispheres were centered in regions fertile for cereals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-One_source plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-one_source" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="Question book-new.svg" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>relies largely or entirely on a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Articles_with_a_single_source" title="Wikipedia:Articles with a single source">single source</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Cereal##" title="Talk:Cereal">talk page</a>. <p>This historic pattern did not change, not even in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Age" title="Industrial Age">Industrial Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 111–126">: 111–126 </span></sup> All modern great powers have traditionally remained first and foremost great <i>cereal</i> powers. The “finest hour” of the Axis powers “ended precisely the moment they threw themselves against the two largest cereal lebensraums” (the United States and the USSR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 159">: 159 </span></sup> The outcome of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> followed the Soviet grave and long-lasting cereal crisis, exacerbated by the cereal embargo imposed on the USSR in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 172">: 172 </span></sup> And, called “the grain basket of the world,” the most productive “cereal lebensraum” dominates the world ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 206">: 206 </span></sup>
</p><p>Having analyzed the mechanism at work behind this pattern, Ostrovsky outlined that the cereal power determines the percentage of manpower available to non-agricultural sectors including the <a href="/wiki/Heavy_industry" title="Heavy industry">heavy industry</a> vital for military power. He emphasized that chronologically the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> follows the modern Agricultural Revolution and spatially the world's industrial regions are bound to cereal regions. Taken from space, map of the global illumination is said to indicate by its brightest parts the industrial regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (July 2022)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> These regions coincide with cereal regions. Ostrovsky formulized a universal indicator of national power valid for all periods: total cereal tonnage produced by one percent of nation's manpower. For the present, this indicator demonstrates a unipolar international <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 119">: 119 </span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Revolution">Green Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Green Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a></div>
<p>During the second half of the 20th century there was a significant increase in the production of high-yield cereal crops worldwide, especially wheat and rice, due to an initiative known as the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAOGreenRevolution_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAOGreenRevolution-13">[13]</a></sup> The strategies developed by the Green Revolution focused on fending off starvation and increasing yield-per-plant, and were very successful in raising overall yields of cereal grains, but did not give sufficient relevance to nutritional quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SandsMorris2009_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-SandsMorris2009-14">[14]</a></sup> These modern high-yield cereal crops tend to have low <a href="/wiki/Protein_quality" title="Protein quality">quality proteins</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acid</a> deficiencies, are high in <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, and lack balanced <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid" title="Essential fatty acid">essential fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and other quality factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SandsMorris2009_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-SandsMorris2009-14">[14]</a></sup> So-called ancient grains and heirloom varieties have seen an increase in popularity with the "organic" movements of the early 21st century, but there is a tradeoff in yield-per-plant, putting pressure on resource-poor areas as food crops are replaced with cash crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_features">Common features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Common features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical">Botanical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Botanical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="470" data-file-width="740" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg/220px-Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg/330px-Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg/440px-Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Les_Plantes_Cultivades._Cereals._Imatge_119.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From left to right and from top to bottom: <a href="/wiki/Pearl_millet" title="Pearl millet">pearl millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>; <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oats</a>; <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triticale" title="Triticale">triticale</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Cereals belong to the family <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>, commonly known as grass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 5">: 5 </span></sup> Grasses have <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stems</a> that are hollow except at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodes_(botany)" title="Nodes (botany)">nodes</a> and narrow alternate leaves borne in two ranks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayton_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clayton-17">[17]</a></sup> The lower part of each leaf encloses the stem, forming a leaf-sheath. The leaf grows from the base of the blade, an adaptation allowing it to cope with frequent grazing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayton_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clayton-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBI13_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBI13-18">[18]</a></sup> The flowers are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphroditic" title="Hermaphroditic">hermaphroditic</a>—<a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> being an important exception—and mainly <a href="/wiki/Anemophily" title="Anemophily">anemophilous</a> or wind-pollinated, although insects occasionally play a role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayton_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Clayton-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the most-well known cereals are <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triticale" title="Triticale">triticale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 2–3">: 2–3 </span></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Pseudocereal" title="Pseudocereal">pseudocereals</a> are colloquially called cereal, even though botanically they do not belong to the Poaceae family; these include <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amaranth" title="Amaranth">amaranth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 68–69">: 68–69 </span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional">Nutritional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nutritional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some grains are deficient in the <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>. That is why many vegetarian cultures, in order to get a balanced diet, combine their diet of grains with <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a>. Many legumes, however, are deficient in the essential amino acid <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a>, which grains contain. Thus, a <a href="/wiki/Protein_combining" title="Protein combining">combination</a> of legumes with grains forms a well-balanced diet for vegetarians. Common examples of such combinations are <a href="/wiki/Dal" title="Dal">dal</a> (lentils) with <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> by <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South Indians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengali_people" title="Bengali people">Bengalis</a>, dal with wheat in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North India</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beans" title="Beans">beans</a> with <a href="/wiki/Corn_tortilla" title="Corn tortilla">corn tortillas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tofu" title="Tofu">tofu</a> with rice, and <a href="/wiki/Peanut_butter" title="Peanut butter">peanut butter</a> with wheat bread (as sandwiches) in several other cultures, including the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogel301_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-vogel301-20">[20]</a></sup> The amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_protein" title="Crude protein">crude protein</a> measured in grains is expressed as grain crude protein concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cereals contain <a href="/wiki/Exogeny" title="Exogeny">exogenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Opioid_food_peptides" title="Opioid food peptides">opioid food peptides</a> called <a href="/wiki/Exorphin" title="Exorphin">exorphins</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gluten_exorphin" title="Gluten exorphin">gluten exorphin</a>. They mimic the actions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endorphine" title="Endorphine">endorphines</a> because they bind to the same <a href="/wiki/Opioid_receptor" title="Opioid receptor">opioid receptors</a> in the brain.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While each individual species has its own peculiarities, the cultivation of all cereal crops is similar. Most are <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual plants</a>; consequently one planting yields one harvest. Cereals that are adapted to grow in <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate climate</a> are called warm-season cereals, and those grow in <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> are called cold-season cereals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 3–4">: 3–4 </span></sup> Wheat, rye, <a href="/wiki/Triticale" title="Triticale">triticale</a>, oats, barley, and <a href="/wiki/Spelt" title="Spelt">spelt</a> are the "cool-season" cereals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> These are hardy plants that grow well in moderate weather and cease to grow in hot weather (approximately 30 °C or 85 °F, but this varies by species and variety). The "warm-season" cereals are tender and prefer hot weather. Barley and rye are the hardiest cereals, able to overwinter in the <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">[23]</a></sup> Many cool-season cereals are grown in the tropics. However, some are only grown in cooler highlands, where it ' "may" ' be possible to grow multiple crops per year.
</p><p>For the past few decades, there has also been increasing interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perennial" title="Perennial">perennial</a> grain plants. This interest developed due to advantages in <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> control, reduced need for fertilizer, and potentially lowered costs to the farmer. Though research is still in early stages, <a href="/wiki/The_Land_Institute" title="The Land Institute">The Land Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Salina,_Kansas" title="Salina, Kansas">Salina, Kansas</a> has been able to create a few cultivars that produce a fairly good crop yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planting">Planting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Planting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4480" data-file-width="6720" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg/220px-Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg/330px-Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg/440px-Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Green_rice_sheaves_planted_in_a_paddy_field_with_long_shadows_at_golden_hour_in_Don_Det_Laos.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Planting of rice on a <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">paddy field</a></div></div></div>
<p>The warm-season cereals are grown in tropical lowlands year-round and in temperate climates during the frost-free season. Rice is commonly grown in flooded fields, though some strains are grown on dry land. Other warm climate cereals, such as <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, are adapted to arid conditions.
</p><p>Cool-season cereals are well-adapted to temperate climates. Most varieties of a particular species are either <b>winter</b> or <b>spring</b> types. Winter varieties are sown in the autumn, germinate and grow vegetatively, then become <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormant</a> during winter. They resume growing in the springtime and mature in late spring or early summer. This cultivation system makes optimal use of water and frees the land for another crop early in the growing season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Winter varieties do not flower until springtime because they require <a href="/wiki/Vernalization" title="Vernalization">vernalization</a>: exposure to low temperatures for a genetically determined length of time. Where winters are too warm for vernalization or exceed the hardiness of the crop (which varies by species and variety), farmers grow spring varieties. Spring cereals are planted in early springtime and mature later that same summer, without vernalization. Spring cereals typically require more irrigation and yield less than winter cereals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 4">: 4 </span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting">Harvesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once the cereal plants have grown their seeds, they have completed their <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life cycle</a>. The plants die, become brown, and dry. As soon as the parent plants and their seed kernels are reasonably dry, harvest can begin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In developed countries, cereal crops are universally machine-harvested, typically using a <a href="/wiki/Combine_harvester" title="Combine harvester">combine harvester</a>, which cuts, <a href="/wiki/Threshing" title="Threshing">threshes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Winnowing" title="Winnowing">winnows</a> the grain during a single pass across the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In developing countries, a variety of harvesting methods are in use, depending on the cost of labor, from combines to hand tools such as the <a href="/wiki/Scythe" title="Scythe">scythe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grain_cradle" title="Grain cradle">grain cradle</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preprocessing_and_storage">Preprocessing and storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Preprocessing and storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg/220px-Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg/330px-Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg/440px-Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Woman_from_small_village_peeling_corn_-_Zambia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Zambian woman and her kids peeling corns</div></div></div>
<p>If a crop is harvested during <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humid</a> weather, the grain may not dry adequately in the field to prevent spoilage during its storage. In this case, the grain is sent to a dehydrating facility, where artificial heat dries it.
</p><p>In North America, farmers commonly deliver their newly harvested grain to a <a href="/wiki/Grain_elevator" title="Grain elevator">grain elevator</a>, a large storage facility that consolidates the crops of many farmers. The farmer may sell the grain at the time of delivery or maintain ownership of a share of grain in the pool for later sale. Storage facilities should be protected from small grain pests, <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_consumption">Direct consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Direct consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An example of a cereal that require little preparation before human consumption is rice. For example, to make plain <a href="/wiki/Cooked_rice" title="Cooked rice">cooked rice</a>, raw <a href="/wiki/White_rice" title="White rice">milled rice</a> simply needs to be washed and submerged in a <a href="/wiki/Simmering" title="Simmering">simmering</a> water for 10–12 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flour-based_foods">Flour-based foods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Flour-based foods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">Flour</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Various_grains.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1825" data-file-width="2700" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Various_grains.jpg/220px-Various_grains.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Various_grains.jpg/330px-Various_grains.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Various_grains.jpg/440px-Various_grains.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Various_grains.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Various cereals and their products</div></div></div>
<p>Cereals can be <a href="/wiki/Grind" title="Grind">grinded</a> to make flour. Cereal flour, particularly <a href="/wiki/Wheat_flour" title="Wheat flour">wheat flour</a>, is the main ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staple food</a> for many cultures. <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">Corn</a> flour has been important in <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerican</a> cuisine since ancient times and remains a staple in the Americas. <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">Rye</a> flour is a constituent of bread in central and northern Europe, while <a href="/wiki/Rice_flour" title="Rice flour">rice flour</a> is common in Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cereal flour consists either of the <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cereal_germ" title="Cereal germ">germ</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bran" title="Bran">bran</a> together (whole-grain flour) or of the endosperm alone (refined flour). <i>Meal</i> is either differentiable from flour as having slightly coarser particle size (degree of <a href="/wiki/Comminution" title="Comminution">comminution</a>) or is synonymous with flour; the word is used both ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">[29]</a></sup> For example, the word <i><a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">cornmeal</a></i> often connotes a grittier texture whereas <i>corn flour</i> connotes fine powder, although there is no codified dividing line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcohol">Alcohol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cereal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Alcohol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of cereals' high starch content, they are often used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_alcohol" title="Industrial alcohol">Industrial alcohol</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_drink" title="Alcoholic drink">alcoholic drinks</a> via <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a>. For instance, beer is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starches</a>, mainly derived from cereal grains—most commonly from <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malted</a> barley, though wheat, maize (corn), rice, and oats are also used. During the brewing process, fermentation of the starch <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a> in the resulting beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barth_2014_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barth_2014-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Maize, wheat, and rice together accounted for 89% of all cereal production worldwide in 2012, and 43% of the global supply of <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> in 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prodstat_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-prodstat-40">[38]</a></sup> while the production of oats and rye have drastically fallen from their 1960s levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other cereals not included in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> statistics, include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Teff" title="Teff">Teff</a>, an ancient grain that is a staple in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and grown in sub-Saharan Africa as a grass primarily for feeding horses. It is high in fiber and protein. Its flour is often used to make <a href="/wiki/Injera" title="Injera">injera</a>. It can also be eaten as a warm breakfast cereal similar to <a href="/wiki/Farina_(food)" title="Farina (food)">farina</a> with a chocolate or nutty flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_36-9"><a href="#cite_note-:5-36">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wild_rice" title="Wild rice">Wild rice</a>, grown in small amounts in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_36-10"><a href="#cite_note-:5-36">[35]</a></sup></li></ul>
