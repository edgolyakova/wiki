<p><b>Masonry</b> is the building of structures from individual units, which are often laid in and bound together by <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>; the term <i>masonry</i> can also refer to the units themselves. The common materials of masonry construction are <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a>, building <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cast_stone" title="Cast stone">cast stone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concrete_block" title="Concrete block">concrete block</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> block, and <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a>. Masonry is generally a highly durable form of construction. However, the materials used, the quality of the mortar and workmanship, and the pattern in which the units are assembled can substantially affect the durability of the overall masonry construction. A person who constructs masonry is called a mason or <a href="/wiki/Bricklayer" title="Bricklayer">bricklayer</a>. These are both classified as <a href="/wiki/Construction_worker" title="Construction worker">construction trades</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Masonry is commonly used for walls and buildings. Brick and concrete block are the most common types of masonry in use in industrialized nations and may be either load-bearing or non-load-bearing. Concrete blocks, especially those with hollow cores, offer various possibilities in masonry construction. They generally provide great compressive strength and are best suited to structures with light transverse loading when the cores remain unfilled. Filling some or all of the cores with concrete or concrete with steel reinforcement (typically <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a>) offers much greater tensile and lateral strength to structures. Masonry workers are typically paid hourly depending on their position.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The use of materials such as bricks and stones can increase the thermal mass of a building.</li>
<li>Masonry is a non-combustible product and can protect the building from fire.</li>
<li>Masonry walls are more resistant to projectiles, such as debris from hurricanes or tornadoes.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Extreme weather, under certain circumstances, can cause degradation of masonry due to expansion and contraction forces associated with freeze-thaw cycles.</li>
<li>Masonry tends to be heavy and must be built upon a strong foundation, such as reinforced concrete, to avoid settling and cracking.</li>
<li>Other than concrete, masonry construction does not lend itself well to mechanization, and requires more skilled labor than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_framing" title="Balloon framing">stick-framing</a>.</li>
<li>Masonry consists of loose components and has a low tolerance to oscillation as compared to other materials such as reinforced concrete, plastics, wood, or metals.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_limitations">Structural limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structural limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One problem with masonry walls is that they rely mainly on their weight to keep them in place; each block or brick is only loosely connected to the next via a thin layer of mortar. This is why they do not perform well in earthquakes, when entire buildings are shaken horizontally. Many collapses during earthquakes occur in buildings that have load-bearing masonry walls. Besides, heavier buildings having masonry suffer more damage.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_set_masonry">Dry set masonry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dry set masonry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:221px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg/219px-Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg/329px-Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg/438px-Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg 2x" width="219"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vallorcine_bridge_abutment_2003-12-13.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry set masonry supports a rustic <a href="/wiki/Log_bridge" title="Log bridge">log bridge</a>, where it provides a well-drained support for the log (which will increase its service life).</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dry_stone" title="Dry stone">dry stone</a></div>
<p>The strength of a masonry wall is not entirely dependent on the bond between the building material and the mortar; the <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> between the interlocking blocks of masonry is often strong enough to provide a great deal of strength on its own. The blocks sometimes have grooves or other surface features added to enhance this interlocking, and some <i>dry set</i> masonry structures forgo mortar altogether.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stonework">Stonework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stonework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="991" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG/220px-K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG/330px-K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG/440px-K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:K%C3%A9libia_stone_masonry.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stone Masonry</div></div></div>
<p>Stone blocks used in masonry can be dressed or rough, though in both examples: corners, door and window jambs, and similar areas are usually dressed.
Stonemasonry utilizing dressed stones is known as <a href="/wiki/Ashlar" title="Ashlar">ashlar</a> masonry, whereas masonry using irregularly shaped stones is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubble_masonry" title="Rubble masonry">rubble masonry</a>. Both rubble and ashlar masonry can be laid in coursed rows of even height through the careful selection or cutting of stones, but a great deal of stone masonry is uncoursed.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Slipform_stonemasonry" title="Slipform stonemasonry">Slipform stonemasonry</a> produces a hybrid wall of reinforced concrete with a rubble stone face.</li>
<li>Natural <a href="/wiki/Stone_veneer" title="Stone veneer">stone veneers</a> over CMU, cast-in-place, or tilt-up concrete walls are widely used to give the appearance of stone masonry.</li>
<li>Sometimes river rock of smooth oval-shaped stones is used as a veneer. This type of material is not favored for solid masonry as it requires a great amount of mortar and can lack intrinsic structural strength.</li>
<li>Manufactured-stone, or cultured stone, veneers are popular alternatives to natural stones.</li>
<li>Manufactured-stone veneers are typically made from concrete.</li>
<li>Natural stones from quarries around the world are sampled and recreated using molds, aggregate, and colorfast pigments.</li>
<li>To the casual observer there may be no visual difference between veneers of natural and manufactured stone.<b></b></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brick">Brick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Brick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brickwork" title="Brickwork">Brickwork</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg/220px-Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg/330px-Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg/440px-Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brick_Work_2015-02-8_278.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brick Work</div></div></div>
<p>Solid <a href="/wiki/Brickwork" title="Brickwork">brickwork</a> is made of two or more <a href="/wiki/Wythe" title="Wythe">wythes</a> of bricks with the units running horizontally (called <i>stretcher</i> bricks) bound together with bricks running <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/transverse" title="wikt:transverse">transverse</a> to the wall (called "header" bricks). Each row of bricks is known as a course. The pattern of headers and stretchers employed gives rise to different 'bonds' such as the common bond (with every sixth course composed of headers), the English bond, and the Flemish bond (with alternating stretcher and header bricks present on every course). Bonds can differ in strength and in insulating ability. Vertically staggered bonds tend to be somewhat stronger and less prone to major cracking than a non-staggered bond.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniformity_and_rusticity">Uniformity and rusticity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uniformity and rusticity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Athruzmasonry.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="532" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Athruzmasonry.jpg/220px-Athruzmasonry.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Athruzmasonry.jpg/330px-Athruzmasonry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Athruzmasonry.jpg/440px-Athruzmasonry.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Athruzmasonry.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Masonry repair work done to a brick wall.</div></div></div>
<p>The wide selection of brick styles and types generally available in industrialized nations allow much variety in the appearance of the final product. In buildings built during the 1950s-1970s, a high degree of uniformity of brick and accuracy in masonry was typical. In the period since then this style was thought to be too sterile, so attempts were made to emulate older, rougher work. Some brick surfaces are made to look particularly rustic by including <i>burnt</i> bricks, which have a darker color or an irregular shape. Others may use antique salvage bricks, or new bricks may be artificially aged by applying various surface treatments, such as tumbling. The attempts at rusticity of the late 20th century have been carried forward by masons specializing in a free, artistic style, where the courses are intentionally <i>not</i> straight, instead weaving to form more organic impressions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serpentine_masonry">Serpentine masonry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Serpentine masonry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crinkle_crankle_walls" title="Crinkle crankle walls">Crinkle crankle walls</a></div>
<p>A crinkle-crankle wall is a brick wall that follows a serpentine path, rather than a straight line. This type of wall is more resistant to toppling than a straight wall; so much so that it may be made of a single wythe of unreinforced brick and so despite its longer length may be more economical than a straight wall.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concrete_block">Concrete block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Concrete block">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CMUs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1025" data-file-width="1516" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/CMUs.jpg/220px-CMUs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/CMUs.jpg/330px-CMUs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/CMUs.jpg/440px-CMUs.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CMUs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Concrete masonry units (CMUs) or blocks in a basement wall before burial.</div></div></div>
<p>Blocks of cinder concrete (<i><a href="/wiki/Concrete_masonry_unit" title="Concrete masonry unit">cinder blocks</a></i> or <i>breezeblocks</i>), ordinary concrete (<i><a href="/wiki/Concrete_masonry_unit" title="Concrete masonry unit">concrete blocks</a></i>), or hollow tile are generically known as Concrete Masonry Units (CMUs). They usually are much larger than ordinary bricks and so are much faster to lay for a wall of a given size. Furthermore, cinder and concrete blocks typically have much lower <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> absorption rates than brick. They often are used as the structural core for veneered brick masonry or are used alone for the walls of factories, garages, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_Style" title="Industrial Style">industrial-style</a> buildings where such appearance is acceptable or desirable. Such blocks often receive a <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> surface for decoration. Surface-bonding <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>, which contains synthetic fibers for reinforcement, is sometimes used in this application and can impart extra strength to a block wall. Surface-bonding cement is often pre-colored and can be stained or painted thus resulting in a finished stucco-like surface.
</p><p>The primary structural advantage of concrete blocks in comparison to smaller clay-based bricks is that a CMU wall can be reinforced by filling the block voids with <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> with or without steel <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a>. Generally, certain voids are designated for filling and reinforcement, particularly at corners, wall-ends, and openings while other voids are left empty.  This increases wall strength and stability more economically than filling and reinforcing all voids. Typically, structures made of CMUs will have the top course of blocks in the walls filled with concrete and tied together with steel reinforcement to form a bond beam. <a href="/wiki/Bond_beam" title="Bond beam">Bond beams</a> are often a requirement of modern <a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">building codes</a> and controls. Another type of steel reinforcement referred to as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ladder-reinforcement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ladder-reinforcement (page does not exist)">ladder-reinforcement</a>, can also be embedded in horizontal mortar joints of concrete block walls. The introduction of steel reinforcement generally results in a CMU wall having much greater lateral and tensile strength than unreinforced walls.
</p><p>"Architectural masonry is the evolvement of standard concrete masonry blocks into aesthetically pleasing concrete masonry units (CMUs)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2022)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup> CMUs can be manufactured to provide a variety of surface appearances. They can be colored during manufacturing or stained or painted after installation. They can be split as part of the manufacturing process, giving the blocks a rough face replicating the appearance of natural stone, such as <a href="/wiki/Brownstone" title="Brownstone">brownstone</a>. CMUs may also be scored, ribbed, sandblasted, polished, striated (raked or brushed), include decorative aggregates, be allowed to slump in a controlled fashion during curing, or include several of these techniques in their manufacture to provide a decorative appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Glazed concrete masonry units are manufactured by bonding a permanent colored facing (typically composed of polyester resins, silica sand and various other chemicals) to a concrete masonry unit, providing a smooth impervious surface."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_block" title="Glass block">Glass block</a> or glass brick are blocks made from glass and provide a translucent to clear vision through the block.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneer_masonry">Veneer masonry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Veneer masonry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A masonry veneer wall consists of masonry units, usually clay-based bricks, installed on one or both sides of a structurally independent wall usually constructed of wood or masonry. In this context, the brick masonry is primarily decorative, not structural. The brick veneer is generally connected to the structural wall by brick ties (metal strips that are attached to the structural wall, as well as the mortar joints of the brick veneer). There is typically an air gap between the brick veneer and the structural wall. As clay-based brick is usually not completely waterproof, the structural wall will often have a water-resistant surface (usually <a href="/wiki/Tar_paper" title="Tar paper">tar paper</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weep_hole" title="Weep hole">weep holes</a> can be left at the base of the brick veneer to drain moisture that accumulates inside the air gap. Concrete blocks, real and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultured_stone" title="Cultured stone">cultured stones</a>, and veneer <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a> are sometimes used in a very similar veneer fashion.
</p><p>Most insulated buildings that utilize concrete block, brick, adobe, stone, veneers or some combination thereof feature interior <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Glass_wool" title="Glass wool">fiberglass</a> batts between wooden wall studs or in the form of rigid insulation boards covered with <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drywall" title="Drywall">drywall</a>. In most climates this insulation is much more effective on the exterior of the wall, allowing the building interior to take advantage of the aforementioned thermal mass of the masonry. This technique does, however, require some sort of weather-resistant exterior surface over the insulation and, consequently, is generally more expensive.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gabions">Gabions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Gabions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal,_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel,_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1665" data-file-width="2221" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal%2C_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel%2C_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg/220px-A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal%2C_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel%2C_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal%2C_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel%2C_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg/330px-A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal%2C_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel%2C_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal%2C_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel%2C_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg/440px-A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal%2C_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel%2C_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_V%C3%B6r%C3%B6s_%C3%A9s_a_K%C3%A9k_k%C3%A1poln%C3%A1k_k%C3%B6z%C3%B6tti_s%C3%A9t%C3%A1ny_kerti_lugassal,_gabion_t%C3%A1mfalrendszerrel,_2018_Balatonbogl%C3%A1r.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gabion Wall</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gabion" title="Gabion">Gabions</a> are baskets, usually now of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>-protected <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot-dip_galvanizing" title="Hot-dip galvanizing">galvanized steel</a>) that are filled with fractured stone of medium size. These will act as a single unit and are stacked with setbacks to form a revetment or <a href="/wiki/Retaining_wall" title="Retaining wall">retaining wall</a>. They have the advantage of being well drained, flexible, and resistant to flood, water flow from above, frost damage, and soil flow. Their expected useful life is only as long as the wire they are composed of and if used in severe climates (such as shore-side in a salt water environment) must be made of appropriate corrosion-resistant wire.  Most modern gabions are rectangular.
</p><p>Earlier gabions were often cylindrical wicker baskets, open at both ends, used usually for temporary, often military, construction.
</p><p>Similar work can be done with finer aggregates using <a href="/wiki/Cellular_confinement" title="Cellular confinement">cellular confinement</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Passive_fire_protection_.28PFP.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_fire_protection_(PFP)">Passive fire protection (PFP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Passive fire protection (PFP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Masonry walls have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> effect of its <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrates</a>, as in chemically bound <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, unbound moisture from the concrete block, and the poured concrete if the hollow cores inside the blocks are filled. Masonry can withstand temperatures up to 1,000 °F and it can withstand direct exposure to fire for up to 4 hours.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition to that, concrete masonry keeps fires contained to their room of origin 93% of the time. For those reasons, concrete masonry units hold the highest <a href="/wiki/Fire_class" title="Fire class">fire class</a> flame spread classification, a Class A.
</p><p>Masonry buildings can also be built to increase safety by reducing fire damage, such as the use of <a href="/wiki/Fire_cut" title="Fire cut">fire cuts</a> during construction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_modeling_of_masonry_structures">Mechanical modeling of masonry structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masonry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mechanical modeling of masonry structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_masonry.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1948" data-file-width="3455" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Comparison_masonry.jpg/300px-Comparison_masonry.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Comparison_masonry.jpg/450px-Comparison_masonry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Comparison_masonry.jpg/600px-Comparison_masonry.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_masonry.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A comparison (using transmission <a href="/wiki/Photoelasticity" title="Photoelasticity">photoelasticity</a>) between the stress diffusion in an elastic body (on the left) and a model of masonry (on the right). A highly localized stress percolation is visible on the right.</div></div></div>
<p>From the point of view of material modeling, masonry is a special material of extreme mechanical properties (with a very high ratio between strength in compression and in tension), so that the applied loads do not diffuse as they do in elastic bodies, but tend to percolate along lines of high stiffness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
