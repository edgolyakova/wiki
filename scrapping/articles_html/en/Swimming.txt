<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Swimming</span></h1>

<p><b>Swimming</b> is the self-<a href="/wiki/Propulsion" title="Propulsion">propulsion</a> of a person through <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, or other liquid, usually for recreation, sport, exercise, or survival.  Locomotion is achieved through coordinated movement of the limbs and the body to achieve hydrodynamic thrust which results in directional motion. Humans can hold their breath underwater and undertake rudimentary locomotive swimming within weeks of birth, as a survival response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Swimming is consistently among the top public recreational activities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in some countries, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_lessons" title="Swimming lessons">swimming lessons</a> are a compulsory part of the educational curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  As a formalized sport, swimming is featured in a range of local, national, and international competitions, including every modern <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Swimming at the Summer Olympics">Summer Olympics</a>.
</p><p>Swimming involves repeated motions known as <a href="/wiki/Swimming_stroke" title="Swimming stroke">strokes</a> in order to propel the body forward. While the <a href="/wiki/Front_crawl" title="Front crawl">front crawl</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_swimming" title="Freestyle swimming">freestyle</a>, is widely regarded as the fastest out of four primary strokes, other strokes are practiced for special purposes, such as for training.
</p><p>There are various risks present during swimming, mainly due to it taking place in water. Swimmers are at risk of incapacitation due to <a href="/wiki/Panic" title="Panic">panic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exhaustion" title="Exhaustion">exhaustion</a>, which may cause death due to <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowning</a>. Other dangers include getting an <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a> or contact with hostile aquatic fauna. To minimize these risks, most facilities employ a <a href="/wiki/Lifeguard" title="Lifeguard">lifeguard</a> to look for signs of distress.
</p><p>Swimmers often wear specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimwear" title="Swimwear">swimwear</a>, although depending on the culture of the area, some swimmers may also swim <a href="/wiki/Nude_swimming" title="Nude swimming">nude</a> or wear their day attire. In addition to this, a variety of equipment can be used to enhance the swimming experience or performance including but not limited to the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimming_goggles" title="Swimming goggles">swimming goggles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floatation_device" title="Floatation device">floatation devices</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swim_fins" title="Swim fins">swim fins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Snorkel_(swimming)" title="Snorkel (swimming)">snorkels</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science">Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Participants_with_the_Water_Warrior_class_perform_squats_prior_to_entering_the_pool_at_Camp_Foster,_Okinawa,_Japan,_July_6,_2011_110706-M-VD776-004_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1736" data-file-width="2851" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Participants_with_the_Water_Warrior_class_perform_squats_prior_to_entering_the_pool_at_Camp_Foster%2C_Okinawa%2C_Japan%2C_July_6%2C_2011_110706-M-VD776-004_%28cropped%29.jpg/260px-thumbnail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Participants_with_the_Water_Warrior_class_perform_squats_prior_to_entering_the_pool_at_Camp_Foster%2C_Okinawa%2C_Japan%2C_July_6%2C_2011_110706-M-VD776-004_%28cropped%29.jpg/390px-thumbnail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Participants_with_the_Water_Warrior_class_perform_squats_prior_to_entering_the_pool_at_Camp_Foster%2C_Okinawa%2C_Japan%2C_July_6%2C_2011_110706-M-VD776-004_%28cropped%29.jpg/520px-thumbnail.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Participants_with_the_Water_Warrior_class_perform_squats_prior_to_entering_the_pool_at_Camp_Foster,_Okinawa,_Japan,_July_6,_2011_110706-M-VD776-004_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Swimmers perform <a href="/wiki/Squat_(exercise)" title="Squat (exercise)">squats</a> prior to entering the pool in a U.S. military base, 2011</div></div></div>
<p>Swimming relies on the nearly <a href="/wiki/Neutral_buoyancy" title="Neutral buoyancy">neutral buoyancy</a> of the human body. On average, the body has a <a href="/wiki/Relative_density" title="Relative density">relative density</a> of 0.98 compared to water, which causes the body to float. However, buoyancy varies on the basis of body composition, lung inflation, muscle and fat content, centre of gravity and the salinity of the water. Higher levels of body fat and saltier water both lower the relative density of the body and increase its buoyancy. Human males tend to have a lower centre of gravity and higher muscle content, therefore find it more difficult to float or be buoyant.  See also: <i><a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_weighing" title="Hydrostatic weighing">Hydrostatic weighing</a>.</i>
</p><p>Since the human body is less dense than water, water is able to support the weight of the body during swimming. As a result, swimming is “low-impact” compared to land activities such as running. The density and <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of water also create resistance for objects moving through the water. <a href="/wiki/Swimming_stroke" title="Swimming stroke">Swimming strokes</a> use this resistance to create propulsion, but this same resistance also generates drag on the body.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamics" title="Hydrodynamics">Hydrodynamics</a> is important to stroke technique for swimming faster, and swimmers who want to swim faster or exhaust less try to reduce the drag of the body's motion through the water. To be more hydrodynamically effective, swimmers can either increase the power of their strokes or reduce water resistance, though power must increase by a factor of three to achieve the same effect as reducing resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laughlin_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin-7">[7]</a></sup> Efficient swimming by reducing water resistance involves a horizontal water position, rolling the body to reduce the breadth of the body in the water, and extending the arms as far as possible to reduce wave resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laughlin_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laughlin-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Just before plunging into the pool, swimmers may perform exercises such as <a href="/wiki/Squat_(exercise)" title="Squat (exercise)">squatting</a>. Squatting helps in enhancing a swimmer's start by warming up the thigh muscles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2021)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infant_swimming">Infant swimming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Infant swimming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infant_swimming" title="Infant swimming">Infant swimming</a></div>
<p>Human babies demonstrate an innate swimming or <a href="/wiki/Diving_reflex" title="Diving reflex">diving reflex</a> from newborn until the age of approximately 6 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradycardic_response_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradycardic_response-9">[9]</a></sup> Other mammals also demonstrate this phenomenon (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammalian_diving_reflex" title="Mammalian diving reflex">mammalian diving reflex</a>).  The diving response involves <a href="/wiki/Apnea" title="Apnea">apnea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reflex_bradycardia" title="Reflex bradycardia">reflex bradycardia</a>, and peripheral <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstriction" title="Vasoconstriction">vasoconstriction</a>; in other words, babies immersed in water spontaneously hold their breath, slow their heart rate, and reduce blood circulation to the extremities (fingers and toes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradycardic_response_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradycardic_response-9">[9]</a></sup>
Because infants are innately able to swim, classes for babies of about 6 months old are offered in many locations. This helps build muscle memory and makes strong swimmers from a young age.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Swimming_stroke" title="Swimming stroke">Swimming stroke</a></div>
<p>Swimming can be undertaken using a wide range of styles, known as 'strokes,' and these strokes are used for different purposes, or to distinguish between classes in competitive swimming.  It is not necessary to use a defined stroke for propulsion through the water, and untrained swimmers may use a '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doggy_paddle" title="Doggy paddle">doggy paddle</a>' of arm and leg movements, similar to the way four-legged animals swim.
</p><p>There are four main strokes used in competition and recreation swimming: the <a href="/wiki/Front_crawl" title="Front crawl">front crawl</a>, also known as freestyle, <a href="/wiki/Breaststroke" title="Breaststroke">breaststroke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Backstroke" title="Backstroke">backstroke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly_stroke" title="Butterfly stroke">butterfly</a>.  Competitive swimming in Europe started around 1800, mostly using the breaststroke. In 1873, John Arthur Trudgen introduced the <a href="/wiki/Trudgen" title="Trudgen">trudgen</a> to Western swimming competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Butterfly was developed in the 1930s, and was considered a variant of the breaststroke until accepted as a separate style in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Butterfly is considered the hardest stroke by many people, but it is the most effective for all-around toning and the building of muscles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup> It also burns the most calories and can be the second fastest stroke if practiced regularly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In non-competitive swimming, there are some swimming strokes including <a href="/wiki/Sidestroke" title="Sidestroke">sidestroke</a>. The sidestroke toward the end of the 19th century, this pattern was changed by raising one arm above the water first, then the other, and then each in turn. It is still used in lifesaving and recreational swimming.
</p><p>Other strokes exist for particular reasons such as training, school lessons, and rescue, and it is often possible to change strokes to avoid using parts of the body, either to separate specific body parts, such as swimming with only arms or legs to exercise them harder, or for amputees or those affected by paralysis.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_swimming" title="History of swimming">History of swimming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:River_crossing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="453" data-file-width="650" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/River_crossing.jpg/220px-River_crossing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/River_crossing.jpg/330px-River_crossing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/River_crossing.jpg/440px-River_crossing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:River_crossing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Timurid_dynasty" title="Timurid dynasty">Timurid</a> conqueror <a href="/wiki/Babur" title="Babur">Babur</a>'s troops swim across a river.</div></div></div>
<p>Swimming has been recorded since prehistoric times, and the earliest records of swimming date back to <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Swimmers" title="Cave of Swimmers">paintings</a> from around 7,000 years ago. Written references date from 2000 BC.  Some of the earliest references include the <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> (Ezekiel 47:5, Acts 27:42, Isaiah 25:11), <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a>, and other sagas.
</p><p>The coastal tribes living in the volatile<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title='What does "volatile" mean here? (August 2021)'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> were known as excellent swimmers by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>. Men and horses of the <a href="/wiki/Batavi_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Batavi (Germanic tribe)">Batavi tribe</a> could cross the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> without losing formation, according to <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dio_Cassius" title="Dio Cassius">Dio Cassius</a> describes one surprise tactic employed by <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Plautius" title="Aulus Plautius">Aulus Plautius</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Medway" title="Battle of the Medway">Battle of the Medway</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The [British Celts] thought that Romans would not be able to cross it without a bridge, and consequently bivouacked in rather careless fashion on the opposite bank; but he sent across a detachment of [Batavii], who were accustomed to swim easily in full armour across the most turbulent streams. . . . Thence the Britons retired to the river Thames at a point near where it empties into the ocean and at flood-tide forms a lake. This they easily crossed because they knew where the firm ground and the easy passages in this region were to be found, but the Romans in attempting to follow them were not so successful. However, the [Batavii] swam across again and some others got over by a bridge a little way up-stream, after which they assailed the barbarians from several sides at once and cut down many of them."</p></blockquote>
<p>In 1538, Nikolaus Wynmann, a Swiss–German professor of languages, wrote the earliest known complete book about swimming, <i>Colymbetes, sive de arte natandi dialogus et festivus et iucundus lectu</i> (<i>The Swimmer, or A Dialogue on the Art of Swimming and Joyful and Pleasant to Read</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many reasons why people swim, from swimming as a recreational pursuit to swimming as a necessary part of a job or other activity. Swimming may also be used to rehabilitate injuries, especially various cardiovascular and muscle injuries. People may also pursue swimming as a career or field of interest. Some may be gifted and choose to compete professionally and go onto claim fame.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Children%27s_bathing_beach,_Lincoln_Park,_Chicago,_Illinois,_1905.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2076" data-file-width="3296" decoding="async" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Children%27s_bathing_beach%2C_Lincoln_Park%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois%2C_1905.jpg/220px-Children%27s_bathing_beach%2C_Lincoln_Park%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois%2C_1905.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Children%27s_bathing_beach%2C_Lincoln_Park%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois%2C_1905.jpg/330px-Children%27s_bathing_beach%2C_Lincoln_Park%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois%2C_1905.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Children%27s_bathing_beach%2C_Lincoln_Park%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois%2C_1905.jpg/440px-Children%27s_bathing_beach%2C_Lincoln_Park%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois%2C_1905.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Children%27s_bathing_beach,_Lincoln_Park,_Chicago,_Illinois,_1905.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Children's bathing beach, Lincoln Park, Chicago, Illinois, United States, 1905</div></div></div>
<p>Many swimmers swim for recreation, with swimming consistently ranking as one of the physical activities people are most likely to take part in.  Recreational swimming can also be used for exercise, relaxation or rehabilitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  The support of the water, and the reduction in impact, makes swimming accessible for people who are unable to undertake activities such as running. Swimming is one of the most relaxing activities, water is known to calm us and can help reduce stress.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming_and_health&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swimming and health (page does not exist)">Swimming and health</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chuck_Sketch,_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team,_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="2880" decoding="async" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Chuck_Sketch%2C_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team%2C_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg/220px-Chuck_Sketch%2C_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team%2C_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Chuck_Sketch%2C_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team%2C_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg/330px-Chuck_Sketch%2C_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team%2C_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Chuck_Sketch%2C_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team%2C_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg/440px-Chuck_Sketch%2C_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team%2C_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chuck_Sketch,_a_Wounded_Warrior_with_the_veteran_swim_team,_swims_laps_during_a_practice_session_120214-M-YO938-177.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chuck Sketch, a former U.S. Marine and a Wounded Warrior with the veteran swim team, swims laps during a practice session at the 2012 Marine Corps Trials at <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Base_Camp_Pendleton" title="Marine Corps Base Camp Pendleton">Marine Corps Base Camp Pendleton</a>, California, February 14, 2012. Wounded Warriors, veterans, and allies competed in the second annual trials, which included swimming.</div></div></div>
<p>Swimming is primarily a <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_exercise" title="Aerobic exercise">cardiovascular/aerobic exercise</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> due to the long exercise time, requiring a constant oxygen supply to the muscles, except for short sprints where the muscles work anaerobically. Furthermore, swimming can help tone and strengthen muscles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Swimming allows sufferers of <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a> to exercise affected joints without worsening their symptoms. However, swimmers with arthritis may wish to avoid swimming breaststroke, as improper technique can exacerbate arthritic knee pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As with most aerobic exercise, swimming reduces the harmful effects of stress. Swimming is also effective in improving health for people with cardiovascular problems and chronic illnesses. It is proven to positively impact the mental health of pregnant women and mothers. Swimming can even improve mood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Although many forms of physical activity have been shown to improve <a href="/wiki/Bone_density" title="Bone density">bone density</a> and health, this is where swimming has its downfalls. Due to the low-impact nature of the sport, studies have demonstrated that bone mass acquisition will be negatively impacted, which could be an issue for adolescent athletes in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Regular swimming activities contribute to the restoration of health, and in many cases, complete recovery. Activity in water is the basis for a good work of the heart muscle, the endocrine system of the body, stable blood pressure, full blood circulation in the vessels, which is not only the prevention of a lot of problems but also a powerful healing effect without any side effects. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Disabled_swimmers">Disabled swimmers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disabled swimmers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act" title="Americans with Disabilities Act">Americans with Disabilities Act</a> has required that <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">swimming pools</a> in the United States be accessible to disabled swimmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Elderly_swimmers">Elderly swimmers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elderly swimmers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"Water-based exercise can benefit older adults by improving quality of life and decreasing disability. It also improves or maintains the bone health of post-menopausal women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
Swimming is an ideal workout for the elderly, as it is a low-impact sport with very little risk of injury. Exercise in the water works out all muscle groups, helping with conditions such as muscular dystrophy which is common in seniors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">Swimming (sport)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Depart4x100.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1004" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Depart4x100.jpg/220px-Depart4x100.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Depart4x100.jpg/330px-Depart4x100.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Depart4x100.jpg/440px-Depart4x100.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Depart4x100.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Start of the 4 × 100 meters men's relay during the <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics" title="Swimming at the 2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Swimming as a sport predominantly involves participants competing to be the fastest over a given distance in a certain period of time.  Competitors swim different distances in different levels of competition. For example, swimming has been an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_Swimming" title="Olympic Swimming">Olympic sport</a> since 1896, and the current program includes events from 50 m to 1500 m in length, across all four main strokes and medley.  During the season competitive swimmers typically train several times a week, this is in order to preserve fitness as well as promoting overload in training. Furthermore when the cycle of work is completed swimmers go through a stage called taper where intensity is reduced in preparation for racing, during taper power and feel in the water are concentrated.
</p><p>The sport is governed internationally by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Swimming_Federation" title="International Swimming Federation">Fédération Internationale de Natation (FINA)</a>, and competition pools for FINA events are 25 or 50 meters in length. In the United States, a pool 25 yards in length is commonly used for competition.
</p><p>Other swimming and water-related sporting disciplines include <a href="/wiki/Open_water_swimming" title="Open water swimming">open water swimming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diving_(sport)" title="Diving (sport)">diving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synchronized_swimming" title="Synchronized swimming">synchronized swimming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_polo" title="Water polo">water polo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Modern_pentathlon" title="Modern pentathlon">modern pentathlon</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">Drowning</a></div>
<p>As a popular leisure activity done all over the world, one of the primary risks of swimming is <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowning</a>. Drowning may occur from a variety of factors, from swimming fatigue to simply inexperience in the water. From 2005 to 2014, an average of 3,536 fatal unintentional drownings occurred in the United States, approximating 10 deaths a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>To minimize the risk and prevent potential drownings from occurring, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lifeguards" title="Lifeguards">lifeguards</a> are often employed to supervise swimming locations such as pools, waterparks, lakes and beaches. Different lifeguards receive different training depending on the sites that they are employed at; i.e. a waterfront lifeguard receives more rigorous training than a poolside lifeguard. Well-known aquatic training services include the <a class="external text" href="https://www.lifesavingsociety.com/home.aspx" rel="nofollow">National Lifesaving Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Red_Cross" title="Canadian Red Cross">Canadian Red Cross</a>, which specialize in training lifeguards in North America.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupation">Occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some occupations require workers to swim, such as <a href="/wiki/Abalone" title="Abalone">abalone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearl</a> diving, and <a href="/wiki/Spearfishing" title="Spearfishing">spearfishing</a>.
</p><p>Swimming is used to rescue people in the water who are in distress, including exhausted swimmers, non-swimmers who have accidentally entered the water, and others who have come to harm on the water. <a href="/wiki/Lifeguard" title="Lifeguard">Lifeguards</a> or volunteer <a href="/wiki/Lifesaving" title="Lifesaving">lifesavers</a> are deployed at many pools and beaches worldwide to fulfil this purpose, and they, as well as <a href="/wiki/Rescue_swimmer" title="Rescue swimmer">rescue swimmers</a>, may use specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_swimming_styles" title="List of swimming styles">swimming styles</a> for rescue purposes.
</p><p>Swimming is also used in <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biology</a> to observe plants and animals in their natural habitat. Other sciences use swimming; for example, <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Lorenz" title="Konrad Lorenz">Konrad Lorenz</a> swam with geese as part of his studies of <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">animal behavior</a>.
</p><p>Swimming also has military purposes. Military swimming is usually done by special operation forces, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navy_SEALs" title="Navy SEALs">Navy SEALs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army_Special_Forces" title="US Army Special Forces">US Army Special Forces</a>. Swimming is used to approach a location, gather intelligence, engage in sabotage or combat, and subsequently depart. This may also include airborne insertion into water or exiting a submarine while it is submerged. Due to regular exposure to large bodies of water, all recruits in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a> are required to complete basic swimming or water survival training.
</p><p>Swimming is also a professional sport. Companies sponsor swimmers who have the skills to compete at the international level. Many swimmers compete competitively to represent their home countries in the Olympics. Professional swimmers may also earn a living as entertainers, performing in <a href="/wiki/Synchronized_swimming" title="Synchronized swimming">water ballets</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotion">Locomotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Locomotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Locomotion by swimming over brief distances is frequent when alternatives are precluded. There have been cases of political refugees swimming in the Baltic Sea<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and of people jumping in the water and swimming ashore from vessels not intended to reach land where they planned to go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>There are many risks associated with voluntary or involuntary human presence in water, which may result in death directly or through <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowning asphyxiation</a>. Swimming is both the goal of much voluntary presence and the prime means of regaining land in accidental situations.
</p><p>Most recorded water deaths fall into these categories:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Panic" title="Panic">Panic</a> occurs when an inexperienced swimmer or a nonswimmer becomes mentally overwhelmed by the circumstances of their immersion, leading to sinking and drowning. Occasionally, panic kills through <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">hyperventilation</a>, even in shallow water.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">Exhaustion</a> can make a person unable to sustain efforts to swim or tread water, often leading to death through drowning. An adult with fully developed and extended lungs has generally positive or at least neutral <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>, and can float with modest effort when calm and in still water. A small child has negative buoyancy and must make a sustained effort to avoid sinking rapidly.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">Hypothermia</a>, in which a person loses critical core temperature, can lead to unconsciousness or heart failure.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">Dehydration</a> from prolonged exposure to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypertonic" title="Hypertonic">hypertonic</a> salt water—or, less frequently, <a href="/wiki/Salt_water_aspiration_syndrome" title="Salt water aspiration syndrome">salt water aspiration syndrome</a> where inhaled salt water creates foam in the lungs that restricts breathing—can cause loss of physical control or kill directly without actual drowning. Hypothermia and dehydration also kill directly, without causing drowning, even when the person wears a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_vest" title="Life vest">life vest</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blunt_trauma" title="Blunt trauma">Blunt trauma</a> in a fast moving flood or river water can kill a swimmer outright, or lead to their drowning.</li></ul>
<p>Adverse effects of swimming can include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Exostosis" title="Exostosis">Exostosis</a>, an abnormal bony overgrowth narrowing the ear canal due to frequent, long-term splashing or filling of cold water into the ear canal, also known as <a href="/wiki/Surfer%27s_ear" title="Surfer's ear">surfer's ear</a></li>
<li>Infection from water-borne bacteria, viruses, or parasites</li>
<li>Chlorine inhalation (in swimming pools)</li>
<li>Heart attacks while swimming (the primary cause of sudden death among <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a> participants, occurring at the rate of 1 to 2 per 100,000 participations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>Adverse encounters with aquatic life:
<ul><li>Stings from <a href="/wiki/Sea_louse" title="Sea louse">sea lice</a>, jellyfish, fish, <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">seashells</a>, and some species of <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a></li>
<li>Puncture wounds caused by crabs, lobsters, <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zebra_mussel" title="Zebra mussel">zebra mussels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stingrays" title="Stingrays">stingrays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flying_fish" title="Flying fish">flying fish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_birds" title="Sea birds">sea birds</a>, and debris</li>
<li>Hemorrhaging bites from fish, marine mammals, and <a href="/wiki/Marine_reptile" title="Marine reptile">marine reptiles</a>, occasionally resulting from <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a></li>
<li>Venomous bites from <a href="/wiki/Sea_snake" title="Sea snake">sea snakes</a> and certain species of octopus</li>
<li>Electrocution or mild shock from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_eels" title="Electric eels">electric eels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_rays" title="Electric rays">electric rays</a></li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Around any pool area, safety equipment is often important, and is a zoning requirement for most residential pools in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Supervision by personnel trained in rescue techniques is required at most competitive swimming meets and public pools.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lessons">Lessons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lessons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2912" data-file-width="4368" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg/220px-Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg/330px-Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg/440px-Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Girl_with_swimming_board.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Styrofoam" title="Styrofoam">Styrofoam</a> flotation aid being used.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Swimming_lessons" title="Swimming lessons">Swimming lessons</a></div>
<p>Traditionally, children were considered not able to swim independently until 4 years of age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
although now <a href="/wiki/Infant_swimming" title="Infant swimming">infant swimming</a> lessons are recommended to prevent drowning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Sweden, Denmark, Norway, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> and Finland, the <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">curriculum</a> for the fifth grade (fourth grade in Estonia) states that all children should learn how to swim as well as how to handle emergencies near water. Most commonly, children are expected to be able to swim 200 metres (660 ft)—of which at least 50 metres (160 ft) on their back – after first falling into deep water and getting their head under water. Even though about 95 percent of Swedish school children know how to swim, drowning remains the third most common cause of death among children.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In both the Netherlands and Belgium swimming lessons under school time (<i>schoolzwemmen</i>, school swimming) are supported by the government. Most schools provide swimming lessons. There is a long tradition of swimming lessons in the Netherlands and Belgium, the Dutch translation for the breaststroke swimming style is even <i>schoolslag</i> (schoolstroke). In France, swimming is a compulsory part of the curriculum for primary schools. Children usually spend one semester per year learning swimming during CP/CE1/CE2/CM1 (1st, 2nd, 3rd and 4th grade).
</p><p>In many places, swimming lessons are provided by local swimming pools, both those run by the local authority and by private leisure companies. Many schools also include swimming lessons into their Physical Education <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curricula" title="Curricula">curricula</a>, provided either in the schools' own pool or in the nearest public pool.
</p><p>In the UK, the "Top-ups scheme" calls for school children who cannot swim by the age of 11 to receive intensive daily lessons. Children who have not reached Great Britain's National Curriculum standard of swimming 25 meters by the time they leave primary school receive a half-hour lesson every day for two weeks during term-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Canada and Mexico there has been a call to include swimming in public school curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States there is the Infant Swimming Resource (ISR)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> initiative that provides lessons for infant children, to cope with an emergency where they have fallen into the water. They are taught how to roll-back-to-float (hold their breath underwater, to roll onto their back, to float unassisted, rest and breathe until help arrives), while clothed and unclothed. In ISR they teach the children how to roll with their clothes on, as a simulation, if they were to actually fall in walking or crawling by.
</p><p>In Switzerland, swimming lessons for babies are popular, to help them getting used to be in another element. At the competition level, unlike in other countries - such as the Commonwealth countries, swimming teams are not related to educational institutions (high-schools and universities), but rather to cities or regions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clothing_and_equipment">Clothing and equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Clothing and equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swimsuits">Swimsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Swimsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Standard everyday clothing is usually impractical for swimming and is unsafe under some circumstances. Most cultures today expect swimmers to wear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimsuits" title="Swimsuits">swimsuits</a>.
</p><p>Men's swimsuits commonly resemble <a href="/wiki/Shorts" title="Shorts">shorts</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Briefs" title="Briefs">briefs</a>. Men's casual swimsuits (for example, <a href="/wiki/Boardshorts" title="Boardshorts">boardshorts</a>) are rarely skintight, unlike <a href="/wiki/Competitive_swimwear" title="Competitive swimwear">competitive swimwear</a>, like jammers or diveskins. In most cases, boys and men swim with their upper body exposed, except in countries where custom or law prohibits it in a public setting, or for practical reasons such as sun protection.
</p><p>Modern women's swimsuits are generally skintight, covering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pubic_region" title="Pubic region">pubic region</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breasts" title="Breasts">breasts</a> (See <a href="/wiki/Bikini" title="Bikini">bikini</a>). Women's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimwear" title="Swimwear">swimwear</a> may also cover the <a href="/wiki/Midriff" title="Midriff">midriff</a> as well. Women's swimwear is often a fashion statement, and whether it is <a href="/wiki/Modesty" title="Modesty">modest</a> or not is a subject of debate by many groups, religious and secular.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:300px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dance_With_Waves.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3543" data-file-width="5315" decoding="async" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Dance_With_Waves.jpg/298px-Dance_With_Waves.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Dance_With_Waves.jpg/447px-Dance_With_Waves.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Dance_With_Waves.jpg/596px-Dance_With_Waves.jpg 2x" width="298"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dance_With_Waves.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Different cultures have different standards for swimsuits. In Muslim communities, women may choose to wear <a href="/wiki/Hijab" title="Hijab">Hijab</a> modified swimsuits, sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Burkini" title="Burkini">burkini</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Competitive_swimwear" title="Competitive swimwear">Competitive swimwear</a> is built so that the wearer can swim faster and more efficiently. Modern competitive swimwear is skintight and lightweight. There are many kinds of competitive swimwear for each gender. It is used in aquatic competitions, such as water polo, swim racing, diving, and <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetsuits" title="Wetsuits">Wetsuits</a> provide both thermal insulation and flotation. Many swimmers lack buoyancy in the leg. The wetsuit provides additional volume at a lower density and therefore improves buoyancy and trim while swimming. It provides insulation between the skin and water which reduces heat loss. The wetsuit is the usual choice for those who swim in cold water for long periods of time, as it reduces susceptibility to <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>.
</p><p>Some people also choose to wear no clothing while swimming; this is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skinny_dipping" title="Skinny dipping">skinny dipping</a>. In some European countries public pools have naturist sessions to allow clothes-free swimming and many countries have naturist beaches where one can swim naked. It is legal to swim naked in the sea at all UK beaches. It was common for males to swim naked in a public setting up to the early 20th century. Today, skinny dipping can be a rebellious activity or merely a casual one.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessories">Accessories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Accessories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Earplug" title="Earplug">Ear plugs</a> can prevent water from getting in the ears.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Noseclip" title="Noseclip">Noseclips</a> can prevent water from getting in the nose. However, using noseclips in competitive swimming can cause disadvantage, so many competitive swimmer choose not to use one. For this reason, nose clips are primarily used for synchronized swimming and recreational swimming.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Goggles" title="Goggles">Goggles</a> protect the eyes from chlorinated water, and improve underwater visibility. Tinted goggles protect the eyes from sunlight that reflects from the bottom of the pool.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swim_cap" title="Swim cap">Swim caps</a> keep the body streamlined and protect the hair from chlorinated water, though they are not entirely watertight.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kickboard" title="Kickboard">Kickboards</a> are used to keep the upper body afloat while exercising the lower body.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pull_buoy" title="Pull buoy">Pull buoys</a> are used to keep the lower body afloat while exercising the upper body.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimfins" title="Swimfins">Swimfins</a> are used in training to elongate the kick and improve technique and speed. Fins also build upper calf muscles. Fins provide a significantly greater and more efficient conversion of muscle power to thrust than available from the feet, and allow the powerful leg muscles to be used effectively for propulsion through water. The value of fins as an active aid in the teaching, learning and practice of swimming has long been recognised. In the US, as early as 1947, they were used experimentally to build the confidence of reluctant beginners in swimming,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> while a 1950 <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">YMCA</a> lifesaving and water safety manual reminded swimming instructors how "flippers can be used to great advantage for treading water, surface diving, towing, underwater searching and supporting a tired swimmer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1967, research was conducted on fin use in teaching the crawl stroke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> During the 1970s, the so-called "flipper-float" method came into vogue in Europe with the aim of helping beginners learn to swim faster and more safely,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hand_paddle" title="Hand paddle">Hand paddles</a> are used to increase resistance during arm movements, with the goal of improving technique and power.</li>
<li>Finger paddles have a similar effect to handle paddles however due to their smaller size create less resistance. They also help with improving a swimmers 'catch' in the water.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snorkel_(swimming)" title="Snorkel (swimming)">Snorkels</a> are used to help improve and maintain a good head position in the water. They may also be used by some during <a href="/wiki/Physical_therapy" title="Physical therapy">physical therapy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pool_noodle" title="Pool noodle">Pool noodles</a> are used to keep the user afloat during the time in the water.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pool_fence" title="Pool fence">Safety fencing</a> and equipment is mandatory at public pools and a zoning requirement at most residential pools in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>Swimming Parachutes are used in competitive training, adding an element of resistance in the water helping athletes to increase power in the strokes central movements.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inflatable_armbands" title="Inflatable armbands">Inflatable armbands</a> are swimming aids designed to provide buoyancy for the swimmer which helps the wearer to float.</li></ul>
