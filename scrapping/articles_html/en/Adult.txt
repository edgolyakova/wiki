<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biologically" title="Biologically">Biologically</a>, an <b>adult</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> that has reached <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">sexual maturity</a>. In human context, the term <i>adult</i> has meanings associated with <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">social</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">legal</a> concepts. In contrast to a "<a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">minor</a>", a <b>legal adult</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a> who has attained the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of majority</a> and is therefore regarded as independent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-sufficient" title="Self-sufficient">self-sufficient</a>, and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/responsible" title="wikt:responsible">responsible</a>. The typical age of attaining legal adulthood is 18, although definition may vary by legal rights, country, and psychological development.
</p><p>Human adulthood encompasses <a href="/wiki/Adult_development" title="Adult development">psychological adult development</a>. Definitions of adulthood are often inconsistent and contradictory; a person may be biologically an adult, and have adult behavior, but still be treated as a child if they are under the legal age of majority. Conversely, one may legally be an adult but possess none of the <a href="/wiki/Maturity_(psychological)" title="Maturity (psychological)">maturity</a> and responsibility that may define an adult character.
</p><p>In different cultures there are events that relate passing from being a child to becoming an adult or <a href="/wiki/Coming_of_age" title="Coming of age">coming of age</a>. This often encompasses the passing a series of tests to demonstrate that a person is prepared for adulthood, or reaching a specified age, sometimes in conjunction with demonstrating preparation. Most modern societies determine legal adulthood based on reaching a legally specified age without requiring a demonstration of physical maturity or preparation for adulthood.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_adulthood">Biological adulthood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biological adulthood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RudolfMilly.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="828" decoding="async" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/RudolfMilly.jpg/270px-RudolfMilly.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/RudolfMilly.jpg/405px-RudolfMilly.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/RudolfMilly.jpg/540px-RudolfMilly.jpg 2x" width="270"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RudolfMilly.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A group of adult people</div></div></div>
<p>Historically and cross-culturally, adulthood has been determined primarily by the start of <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a> (the appearance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_sex_characteristics" title="Secondary sex characteristics">secondary sex characteristics</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstruation</a> and the development of breasts in women, <a href="/wiki/Ejaculation" title="Ejaculation">ejaculation</a>, the development of facial hair, and a deeper voice in men, and pubic hair in both sexes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNamara_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNamara-1">[1]</a></sup> In the past, a person usually moved from the status of child directly to the status of adult, often with this shift being marked by some type of coming-of-age test or ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> During the Industrial Revolution, children went to work as soon as they could in order to help provide for their family. There was not a huge emphasis on school or education in general. Many children could get a job and were not required to have experience as adults are nowadays.
</p><p>After the social construct of <a href="/wiki/Adolescence" title="Adolescence">adolescence</a> was created,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> adulthood split into two forms: <i>biological adulthood</i> and <i>social adulthood.</i> Thus, there are now two primary forms of adults: biological adults (people who have attained reproductive ability, are fertile, or who evidence secondary sex characteristics) and social adults (people who are recognized by their culture or law as being adults). Depending on the context, <i>adult</i> can indicate either definition. Another aspect of adulthood that is relatively new is psychological functioning. According to Jeffrey Arnett, adults can make financial decisions on their own, are financially independent, and are able to make difficult life decisions on their own.
</p><p>Although few or no established dictionaries provide a definition for the two word term biological adult, the first definition of adult in multiple dictionaries includes "the stage of the life cycle of an animal after reproductive capacity has been attained".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Thus, the base definition of the word adult is the period beginning at physical sexual maturity, which occurs sometime after the onset of puberty. Although this is the primary definition of the base word "adult", the term is also frequently used to refer to social adults. The two-word term <i>biological adult</i> stresses or clarifies that the original definition, based on physical maturity (i.e. having reached reproductive competency), is being used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The time of <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a> varies from child to child, but usually begins around 10 or 11 years old. Girls typically begin the process of puberty at age 10 or 11, and boys at age 11 or 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kail_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kail-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuiling_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuiling-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-9">[9]</a></sup> Girls generally complete puberty by 15–17, and boys by age 16 or 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solomon_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solomon-10">[10]</a></sup> Nutrition, genetics and environment also usually play a part in the onset of puberty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xia_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-xia-11">[11]</a></sup> Girls will go through a growth spurt and gain weight in several areas of their body. Boys will go through similar spurts in growth though it is usually not in a similar style or time frame. This is due to the natural processes of puberty, but genetics also plays a part in how much weight they gain or how much taller they get.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>One recent area of debate within the science of brain development is the most likely chronological age for full <a href="/wiki/Maturity_(psychological)" title="Maturity (psychological)">mental maturity</a>, or indeed, if such an age even exists. Common claims repeated in the media (based upon interpretations of imaging data), suggest an "end-point" of 25 or 30 years, referring to the prefrontal cortex as one area that may not yet be fully mature at the age of 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> These interpretations, and their implications for impulse control in teenagers have been disputed by many authors working within the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_adulthood">Legal adulthood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal adulthood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">Age of majority</a></div>
<p>Legally, <i>adulthood</i> typically means that one has reached the age of majority – when parents lose parental rights and responsibilities regarding the person concerned. Depending on one's jurisdiction, the age of majority may or may not be set independently of and should not be confused with the minimum ages applicable to other activities, such as engaging in a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">voting</a>, having a <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">job</a>, serving in the <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a>, buying/possessing <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Driving" title="Driving">driving</a>, traveling abroad, involvement with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverages" title="Alcoholic beverages">alcoholic beverages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">smoking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_activity" title="Human sexual activity">sexual activity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, being a model or actor in <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a>, running for <a href="/wiki/President_(government_title)" title="President (government title)">President</a>, etc. Admission of a young person to a place may be restricted because of danger for that person, concern that the place may lead the person to immoral behavior, or because of the risk that the young person causes damage (for example, at an exhibition of fragile items).
</p><p>One can distinguish the legality of acts of a young person, or of enabling a young person to carry out that act, by selling, renting out, showing, permitting entrance, allowing participation, etc. There may be distinction between commercially and socially enabling. Sometimes there is the requirement of supervision by a <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardian" title="Legal guardian">legal guardian</a>, or just by an adult. Sometimes there is no requirement, but rather a recommendation.
</p><p>Using the example of pornography, one can distinguish between:
</p>
<ul><li>being allowed inside an adult establishment</li>
<li>being allowed to purchase pornography</li>
<li>being allowed to possess pornography</li>
<li>another person being allowed to sell, rent out, or show the young person pornography, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disseminating_pornography_to_a_minor" title="Disseminating pornography to a minor">disseminating pornography to a minor</a></li>
<li>being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pornographic_actor" title="Pornographic actor">pornographic actor</a>: rules for the young person, and for other people, regarding production, possession, etc. (see <a href="/wiki/Child_pornography" title="Child pornography">child pornography</a>)</li></ul>
<p>With regard to films with violence, etc.:
</p>
<ul><li>another person being allowed to sell, rent out, or show the young person a film; a cinema being allowed to let a young person enter</li></ul>
<p>The age of majority ranges internationally from ages 15 to 21, with 18 being the most common age. <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of Congo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> define adulthood at age 15, but marriage of girls at an earlier age is common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In most of the world, including most of the United States, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal" title="Legal">legal</a> adult age is 18 (historically 21) for most purposes, with some notable exceptions:
</p>
<ol><li>The legal age of adulthood in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> is 19 (though there are some exceptions in which Canadians may be considered legal adults in certain situations like sexual consent, which is age 16, and criminal law, federal elections and the military, which is at 18);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>The legal age of adulthood in <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_United_States" title="The United States">The United States</a>, is 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>The legal age of adulthood in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> is 19.</li>
<li>The legal age of adulthood in <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> in the U.S. is 21.</li></ol>
<p>Prior to the 1970s, young people were not classed as adults until 21 in most western nations. For example, in the United States, young citizens could not vote in any election until 21 until July 1971 when the 26th Amendment passed mandating that the right to vote cannot be abridged for anyone 18 or older. The voting age was lowered in response to the fact that young men between the ages of 18 and 21 were drafted into the army to fight in the Vietnam War, hence the popular slogan "old enough to fight, old enough to vote" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Young people under 21 in the US could also not purchase alcohol, purchase handguns, sign a binding contract, or marry without permission from parents. After the voting age was lowered, many states also moved to lower the drinking age (with most states having a minimum age of 18 or 19) and also to lower the age of legal majority (adulthood) to 18. However, there are legal activities where 18 is not the default age of adulthood. There are still some exceptions where 21 (or even higher) is still the benchmark for certain rights or responsibilities. For example, in the US The Gun Control Act of 1968 prohibits those under 21 from purchasing a handgun from a federally licensed dealer (although federal law makes an exception for individuals between the ages of 18 and 21 to obtain one from a private dealer if state law permits.) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of July 1984, The National Minimum Drinking Age Act mandated that all states raise their respective drinking ages to 21 to create a uniform standard for legally purchasing, drinking, or publicly possessing alcohol with exceptions made for consumption only in private residences under parental supervision and permission. This was done in response to reduce the number of drunk driving fatalities prevalent among young drivers. States that choose not to comply can lose up to 10% of highway funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Credit Card Act of 2009 imposed tougher safeguards for young adults between the ages of 18 and 21 obtaining a credit card. Young adults under the age of 21 must either have a co-signer over 21 or show proof (usually a source of income) that they can repay their credit card balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Unless that requirement is met, one must wait until 21 to be approved for a credit card on their own.
</p><p>The Affordable Care Act of 2010 expands the age that young adults can remain on their parent's health insurance plan up to age 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of December 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Adult&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the federal government raised the legal age to purchase tobacco and vaping products from 18 to 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In States where recreational marijuana is legalized, the default age is also 21 though those younger may be able to obtain medical marijuana prescriptions or cards upon seeing a physician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gambling also varies from 18 to 21 depending on the state and many rental car companies do not rent cars to those under 21 and have surcharges for drivers under 25 (although this is not codified, and is company policy).
</p><p>In Quebec, Canada the Quebec legislature in 2020 raised the age one could purchase recreational marijuana from 18 to 21 stepping out of line with most of the country that set a minimum age of 19 (except Alberta, which is 18.) The Quebec government cited the risk that marijuana poses to the brain development of people under 21 as justification for the age raise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2021, the state of Washington in a 5–4 decision, justices in the Supreme Court of the State of Washington tossed the life without parole sentences of a 19-year-old and a 20-year-old convicted in separate cases of first degree aggravated murder decades ago, saying, as with juveniles, the court must first consider the age of those under 21 before sentencing them to die behind bars. This comes at a time where there are ongoing debates about whether those between 18 and 21 should be exempted from the death penalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Germany, courts largely sentence defendants under the age of 21 according to juvenile law in a bid to help them reintegrate into society and mete out punishments that fit the crime as well as the offender.
</p><p>In May 2021, the state of Texas raised the age that one can be an exotic dancer and work and patronize <a href="/wiki/Sexually_oriented_business" title="Sexually oriented business">sexually oriented businesses</a> from 18 to 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the UK, there have been many proposals to raise the age that one can buy tobacco from 18 to 21 in an attempt to curb young adult and teen use to get to a "smoke-free" UK by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
All of these laws made over the years reflect the growing awareness that young adults, while not children, are still in a transitional stage between adolescence and full adulthood and that there should be policy adjustments or restrictions where necessary, especially where it pertains to activities that carry certain degrees of risk or harm to themselves or others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the same time, however, even though the generally accepted age of majority is 18 in most nations, there are rights or privileges afforded to adolescents who have not yet reached legal adulthood. In the United States, youth are able to get a part-time job at 14 provided they have a work permit. At 16, one is able to obtain a driver's permit or license depending on state laws and is able to work most jobs (except ones requiring heavy machinery) and consent to sexual activity (depending on the state). At 17, one is able to enlist in the armed forces with parental consent although they cannot be deployed to be in combat roles until age 18.
</p><p>The voting age for local elections in most American cities is 18. But in five localities nationwide — four of which are in Maryland — 16 and 17-year-olds are eligible to vote. The cities are Takoma Park, Riverdale, Greenbelt, and Hyattsville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
In 2020, students 16 or older in Oakland, California gained the right to vote in school board elections. There is a growing movement to lower the voting in the US and many other countries from 18 to 16 in hopes of engaging the youth vote and encouraging greater electoral participation. Some countries already have a voting age of 16 which includes Austria, Scotland, Argentina, Brazil, Wales, Cuba, and Ecuador.
</p><p>In Germany, one can purchase beer and wine at the age of 16 although they cannot purchase spirits or hard liquor until 18. The age of consent in Germany is 14 if both partners are under 18. Sexual activity with a person under 18 is punishable if the adult is a person of authority over the minor in upbringing, education, care, or employment.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_construction_of_adulthood">Social construction of adulthood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social construction of adulthood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In contrast to biological perspectives of aging and adulthood, social scientists conceptualize adulthood as socially constructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> While aging is an established biological process, the attainment of adulthood is social in its criteria. In contrast to other perspectives that conceptualize aging and the attainment of adulthood as a largely universal development, regardless of context, nation, generation, gender, race, or social class. Social scientists regard these aspects as paramount in cultural definitions of adulthood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Further evidence of adulthood as a social construction is illustrated by the changing criteria of adulthood over time. Historically, adulthood in the U.S. has rested on completing one's education, moving away from the family of origin, and beginning one's career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Other key historical criteria include entering a marriage and becoming a parent. These criteria are social and subjective; they are organized by gender, race, ethnicity, social class, among other key identity markers. As a result, particular populations feel adult earlier in the life course than do others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aronson,_Pamela_2008_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aronson,_Pamela_2008-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Contemporary experiences of and research on young adults today substitute more seemingly subjective criteria for adulthood which resonate more soundly with young adults' experiences of aging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aronson,_Pamela_2008_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aronson,_Pamela_2008-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The criteria are marked by a growing "importance of individualistic criteria and the irrelevance of the demographic markers of normative conceptions of adulthood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup>:230</sup> In particular, younger cohorts' attainment of adulthood centers on three criteria: gaining a sense of responsibility, independent decision-making, and financial independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jeffrey Arnett, a psychologist and professor at Clark University in Massachusetts, studied the development of adults and argues that there is a new and distinct period of development in between adolescence and adulthood. This stage, which he calls "emerging adulthood," occurs between the ages of 18 and 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Arnett describes these individuals as able to take some responsibility for their lives, but still not completely feeling like an adult. Arnett articulates five distinct features that are unique to this period of development: identity exploration, feeling in between, instability, self-focus, and having possibilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first feature, identity exploration, describes emerging adults making decisions for themselves about their career, education, and love life. This is a time of life when a young person has yet to finalize these decisions but are pondering these decisions, making them feel somewhere in between adolescent and adult. This leads into a second feature of this phase of life—feeling in between. Emerging adults feel that they are taking on responsibilities but do not feel like a 'full' adult quite yet. Next, the instability feature notes that emerging adults often move around after their high school years whether that is to college, friends' houses, or living with a romantic partner. This moving around often ends once the individual's family and career have been set. Tagging along with the instability feature is having self-focus. Emerging adults, being away from their parental and societal routines, are now able to do what they want when they want and where they want before they are put back into a routine when they start a marriage, family, and career. Arnett's last feature of emerging adulthood, an age of possibilities, characterizes this stage as one where "optimism reigns".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-54">[54]</a></sup> These individuals believe they have a good chance of turning out better than their parents did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> tradition, adulthood is reached at age 13 (the minimum age of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_and_Bat_Mitzvah" title="Bar and Bat Mitzvah">Bar Mitzvah or Bat Mitzvah</a>) for Jewish boys and girls; they are expected to demonstrate preparation for adulthood by learning the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> and other Jewish practices. The <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_scripture" title="Jewish scripture">Jewish scripture</a> contain no age requirement for adulthood or <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marrying</a>, which includes engaging in sexual activity.
</p><p>The 1983 Code of Canon Law states, "A man before he has completed his sixteenth year of age, and likewise a woman before she has completed her fourteenth year of age, cannot enter a valid marriage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> According to <i>The Disappearance of Childhood</i> by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Postman" title="Neil Postman">Neil Postman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Christian Church</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> considered the age of accountability, when a person could be tried and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution" title="Execution">executed</a> as an adult, to be age 7. While certain religions have their guidelines on what it means to be an adult, generally speaking, there are trends that occur regarding religiosity as individuals transition from adolescence to adulthood. The role of religion in one's life can impact development during adolescence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The National Library of Medicine (NCBI) highlights some studies that show rates of religiosity declining as people move out of the house and live on their own. Oftentimes when people live on their own, they change their life goals and religion tends to be less important as they discover who they are. Other studies from the NCBI show that as adults get married and have children they settle down, and as they do, there tends to be an increase in religiosity. Everyone's level of religiosity builds at a different pace, meaning that religion relative to adult development varies across cultures and time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
