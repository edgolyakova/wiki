<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Representation of language in a textual medium</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Write" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Write_(disambiguation)" title="Write (disambiguation)">Write (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rosetta_Stone.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4288" data-file-width="3665" decoding="async" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Rosetta_Stone.JPG/220px-Rosetta_Stone.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Rosetta_Stone.JPG/330px-Rosetta_Stone.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Rosetta_Stone.JPG/440px-Rosetta_Stone.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rosetta_Stone.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_Stone" title="Rosetta Stone">Rosetta Stone</a>, with writing in three different scripts, was instrumental in deciphering Ancient Egyptian.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Writing</b> is a medium of human <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a> that involves the representation of a <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> through a system of physically <a href="/wiki/Epigraphy" title="Epigraphy">inscribed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">mechanically transferred</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Word_processor" title="Word processor">digitally represented</a> symbols.
</p><p>Writing systems are not themselves human languages (with the debatable exception of <a href="/wiki/Computer_language" title="Computer language">computer languages</a>); they are means of rendering a language into a form that can be reconstructed by other humans separated by time and/or space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While not all languages use a writing system, those with systems of inscriptions can complement and extend capacities of <a href="/wiki/Spoken_language" title="Spoken language">spoken language</a> by enabling the creation of durable forms of speech that can be transmitted across space (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Letter_(message)" title="Letter (message)">correspondence</a>) and stored over time (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libraries" title="Libraries">libraries</a> or other public records).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It has also been observed that the activity of writing itself can have knowledge-transforming effects, since it allows humans to externalize their thinking in forms that are easier to reflect on, elaborate, reconsider, and revise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Writing relies on many of the same <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> structures as the speech it represents, such as <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, with the added dependency of a system of <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a> to represent that language's <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>. The result of the activity of writing is called a <i>text</i>, and the interpreter or activator of this text is called a <i>reader</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As human societies emerged, collective motivations for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Development_of_writing" title="Development of writing">development of writing</a> were driven by pragmatic exigencies like keeping <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, maintaining <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, codifying knowledge through <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">curricula</a> and lists of texts deemed to contain foundational knowledge (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i>) or to be artistically exceptional (e.g., a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_canon" title="Literary canon">literary canon</a>), organizing and governing societies through the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_systems" title="Legal systems">legal systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> records, <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contracts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deed" title="Deed">deeds</a> of ownership, <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade_agreement" title="Trade agreement">trade agreements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a>, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Amateur historians, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.G._Wells" title="H.G. Wells">H.G. Wells</a>, had speculated since the early 20th century on the likely correspondence between the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">systems of writing</a> and the development of <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a> into <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empires</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bazerman" title="Charles Bazerman">Charles Bazerman</a> explains, the "marking of signs on stones, clay, paper, and now digital memories—each more portable and rapidly traveling than the previous—provided means for increasingly coordinated and extended action as well as memory across larger groups of people over time and space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> For example, around the 4th millennium BC, the complexity of trade and administration in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> outgrew human memory, and writing became a more dependable method of recording and presenting transactions in a permanent form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In both <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, on the other hand, writing may have evolved through calendric and political necessities for recording historical and environmental events. Further innovations included more uniform, predictable, and widely dispersed <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_legal_systems" title="List of national legal systems">legal systems</a>, distribution and discussion of accessible versions of <a href="/wiki/Religious_text" title="Religious text">sacred texts</a>, and the origins of modern practices of <a href="/wiki/Models_of_scientific_inquiry" title="Models of scientific inquiry">scientific inquiry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge-consolidation</a>, all largely reliant on portable and easily reproducible forms of inscribed language.
</p><p>Individual, as opposed to collective, motivations for writing include improvised additional capacity for the limitations of human <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Time_management" title="Time management">to-do lists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recipe" title="Recipe">recipes</a>, reminders, <a href="/wiki/Logbook" title="Logbook">logbooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a>, the proper sequence for a complicated task or important <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a>), dissemination of ideas (as in an <a href="/wiki/Essay" title="Essay">essay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monograph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broadside_(printing)" title="Broadside (printing)">broadside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petition" title="Petition">petition</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a>), imaginative narratives and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">storytelling</a>, maintaining <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> and other social networks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> negotiating household matters with <a href="/wiki/Business_correspondence" title="Business correspondence">providers</a> of goods and services and with local and regional governing <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">bodies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Life_writing" title="Life writing">lifewriting</a> (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Diary" title="Diary">diary</a> or journal).
</p><p>The nearly global spread of digital communication systems such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail" title="E-mail">e-mail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> has made writing an increasingly important feature of daily life, where these systems mix with older technologies like paper, pencils, whiteboards, printers, and copiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Substantial amounts of everyday writing characterize most workplaces in <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In many occupations (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_design" title="Software design">software-design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_resource_management" title="Human resource management">human-resources</a>, etc.) written documentation is not only the main deliverable but also the mode of work itself. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Even in occupations not typically associated with writing, routine <a href="/wiki/Workflow" title="Workflow">workflows</a> (maintaining <a href="/wiki/Records_management" title="Records management">records</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incident_report" title="Incident report">reporting incidents</a>, record-keeping, <a href="/wiki/Inventory" title="Inventory">inventory</a>-tracking, documenting <a href="/wiki/Sales" title="Sales">sales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timesheet" title="Timesheet">accounting for time</a>, fielding inquiries from clients, etc.) have most employees writing at least some of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_systems">Writing systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Writing systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">Writing system</a></div>
<p>The major writing systems broadly fall into four categories: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logographic" title="Logographic">logographic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllabic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphabetic" title="Alphabetic">alphabetic</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Featural" title="Featural">featural</a>. As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictographic" title="Pictographic">pictography</a> does not represent a language's sounds, it has been argued to not be a writing system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logographies">Logographies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Logographies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform,_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="809" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform%2C_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg/220px-Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform%2C_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform%2C_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg/330px-Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform%2C_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform%2C_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg/440px-Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform%2C_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comparative_evolution_of_Cuneiform,_Egyptian_and_Chinese_characters.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparative evolution from pictograms to abstract shapes, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian" title="Mesopotamian">Mesopotamian</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiforms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyph" title="Hieroglyph">hieroglyphs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>A logography (also called a logosyllabary) is written using <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logograms</a> — written characters which represent individual <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">words</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a> or certain <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllables</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> For example, in Mayan, the glyph for "fin", pronounced <i>ka</i>, was also used to represent the syllable <i>ka</i> whenever the pronunciation of a logogram needed to be indicated. Many logograms have an <a href="/wiki/Ideogram" title="Ideogram">ideographic</a> component (Chinese "radicals", hieroglyphic "determiners"). In Chinese, about 90% of characters are compounds of a semantic (meaning) element called a <i>radical</i> with an existing character to indicate the pronunciation, called a <i>phonetic</i>. However, such phonetic elements complement the logographic elements, rather than vice versa.
</p><p>The main logographic system in use today is <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>, used with some modification for the various languages or dialects of <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_China" title="Languages of China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Japan" title="Languages of Japan">Japan</a>, and sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a> despite the fact that in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, the phonetic <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a> system is mainly used. Older logographic systems include <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayan_script" title="Mayan script">Mayan</a>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllabaries">Syllabaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Syllabaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Syllabary" title="Syllabary">syllabary</a> is a set of written symbols that represent <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllables</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> typically a consonant followed by a vowel, or just a vowel alone. In some scripts more complex syllables (such as consonant-vowel-consonant, or consonant-consonant-vowel) may have dedicated glyphs. Phonetically similar syllables are not written similarly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> For instance, the syllable "ka" may look nothing like the syllable "ki", nor will syllables with the same vowels be similar.
</p><p>Syllabaries are best suited to languages with a relatively simple syllable structure, such as Japanese. Other languages that use syllabic writing include the <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> script for <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">Mycenaean Greek</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_syllabary" title="Cherokee syllabary">Cherokee</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_language" title="Ndyuka language">Ndjuka</a>, an English-based <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surinam" title="Surinam">Surinam</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Vai_language" title="Vai language">Vai</a> script of <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabets">Alphabets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alphabets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_alphabet" title="History of the alphabet">History of the alphabet</a></div><p>An <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a> is a set of written symbols that represent <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> In a perfectly <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a> alphabet, the letters would correspond perfectly to the language's <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a>. Thus, a writer could predict the spelling of a word given its pronunciation, and a speaker could predict the pronunciation of a word given its spelling. However, as languages often evolve independently of their writing systems, and writing systems have been borrowed for languages they were not designed for, the degree to which letters of an alphabet correspond to phonemes of a language varies greatly from one language to another and even within a single language.
</p><p>Sometimes the term "alphabet" is restricted to systems with separate letters for consonants and vowels, such as the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, although abugidas and abjads may also be accepted as alphabets. Because of this use, <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek</a> is often considered to be the first alphabet.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abjads">Abjads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Abjads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In most of the alphabets of the Middle East, it is usually only the consonants of a word that are written, although vowels may be indicated by the addition of various diacritical marks. Writing systems based primarily on writing just consonants phonemes date back to the hieroglyphs of ancient Egypt. Such systems are called <i><a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">abjads</a></i>, derived from the Arabic word for "alphabet", or <i>consonantaries</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abugidas">Abugidas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Abugidas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In most of the alphabets of India and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, vowels are indicated through diacritics or modification of the shape of the consonant. These are called <i><a href="/wiki/Abugida" title="Abugida">abugidas</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> Some abugidas, such as <a href="/wiki/Ge%CA%BDez_script" title="Geʽez script">Ethiopic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Aboriginal_syllabics" title="Canadian Aboriginal syllabics">Cree</a>, are learned by children as syllabaries, and so are often called "syllabics". However, unlike true syllabaries, there is not an independent glyph for each syllable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Featural_scripts">Featural scripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Featural scripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Featural_writing_system" title="Featural writing system">featural script</a> represents the features of the phonemes of the language in consistent ways. An example of such a system is Korean <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> For instance, all <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">labial</a> sounds (pronounced with the lips) may have some element in common. In the Latin alphabet, this is accidentally the case with the letters "b" and "p"; however, labial "m" is completely dissimilar, and the similar-looking "q" and "d" are not labial. In Korean <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a>, however, all four labial consonants are based on the same basic element, but in practice, Korean is learned by children as an ordinary alphabet, and the featural elements tend to pass unnoticed.
</p><p>Another featural script is <a href="/wiki/SignWriting" title="SignWriting">SignWriting</a>, the most popular writing system for many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sign_languages" title="Sign languages">sign languages</a>, where the shapes and movements of the hands and face are represented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secular_icon" title="Secular icon">iconically</a>. Featural scripts are also common in fictional or invented systems, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tengwar" title="Tengwar">Tengwar</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_and_materials">Tools and materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tools and materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Writing_implement" title="Writing implement">Writing implement</a></div>
<p>The many tools and writing materials used throughout history include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_tablet" title="Stone tablet">stone tablets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay_tablet" title="Clay tablet">clay tablets</a>, bamboo slats, <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wax_tablet" title="Wax tablet">wax tablets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intaglio_printing" title="Intaglio printing">copperplate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stylus" title="Stylus">styluses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quill" title="Quill">quills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ink_brush" title="Ink brush">ink brushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">pencils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pen" title="Pen">pens</a>, and many styles of <a href="/wiki/Lithography" title="Lithography">lithography</a>. The Incas used knotted cords known as <a href="/wiki/Quipu" title="Quipu">quipu</a> (or khipu) for keeping records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">typewriter</a> and various forms of <a href="/wiki/Word_processor" title="Word processor">word processors</a> have subsequently become widespread writing tools, and various studies have compared the ways in which writers have framed the experience of writing with such tools as compared with the pen or pencil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Advancements in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_generation" title="Natural language generation">natural language generation</a> allow certain tools (in the form of <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>) to produce certain kinds of highly formulaic writing (e.g., weather forecasts and brief sports reporting) without the direct involvement of humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Writings" redirects here. Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Ketuvim" title="Ketuvim">Ketuvim</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mesoamerica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A stone slab with 3,000-year-old writing, known as the <a href="/wiki/Cascajal_Block" title="Cascajal Block">Cascajal Block</a>, was discovered in the Mexican state of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> and is an example of the oldest script in the Western Hemisphere, preceding the oldest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zapotec_writing" title="Zapotec writing">Zapotec writing</a> by approximately 500 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It is thought to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olmec" title="Olmec">Olmec</a>.
</p><p>Of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> scripts in <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, the one that appears to have been best developed, and the only one to be deciphered, is the <a href="/wiki/Maya_script" title="Maya script">Maya script</a>. The earliest inscription identified as Maya dates to the 3rd century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saturno2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saturno2006-29">[29]</a></sup> Maya writing used logograms complemented by a set of syllabic glyphs, somewhat similar in function to modern Japanese writing.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Asia">Central Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Central Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, archaeologists discovered that there was a civilization in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> that used writing c. 2000 BC. An excavation near <a href="/wiki/Ashgabat" title="Ashgabat">Ashgabat</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>, revealed an inscription on a piece of stone that was used as a stamp seal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">Oracle bone script</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronzeware_script" title="Bronzeware script">Bronzeware script</a></div>
<p>The earliest surviving examples of writing in China—inscriptions on so-called "<a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone" title="Oracle bone">oracle bones</a>", tortoise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastron" title="Plastron">plastrons</a> and ox <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapulae</a> used for divination—date from around 1200 BC in the late <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>. A small number of bronze inscriptions from the same period have also survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, archaeologists reported discoveries of <a href="/wiki/Jiahu_symbols" title="Jiahu symbols">isolated tortoise-shell carvings</a> dating back to the 7th millennium BC, but whether or not these symbols are related to the characters of the later oracle-bone script is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:198px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1705" data-file-width="1155" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg/196px-Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg/294px-Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg/392px-Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg 2x" width="196"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Narmer_Palette_serpopard_side.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Narmer_Palette" title="Narmer Palette">Narmer Palette</a>, with the two <a href="/wiki/Serpopard" title="Serpopard">Serpopards</a> representing unification of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>, circa <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=3100_B.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="3100 B.C. (page does not exist)">3100 B.C.</a></div></div></div>
<p>The earliest known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyph" title="Egyptian hieroglyph">hieroglyphs</a> date back to the second half of the 4th millennium BC, such as the clay labels of a Predynastic ruler called "Scorpion I" (Naqada IIIA period, c. 32nd century BC) recovered at Abydos (modern Umm el-Qa'ab) in 1998 or the <a href="/wiki/Narmer_Palette" title="Narmer Palette">Narmer Palette</a>, dating to c. 3100 BC, and several recent discoveries that may be slightly older, though these glyphs were based on a much older artistic rather than written tradition. The hieroglyphic script was <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logographic</a> with phonetic adjuncts that included an effective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyph#Script" title="Egyptian hieroglyph">alphabet</a>. The world's oldest deciphered sentence was found on a seal impression found in the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Seth-Peribsen" title="Seth-Peribsen">Seth-Peribsen</a> at Umm el-Qa'ab, which dates from the Second Dynasty (28th or 27th century BC). There are around 800 hieroglyphs dating back to the Old Kingdom, Middle Kingdom and New Kingdom Eras. By the Greco-Roman period, there are more than 5,000.
</p><p>Writing was very important in maintaining the Egyptian empire, and literacy was concentrated among an educated elite of <a href="/wiki/Scribe" title="Scribe">scribes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Only people from certain backgrounds were allowed to train to become scribes, in the service of temple, pharaonic, and military authorities. The hieroglyph system was always difficult to learn, but in later centuries was purposely made even more so, as this preserved the scribes' status.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age_alphabets" title="Middle Bronze Age alphabets">oldest known alphabet</a> appears to have been developed by Canaanite turquoise miners in the Sinai desert around the mid-19th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Around 30 crude inscriptions have been found at a mountainous Egyptian mining site known as Serabit el-Khadem. This site was also home to a temple of Hathor, the "Mistress of turquoise". A later, two line inscription has also been found at <a href="/wiki/Wadi_el-Hol" title="Wadi el-Hol">Wadi el-Hol</a> in Central Egypt. Based on <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyph" title="Hieroglyph">hieroglyphic</a> prototypes, but also including entirely new symbols, each sign apparently stood for a consonant rather than a word: the basis of an alphabetic system. It was not until the 12th to 9th centuries, however, that the alphabet took hold and became widely used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elamite_scripts">Elamite scripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Elamite scripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over the centuries, three distinct Elamite scripts developed.
<a href="/wiki/Proto-Elamite" title="Proto-Elamite">Proto-Elamite</a> is the oldest known writing system from Iran. In use only for a brief time (c. 3200–2900 BC), clay tablets with Proto-Elamite writing have been found at different sites across Iran. The Proto-Elamite script is thought to have developed from early <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a> (proto-cuneiform). The Proto-Elamite script consists of more than 1,000 signs and is thought to be partly <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logographic</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Linear_Elamite" title="Linear Elamite">Linear Elamite</a> is a writing system attested in a few monumental inscriptions in Iran. It was used for a very brief period during the last quarter of the 3rd millennium BC. It is often claimed that Linear Elamite is a syllabic writing system derived from Proto-Elamite, although this cannot be proven since Linear-Elamite has not been deciphered. Several scholars have attempted to decipher the script, most notably <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_Hinz" title="de:Walther Hinz">Walther Hinz</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piero_Meriggi" title="it:Piero Meriggi">Piero Meriggi</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Elamite_cuneiform" title="Elamite cuneiform">Elamite cuneiform</a> script was used from about 2500 to 331 BC, and was adapted from the <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform_(script)" title="Cuneiform (script)">cuneiform</a>. The Elamite cuneiform script consisted of about 130 symbols, far fewer than most other cuneiform scripts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretan_and_Greek_scripts">Cretan and Greek scripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cretan and Greek scripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cretan_hieroglyphs" title="Cretan hieroglyphs">Cretan hieroglyphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linear_A" title="Linear A">Linear A</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cretan_hieroglyphs" title="Cretan hieroglyphs">Cretan hieroglyphs</a> are found on artifacts of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> (early-to-mid-2nd millennium BC, MM I to MM III, overlapping with Linear A from MM IIA at the earliest). <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a>, the writing system of the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean Greeks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olivier_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olivier-35">[35]</a></sup> has been deciphered while <a href="/wiki/Linear_A" title="Linear A">Linear A</a> has yet to be deciphered. The sequence and the geographical spread of the three overlapping, but distinct writing systems can be summarized as follows (beginning date refers to first attestations, the assumed origins of all scripts lie further back in the past): Cretan hieroglyphs were used in Crete from c. 1625 to 1500 BC; Linear A was used in the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Islands" title="Aegean Islands">Aegean Islands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kea_(island)" title="Kea (island)">Kea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cythera_(island)" title="Cythera (island)">Kythera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milos" title="Milos">Melos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santorini" title="Santorini">Thera</a>), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_mainland" title="Greek mainland">Greek mainland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Laconia" title="Laconia">Laconia</a>) from c. 18th century to 1450 BC; and Linear B was used in Crete (<a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>), and mainland (<a href="/wiki/Pylos" title="Pylos">Pylos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">Thebes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiryns" title="Tiryns">Tiryns</a>) from c. 1375 to 1200 BC.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indus_Valley">Indus Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Indus Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indus_script" title="Indus script">Indus script</a></div>
<p>Indus script refers to short strings of symbols associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" title="Indus Valley civilization">Indus Valley civilization</a> (which spanned modern-day <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North India</a>) used between 2600 and 1900 BC. In spite of many attempts at decipherments and claims, it is as yet undeciphered. The term 'Indus script' is mainly applied to that used in the mature Harappan phase, which perhaps evolved from a few signs found in early <a href="/wiki/Harappa" title="Harappa">Harappa</a> after 3500 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and was followed by the mature Harappan script. The script is written from right to left,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and sometimes follows a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boustrophedonic" title="Boustrophedonic">boustrophedonic</a> style. Since the number of principal signs is about 400–600,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> midway between typical logographic and syllabic scripts, many scholars accept the script to be logo-syllabic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> (typically syllabic scripts have about 50–100 signs whereas logographic scripts have a very large number of principal signs). Several scholars maintain that structural analysis indicates that an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agglutinative" title="Agglutinative">agglutinative</a> language underlies the script.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mesopotamia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While research into the development of writing during the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">late Stone Age</a> is ongoing, the current consensus is that it first evolved from economic necessity in the ancient Near East. Writing most likely began as a consequence of political expansion in ancient cultures, which needed reliable means for transmitting information, maintaining financial accounts, keeping historical records, and similar activities. Around the 4th millennium BC, the complexity of trade and administration outgrew the power of memory, and writing became a more dependable method of recording and presenting transactions in a permanent form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobinson200336_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobinson200336-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>The invention of the first writing systems is roughly contemporary with the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> of the late <a href="/wiki/4th_millennium_BC" title="4th millennium BC">4th millennium BC</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> archaic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform_script" title="Cuneiform script">cuneiform script</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a> are generally considered the earliest writing systems, both emerging out of their ancestral proto-literate symbol systems from 3400 to 3300 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> with earliest coherent texts from about <a href="/wiki/26th_century_BC" title="26th century BC">2600 BC</a>. It is generally agreed that Sumerian writing was an independent invention; however, it is debated whether Egyptian writing was developed completely independently of Sumerian, or was a case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_diffusion" title="Cultural diffusion">cultural diffusion</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="309" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg/170px-Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg/255px-Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg/340px-Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Accountancy_clay_envelope_Louvre_Sb1932.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Globular envelope with a cluster of accountancy tokens, Uruk period, from <a href="/wiki/Susa" title="Susa">Susa</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louvre_Museum" title="Louvre Museum">Louvre Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>Archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Denise_Schmandt-Besserat" title="Denise Schmandt-Besserat">Denise Schmandt-Besserat</a> determined the link between previously uncategorized clay "tokens", the oldest of which have been found in the Zagros region of Iran, and the first known writing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian" title="Mesopotamian">Mesopotamian</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rudgley_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rudgley-42">[42]</a></sup> In approximately 8000 BC, the Mesopotamians began using clay tokens to count their agricultural and manufactured goods. Later they began placing these tokens inside large, hollow clay containers (bulla, or globular envelopes) which were then sealed. The quantity of tokens in each container came to be expressed by impressing, on the container's surface, one picture for each instance of the token inside. They next dispensed with the tokens, relying solely on symbols for the tokens, drawn on clay surfaces. To avoid making a picture for each instance of the same object (for example: 100 pictures of a hat to represent 100 hats), they 'counted' the objects by using various small marks. In this way the Sumerians added "a system for enumerating objects to their incipient system of symbols".
</p><p>The original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian" title="Mesopotamian">Mesopotamian</a> writing system was derived around 3200 BC from this method of keeping accounts. By the end of the 4th millennium BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the Mesopotamians were using a triangular-shaped stylus pressed into soft clay to record numbers. This system was gradually augmented with using a sharp stylus to indicate what was being counted by means of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictographs" title="Pictographs">pictographs</a>. Round-stylus and sharp-stylus writing was gradually replaced by writing using a wedge-shaped stylus (hence the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform_script" title="Cuneiform script">cuneiform</a>), at first only for <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logograms</a>, but by the 29th century BC also for phonetic elements. Around 2700 BC, cuneiform began to represent syllables of spoken <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a>. About that time, Mesopotamian cuneiform became a general purpose writing system for logograms, syllables, and numbers. This script was adapted to another Mesopotamian language, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Semitic" title="East Semitic">East Semitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Assyrian_period" title="Old Assyrian period">Assyrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonian</a>) around 2600 BC, and then to others such as <a href="/wiki/Elamite_language" title="Elamite language">Elamite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hattian_language" title="Hattian language">Hattian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurrian_language" title="Hurrian language">Hurrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hittite_language" title="Hittite language">Hittite</a>. Scripts similar in appearance to this writing system include those for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ugaritic_language" title="Ugaritic language">Ugaritic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Persian_language" title="Old Persian language">Old Persian</a>. With the adoption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a> as the 'lingua franca' of the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Neo-Assyrian Empire</a> (911–609 BC), Old Aramaic was also adapted to Mesopotamian cuneiform. The last cuneiform scripts in Akkadian discovered thus far date from the 1st century AD.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phoenician_writing_system_and_descendants">Phoenician writing system and descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Phoenician writing system and descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Sinaitic" title="Proto-Sinaitic">Proto-Sinaitic</a> script, in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Canaanite" title="Proto-Canaanite">Proto-Canaanite</a> is believed to have been first written, is attested as far back as the 19th century BC. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenician_script" title="Phoenician script">Phoenician writing system</a> was adapted from the Proto-Canaanite script sometime before the 14th century BC, which in turn borrowed principles of representing phonetic information from <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a>. This writing system was an odd sort of syllabary in which only consonants are represented. This script was adapted by the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greeks</a>, who adapted certain consonantal signs to represent their vowels. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumae_alphabet" title="Cumae alphabet">Cumae alphabet</a>, a variant of the early Greek alphabet, gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_alphabet" title="Etruscan alphabet">Etruscan alphabet</a> and its own descendants, such as the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rune" title="Rune">Runes</a>. Other descendants from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> include <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a>, used to write <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, among others. The Phoenician system was also adapted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic_script" title="Aramaic script">Aramaic script</a>, from which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_script" title="Hebrew script">Hebrew</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">Arabic scripts</a> are descended.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tifinagh" title="Tifinagh">Tifinagh</a> script (Berber languages) is descended from the Libyco-Berber script, which is assumed to be of Phoenician origin.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_efforts_to_foster_writing_acquisition">Contemporary efforts to foster writing acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Contemporary efforts to foster writing acquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Multiple programs are in place to aid both children and adults in improving their <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">literacy</a> skills. For example, the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Writing_center" title="Writing center">writing center</a> and community-wide literacy councils aim to help students and community members sharpen their writing skills. These resources, and many more, span across different age groups in order to offer each individual a better understanding of their language and how to express themselves via writing in order to perhaps improve their <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">socioeconomic status</a>. As William J. Farrell puts it: · “Did you ever notice that, when people become serious about communication, they want it in writing?”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farrell1992_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farrell1992-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other parts of the world have seen an increase in writing abilities as a result of programs such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Literacy_Foundation" title="World Literacy Foundation">World Literacy Foundation</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Literacy_Foundation" title="International Literacy Foundation">International Literacy Foundation</a>, as well as a general push for increased global communication.
</p>
