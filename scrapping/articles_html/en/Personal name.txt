<p>A <b>personal name</b>, or <b>full name</b>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onomastic" title="Onomastic">onomastic</a> terminology also known as <b>prosoponym</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> πρόσωπον / <i>prósōpon</i> - person, and ὄνομα / <i>onoma</i> - name),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeats-Rohan2007164-165_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeats-Rohan2007164-165-1">[1]</a></sup> is the set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Names" title="Names">names</a> by which an individual person is known, and that can be recited as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word-group" title="Word-group">word-group</a>, with the understanding that, taken together, they all relate to that one individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoom199679_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoom199679-2">[2]</a></sup> In many cultures, the term is synonymous with the <i><a href="/wiki/Birth_name" title="Birth name">birth name</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Legal_name" title="Legal name">legal name</a></i> of the individual. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic" title="Linguistic">linguistic</a> classification, personal names are studied within a specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onomastic" title="Onomastic">onomastic</a> discipline, called <a href="/wiki/Anthroponymy" title="Anthroponymy">anthroponymy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoom19968_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoom19968-3">[3]</a></sup>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>, nearly all individuals possess at least one <i><a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a></i> (also known as a <i>first name</i>, <i>forename</i>, or <i>Christian name</i>), together with a <i><a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a></i> (also known as a <i>last name</i> or <i>family name</i>). In the name "<a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>", for example, <i>Abraham</i> is the first name and <i>Lincoln</i> is the surname. Surnames in the West generally indicate that the individual belongs to a family, a tribe, or a clan, although the exact relationships vary: they may be given at birth, taken upon adoption, <a href="/wiki/Maiden_and_married_names" title="Maiden and married names">changed upon marriage</a>, and so on. Where there are two or more given names, typically only one (in English-speaking cultures usually the first) is used in normal speech.
</p><p>Another naming convention that is used mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_culture" title="Arab culture">Arabic culture</a> and in different other areas across Africa and Asia is connecting the person's given name with a chain of names, starting with the name of the person's father and then the father's father and so on, usually ending with the family name (tribe or clan name). However, the legal full name of a person usually contains the first three names (given name, father's name, father's father's name) and the family name at the end, to limit the name in government-issued ID. Men's names and women's names are constructed using the same convention, and a person's name is not altered if they are married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some cultures, including Western ones, also add (or once added) <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymics</a>. For instance, as a middle name as with <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky</a> (whose father's given name was Ilya), or as a last name as with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rk_Gu%C3%B0mundsd%C3%B3ttir" title="Björk Guðmundsdóttir">Björk Guðmundsdóttir</a> (whose father is named <a href="/wiki/Gu%C3%B0mundur_Gunnarsson" title="Guðmundur Gunnarsson">Guðmundur</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0ar_Helguson" title="Heiðar Helguson">Heiðar Helguson</a> (whose mother was named Helga). Similar concepts are present in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern</a> cultures. However, in some areas of the world, many people are known by a single name, and so are said to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">mononymous</a>. Still other cultures lack the concept of specific, fixed names designating people, either individually or collectively. Certain isolated tribes, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machiguenga_people" title="Machiguenga people">Machiguenga</a> of the Amazon, do not use personal names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[i]</a></sup>
</p><p>A person's personal name is usually their full <a href="/wiki/Legal_name" title="Legal name">legal name</a>; however, some people use only part of their full legal name, a <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">title</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> or other chosen name that is different from their legal name, and reserve their legal name for legal and administrative purposes. The use of chosen names is very common on <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-binary" title="Non-binary">non-binary</a> communities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>It is nearly universal for people to have names; the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> declares that a child has the right to a name from birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Common components of names given at birth include:
</p>
<ul><li>Personal name: The <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (or acquired name in some cultures) can precede a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_name" title="Family name">family name</a> (as in most European cultures), or it can come after the family name (as in some East Asian cultures and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>), or be used without a family name.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">Patronymic</a>: A surname based on the given name of the father.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">Matronymic</a>: A surname based on the given name of the mother.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_name" title="Family name">Family name</a>: A name used by all members of a family. In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, surnames gradually came into common use beginning in the 3rd century BC (having been common only among the nobility before that). In some areas of East Asia (e.g. Vietnam and Korea), surnames developed in the next several centuries, while in other areas (like Japan), surnames did not become prevalent until the 19th century. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, after the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman system</a>, the common use of family names started quite early in some areas (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in the 13th century, and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in the 16th century), but it often did not happen until much later in areas that used a patronymic naming custom, such as the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> countries, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and some areas of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. The compulsory use of surnames varied greatly. France required a priest to write surnames in baptismal records in 1539 (but did not require surnames for Jews, who usually used <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>, until 1808). On the other hand, surnames were not compulsory in the Scandinavian countries until the 19th or 20th century (1923 in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> still <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">does not use surnames</a> for its native inhabitants. In most of the cultures of the Middle East and South Asia, surnames were not generally used until European influence took hold in the 19th century.</li></ul>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and most Latin American countries, two surnames are used, one being the father's family name and the other being the mother's family name. In Spain, though, the second surname is frequently used if the first one is too common to allow an easy identification. For example, Former <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a> is often called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zapatero" title="Zapatero">Zapatero</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, only the father's last name is used, in most cases.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and most other Portuguese-speaking countries, at least two surnames are used, often three or four, typically some or none inherited from the mother and some or all inherited from the father, in that order. Co-parental siblings most often share an identical string of surnames. For collation, shortening, and formal addressing, the last of these surnames is typically preferred. A Portuguese person named António de Oliveira Guterres would therefore be known commonly as António Guterres.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slavic_naming_customs" title="Eastern Slavic naming customs">Russia</a>, the first name and family name conform to the usual Western practice, but the middle name is <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>. Thus, all the children of Ivan Volkov would be named "[first name] Ivanovich Volkov" if male, or "[first name] Ivanovna Volkova" if female (-ovich meaning "son of", -ovna meaning "daughter of",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> and -a usually being appended to the surnames of girls). However, in formal Russian name order, the surname comes first, followed by the given name and patronymic, such as "Raskolnikov Rodion Romanovich".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many families, single or multiple <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle names</a> are simply alternative names, names honoring an ancestor or relative, or, for married women, sometimes their maiden names. In some traditions, however, the roles of the first and middle given names are reversed, with the first given name being used to honor a family member and the middle name being used as the usual method to address someone informally. Many <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> families choose a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a>'s name as their child's middle name or this can be left until the child's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confirmation_(sacrament)" title="Confirmation (sacrament)">confirmation</a> when they choose a saint's name for themselves. Cultures that use <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymics</a> will often give middle names to distinguish between two similarly named people: e.g., Einar Karl Stefánsson and Einar Guðmundur Stefánsson. This is especially done in Iceland (as shown in example) where people are known and referred to almost exclusively by their given name/s.
</p><p>Some people (called <i>anonyms</i>) choose to be <a href="/wiki/Anonymity" title="Anonymity">anonymous</a>, that is, to hide their true names, for fear of governmental prosecution or social ridicule of their works or actions. Another method to disguise one's identity is to employ a <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a>.
</p><p>For some people, their name is a single word, known as a <a href="/wiki/Mononym" title="Mononym">mononym</a>. This can be true from birth, or occur later in life. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teller_(entertainer)" title="Teller (entertainer)">Teller</a>, of the magician duo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penn_and_Teller" title="Penn and Teller">Penn and Teller</a>, was named Raymond Joseph Teller at birth, but changed his name both legally and socially to be simply "Teller". In some official government documents, such as his <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_license" title="Driver's license">driver's license</a>, his given name is listed as <i>NFN</i>, an initialism for "no first name".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> believe that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soul_(spirit)" title="Soul (spirit)">souls</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Namesake" title="Namesake">namesakes</a> are one, so they traditionally refer to the junior namesakes, not just by the names (<i>atiq</i>), but also by <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> title, which applies across gender and generation without implications of disrespect or seniority.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, someone's name is considered intimately connected with their fate, and adding a name (e.g. on the sickbed) may avert a particular danger. Among <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a> it is also considered bad luck to take the name of a living ancestor, as the <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)#In_Judaism" title="Death (personification)">Angel of Death</a> may mistake the younger person for their namesake (although there is no such custom among <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a>). Jews may also have a Jewish name for intra-community use and use a different name when engaging with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goyim" title="Goyim">Gentile</a> world.
</p><p>Chinese children are called diminutive or pejorative names to make them appear worthless to evil spirits. They receive a definitive name as they grow up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_emperor" title="Chinese emperor">Chinese</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_emperor" title="Japanese emperor">Japanese emperors</a> receive <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous names</a>.
</p><p>In some <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_culture" title="Polynesian culture">Polynesian cultures</a>, the name of a deceased chief becomes <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>. If he is named after a common object or concept, a different word has to be used for it.
</p><p>Depending on national convention, additional given names (and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a>) are considered part of the name.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feudal_names">Feudal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Feudal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Disputed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-disputed" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><img alt="System-search.svg" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="45" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/45px-System-search.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/68px-System-search.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/90px-System-search.svg.png 2x" width="45"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section's <b>factual accuracy is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">disputed</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on <a href="/wiki/Talk:Personal_name#Dispute" title="Talk:Personal name">Talk:Personal name</a>. Please help to ensure that disputed statements are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources">reliably sourced</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2013</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royalty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> of Europe traditionally have many names, including phrases for the lands that they own. The French developed the method of putting the term by which the person is referred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_capital" title="Small capital">small capital</a> letters. It is this habit which transferred to names of Eastern Asia, as seen below. An example is that of Marie-Joseph-Paul-Yves-Roch Gilbert du Motier, who is known as the <span style="font-variant:small-caps"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilbert_du_Motier,_marquis_de_Lafayette" title="Gilbert du Motier, marquis de Lafayette">Marquis de Lafayette</a></span>. Note that he possessed both the lands of Motier and Lafayette.
</p><p>The bare place name was used formerly to refer to the person who owned it, rather than the land itself (the word "Gloucester" in "What will Gloucester do?" meant the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Gloucester" title="Duke of Gloucester">Duke of Gloucester</a>). As a development, the bare name of a ship in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> meant its captain (e.g., "Cressy didn't learn from Aboukir") while the name with an article referred to the ship (e.g., "The <i>Cressy</i> is foundering").
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_conventions">Naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A personal naming system, or anthroponymic system, is a system describing the choice of personal name in a certain society. Personal names consists of one or more parts, such as <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>. Personal naming systems are studied within the field of <a href="/wiki/Anthroponymy" title="Anthroponymy">anthroponymy</a>.
</p><p>In contemporary Western societies (except for <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, depending on the occasion), the most common naming convention is that a person must have a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, which is usually gender-specific, followed by the parents' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_name" title="Family name">family name</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onomastic" title="Onomastic">onomastic</a> terminology, given names of male persons are called <i>andronyms</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> ἀνήρ / man, and ὄνομα / name),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoom19966_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoom19966-11">[10]</a></sup> while given names of female persons are called <i>gynonyms</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> γυνή / woman, and ὄνομα / name).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarolini200591,_98_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarolini200591,_98-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some given names are bespoke, but most are repeated from earlier generations in the same culture. Many are drawn from mythology, some of which span multiple language areas. This has resulted in related names in different languages (e.g. <a href="/wiki/George_(given_name)" title="George (given name)">George</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Georg_(disambiguation)" title="Georg (disambiguation)">Georg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jorge" title="Jorge">Jorge</a>), which might be translated or might be maintained as immutable proper nouns.
</p><p>In earlier times, Scandinavian countries followed patronymic naming, with people effectively called "X's son/daughter"; this is now the case only in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and was recently re-introduced as an option in the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>. It is legally possible in Finland as people of Icelandic ethnic naming are specifically named in the name law. When people of this name convert to standards of other cultures, the phrase is often condensed into one word, creating last names like Jacobsen (Jacob's Son).
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kafirstan" title="Kafirstan">Kafirstan</a> (now part of Afghanistan) "Children are named as soon as born. The infant is given to the mother to suckle, while a wise woman rapidly recites the family ancestral names; the name pronounced at the instant the baby begins to feed is that by which it is thereafter known."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a range of personal naming systems:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Binomial systems: apart from their given name, people are described by their surnames, which they obtain from one of their parents. Most modern European personal naming systems are of this type.</li>
<li>Patronymic systems: apart from their given name, people are described by their <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>, that is given names (not surnames) of parents or other ancestors. Such systems were in wide use throughout Europe in the first millennium CE, but were replaced by binomial systems. The <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic system</a> is still patronymic.</li>
<li>More complex systems like <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name" title="Arabic name">Arabic system</a>, consisting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paedonymic" title="Paedonymic">paedonymic</a> (son's name), given name, patronymic and one or two bynames.</li></ul>
<p>Different cultures have different conventions for personal names. This is a list of articles about particular cultures' naming conventions.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_name_order">Western name order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western name order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2021</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The order <i>given name, family name</i> is commonly known as the <b>Western name order</b> and is usually used in most <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries and in countries that have cultures predominantly influenced by Western Europe (e.g. <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>; <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_India" title="East India">East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_India" title="Central India">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_India" title="Western India">West India</a>; <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>; <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>; <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> (most of); <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>; <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>).
</p><p>Within alphabetic lists and catalogs, however, the family name is generally put first, with the given name(s) following and separated by a <a href="/wiki/Comma" title="Comma">comma</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Jobs, Steve</a>), representing the "lexical name order". This convention is followed by most Western libraries, as well as on many administrative forms. In some countries, such as France, or countries previously part of the former Soviet Union, the comma may be dropped and the swapped form of the name be uttered as such, perceived as a mark of bureaucratic formality.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_name_order">Eastern name order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern name order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The order <i>family name, given name</i>, commonly known as the <b>Eastern name order</b>, began to be prominently used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">Ancient China</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> and subsequently influenced the <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_cultural_sphere" title="East Asian cultural sphere">East Asian cultural sphere</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China_(region)" title="China (region)">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>) along with <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> (among the Chinese communities). It is also used in the <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_India" title="Northeast India">northeastern</a> parts of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_names" title="Hungarian names">Hungarians</a>. In Uganda, the ordering "traditional family name first, Western-origin given name second" is also frequently used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>When East Asian names are <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterated</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, some people prefer to convert them to the Western order, while others leave them in the Eastern order but write the family name in <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">capital letters</a>. To avoid confusion, there is a convention in some language communities, e.g., French, that the family name should be written in all capitals when engaging in formal correspondence or writing for an international audience. In Hungarian, the Eastern order of Japanese names is officially kept and Hungarian transliteration is used (e.g. <a class="extiw" href="https://hu.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mijazaki_Hajao" title="hu:Mijazaki Hajao">hu:Mijazaki Hajao</a>), but Western name order is also sometimes used with English transliteration (e.g. Hayao Miyazaki).
</p><p>Starting from the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a> in 1868, the Western name order was primarily used among the Japanese nobility when identifying themselves to non-Asians with their <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">romanized</a> names. As a result, in popular Western journalism publications, this order became increasingly used for Japanese names in the subsequent decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saeki_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-17">[16]</a></sup> In 2020 the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Japan" title="Government of Japan">Government of Japan</a> reverted the order of romanized names back to the Eastern name order in official documents (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identity_documents" title="Identity documents">identity documents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic_certificate" title="Academic certificate">academic certificates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_certificates" title="Birth certificates">birth certificates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marriage_certificate" title="Marriage certificate">marriage certificates</a>, among others), which means writing family name first in capital letters and has recommended that the same format shall be used among the general Japanese public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPN_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-JPN-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Japan has also requested Western publications to respect this change, such as not using <a href="/wiki/Shinzo_Abe" title="Shinzo Abe">Shinzo Abe</a> but rather Abe Shinzo, similar to how Chinese leader <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a> is not referred to as Jinping Xi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Its sluggish response by Western publications was met with ire by Japanese politician <a href="/wiki/Taro_Kono" title="Taro Kono">Taro Kono</a>, which stated that "If you can write <a href="/wiki/Moon_Jae-in" title="Moon Jae-in">Moon Jae-in</a> and Xi Jinping in correct order, you can surely write Abe Shinzo the same way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China_and_Korea">China and Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: China and Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Chinese, Korean and other East Asian peoples, except for those traveling or living outside of China and areas influenced by China, rarely reverse their Chinese and Korean language names to the Western naming order. Western publications also preserve this Eastern naming order for Chinese, Korean and other East Asian individuals, with the family name first, followed by the given name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> In Hong Kong, <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> names of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_people" title="Hong Kong people">Hong Kong people</a> are usually written in the Eastern order with or without a comma (eg. Wong Yat Sum or Wong, Yat Sum). Outside Hong Kong, they are usually written in Western order. Unlike other East Asian countries, the syllables or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logograms" title="Logograms">logograms</a> of given names are not hyphenated or compounded but instead separated by a space (eg. Yat Sum). People outside Hong Kong often confuse the second syllables with middle names regardless of name order. Some computer systems could not handle given name inputs with space characters.
</p><p>Some Hong Kongers and Singaporeans may have an anglicised given name, always written in the Western order. The English and transliterated Chinese full names can be written in various orders. A hybrid order is preferred in official documents including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legislative_Council_(Hong_Kong)" title="Legislative Council (Hong Kong)">legislative</a> records in the case for Hong Kong. Examples of the hybrid order goes in the form of Hong Kong actor “<a href="/wiki/Tony_Leung_Chiu-wai" title="Tony Leung Chiu-wai">Tony Leung Chiu-wai</a>” or Singaporean politician "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Wong_Shyun_Tsai" title="Lawrence Wong Shyun Tsai">Lawrence Wong Shyun Tsai</a>", with family names (in the example, Leung and Wong) shared in the middle. Therefore, the anglicised names are written in the Western order (Tony Leung, Lawrence Wong) and the Chinese names are written in the Eastern order (Leung Chiu-wai, 梁朝偉; Wong Shyun Tsai, 黄循财).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Japanese use the Eastern naming order (family name followed by given name). In contrast to China and Korea, due to familiarity, Japanese names of contemporary people are usually "switched" when people who have such names are mentioned in media in Western countries; for example, <i>Koizumi Jun'ichirō</i> is known as <a href="/wiki/Junichiro_Koizumi" title="Junichiro Koizumi">Junichiro Koizumi</a> in English. Japan has requested that Western publications cease this practice of placing their names in the Western name order and revert back to the Eastern name order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPN_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-JPN-18">[17]</a></sup> Hungarian names are also switched; Puskás Ferenc is known as <a href="/wiki/Ferenc_Pusk%C3%A1s" title="Ferenc Puskás">Ferenc Puskás</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongolia">Mongolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mongolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> use the Eastern naming order (<a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> followed by given name), which is also used there when rendering the names of other East Asians and Hungarians. Russian and other Western names, however, are still written in Western order.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_India">Southern India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Southern India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Telugu_people" title="Telugu people">Telugu people</a> from <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telangana" title="Telangana">Telangana</a> traditionally use <i>family name, given name</i> order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The family name first format is different from <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North India</a> where family name typically appears last or other parts of South India where <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> names are widely used instead of family names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mordvins">Mordvins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mordvins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mordvins" title="Mordvins">Mordvins</a> use two names – a Mordvin name and a Russian name. The Mordvin name is written in the Eastern name order. Usually, the Mordvin surname is the same as the Russian surname, for example Sharononj Sandra (Russian: <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Sharonov" title="Aleksandr Sharonov">Aleksandr Sharonov</a>), but it can be different at times, for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yovlan_Olo" title="Yovlan Olo">Yovlan Olo</a> (Russian: Vladimir Romashkin).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_personal_names">Non-human personal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Non-human personal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from the <a href="/wiki/Linnaean_taxonomy" title="Linnaean taxonomy">Linnaean taxonomy</a>, some humans give individual non-human animals and plants names, usually of endearment.
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onomastic" title="Onomastic">onomastic</a> classification, names of <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_individual_animals" title="Lists of individual animals">individual animals</a> are called <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/zoonym" title="wikt:zoonym">zoonyms</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoom1996106_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoom1996106-24">[23]</a></sup> while names of individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a> are called <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phytonym" title="wikt:phytonym">phytonyms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoom199680_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoom199680-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_pets">Names of pets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Names of pets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The practice of naming pets dates back at least to the 23rd century BC; an Egyptian inscription from that period mentions a dog named <a href="/wiki/Abuwtiyuw" title="Abuwtiyuw">Abuwtiyuw</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many pet owners give human names to their pets. This has been shown to reflect the owner having a human-like relationship with the pet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003-27">[26]</a></sup> The name given to a pet may refer to its appearance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003-27">[26]</a></sup> or personality,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003-27">[26]</a></sup> or be chosen for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endearment" title="Endearment">endearment</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEldridge2003-27">[26]</a></sup> or in honor of a favorite <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Pet names often reflect the owner's view of the animal, and the expectations they may have for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dog <a href="/wiki/Animal_breeding" title="Animal breeding">breeders</a> often choose specific themes for their names, sometimes based on the number of the <a href="/wiki/Litter_(zoology)" title="Litter (zoology)">litter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> In some countries, like <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> or <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, names are chosen alphabetically, with names starting with 'A' for puppies from the first litter, 'B' for the second litter, and so on. A puppy called "Dagmar" would belong to a fourth litter. Dog owners can choose to keep the original name, or rename their pet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been argued that the giving of names to their pets allows researchers to view their pets as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontologically" title="Ontologically">ontologically</a> different from unnamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laboratory_animal" title="Laboratory animal">laboratory animals</a> with which they work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dolphin_names_for_each_other">Dolphin names for each other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dolphin names for each other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences claims that humans are not the only animals that use personal names. Researchers from the University of North Carolina Wilmington studying <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin" title="Bottlenose dolphin">bottlenose dolphins</a> in Sarasota Bay, Florida, found that the dolphins had names for each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> A dolphin chooses its name as an infant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
