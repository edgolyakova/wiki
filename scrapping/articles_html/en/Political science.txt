<p><b>Political science</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> study of <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>. It is a social science dealing with systems of <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> and power, and the analysis of <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> activities, political thought, political behavior, and associated <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitutions</a> and laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern political science can generally be divided into the three subdisciplines of <a href="/wiki/Comparative_politics" title="Comparative politics">comparative politics</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Other notable subdisciplines are <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public policy and administration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_politics" title="Domestic politics">domestic politics and government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_methodology" title="Political methodology">political methodology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Furthermore, political science is related to, and draws upon, the fields of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legal_education" title="Legal education">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">human geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_anthropology" title="Political anthropology">political anthropology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>.
</p><p>Political science is methodologically diverse and appropriates many methods originating in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_research" title="Social research">social research</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>. Approaches include <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">positivism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verstehen" title="Verstehen">interpretivism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rational_choice_theory" title="Rational choice theory">rational choice theory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behaviouralism" title="Behaviouralism">behaviouralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">structuralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">post-structuralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">realism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Historical_institutionalism" title="Historical institutionalism">institutionalism</a>, and pluralism. Political science, as one of the social sciences, uses methods and techniques that relate to the kinds of inquires sought: <a href="/wiki/Primary_source" title="Primary source">primary sources</a>, such as historical documents and official records, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_source" title="Secondary source">secondary sources</a>, such as scholarly journal articles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_survey" title="Sample survey">survey</a> research, <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical analysis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case_studies" title="Case studies">case studies</a>, experimental research, and model building.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_political_science" title="History of political science">History of political science</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a social political science, contemporary political science started to take shape in the latter half of the 19th century and began separate itself from political <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> Into the late 19th century, it was still uncommon that political science was considered a distinct field from history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> The term "political science" was not always distinguished from political philosophy, and the modern discipline has a clear set of antecedents including also moral philosophy, political economy, <a href="/wiki/Political_theology" title="Political theology">political theology</a>, history, and other fields concerned with normative determinations of what ought to be and with deducing the characteristics and functions of the ideal state.
</p><p>The advent of political science as a university discipline was marked by the creation of university departments and chairs with the title of political science arising in the late 19th century. The designation "political scientist" is commonly used to denote someone with a doctorate or master's degree in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Integrating political studies of the past into a unified discipline is ongoing, and the history of political science has provided a rich field for the growth of both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" title="Norm (sociology)">normative</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_(social_sciences)" title="Positive (social sciences)">positive</a> political science, with each part of the discipline sharing some historical predecessors. The <a href="/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association" title="American Political Science Association">American Political Science Association</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Political_Science_Review" title="American Political Science Review">American Political Science Review</a></i> were founded in 1903 and 1906, respectively, in an effort to distinguish the study of <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> from economics and other social phenomena. APSA membership rose from 204 in 1904 to 1,462 in 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> APSA members played a key role in setting up political science departments that were distinct from history, philosophy, law, sociology, and economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The journal <i><a href="/wiki/Political_Science_Quarterly" title="Political Science Quarterly">Political Science Quarterly</a></i> was established in 1886 by the Academy of Political Science. In the inaugural issue of <i>Political Science Quarterly</i>, <a href="/wiki/Munroe_Smith" title="Munroe Smith">Munroe Smith</a> defined political science as "the science of the state. Taken in this sense, it includes the organization and functions of the state, and the relation of states one to another."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of a UNESCO initiative to promote political science in the late 1940s, the International Political Science Association was founded in 1949, as well as national associations in France in 1949, Britain in 1950, and West Germany in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioural_revolution_and_new_institutionalism">Behavioural revolution and new institutionalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavioural revolution and new institutionalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1950s and the 1960s, a behavioral revolution stressing the systematic and rigorously scientific study of individual and group behavior swept the discipline. A focus on studying political behavior, rather than institutions or interpretation of legal texts, characterized early behavioral political science, including work by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dahl" title="Robert Dahl">Robert Dahl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Converse" title="Philip Converse">Philip Converse</a>, and in the collaboration between sociologist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lazarsfeld" title="Paul Lazarsfeld">Paul Lazarsfeld</a> and public opinion scholar <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Berelson" title="Bernard Berelson">Bernard Berelson</a>.
</p><p>The late 1960s and early 1970s witnessed a takeoff in the use of deductive, <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game-theoretic</a> formal modelling techniques aimed at generating a more analytical corpus of knowledge in the discipline. This period saw a surge of research that borrowed theory and methods from economics to study political institutions, such as the United States Congress, as well as political behavior, such as voting. <a href="/wiki/William_H._Riker" title="William H. Riker">William H. Riker</a> and his colleagues and students at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester" title="University of Rochester">University of Rochester</a> were the main proponents of this shift.
</p><p>Despite considerable research progress in the discipline based on all the kinds of scholarship discussed above, it has been observed that progress toward systematic theory has been modest and uneven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perestroika_Movement" title="Perestroika Movement">Perestroika Movement</a> in political science was introduced as a reaction against what supporters of the movement called the mathematicization of political science. Those who identified with the movement argued for a plurality of methodologies and approaches in political science and for more relevance of the discipline to those outside of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a> theories argue that humans have evolved a highly developed set of psychological mechanisms for dealing with politics. However, these mechanisms evolved for dealing with the small group politics that characterized the ancestral environment and not the much larger political structures in today's world. This is argued to explain many important features and systematic <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_bias" title="Cognitive bias">cognitive biases</a> of current politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEP_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AEP-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

policy</a></div></div></div><p>Political science is a social study concerning the allocation and transfer of <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">decision making</a>, the roles and systems of governance including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governments" title="Governments">governments</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_organizations" title="International organizations">international organizations</a>, political behaviour, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_policies" title="Public policies">public policies</a>. It measures the success of <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> and specific policies by examining many factors, including <a href="/wiki/Economic_stability" title="Economic stability">stability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_wealth" title="Material wealth">material wealth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">peace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>. Some political scientists seek to advance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_(social_sciences)" title="Positive (social sciences)">positive</a> theses (which attempt to describe how things are, as opposed to how they should be) by analysing politics; others advance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" title="Norm (sociology)">normative</a> theses, such as by making specific policy recommendations. The study of politics and policies can be closely connected—for example, in comparative analyses of which types of political institutions tend to produce certain types of policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Political science provides analysis and predictions about political and governmental issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maddocks_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maddocks-11">[11]</a></sup> Political scientists examine the processes, systems and political dynamics of countries and regions of the world, often to raise public awareness or to influence specific governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maddocks_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maddocks-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Political scientists may provide the frameworks from which journalists, special interest groups, politicians, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constituency" title="Constituency">electorate</a> analyze issues. According to Chaturvedy,
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Political scientists may serve as advisers to specific politicians, or even run for office as politicians themselves. Political scientists can be found working in governments, in political parties, or as civil servants. They may be involved with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" title="Non-governmental organizations">non-governmental organizations</a> (NGOs) or political movements. In a variety of capacities, people educated and trained in political science can add value and expertise to <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a>. Private enterprises such as <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tanks</a>, research institutes, polling and <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> firms often employ political scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Country-specific_studies">Country-specific studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Country-specific studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Political scientists may study political phenomena within one specific country; for example, they may study just the politics of the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> or just the politics of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Political scientists look at a variety of data, including constitutions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elections" title="Elections">elections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">public opinion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a>, legislatures, and judiciaries. Political scientists will often focus on the politics of their own country; for example, a political scientist from Indonesia may become an expert in the politics of Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anticipating_crises">Anticipating crises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anticipating crises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theory of political transitions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the methods of analyzing and anticipating<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> crises,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> form an important part of political science. Several general indicators of crises and methods were proposed for anticipating critical transitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Among them, one statistical indicator of crisis, a simultaneous increase of <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">variance</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlations" title="Correlations">correlations</a> in large groups, was proposed for crisis anticipation and may be successfully used in various areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Its applicability for early diagnosis of political crises was demonstrated by the analysis of the prolonged stress period preceding the 2014 Ukrainian economic and political crisis. There was a simultaneous increase in the total correlation between the 19 major public fears in the Ukrainian society (by about 64%) and in their statistical dispersion (by 29%) during the pre-crisis years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A feature shared by certain major revolutions is that they were not predicted. The theory of apparent inevitability of crises and revolutions was also developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The study of major crises, both political crises and external crises that can affect politics, is not limited to attempts to predict regime transitions or major changes in political institutions. Political scientists also study how governments handle unexpected disasters, and how voters in democracies react to their governments' preparations for and responses to crises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognate_fields">Cognate fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cognate fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most political scientists work broadly in one or more of the following five areas:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">Political philosophy</a> or political theory</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">Public administration</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Public_law" title="Public law">Public law</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">Public policy</a></li>
<li>Program evaluation</li></ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Program_Evaluation" title="Program Evaluation">Program evaluation</a> is a systematic method for collecting, analyzing, and using information to answer questions about projects, policies, and programs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> particularly about their effectiveness and efficiency. In both the public and private sectors, stakeholders often want to know whether the programs they are funding, implementing, voting for, receiving, or objecting to are producing the intended effect. While program evaluation first focuses on this definition, important considerations often include how much the program costs per participant, how the program could be improved, whether the program is worthwhile, whether there are better alternatives, whether there are unintended outcomes, and whether the program goals are appropriate and useful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Policy_analysis" title="Policy analysis">Policy analysis</a> is a technique used in public administration to enable civil servants, activists, and others to examine and evaluate the available options to implement the goals of laws and elected officials.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subfields">Subfields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Subfields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many political scientists conduct research in one of four areas, described below:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_Theory" title="Political Theory">Political philosophy</a>: Concerned with the foundations of political community and institutions, while focusing on human nature and the moral purposes of political association.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Comparative_politics" title="Comparative politics">Comparative politics</a>: Compares contemporary political systems and discovers general laws and theories.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">International relations</a>: Concerned with developing an understanding of why states and non-state international actors interact.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Political_methodology" title="Political methodology">Political methodology</a>: Studies the philosophical bases of social science, political science, empirical research design and analysis.</li></ul>
<p>Some political science departments also classify <a href="/wiki/Political_methodology" title="Political methodology">methodology</a> as well as scholarship on the domestic politics of a particular country as distinct fields. In the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_political_science" title="American political science">American politics</a> is often treated as a separate subfield. In contrast to this traditional classification, some academic departments organize scholarship into thematic categories, including political philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Theories_of_political_behavior" title="Theories of political behavior">political behaviour</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">public opinion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collective_action" title="Collective action">collective action</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">identity</a>), and political institutions (including <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislatures</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_organization" title="International organization">international organizations</a>). Political science conferences and journals often emphasize scholarship in more specific categories. The American Political Science Association, for example, has 42 organized sections that address various methods and topics of political inquiry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_methods">Research methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Research methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_methodology" title="Political methodology">Political methodology</a></div>
<p>Political science is methodologically diverse; political scientists approach the study of politics from a host of different ontological orientations and with a variety of different tools. Because political science is essentially a study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_behaviour" title="Human behaviour">human behaviour</a>, in all aspects of <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, observations in controlled environments are often challenging to reproduce or duplicate, though <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimental</a> methods are increasingly common (see <a href="/wiki/Experimental_political_science" title="Experimental political science">experimental political science</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Citing this difficulty, former <a href="/wiki/American_Political_Science_Association" title="American Political Science Association">American Political Science Association</a> President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Lowell" title="Lawrence Lowell">Lawrence Lowell</a> once said "We are limited by the impossibility of experiment. Politics is an observational, not an experimental science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> Because of this, political scientists have historically observed political elites, institutions, and individual or group behaviour in order to identify patterns, draw generalizations, and build theories of politics.
</p><p>Like all social sciences, political science faces the difficulty of observing human actors that can only be partially observed and who have the capacity for making conscious choices, unlike other subjects such as non-human organisms in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> or inanimate objects as in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>. Despite the complexities, contemporary political science has progressed by adopting a variety of methods and theoretical approaches to understanding politics, and <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodological</a> pluralism is a defining feature of contemporary political science.
</p><p>Empirical political science methods include the use of field experiments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> surveys and survey experiments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> case studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> process tracing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> historical and institutional analysis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> ethnography,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> participant observation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and interview research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Political scientists also use and develop theoretical tools like game theory and agent-based models to study a host of political systems and situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Political theorists approach theories of political phenomena with a similar diversity of positions and tools, including <a href="/wiki/Feminist_political_theory" title="Feminist political theory">feminist political theory</a>, historical analysis associated with the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_School_(intellectual_history)" title="Cambridge School (intellectual history)">Cambridge school</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leo_Strauss#Straussianism" title="Leo Strauss">Straussian approaches</a>.
</p><p>Political science may overlap with topics of study that are the traditional focuses of other social sciences—for example, when sociological <a href="/wiki/Social_norm" title="Social norm">norms</a> or psychological <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_bias" title="Cognitive bias">biases</a> are connected to political phenomena. In these cases, political science may either inherit their methods of study or develop a contrasting approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wedeen02_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-wedeen02-39">[39]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Wedeen" title="Lisa Wedeen">Lisa Wedeen</a> has argued that political science's approach to the idea of culture, originating with <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Almond" title="Gabriel Almond">Gabriel Almond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Verba" title="Sidney Verba">Sidney Verba</a> and exemplified by authors like <a href="/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington" title="Samuel P. Huntington">Samuel P. Huntington</a>, could benefit from aligning more closely with the study of culture in anthropology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wedeen02_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-wedeen02-39">[39]</a></sup> In turn, methodologies that are developed within political science may influence how researchers in other fields, like public health, conceive of and approach political processes and policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Political science, possibly like the social sciences as a whole, can be described "as a discipline which lives on the fault line between the 'two cultures' in the academy, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sciences" title="Sciences">sciences</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stoner_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stoner-41">[41]</a></sup> Thus, in some American colleges where there is no separate <a href="/wiki/College_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="College of Arts and Sciences">school or college of arts and sciences</a> per se, political science may be a separate department housed as part of a division or school of humanities or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_Arts" title="Liberal Arts">liberal arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marist_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marist-42">[42]</a></sup> Whereas classical <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> is primarily defined by a concern for <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Hellenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> thought, political scientists are also marked by a great concern for "<a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>" and the contemporary <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a>, along with the study of classical thought, and as such share more terminology with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociologists" title="Sociologists">sociologists</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Structure_and_agency" title="Structure and agency">structure and agency</a>).
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Higher_education_in_the_United_States" title="Higher education in the United States">colleges and universities</a> offer BA programs in political science. MA or MAT and PhD or EdD programs are common at larger universities. The term <i>political science</i> is more popular in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> than elsewhere; other institutions, especially those outside the United States, see political science as part of a broader discipline of <i>political studies,</i> <i>politics,</i> or <i>government.</i> While <i>political science</i> implies the use of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>, <i>political studies</i> implies a broader approach, although the naming of degree courses does not necessarily reflect their content.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Separate programs (often professional degrees) in international relations and <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a> are not uncommon at both the undergraduate and postgraduate levels. Master's-level programs in <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a> are professional degrees covering public policy along with other applied subjects; they are often seen as more linked to politics than any other discipline, which may be reflected by being housed in that department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>The national honor society for college and university students of government and politics in the United States is <a href="/wiki/Pi_Sigma_Alpha" title="Pi Sigma Alpha">Pi Sigma Alpha</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are different genres of writings in political sciences; including but not limited to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Argument essays and research papers</li>
<li>Political theory writing</li>
<li>Responses to articles, texts, events thoughts and reflective papers</li></ul>
<p>The most common piece of writing in political sciences are research papers, which investigate an original research question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
