<p><b>Grasslands</b> are areas where the <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> is dominated by grasses (<a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>). However, sedge (<a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">Cyperaceae</a>) and rush (<a href="/wiki/Juncaceae" title="Juncaceae">Juncaceae</a>) can also be found along with variable proportions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legumes" title="Legumes">legumes</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbs</a>. Grasslands occur naturally on all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and are found in most <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Furthermore, grasslands are one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biomes</a> on earth and dominate the landscape worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> There are different types of grasslands: natural grasslands, semi-natural grasslands, and agricultural grasslands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> They cover 31–69% of the Earth's land area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coxilhas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2576" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Coxilhas.jpg/220px-Coxilhas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Coxilhas.jpg/330px-Coxilhas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Coxilhas.jpg/440px-Coxilhas.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coxilhas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Coxilhas</i> (hills covered by grasslands) in the <a href="/wiki/Pampas" title="Pampas">Pampas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a> state, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a></div></div></div>
<p>There is a variety of definitions for grasslands:
</p>
<ul><li>"...any plant community, including harvested forages, in which grasses and/or legumes make up the dominant vegetation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>"...terrestrial ecosystems dominated by herbaceous and shrub vegetation, and maintained by fire, grazing, drought and/or freezing temperatures." (Pilot Assessment of Global Ecosystems, 2000)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>"A region with sufficient average annual precipitation (25-75 cm) to support grass..." (Stiling, 1999)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Semi-natural grasslands are a very common subcategory of the grasslands biome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These can be defined as:
</p>
<ul><li>Grassland existing as a result of human activity (mowing or livestock grazing), where environmental conditions and the species pool are maintained by natural processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>They can also be described as the following:
</p>
<ul><li>"Semi-natural grasslands are one of the world's most biodiverse habitats on a small spatial scales."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Semi-natural grasslands belong to the most species rich ecosystems in the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>"...have been formed over the course of centuries through extensive grazing and mowing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>"...without the use of pesticides or fertilisers in modern time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>There are many different types of semi-natural grasslands, e.g. hay <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_history">Evolutionary history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolutionary history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Graminoid" title="Graminoid">graminoids</a> are among the most versatile <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">life forms</a>. They became widespread toward the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period, and <a href="/wiki/Coprolite" title="Coprolite">coprolites</a> of fossilized <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaur</a> feces have been found containing <a href="/wiki/Phytolith" title="Phytolith">phytoliths</a> of a variety of grasses that include grasses that are related to modern <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>.
</p><p>The appearance of mountains in the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">western United States</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> epochs, a period of some 25 million years, created a continental climate favourable to the evolution of grasslands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Around 5 million years ago during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Miocene" title="Late Miocene">Late Miocene</a> in the New World and the Pliocene in the Old World, the first true grasslands occurred. Existing forest biomes declined, and grasslands became much more widespread. It is known that grasslands have existed in Europe throughout the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> (the last 1.8 million years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a> (with their <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacials</a>), grasslands expanded in the hotter, drier climates, and began to become the dominant land feature worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup> Since the grasslands have existed for over 1.8 million years, there is high variability. For example <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_steppe" title="Mammoth steppe">steppe-tundra</a> dominated in Northern and Central Europe whereas a higher amount of xerothermic grasslands occurred in the Mediterranean area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> Within temperate Europe, the range of types is quite wide and also became unique due to the exchange of species and genetic material between different biomes.
</p><p>The semi-natural grasslands first appeared when humans started farming. So for the use of agriculture, forests got cleared in Europe. Ancient meadows and pastures were the parts that were suitable for cultivation. The semi-natural grasslands were formed from these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> However, there's also evidence for the local persistence of natural grasslands in Europe, originally maintained by wild herbivores, throughout the pre-neolithic Holocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The removal of the plants by the grazing animals and later the mowing farmers led to co-existence of other plant species around. In the following, the biodiversity of the plants evolve. Also, the species that already lived there adapted to the new conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the grassland areas have been turned to arable fields and disappeared again.
</p><p>Nowadays, semi-natural grasslands are rather located in areas that are unsuitable for agricultural farming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grasslands dominated by unsown wild-plant communities ("unimproved grasslands") can be called either natural or "semi-natural" habitat. Although their plant communities are natural, their maintenance depends upon anthropogenic activities such as grazing and cutting regimes. The semi-natural grasslands contain many species of wild plants, including grasses, sedges, rushes, and herbs; 25 plant-species per 100 square centimeters can be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> A European record that was found on a meadow in Estonia described 76 species of plants in one square meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> Chalk downlands in England can support over 40 species per square meter.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_rhino_with_calf_(male).jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2250" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Black_rhino_with_calf_%28male%29.jpeg/220px-Black_rhino_with_calf_%28male%29.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Black_rhino_with_calf_%28male%29.jpeg/330px-Black_rhino_with_calf_%28male%29.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Black_rhino_with_calf_%28male%29.jpeg/440px-Black_rhino_with_calf_%28male%29.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_rhino_with_calf_(male).jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Black rhino</div></div></div>
<p>In many parts of the world, few examples have escaped agricultural improvement (fertilizing, weed killing, plowing, or re-seeding). For example, original North American prairie grasslands or lowland wildflower meadows in the UK are now rare and their associated wild flora equally threatened. Associated with the wild-plant diversity of the "unimproved" grasslands is usually a rich invertebrate fauna; there are also many species of birds that are grassland "specialists", such as the <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Little_bustard" title="Little bustard">little bustard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Owing to semi-natural grasslands being referred to as one of the most-species rich ecosystems in the world and essential habitat for many specialists, also including pollinators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-7">[7]</a></sup> there are many approaches to conservation activities lately.
</p><p>Agriculturally improved grasslands, which dominate modern intensive agricultural landscapes, are usually poor in wild plant species due to the original diversity of plants having been destroyed by cultivation and by the use of fertilizers.
</p><p>Almost 90% of the European semi-natural grasslands do not exist anymore due to political and economic reasons. This loss only took place during the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> The ones in Western and Central Europe have almost disappeared completely. There are a few left in Northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unfortunately, a large amount of red-listed species are specialists of semi-natural grasslands and are affected by the landscape change due to agriculture of the last century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The original wild-plant communities having been replaced by sown monocultures of cultivated varieties of grasses and clovers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perennial_ryegrass" title="Perennial ryegrass">perennial ryegrass</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_clover" title="White clover">white clover</a>. In many parts of the world, "unimproved" grasslands are one of the most threatened types of habitat, and a target for acquisition by wildlife conservation groups or for special grants to landowners who are encouraged to manage them appropriately.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation">Vegetation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vegetation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG/170px-Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG/255px-Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG/340px-Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quercus_robur_%27Pendula%27.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Quercus robur</i>, also known as the English oak, dominating the semi-natural grasslands</div></div></div>
<p>Grassland <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> can vary considerably depending on the grassland type and on how strong it is affected by human impact. Dominant trees for the semi-natural grassland are <i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_robur" title="Quercus robur">Quercus robur</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Betula_pendula" title="Betula pendula">Betula pendula</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Corylus_avellana" title="Corylus avellana">Corylus avellana</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crataegus" title="Crataegus">Crataegus</a></i> and many kinds of herbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Calcareous_grassland" title="Calcareous grassland">chalk grassland</a>, the plants can vary from height to very short. Quite tall grasses can be found in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Tallgrass_prairie" title="Tallgrass prairie">tallgrass prairie</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> grasslands, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>. Woody plants, shrubs or trees may occur on some grasslands—forming savannas, scrubby grassland or semi-wooded grassland, such as the African savannas or the Iberian deheza.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>As flowering <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and trees, grasses grow in great concentrations in <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climates</a> where annual <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a> ranges between 500 and 900 mm (20 and 35 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-15">[15]</a></sup> The root systems of perennial grasses and <a href="/wiki/Forb" title="Forb">forbs</a> form complex mats that hold the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> in place.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_Plover,_Charadrius_montanus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mountain_Plover%2C_Charadrius_montanus.jpg/220px-Mountain_Plover%2C_Charadrius_montanus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mountain_Plover%2C_Charadrius_montanus.jpg/330px-Mountain_Plover%2C_Charadrius_montanus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mountain_Plover%2C_Charadrius_montanus.jpg/440px-Mountain_Plover%2C_Charadrius_montanus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_Plover,_Charadrius_montanus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mountain plover</div></div></div>
<p>Grasslands support the greatest aggregations of large animals on earth, including jaguars, African wild dogs, <a href="/wiki/Pronghorn" title="Pronghorn">pronghorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-footed_ferret" title="Black-footed ferret">black-footed ferret</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plains_bison" title="Plains bison">plains bison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_plover" title="Mountain plover">mountain plover</a>, African elephant, Sunda tiger, black rhino, white rhino, savanna elephant, greater one-horned rhino, Indian elephant and <a href="/wiki/Swift_fox" title="Swift fox">swift fox</a>. Grazing animals, herd animals, and <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a> in grasslands, like lions and <a href="/wiki/Cheetah" title="Cheetah">cheetahs</a> live in the grasslands of the African savanna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">Mites</a>, insect <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a><a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> inhabit deep soil, which can reach 6 metres (20 feet) underground in undisturbed grasslands on the richest soils of the world. These invertebrates, along with symbiotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, extend the root systems, break apart hard soil, enrich it with <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and other natural fertilizers, trap minerals and water and promote growth. Some types of fungi make the plants more resistant to insect and microbial attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg/220px-Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg/330px-Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg/440px-Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cheetah_Brothers_AdF.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cheetah</div></div></div>
<p>Grassland in all its form supports a vast variety of mammals, reptiles, birds, and insects. Typical large mammals include the <a href="/wiki/Blue_wildebeest" title="Blue wildebeest">blue wildebeest</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">American bison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giant_anteater" title="Giant anteater">giant anteater</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Przewalski%27s_horse" title="Przewalski's horse">Przewalski's horse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The plants and animals that live in grasslands are connected through an unlimited web of interactions. But the removal of key species—such as buffalo and prairie dogs within the American West—and introduction of <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, like cane toads in northern Australia, have disrupted the balance in these ecosystems and damaged a number of other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">[16]</a></sup> Grasslands are home to a number of the foremost magnificent animals on the planet—elephants, bison, lions—and hunters have found them to be enticing prey. But when hunting is not controlled or is conducted illegally, species can become extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_services">Ecosystem services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ecosystem services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grasslands provide a range of marketed and non-marketed ecosystem services that are fundamental to the livelihoods of an estimated one billion people globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:9-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_sequestration">Carbon sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Carbon sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grasslands hold about 20 percent of global soil carbon stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-2">[2]</a></sup> Herbaceous (non-wooded) vegetation dominates grasslands and, unlike forests, carbon is stored in the roots and soil underground. Furthermore, this above-ground biomass carbon is relatively short-lived due to grazing, fire, and senescence. In contrast, grassland species have an extensive fibrous root system, with grasses often accounting for 60-80% of the biomass carbon in this ecosystem. This underground biomass can extend several meters below the surface and store abundant carbon into the soil, resulting in deep, fertile soils with high organic matter content. For this reason, soil carbon accounts for about 81% of the total ecosystem carbon in grasslands. The close link between soil carbon and underground biomass leads to similar responses of these carbon pools to fluctuations in annual precipitation and temperature on a broad spatial scale. Because plant productivity is limited by grassland precipitation, carbon stocks are highest in regions where precipitation is heaviest, such as the high grass prairie in the humid temperate region of the United States. Similarly, as annual temperatures rise, grassland carbon stocks decrease due to increased evapotranspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grasslands have suffered large losses of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_carbon" title="Organic carbon">organic carbon</a> due to soil disturbances, vegetation degradation, fires, erosion, nutrient deficiencies, and water shortages. The type, frequency and intensity of the disturbance can play a key role in the soil organic carbon (<a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">SOC</a>) balance of grasslands. <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">Bedrock</a>, irrigation practices, <a href="/wiki/Soil_acidification" title="Soil acidification">soil acidification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liming_(soil)" title="Liming (soil)">liming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> management can all have potential impacts on grassland organic carbon stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Good grassland management can reverse historical soil carbon losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:6-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The relationship of improved biodiversity with carbon storage is subject of research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degradation">Degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grasslands are among the most threatened ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> According to the International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN), the most significant threat to grasslands is human land use, especially agriculture and mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The vulnerability of grasslands stems from a range of factors, such as misclassification, poor protection and cultivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:9-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use_intensification">Land use intensification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Land use intensification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Grasslands have an extensive history of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_activity" title="Human activity">human activity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">disturbance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs.usda.gov_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-fs.usda.gov-27">[27]</a></sup> To feed a growing human population, most of the world's grasslands are converted from natural landscapes to fields of corn, wheat or other crops. Grasslands that have remained largely intact thus far, like East African <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a>, are in danger of being lost to agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">[16]</a></sup> Grasslands are very sensitive to disturbances, such as people hunting and killing key species, or plowing the land to make more space for farms.
</p><p>Grassland vegetation is often a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plagioclimax" title="Plagioclimax">plagioclimax</a>; it remains dominant in a particular area usually due to <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a>, cutting, or natural or man-made fires, all discouraging colonization by and survival of tree and shrub <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedlings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Some of the world's largest expanses of grassland are found in the African savanna, and these are maintained by wild <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> as well as by nomadic <a href="/wiki/Pastoralism" title="Pastoralism">pastoralists</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, sheep or goats. Grasslands have an impact on climate change by slower decomposition rates of litter compared to forest environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg/300px-Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg/450px-Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg/600px-Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Main_land-cover_trajectories_from_the_1960s_to_2015.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main land-cover trajectories from the 1960s to 2015</div></div></div>
<p>Grasslands may occur naturally or as a result of human activity. Hunting cultures around the world often set regular fires to maintain and extend grasslands and prevent fire-intolerant trees and shrubs from taking hold. The tallgrass <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">U.S. Midwest</a> may have been extended eastward into <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> by human agency. Much grassland in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Europe" title="Northwest Europe">northwest Europe</a> developed after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_Period" title="Neolithic Period">Neolithic Period</a> when people gradually cleared the forest to create areas for raising their livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Grasslands often occur in areas with annual precipitation is between 600 mm (24 in) and 1,500 mm (59 in)  and average mean annual temperatures ranges from −5 and 20 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, some grasslands occur in colder (−20 °C) and hotter (30 °C) climatic conditions. Grassland can exist in habitats that are frequently disturbed by grazing or fire, as such disturbance prevents the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_encroachment" title="Bush encroachment">encroachment of woody species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Species richness is particularly high in grasslands of low <a href="/wiki/Soil_fertility" title="Soil fertility">soil fertility</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_barrens" title="Serpentine barrens">serpentine barrens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcareous" title="Calcareous">calcareous</a> grasslands, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_encroachment" title="Bush encroachment">woody encroachment</a> is prevented as low nutrient levels in the soil may inhibit the growth of forest and shrub species. Another common predicament often experienced by the ill-fated grassland creatures is the constant burning of plants, fueled by oxygen and many expired photosynthesizing organisms, with the lack of rain pushing this problem to further heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> When not limited by other factors, increasing CO<sub>2</sub> concentration in the air increases plant growth, similarly as water use efficiency, which is very important in drier regions. However, the advantages of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> are limited by factors including water availability and available <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>, particularly nitrogen. Thus effects of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> on plant growth will vary with local climate patterns, species adaptations to water limitations, and nitrogen availability. Studies indicate that nutrient depletion may happen faster in drier regions, and with factors like plant community composition and grazing. Nitrogen deposition from air pollutants and increased <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(soil_science)" title="Mineralization (soil science)">mineralization</a> from higher temperatures can increase plant productivity, but increases are often among a discount in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> as faster-growing plants outcompete others. A study of a California grassland found that global change may speed reductions in diversity and forb species are most prone to this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Afforestation_or_introduction_of_invasive_species">Afforestation or introduction of invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Afforestation or introduction of invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Misguided afforestation efforts, for example as part of the global effort to increase carbon sequestration, can harm grasslands and their core ecosystem services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Forest centric restoration efforts can create the risk of misreading and misclassifying of landscapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-:9-19">[19]</a></sup> A map created by the <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a> in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a> identifies 2 billion hectares for potential <a href="/wiki/Forest_restoration" title="Forest restoration">forest restoration</a>. It is criticised for including 900 million hectares of grasslands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It is expected that non-native grasses will continue to outperform native species under warmer and drier conditions that occur in many grasslands due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The type of land management used in grasslands can also lead to grassland loss/degradation. Many grasslands and other open ecosystems depend on <a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">disturbances</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Controlled_burn" title="Controlled burn">controlled burns</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> to persist, although this subject is still controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A study in Brazilian Subtropical Highland Grasslands found that grasslands without traditional land management—which uses fire every two years and extensive cattle grazing—can disappear within 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This study showed that grasslands inside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_areas" title="Protected areas">protected areas</a>, in which fire is not allowed and cattle grazing is banned, grasslands were quickly replaced by shrubs (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrub_encroachment" title="Shrub encroachment">shrub encroachment</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_degradation">Types of degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Types of degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_cover_change">Land cover change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Land cover change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Land cover has always changed during the years. The following relates to the changes between 1960 and 2015. There has been a decrease in semi-natural grasslands and an increase in areas with <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a>, forest and land used for <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> and buildings. The line style and relative thickness of the lines indicates the percentage of the total area that changed. Changes less than 1% and land-cover classes with all changes less than 1% (i.e. semi-natural wetlands and water) are not included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960 most of the land, 49.7%, was covered with <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> and there was also more semi-natural grassland (18.8%) than arable land (15.8%). In 2015 this has changed drastically. The forest cover has increased (50.8%) and arable land has also increased (20.4%), but the semi-natural grassland cover has decreased. Although it still covers a large area of the earth (10.6%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>A quarter of semi-natural grassland was lost through intensification, i.e. it was converted into arable or pasture land and forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>  It is more likely that intensification will occur in flat semi-natural grasslands, especially if the soil is fertile. On the other hand, grasslands, where the land is drought-prone or less productive, are more likely to persist as semi-natural grasslands than grasslands with fertile soil and low gradient of the terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Furthermore, the accessibility of the land is also important, as it is then easier to fertilize, for example. For instance, if it is located near a road. With the development of technology, it is becoming increasingly easy to cultivate land with a steeper gradient, to the detriment of grasslands. The management of grasslands is also changing permanently. There is increased use of mineral fertilizers, furthermore borders and field edges are removed to enlarge fields and leveling the terrain to facilitate the use of agricultural machinery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>
The professional study of dry grasslands falls under the category of <a href="/wiki/Rangeland_management" title="Rangeland management">rangeland management</a>, which focuses on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> associated with the grass-dominated arid and semi-arid rangelands of the world. Rangelands account for an estimated 70% of the earth's landmass; thus, many cultures including those of the United States are indebted to the economics that the world's grasslands have to offer, from producing grazing animals, tourism, ecosystems services such as clean water and air, and energy extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_encroachment" title="Bush encroachment">Bush encroachment</a></div>
<p>Vast areas of grassland are affected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_encroachment" title="Bush encroachment">woody encroachment</a>, which is the expansion of woody plants at the expense of the herbaceous layer. Woody encroachment is caused by a combination of human impact (e.g. fire exclusion, overstocking and resulting overgrazing) and environmental factors (i.e. increased CO<sub>2</sub> levels in the atmosphere). It can have severe negative consequences on key ecosystem services, like land productivity and groundwater recharge.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_and_restoration">Conservation and restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conservation and restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite growing recognition of the importance of grasslands, understanding of restoration options remains limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-44">[44]</a></sup> Cost of grassland retoration is highly variable and respective data is scarce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Successful grassland restoration has several dimensions, including recognition in policy, standardisation of indicators of degradation, scientific innovation, knowledge transfer and data sharing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Restoration methods and measures include the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>prescribed fires</li>
<li>appropriate management of livestock and wild herbivores: in light of land use intensification caused by global food demand, grassland land use practices may need to be adjusted to better support key ecosystem services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>tree cutting</li>
<li>shrub removal</li>
<li>invasive species control</li>
<li>reintroduction of native grasses and forbs via seeding or transplant: a main challenge for grassland restoration is how to overcome seed limitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-44">[44]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>For the period 2021–2030 the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> has proclaimed the UN Decade on Restoration, involving a joint resolution by over 70 countries. It is led by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_grasslands_types">General grasslands types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: General grasslands types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_and_subtropical">Tropical and subtropical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Tropical and subtropical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4><p>
These grasslands can be classified as the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_grasslands,_savannas,_and_shrublands" title="Tropical and subtropical grasslands, savannas, and shrublands">tropical and subtropical grasslands, savannas and shrublands biome</a>. The rainfall level for that grassland type is between 90 and 150 centimeters per year. Grasses and scattered trees are common for that ecoregion, as well as large <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Wildebeest" title="Wildebeest">wildebeest</a> (<i>Connochaetes taurinus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Zebra" title="Zebra">zebra</a> (<i>Equus zebra</i>). Notable tropical and subtropical grasslands include the <a href="/wiki/Llanos" title="Llanos">Llanos</a> grasslands of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Western_Sydney_(Badgerys_Creek)_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2209" data-file-width="3994" decoding="async" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Western_Sydney_%28Badgerys_Creek%29_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG/220px-Western_Sydney_%28Badgerys_Creek%29_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Western_Sydney_%28Badgerys_Creek%29_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG/330px-Western_Sydney_%28Badgerys_Creek%29_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Western_Sydney_%28Badgerys_Creek%29_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG/440px-Western_Sydney_%28Badgerys_Creek%29_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Western_Sydney_(Badgerys_Creek)_Airport_site_-_Longleys_Rd.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Plain_Woodland" title="Cumberland Plain Woodland">Cumberland Plain Woodland</a>, a grassy woodland that covers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Sydney" title="Western Sydney">Western Sydney</a></div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperate">Temperate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Temperate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Mid-latitude grasslands, including the <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairie</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_California_native_plants#Selected_grasses" title="List of California native plants">Pacific grasslands</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pampas" title="Pampas">Pampas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, calcareous <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. They are classified with temperate savannas and shrublands as the <a href="/wiki/Temperate_grasslands,_savannas,_and_shrublands" title="Temperate grasslands, savannas, and shrublands">temperate grasslands, savannas, and shrublands biome</a>. Temperate grasslands are the home to many large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivores" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">bison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">gazelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zebra" title="Zebra">zebras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">rhinoceroses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wild_horse" title="Wild horse">wild horses</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivores" title="Carnivores">Carnivores</a> like <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheetah" title="Cheetah">cheetahs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a> are also found in temperate grasslands. Other animals of this region include <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prairie_dog" title="Prairie dog">prairie dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_rabbit" title="Jack rabbit">jack rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a>, blackbirds, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meadowlark" title="Meadowlark">meadowlarks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_World_sparrow" title="Old World sparrow">sparrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quail" title="Quail">quails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyena" title="Hyena">hyenas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flooded">Flooded</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Flooded">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Grasslands that are flooded seasonally or year-round, like the <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a> of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pantanal" title="Pantanal">Pantanal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esteros_del_Ibera" title="Esteros del Ibera">Esteros del Ibera</a> in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, are classified with flooded savannas as the <a href="/wiki/Flooded_grasslands_and_savannas" title="Flooded grasslands and savannas">flooded grasslands and savannas biome</a> and occur mostly in the tropics and subtropics. The species that live in these grasslands are well adapted to the hydrologic regimes and soil conditions. The Everglades—the world's largest rain-fed flooded grassland—is rich in 11,000 species of seed-bearing plants, 25 species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchidaceae" title="Orchidaceae">orchids</a>, 300 <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> species, and 150 <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> species.
</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watermeadow" title="Watermeadow">Water-meadows</a> are grasslands that are deliberately flooded for short periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG/220px-AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG/330px-AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG/440px-AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AntelopeValleyCAgrassland.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grassland in the <a href="/wiki/Antelope_Valley" title="Antelope Valley">Antelope Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a></div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Montane">Montane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Montane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>High-altitude grasslands located on high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_ranges" title="Mountain ranges">mountain ranges</a> around the world, like the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1ramo" title="Páramo">Páramo</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andes_Mountains" title="Andes Mountains">Andes Mountains</a>. They are part of the <a href="/wiki/Montane_grasslands_and_shrublands" title="Montane grasslands and shrublands">montane grasslands and shrublands biome</a> and can be tropical, subtropical, and temperate. The plants and animals, that can be found in the tropical montane, are able to adapt to cool, wet conditions as well as intense sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tundra_grasslands">Tundra grasslands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Tundra grasslands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Similar to montane grasslands, polar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_tundra" title="Arctic tundra">Arctic tundra</a> can have grasses, but high soil moisture means that few tundras are grass-dominated today. However, during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_periods" title="Glacial periods">glacial periods</a> (commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>), a grassland known as steppe-tundra or <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_steppe" title="Mammoth steppe">mammoth steppe</a> occupied large areas of the Northern Hemisphere. These areas were very cold and arid and featured sub-surface <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> (hence tundra) but were nevertheless productive grassland ecosystems supporting a wide variety of fauna. As the temperature increased and the climate became wetter at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> much of the mammoth steppe transitioned to forest, while the drier parts in central Eurasia remained as a grassland, becoming the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasian_steppe" title="Eurasian steppe">Eurasian steppe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Desert_and_xeric">Desert and xeric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Desert and xeric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Also called desert grasslands, they are composed of sparse grassland ecoregions located in the <a href="/wiki/Deserts_and_xeric_shrublands" title="Deserts and xeric shrublands">deserts and xeric shrublands biome</a>. Temperature extremes and low amount of rainfall characterise these kinds of grasslands. Therefore, plants and animals are well adapted to minimize water loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
