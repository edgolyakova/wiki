<p>A <b>calendar</b> is a system of organizing days. This is done by giving names to periods of <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, typically <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a>, <a href="/wiki/Week" title="Week">weeks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">months</a> and <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">years</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">date</a> is the designation of a single, specific day within such a system. A calendar is also a physical record (often paper) of such a system. A calendar can also mean a list of planned events, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_calendar" title="Court calendar">court calendar</a> or a partly or fully chronological list of documents, such as a calendar of wills.
</p><p>Periods in a calendar (such as years and months) are usually, though not necessarily, synchronized with the cycle of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">sun</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">moon</a>. The most common type of pre-modern calendar was the <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar calendar</a>, a lunar calendar that occasionally adds one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercalary_month" title="Intercalary month">intercalary month</a> to remain synchronized with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_year" title="Solar year">solar year</a> over the long term.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>calendar</i> is taken from <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Calends" title="Calends">kalendae</a></i></span>, the term for the first day of the month in the Roman calendar, related to the verb <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">calare</i></span> 'to call out', referring to the "calling" of the new moon when it was first seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Latin <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">calendarium</i></span> meant 'account book, register' (as accounts were settled and debts were collected on the calends of each month). The Latin term was adopted in <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> as <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">calendier</i></span> and from there in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> as <span title="Middle English (1100-1500)-language text"><i lang="enm">calender</i></span> by the 13th century (the spelling <i>calendar</i> is early modern).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_calendars" title="History of calendars">History of calendars</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Week" title="Week">Week</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calendar_epoch" title="Calendar epoch">Calendar epoch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">Month</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">Lunisolar calendar</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">Computus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento,tramonto_fondachelli_fantina,_sicilia.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento%2Ctramonto_fondachelli_fantina%2C_sicilia.JPG/220px-Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento%2Ctramonto_fondachelli_fantina%2C_sicilia.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento%2Ctramonto_fondachelli_fantina%2C_sicilia.JPG/330px-Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento%2Ctramonto_fondachelli_fantina%2C_sicilia.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento%2Ctramonto_fondachelli_fantina%2C_sicilia.JPG/440px-Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento%2Ctramonto_fondachelli_fantina%2C_sicilia.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Equinozio_da_Pizzo_Vento,tramonto_fondachelli_fantina,_sicilia.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">Equinox</a> seen from the astronomic calendar of Pizzo Vento at <a href="/wiki/Fondachelli-Fantina" title="Fondachelli-Fantina">Fondachelli Fantina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a></div></div></div>
<p>The course of the sun and the moon are the most salient natural, regularly recurring events useful for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timekeeping" title="Timekeeping">timekeeping</a>, thus in pre-modern societies worldwide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">lunation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a> were most commonly used as time units. Nevertheless, the Roman calendar contained remnants of a very ancient pre-Etruscan 10-month solar year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first recorded physical calendars, dependent on the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_writing" title="Bronze Age writing">writing</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a>, are the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_calendar" title="Egyptian calendar">Egyptian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerian_calendar" title="Sumerian calendar">Sumerian</a> calendars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">Vedic India</a> developed a sophisticated time keeping methodology and calendars for <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedic</a> rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to Yukio Ohashi, the Vedanga calendar in ancient India was based on actual astronomical studies during the Vedic Period, and not derived from other cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A large number of Ancient Near East calendar systems based on the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_calendar" title="Babylonian calendar">Babylonian calendar</a> date from the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>, among them the calendar system of the Persian Empire, which in turn gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrian_calendar" title="Zoroastrian calendar">Zoroastrian calendar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A great number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenic_calendars" title="Hellenic calendars">Hellenic calendars</a> developed in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical Greece</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> gave rise to both the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">Roman calendar</a> and to various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu_calendars" title="Hindu calendars">Hindu calendars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calendars in antiquity were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunisolar" title="Lunisolar">lunisolar</a>, depending on the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercalary_month" title="Intercalary month">intercalary months</a> to align the solar and the lunar years. This was mostly based on observation, but there may have been early attempts to model the pattern of intercalation algorithmically, as evidenced in the fragmentary 2nd-century <a href="/wiki/Coligny_calendar" title="Coligny calendar">Coligny calendar</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">Roman calendar</a> was reformed by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> in 46 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Julian calendar was no longer dependent on the observation of the new moon but simply followed an algorithm of introducing a leap day every four years. This created a dissociation of the calendar <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">month</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">lunation</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic calendar</a> is based on the prohibition of intercalation (<i><a href="/wiki/Nasi%27" title="Nasi'">nasi'</a></i>) by <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>, in Islamic tradition dated to a sermon held on 9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhu_al-Hijjah" title="Dhu al-Hijjah">Dhu al-Hijjah</a> AH 10 (Julian date: 6 March 632). This resulted in an observation-based lunar calendar that shifts relative to the seasons of the solar year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_reforms">Modern reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Calendar_reform" title="Calendar reform">Calendar reform</a></div>
<p>The first calendar reform of the early modern era was the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, introduced in 1582 based on the observation of a long-term shift between the Julian calendar and the solar year.
</p><p>There have been several modern proposals for reform of the calendar, such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Calendar" title="World Calendar">World Calendar</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Fixed_Calendar" title="International Fixed Calendar">International Fixed Calendar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holocene_calendar" title="Holocene calendar">Holocene calendar</a>, and, recently, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanke-Henry_Permanent_Calendar" title="Hanke-Henry Permanent Calendar">Hanke-Henry Permanent Calendar</a>. Such ideas are mooted from time to time but have failed to gain traction because of the loss of continuity, massive upheaval in implementation, and religious objections.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1382" data-file-width="972" decoding="async" height="498" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg/350px-Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg/525px-Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg/700px-Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Universal_Calendar_including_a_meton_cycle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A so-called universal calendar, combining different calendars</div></div></div>
<p>A full calendar system has a different calendar date for every day. Thus the week cycle is by itself not a full calendar system; neither is a system to name the days within a year without a system for identifying the years.
</p><p>The simplest calendar system just counts time periods from a reference date. This applies for the <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">Julian day</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unix_Time" title="Unix Time">Unix Time</a>. Virtually the only possible variation is using a different reference date, in particular, one less distant in the past to make the numbers smaller. Computations in these systems are just a matter of addition and subtraction.
</p><p>Other calendars have one (or multiple) larger units of time.
</p><p>Calendars that contain one level of cycles:
</p>
<ul><li>week and weekday – this system (without year, the week number keeps on increasing) is not very common</li>
<li>year and ordinal date within the year, e.g., the <a href="/wiki/ISO_8601#Ordinal_dates" title="ISO 8601">ISO 8601 ordinal date system</a></li></ul>
<p>Calendars with two levels of cycles:
</p>
<ul><li>year, month, and day – most systems, including the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> (and its very similar predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic calendar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Hijri_calendar" title="Solar Hijri calendar">Solar Hijri calendar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a></li>
<li>year, week, and weekday – e.g., the <a href="/wiki/ISO_week_date" title="ISO week date">ISO week date</a></li></ul>
<p>Cycles can be synchronized with periodic phenomena:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:247px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="237" data-file-width="245" decoding="async" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg" width="245"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, Schedel's Nuremberg Chronicle, 1493</div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">Lunar calendars</a> are synchronized to the motion of the Moon (<a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phases</a>); an example is the Islamic calendar.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">Solar calendars</a> are based on perceived <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_year" title="Seasonal year">seasonal</a> changes synchronized to the apparent motion of the Sun; an example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_calendar" title="Persian calendar">Persian calendar</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">Lunisolar calendars</a> are based on a combination of both solar and lunar reckonings; examples include the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">traditional calendar of China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu calendar</a> in India and Nepal, and the Hebrew calendar.</li>
<li>The week cycle is an example of one that is not synchronized to any external phenomenon (although it may have been derived from lunar phases, beginning anew every month).</li></ul>
<p>Very commonly a calendar includes more than one type of cycle or has both cyclic and non-cyclic elements.
</p><p>Most calendars incorporate more complex cycles.   For example, the vast majority of them track years, months, weeks and days.   The seven-day week is practically universal, though its use varies. It has run uninterrupted for millennia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZerubavel1985_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZerubavel1985-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">Solar calendar</a></div>
<p>Solar calendars assign a <i>date</i> to each <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">solar day</a>. A day may consist of the period between <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>, with a following period of <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>, or it may be a period between successive events such as two sunsets. The length of the interval between two such successive events may be allowed to vary slightly during the year, or it may be averaged into a <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">mean solar day</a>. Other types of calendar may also use a solar day.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar">Lunar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lunar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">Lunar calendar</a></div>
<p>Not all calendars use the solar year as a unit. A lunar calendar is one in which days are numbered within each <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phase</a> cycle.  Because the length of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">lunar month</a> is not an even fraction of the length of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical year</a>, a purely lunar calendar quickly drifts against the seasons, which do not vary much near the equator.  It does, however, stay constant with respect to other phenomena, notably <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic calendar</a>.
Alexander Marshack, in a controversial reading,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> believed that marks on a bone baton (c. 25,000 BC) represented a lunar calendar. Other marked bones may also represent lunar calendars. Similarly, Michael Rappenglueck believes that marks on a 15,000-year-old cave painting represent a lunar calendar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunisolar">Lunisolar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lunisolar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar calendar</a> is a lunar calendar that compensates by adding an extra month as needed to realign the months with the seasons. Prominent examples of lunisolar calendar are <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu calendar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_calendar" title="Buddhist calendar">Buddhist calendar</a> that are popular in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. Another example is the Hebrew calendar, which uses a <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">19-year cycle</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">Subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Decade" title="Decade">Decade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Century" title="Century">Century</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">Millennium</a></div>
<p>Nearly all calendar systems group consecutive days into "months" and also into "years". In a <i>solar calendar</i> a <i>year</i> approximates Earth's tropical year (that is, the time it takes for a complete cycle of <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>), traditionally used to facilitate the planning of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> activities. In a <i>lunar calendar</i>, the <i>month</i> approximates the cycle of the moon phase. Consecutive days may be grouped into other periods such as the week.
</p><p>Because the number of days in the <i>tropical year</i> is not a whole number, a solar calendar must have a different number of days in different years. This may be handled, for example, by adding an extra day in <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">leap years</a>. The same applies to months in a lunar calendar and also the number of months in a year in a lunisolar calendar. This is generally known as <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">intercalation</a>. Even if a calendar is solar, but not lunar, the year cannot be divided entirely into months that never vary in length.
</p><p>Cultures may define other units of time, such as the week, for the purpose of scheduling regular activities that do not easily coincide with months or years. Many cultures use different baselines for their calendars' starting years. Historically, several countries have based their calendars on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regnal_years" title="Regnal years">regnal years</a>, a calendar based on the reign of their current sovereign. For example, the year 2006 in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> is year 18 Heisei, with Heisei being the era name of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Akihito</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types">Other types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arithmetical_and_astronomical">Arithmetical and astronomical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arithmetical and astronomical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1308" data-file-width="831" decoding="async" height="346" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg/220px-JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg/330px-JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg/440px-JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JudischerKalender-1831_ubt.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Calendar of the <a href="/wiki/Qahal" title="Qahal">Qahal</a>, 5591 (1831)</div></div></div>
<p>An <i>astronomical calendar</i> is based on ongoing observation; examples are the religious Islamic calendar and the old religious Jewish calendar in the time of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Temple" title="Second Temple">Second Temple</a>. Such a calendar is also referred to as an <i>observation-based</i> calendar. The advantage of such a calendar is that it is perfectly and perpetually accurate. The disadvantage is that working out when a particular date would occur is difficult.
</p><p>An <i>arithmetic calendar</i> is one that is based on a strict set of rules; an example is the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_calendar" title="Jewish calendar">Jewish calendar</a>. Such a calendar is also referred to as a <i>rule-based</i> calendar. The advantage of such a calendar is the ease of calculating when a particular date occurs. The disadvantage is imperfect accuracy. Furthermore, even if the calendar is very accurate, its accuracy diminishes slowly over time, owing to changes in Earth's rotation. This limits the lifetime of an accurate arithmetic calendar to a few thousand years. After then, the rules would need to be modified from observations made since the invention of the calendar.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complete_and_incomplete">Complete and incomplete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Complete and incomplete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Disputed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-disputed" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><img alt="System-search.svg" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="45" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/45px-System-search.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/68px-System-search.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/90px-System-search.svg.png 2x" width="45"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section's <b>factual accuracy is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">disputed</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on <a href="/wiki/Talk:Calendar#Dispute" title="Talk:Calendar">Talk:Calendar</a>. Please help to ensure that disputed statements are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources">reliably sourced</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2017</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Calendars may be either complete or incomplete. Complete calendars provide a way of naming each consecutive day, while incomplete calendars do not. The early <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">Roman calendar</a>, which had no way of designating the days of the winter months other than to lump them together as "winter", is an example of an incomplete calendar, while the Gregorian calendar is an example of a complete calendar.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:802px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_calendars_map.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="414" data-file-width="941" decoding="async" height="352" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/World_calendars_map.svg/800px-World_calendars_map.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/World_calendars_map.svg/1200px-World_calendars_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/World_calendars_map.svg/1600px-World_calendars_map.svg.png 2x" width="800"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_calendars_map.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Current usage of civil calendars in the world. Some countries use an additional calendar not listed here that determine holidays and other traditions, for example the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">Chinese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic</a> calendars.</div></div></div></div>
<p>The primary practical use of a calendar is to identify days: to be informed about or to agree on a future event and to record an event that has happened. Days may be significant for agricultural, civil, religious, or social reasons. For example, a calendar provides a way to determine when to start planting or harvesting, which days are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_holiday" title="Religious holiday">religious</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_holiday" title="Civil holiday">civil holidays</a>, which days mark the beginning and end of business accounting periods, and which days have legal significance, such as the day taxes are due or a contract expires. Also, a calendar may, by identifying a day, provide other useful information about the day such as its season.
</p><p>Calendars are also used to help people manage their personal schedules, time, and activities, particularly when individuals have numerous work, school, and family commitments. People frequently use multiple systems and may keep both a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> and <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> calendar to help prevent them from overcommitting their time.
</p><p>Calendars are also used as part of a complete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timekeeping" title="Timekeeping">timekeeping</a> system: date and time of day together specify a moment in <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>.  In the modern world, timekeepers can show time, date, and weekday. Some may also show the lunar phase.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gregorian">Gregorian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gregorian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> is the <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> international standard and is used almost everywhere in the world for civil purposes. The widely used  solar aspect is a cycle of leap days in a 400-year cycle designed to keep the duration of the year aligned with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_year" title="Solar year">solar year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003682–683_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003682–683-11">[11]</a></sup> There is a lunar aspect which approximates the position of the moon during the year, and is used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus"> calculation of the date of Easter</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003817–820_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003817–820-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Each Gregorian year has either 365 or 366 days (the leap day being inserted as 29 February), amounting to an average Gregorian year of 365.2425 days (compared to a solar year of 365.2422 days).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDershowitzReingold200847,_187_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDershowitzReingold200847,_187-13">[13]</a></sup> It was introduced in 1582 as a refinement to the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> which had been in use throughout the European Middle Ages, amounting to a 0.002% correction in the length of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003682–683_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003682–683-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Early Modern period, however, <a href="/wiki/Adoption_of_the_Gregorian_calendar" title="Adoption of the Gregorian calendar">its adoption was mostly limited</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> nations, but by the 19th century, it became widely adopted worldwide for the sake of convenience in international trade. The last European country to adopt the reform was Greece, in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003682–689_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003682–689-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calendar_epoch" title="Calendar epoch">calendar epoch</a> used by the Gregorian calendar is inherited from the medieval convention established by <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Exiguus" title="Dionysius Exiguus">Dionysius Exiguus</a> and associated with the Julian calendar. The year number is variously given as AD (for <i><a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">Anno Domini</a></i>) or CE (for <i><a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">Common Era</a></i> or <i>Christian Era</i>).<sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003Chapter:_"Christian_Chronology"_15-0'><a href='#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlackburnHolford-Strevens2003Chapter:_"Christian_Chronology"-15'>[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious">Religious</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Religious">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1731" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="595" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg/220px-Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg/330px-Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg/440px-Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hindu_calendar_1871-72.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Hindu <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">almanac</a> (<i>pancanga</i>) for the year 1871/2 from <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> (Library of Congress, Asian Division)</div></div></div>
<p>The most important use of pre-modern calendars is keeping track of the <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a> and the observation of religious feast days.
</p><p>While the Gregorian calendar is itself historically motivated to the calculation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_date" title="Easter date">Easter date</a>, it is now in worldwide secular use as the <i>de facto</i> standard. Alongside the use of the Gregorian calendar for secular matters, there remain several calendars in use for religious purposes.
</p><p>Western Christian liturgical calendars are based on the cycle of the Roman Rite of the Catholic Church and generally include the liturgical seasons of <a href="/wiki/Advent" title="Advent">Advent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, Ordinary Time (Time after <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Epiphany</a>), <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, and Ordinary Time (Time after <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>). Some Christian calendars do not include Ordinary Time and every day falls into a denominated season.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Christians" title="Eastern Christians">Eastern Christians</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church</a>, use the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic calendar</a> or Hijri calendar is a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendar</a> consisting of 12 lunar months in a year of 354 or 355 days. It is used to date events in most of the Muslim countries (concurrently with the Gregorian calendar) and used by Muslims everywhere to determine the proper day on which to celebrate Islamic holy days and festivals. Its epoch is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anno_Hegirae" title="Anno Hegirae">Hijra</a> (corresponding to AD 622)
With an annual drift of 11 or 12 days, the seasonal relation is repeated approximately every 33 Islamic years.
</p><p>Various <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu calendars</a> remain in use in the Indian subcontinent, including the <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Nepali_calendar_(disambiguation)" title="Nepali calendar (disambiguation)">Nepali calendars</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengali_calendar" title="Bengali calendar">Bengali calendar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malayalam_calendar" title="Malayalam calendar">Malayalam calendar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_calendar" title="Tamil calendar">Tamil calendar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vikrama_Samvat" title="Vikrama Samvat">Vikrama Samvat</a> used in Northern India, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shalivahana_era" title="Shalivahana era">Shalivahana</a> calendar in the <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Plateau" title="Deccan Plateau">Deccan</a> states.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_calendar" title="Buddhist calendar">Buddhist calendar</a> and the traditional lunisolar calendars of <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> are also based on an older version of the Hindu calendar.
</p><p>Most of the Hindu calendars are inherited from a system first enunciated in <a href="/wiki/Vedanga_Jyotisha" title="Vedanga Jyotisha">Vedanga Jyotisha</a> of Lagadha, standardized in the <i><a href="/wiki/Surya_Siddhanta" title="Surya Siddhanta">Sūrya Siddhānta</a></i> and subsequently reformed by astronomers such as <a href="/wiki/Aryabhata" title="Aryabhata">Āryabhaṭa</a> (AD 499), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varahamihira" title="Varahamihira">Varāhamihira</a> (6th century) and <a href="/wiki/Bh%C4%81skara_II" title="Bhāskara II">Bhāskara II</a> (12th century).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a> is used by <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> worldwide for religious and cultural affairs, also influences civil matters in Israel (such as <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">national holidays</a>) and can be used business dealings (such as for the dating of <a href="/wiki/Cheque" title="Cheque">cheques</a>).
</p><p>Followers of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%CA%BC%C3%AD_Faith" title="Baháʼí Faith">Baháʼí Faith</a> use the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%CA%BC%C3%AD_calendar" title="Baháʼí calendar">Baháʼí calendar</a>. The Baháʼí Calendar, also known as the Badi Calendar was first established by the Bab in the Kitab-i-Asma. The Baháʼí Calendar is also purely a solar calendar and comprises 19 months each having nineteen days.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National">National</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: National">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian</a> calendars are widely used for religious and social purposes.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_calendar" title="Iranian calendar">Iranian (Persian) calendar</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and some parts of <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_calendar" title="Assyrian calendar">Assyrian calendar</a> is in use by the members of the Assyrian community in the Middle East (mainly Iraq, Syria, Turkey, and Iran) and the diaspora. The first year of the calendar is exactly 4750 years prior to the start of the Gregorian calendar. The <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_calendar" title="Ethiopian calendar">Ethiopian calendar</a> or Ethiopic calendar is the principal calendar used in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oromo_calendar" title="Oromo calendar">Oromo calendar</a> also in use in some areas. In neighboring <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Somali_calendar" title="Somali calendar">Somali calendar</a> co-exists alongside the Gregorian and Islamic calendars. In <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Thai_solar_calendar" title="Thai solar calendar">Thai solar calendar</a> is used, the months and days have adopted the western standard, although the years are still based on the traditional <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_calendar" title="Buddhist calendar">Buddhist calendar</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiscal">Fiscal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Fiscal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiscal_calendar" title="Fiscal calendar">Fiscal calendar</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_(or_workshop)_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3780" data-file-width="6062" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_%28or_workshop%29_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg/220px-Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_%28or_workshop%29_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_%28or_workshop%29_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg/330px-Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_%28or_workshop%29_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_%28or_workshop%29_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg/440px-Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_%28or_workshop%29_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger_(or_workshop)_The_Payment_of_the_Tithes_Bonhams.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Payment of the Tithes</i> (The tax-collector), also known as <i>Village Lawyer</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger" title="Pieter Brueghel the Younger">Pieter Brueghel the Younger</a></div></div></div>
<p>A fiscal calendar generally means the accounting year of a government or a business. It is used for budgeting, keeping accounts, and taxation. It is a set of 12 months that may start at any date in a year. The US government's fiscal year starts on 1 October and ends on 30 September. The government of India's fiscal year starts on 1 April and ends on 31 March. Small traditional businesses in India start the fiscal year on <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a> festival and end the day before the next year's Diwali festival.
</p><p>In accounting (and particularly accounting software), a fiscal calendar (such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-4-5_Calendar" title="4-4-5 Calendar">4/4/5 calendar</a>) fixes each month at a specific number of weeks to facilitate comparisons from month to month and year to year. January always has exactly 4 weeks (Sunday through Saturday), February has 4 weeks, March has 5 weeks, etc. Note that this calendar will normally need to add a 53rd week to every 5th or 6th year, which might be added to December or might not be, depending on how the organization uses those dates. There exists an international standard way to do this (the <a href="/wiki/ISO_8601" title="ISO 8601">ISO week</a>). The ISO week starts on a Monday and ends on a Sunday. Week 1 is always the week that contains 4 January in the Gregorian calendar.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formats">Formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3728" data-file-width="2663" decoding="async" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg/220px-Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg/330px-Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg/440px-Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad_Co._Calendar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A calendar from the <a href="/wiki/Petaluma_and_Santa_Rosa_Railroad" title="Petaluma and Santa Rosa Railroad">Petaluma and Santa Rosa Railroad</a></div></div></div>
<p>The term <i>calendar</i> applies not only to a given scheme of timekeeping but also to a specific record or device displaying such a scheme, for example, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appointment_book" title="Appointment book">appointment book</a> in the form of a pocket calendar (or <a href="/wiki/Personal_organizer" title="Personal organizer">personal organizer</a>), desktop calendar, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_calendar" title="Wall calendar">wall calendar</a>, etc.
</p><p>In a paper calendar, one or two sheets can show a single day, a week, a month, or a year. If a sheet is for a single day, it easily shows the date and the weekday. If a sheet is for multiple days it shows a conversion table to convert from weekday to date and back. With a special pointing device, or by crossing out past days, it may indicate the current date and weekday. This is the most common usage of the word.
</p><p>In the US Sunday is considered the first day of the week and so appears on the far left and Saturday the last day of the week appearing on the far right. In Britain, the weekend may appear at the end of the week so the first day is Monday and the last day is Sunday. The US calendar display is also used in Britain.
</p><p>It is common to display the Gregorian calendar in separate monthly grids of seven columns (from Monday to Sunday, or Sunday to Saturday depending on which day is considered to start the week – this varies according to country) and five to six rows (or rarely, four rows when the month of February contains 28 days in <a href="/wiki/Common_year" title="Common year">common years</a> beginning on the first day of the week), with the day of the month numbered in each cell, beginning with 1.   The sixth row is sometimes eliminated by marking 23/30 and 24/31 together as necessary.
</p><p>When working with weeks rather than months, a continuous format is sometimes more convenient, where no blank cells are inserted to ensure that the first day of a new month begins on a fresh row.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Calendaring_software" title="Calendaring software">Calendaring software</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Category:Calendaring_standards" title="Category:Calendaring standards">Category:Calendaring standards</a></div>
<p>Calendaring software provides users with an electronic version of a calendar, and may additionally provide an appointment book, address book, or contact list.
Calendaring is a standard feature of many <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">PDAs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enterprise_digital_assistant" title="Enterprise digital assistant">EDAs</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smartphones" title="Smartphones">smartphones</a>. The software may be a local package designed for individual use (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lightning_(software)" title="Lightning (software)">Lightning</a> extension for <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird" title="Mozilla Thunderbird">Mozilla Thunderbird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Outlook" title="Microsoft Outlook">Microsoft Outlook</a> without Exchange Server, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_Calendar" title="Windows Calendar">Windows Calendar</a>) or maybe a networked package that allows for the sharing of information between users (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Sunbird" title="Mozilla Sunbird">Mozilla Sunbird</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_Live_Calendar" title="Windows Live Calendar">Windows Live Calendar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Calendar" title="Google Calendar">Google Calendar</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Outlook" title="Microsoft Outlook">Microsoft Outlook</a> with Exchange Server).
</p>
