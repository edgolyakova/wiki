<p><b>Algebra</b> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> <i> </i>‏<span dir="rtl" lang="ar"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%AC%D8%A8%D8%B1#Arabic" title="wikt:جبر">الجبر</a></span>‎<i> (<span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">al-jabr</i></span>)</i> 'reunion of broken parts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonesetter" title="Bonesetter">bonesetting</a>')<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_Press_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC_Press-2">[2]</a></sup> is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Areas_of_mathematics" title="Areas of mathematics">broad areas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. Roughly speaking, algebra is the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_symbol" title="Mathematical symbol">mathematical symbols</a> and the rules for manipulating these symbols in <a href="/wiki/Formula" title="Formula">formulas</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it is a unifying thread of almost all of mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Elementary_algebra" title="Elementary algebra">Elementary algebra</a> deals with the manipulation of <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> as if they were numbers and is therefore essential in all applications of mathematics. <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">Abstract algebra</a> is the name given, mostly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_education" title="Mathematical education">education</a>, to the study of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a> (the term is no more in common use outside educational context). <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">Linear algebra</a>, which deals with <a href="/wiki/Linear_equation" title="Linear equation">linear equations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_mapping" title="Linear mapping">linear mappings</a>, is used for modern presentations of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, and has many practical applications (in <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>, for example). There are many areas of mathematics that belong to algebra, some having "algebra" in their name, such as <a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">commutative algebra</a>, and some not, such as <a href="/wiki/Galois_theory" title="Galois theory">Galois theory</a>.
</p><p>The word <i>algebra</i> is not only used for naming an area of mathematics and some subareas; it is also used for naming some sorts of algebraic structures, such as an <a href="/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field" title="Algebra over a field">algebra over a field</a>, commonly called an <i>algebra</i>. Sometimes, the same phrase is used for a subarea and its main algebraic structures; for example, <a href="/wiki/Boolean_algebra" title="Boolean algebra">Boolean algebra</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(structure)" title="Boolean algebra (structure)">Boolean algebra</a>. A mathematician specialized in algebra is called an algebraist.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="666" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png/180px-Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png/270px-Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png/360px-Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The word <i>algebra</i> comes from the title of a book by <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Musa_al-Khwarizmi" title="Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi">Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>algebra</i> comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">الجبر</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>: </small><span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">al-jabr</i></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a> </small>'reunion of broken parts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonesetting" title="Bonesetting">bonesetting</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_Press_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC_Press-2">[2]</a></sup>' from the title of the early 9th century book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compendious_Book_on_Calculation_by_Completion_and_Balancing" title="The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing"><sup>c</sup>Ilm al-jabr wa l-muqābala</a></i> "The Science of Restoring and Balancing" by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persian</a> mathematician and astronomer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_al-Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB" title="Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī">al-Khwarizmi</a>. In his work, the term <i>al-jabr</i> referred to the operation of moving a term from one side of an equation to the other, المقابلة <i>al-muqābala</i> "balancing" referred to adding equal terms to both sides. Shortened to just <i>algeber</i> or <i>algebra</i> in Latin, the word eventually entered the English language during the 15th century, from either Spanish, Italian, or <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a>. It originally referred to the surgical procedure of setting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_bone" title="Broken bone">broken</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dislocated" title="Dislocated">dislocated bones</a>. The mathematical meaning was first recorded (in English) in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Different_meanings_of_.22algebra.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Different_meanings_of_"algebra"'>Different meanings of "algebra"</span></h2>
<p>The word "algebra" has several related meanings in mathematics, as a single word or with qualifiers.
</p>
<ul><li>As a single word without an article, "algebra" names a broad part of mathematics.</li>
<li>As a single word with an article or in the plural, "an algebra" or "algebras" denotes a specific mathematical structure, whose precise definition depends on the context. Usually, the structure has an addition, multiplication, and scalar multiplication (see <a href="/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field" title="Algebra over a field">Algebra over a field</a>). When some authors use the term "algebra", they make a subset of the following additional assumptions: <a href="/wiki/Associative_property" title="Associative property">associative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commutative_property" title="Commutative property">commutative</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unital_algebra" title="Unital algebra">unital</a>, and/or finite-dimensional. In <a href="/wiki/Universal_algebra" title="Universal algebra">universal algebra</a>, the word "algebra" refers to a generalization of the above concept, which allows for <a href="/wiki/Operation_(mathematics)" title="Operation (mathematics)">n-ary operations</a>.</li>
<li>With a qualifier, there is the same distinction:
<ul><li>Without an article, it means a part of algebra, such as <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elementary_algebra" title="Elementary algebra">elementary algebra</a> (the symbol-manipulation rules taught in elementary courses of mathematics as part of <a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">primary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a>), or <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a> (the study of the algebraic structures for themselves).</li>
<li>With an article, it means an instance of some algebraic structure, like a <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">Lie algebra</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Associative_algebra" title="Associative algebra">associative algebra</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Vertex_operator_algebra" title="Vertex operator algebra">vertex operator algebra</a>.</li>
<li>Sometimes both meanings exist for the same qualifier, as in the sentence: <i><a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">Commutative algebra</a> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Commutative_ring" title="Commutative ring">commutative rings</a>, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algebra_(ring_theory)" title="Algebra (ring theory)">commutative algebras</a> over the integers</i>.</li></ul></li></ul>

That is to say, to find all the solutions of the equation.
</p><p>Historically, and in current teaching, the study of algebra starts with the solving of equations, such as the quadratic equation above. Then more general questions, such as "does an equation have a solution?", "how many solutions does an equation have?", "what can be said about the nature of the solutions?" are considered. These questions led extending algebra to non-numerical objects, such as <a href="/wiki/Permutation" title="Permutation">permutations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(mathematics)" title="Vector (mathematics)">vectors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrices</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomials</a>. The structural properties of these non-numerical objects were then formalized into <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a>.
</p><p>Before the 16th century, mathematics was divided into only two subfields, <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>. Even though some methods, which had been developed much earlier, may be considered nowadays as algebra, the emergence of algebra and, soon thereafter, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infinitesimal_calculus" title="Infinitesimal calculus">infinitesimal calculus</a> as subfields of mathematics only dates from the 16th or 17th century. From the second half of the 19th century on, many new fields of mathematics appeared, most of which made use of both arithmetic and geometry, and almost all of which used algebra.
</p><p>Today, algebra has grown considerably and includes many branches of mathematics, as can be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_Subject_Classification" title="Mathematics Subject Classification">Mathematics Subject Classification</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
where none of the first level areas (two digit entries) are called <i>algebra</i>. Today algebra includes section 08-General algebraic systems, 12-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_theory_(mathematics)" title="Field theory (mathematics)">Field theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomials</a>, 13-<a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">Commutative algebra</a>, 15-<a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">Linear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Multilinear_algebra" title="Multilinear algebra">multilinear algebra</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matrix_theory" title="Matrix theory">matrix theory</a>, 16-<a href="/wiki/Associative_algebra" title="Associative algebra">Associative rings and algebras</a>, 17-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonassociative_ring" title="Nonassociative ring">Nonassociative rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-associative_algebra" title="Non-associative algebra">algebras</a>, 18-<a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">Category theory</a>; <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>, 19-<a href="/wiki/K-theory" title="K-theory">K-theory</a> and 20-<a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">Group theory</a>. Algebra is also used extensively in 11-<a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">Number theory</a> and 14-<a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">Algebraic geometry</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_algebra" title="History of algebra">History of algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra#History" title="Abstract algebra">Abstract algebra § History</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_algebra" title="Timeline of algebra">Timeline of algebra</a></div>
<p>The use of the word "algebra" for denoting a part of mathematics dates probably from the 16th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The word is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic word</a> <i>al-jabr</i> that appears in the title of the treatise <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Kitab_al-muhtasar_fi_hisab_al-gabr_wa-l-muqabala" title="Al-Kitab al-muhtasar fi hisab al-gabr wa-l-muqabala">Al-Kitab al-muhtasar fi hisab al-gabr wa-l-muqabala</a></i> (<i>The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing</i>), written circa 820 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Kwarizmi" title="Al-Kwarizmi">Al-Kwarizmi</a>.
</p><p><i>Al-jabr</i> referred to a method for transforming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equation_(mathematics)" title="Equation (mathematics)">equations</a> by subtracting <a href="/wiki/Like_terms" title="Like terms">like terms</a> from both sides, or passing one term from one side to the other, after changing its sign.
</p><p>Therefore, <i>algebra</i> referred originally to the manipulation of equations, and, by extension, to the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_equations" title="Theory of equations">theory of equations</a>. This is still what historians of mathematics generally mean by <i>algebra</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In mathematics, the meaning of <i>algebra</i> has evolved after the introduction by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te" title="François Viète">François Viète</a> of symbols (<a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a>) for denoting unknown or incompletely specified numbers, and the resulting use of the <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_notation" title="Mathematical notation">mathematical notation</a> for equations and <a href="/wiki/Formula" title="Formula">formulas</a>. So, algebra became essentially the study of the action of <a href="/wiki/Operation_(mathematics)" title="Operation (mathematics)">operations</a> on <a href="/wiki/Expression_(mathematics)" title="Expression (mathematics)">expressions</a> involving variables. This includes but is not limited to the theory of equations.
</p><p>At the beginning of the 20th century, algebra evolved further by considering operations that act not only on numbers but also on elements of so-called <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_structure" title="Mathematical structure">mathematical structures</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vector spaces</a>. This new algebra was called <i><a href="/wiki/Moderne_Algebra" title="Moderne Algebra">modern algebra</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waerden" title="Van der Waerden">van der Waerden</a> in his eponymous treatise, whose name has been changed to <i>Algebra</i> in later editions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Image-Al-Kit%C4%81b_al-mu%E1%B8%ABta%E1%B9%A3ar_f%C4%AB_%E1%B8%A5is%C4%81b_al-%C4%9Fabr_wa-l-muq%C4%81bala.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="240" decoding="async" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Image-Al-Kit%C4%81b_al-mu%E1%B8%ABta%E1%B9%A3ar_f%C4%AB_%E1%B8%A5is%C4%81b_al-%C4%9Fabr_wa-l-muq%C4%81bala.jpg/180px-Image-Al-Kit%C4%81b_al-mu%E1%B8%ABta%E1%B9%A3ar_f%C4%AB_%E1%B8%A5is%C4%81b_al-%C4%9Fabr_wa-l-muq%C4%81bala.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/23/Image-Al-Kit%C4%81b_al-mu%E1%B8%ABta%E1%B9%A3ar_f%C4%AB_%E1%B8%A5is%C4%81b_al-%C4%9Fabr_wa-l-muq%C4%81bala.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Image-Al-Kit%C4%81b_al-mu%E1%B8%ABta%E1%B9%A3ar_f%C4%AB_%E1%B8%A5is%C4%81b_al-%C4%9Fabr_wa-l-muq%C4%81bala.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A page from <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Musa_al-Khwarizmi" title="Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi">Al-Khwārizmī</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compendious_Book_on_Calculation_by_Completion_and_Balancing" title="The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing">al-Kitāb al-muḫtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-ğabr wa-l-muqābala</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>The roots of algebra can be traced to the ancient <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics" title="Babylonian mathematics">Babylonians</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> who developed an advanced arithmetical system with which they were able to do calculations in an <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithmic</a> fashion. The Babylonians developed formulas to calculate solutions for problems typically solved today by using <a href="/wiki/Linear_equation" title="Linear equation">linear equations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quadratic_equation" title="Quadratic equation">quadratic equations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indeterminate_equation" title="Indeterminate equation">indeterminate linear equations</a>. By contrast, most <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_mathematics" title="Ancient Egyptian mathematics">Egyptians</a> of this era, as well as <a href="/wiki/Greek_mathematics" title="Greek mathematics">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mathematics" title="Chinese mathematics">Chinese mathematics</a> in the 1st millennium BC, usually solved such equations by geometric methods, such as those described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus" title="Rhind Mathematical Papyrus">Rhind Mathematical Papyrus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid's Elements">Euclid's <i>Elements</i></a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nine_Chapters_on_the_Mathematical_Art" title="The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art">The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art</a></i>. The geometric work of the Greeks, typified in the <i>Elements</i>, provided the framework for generalizing formulae beyond the solution of particular problems into more general systems of stating and solving equations, although this would not be realized until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematics_in_medieval_Islam" title="Mathematics in medieval Islam">mathematics developed in medieval Islam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the time of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, Greek mathematics had undergone a drastic change. The Greeks created a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_geometric_algebra" title="Greek geometric algebra">geometric algebra</a> where terms were represented by sides of geometric objects, usually lines, that had letters associated with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citeboyer_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-citeboyer-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diophantus" title="Diophantus">Diophantus</a> (3rd century AD) was an <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> Greek mathematician and the author of a series of books called <i><a href="/wiki/Arithmetica" title="Arithmetica">Arithmetica</a></i>. These texts deal with solving <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_equation" title="Algebraic equation">algebraic equations</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and have led, in <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, to the modern notion of <a href="/wiki/Diophantine_equation" title="Diophantine equation">Diophantine equation</a>.
</p><p>Earlier traditions discussed above had a direct influence on the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Iran" title="History of Iran">Persian</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Musa_al-Khwarizmi" title="Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi">Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 780</span>–850). He later wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compendious_Book_on_Calculation_by_Completion_and_Balancing" title="The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing">The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing</a></i>, which established algebra as a mathematical discipline that is independent of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic</a> mathematicians <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> and Diophantus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Indian_mathematics" title="Indian mathematics">Indian mathematicians</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Brahmagupta" title="Brahmagupta">Brahmagupta</a>, continued the traditions of Egypt and Babylon, though Diophantus' <i>Arithmetica</i> and Brahmagupta's <i><a href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hmasphu%E1%B9%ADasiddh%C4%81nta" title="Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta">Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta</a></i> are on a higher level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (October 2017)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, the first complete arithmetic solution written in words instead of symbols,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> including zero and negative solutions, to quadratic equations was described by Brahmagupta in his book <i>Brahmasphutasiddhanta,</i> published in 628 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley-16">[16]</a></sup> Later, Persian and <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arab</a> mathematicians developed algebraic methods to a much higher degree of sophistication. Although Diophantus and the Babylonians used mostly special <i>ad hoc</i> methods to solve equations, Al-Khwarizmi's contribution was fundamental. He solved linear and quadratic equations without algebraic symbolism, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_numbers" title="Negative numbers">negative numbers</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero" title="Zero">zero</a>, thus he had to distinguish several types of equations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meri2004_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meri2004-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the context where algebra is identified with the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_equations" title="Theory of equations">theory of equations</a>, the Greek mathematician Diophantus has traditionally been known as the "father of algebra" and in the context where it is identified with rules for manipulating and solving equations, Persian mathematician al-Khwarizmi is regarded as "the father of algebra".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandz_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gandz-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It is open to debate whether Diophantus or al-Khwarizmi is more entitled to be known, in the general sense, as "the father of algebra". Those who support Diophantus point to the fact that the algebra found in <i>Al-Jabr</i> is slightly more elementary than the algebra found in <i>Arithmetica</i> and that <i>Arithmetica</i> is syncopated while <i>Al-Jabr</i> is fully rhetorical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Those who support Al-Khwarizmi point to the fact that he introduced the methods of "<a href="/wiki/Reduction_(mathematics)" title="Reduction (mathematics)">reduction</a>" and "balancing" (the transposition of subtracted terms to the other side of an equation, that is, the cancellation of <a href="/wiki/Like_terms" title="Like terms">like terms</a> on opposite sides of the equation) which the term <i>al-jabr</i> originally referred to,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer-229_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer-229-26">[26]</a></sup> and that he gave an exhaustive explanation of solving quadratic equations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> supported by geometric proofs while treating algebra as an independent discipline in its own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandz_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gandz-22">[22]</a></sup> His algebra was also no longer concerned "with a series of problems to be resolved, but an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expository_writing" title="Expository writing">exposition</a> which starts with primitive terms in which the combinations must give all possible prototypes for equations, which henceforward explicitly constitute the true object of study". He also studied an equation for its own sake and "in a generic manner, insofar as it does not simply emerge in the course of solving a problem, but is specifically called on to define an infinite class of problems".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rashed-Armstrong_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rashed-Armstrong-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another Persian mathematician <a href="/wiki/Omar_Khayyam" title="Omar Khayyam">Omar Khayyam</a> is credited with identifying the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a> and found the general geometric solution of the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_equation" title="Cubic equation">cubic equation</a>. His book <i>Treatise on Demonstrations of Problems of Algebra</i> (1070), which laid down the principles of algebra, is part of the body of Persian mathematics that was eventually transmitted to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Yet another Persian mathematician, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharaf_al-D%C4%ABn_al-T%C5%ABs%C4%AB" title="Sharaf al-Dīn al-Tūsī">Sharaf al-Dīn al-Tūsī</a>, found algebraic and numerical solutions to various cases of cubic equations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He also developed the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Indian mathematicians <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahavira_(mathematician)" title="Mahavira (mathematician)">Mahavira</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhaskara_II" title="Bhaskara II">Bhaskara II</a>, the Persian mathematician <a href="/wiki/Al-Karaji" title="Al-Karaji">Al-Karaji</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyer_al-Karkhi_ax2n_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boyer_al-Karkhi_ax2n-32">[32]</a></sup> and the Chinese mathematician <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Shijie" title="Zhu Shijie">Zhu Shijie</a>, solved various cases of cubic, <a href="/wiki/Quartic_equation" title="Quartic equation">quartic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quintic_equation" title="Quintic equation">quintic</a> and higher-order <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomial</a> equations using numerical methods. In the 13th century, the solution of a cubic equation by <a href="/wiki/Fibonacci" title="Fibonacci">Fibonacci</a> is representative of the beginning of a revival in European algebra. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ab%C5%AB_al-%E1%B8%A4asan_ibn_%CA%BFAl%C4%AB_al-Qala%E1%B9%A3%C4%81d%C4%AB" title="Abū al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAlī al-Qalaṣādī">Abū al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAlī al-Qalaṣādī</a> (1412–1486) took "the first steps toward the introduction of algebraic symbolism". He also computed Σ<i>n</i><sup>2</sup>, Σ<i>n</i><sup>3</sup> and used the method of successive approximation to determine square roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gerolamo_Cardano_(colour).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="698" data-file-width="541" decoding="async" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Gerolamo_Cardano_%28colour%29.jpg/180px-Gerolamo_Cardano_%28colour%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Gerolamo_Cardano_%28colour%29.jpg/270px-Gerolamo_Cardano_%28colour%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Gerolamo_Cardano_%28colour%29.jpg/360px-Gerolamo_Cardano_%28colour%29.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gerolamo_Cardano_(colour).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Italian mathematician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girolamo_Cardano" title="Girolamo Cardano">Girolamo Cardano</a> published the solutions to the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_equation" title="Cubic equation">cubic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quartic_equation" title="Quartic equation">quartic equations</a> in his 1545 book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ars_Magna_(Gerolamo_Cardano)" title="Ars Magna (Gerolamo Cardano)">Ars magna</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te" title="François Viète">François Viète</a>'s work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_algebra" title="New algebra">new algebra</a> at the close of the 16th century was an important step towards modern algebra. In 1637, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/La_G%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie" title="La Géométrie">La Géométrie</a></i>, inventing <a href="/wiki/Analytic_geometry" title="Analytic geometry">analytic geometry</a> and introducing modern algebraic notation. Another key event in the further development of algebra was the general algebraic solution of the cubic and quartic equations, developed in the mid-16th century. The idea of a <a href="/wiki/Determinant" title="Determinant">determinant</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Japanese_mathematics" title="Japanese mathematics">Japanese mathematician</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seki_K%C5%8Dwa" title="Seki Kōwa">Seki Kōwa</a> in the 17th century, followed independently by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a> ten years later, for the purpose of solving systems of simultaneous linear equations using <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrices</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Cramer" title="Gabriel Cramer">Gabriel Cramer</a> also did some work on matrices and determinants in the 18th century. Permutations were studied by <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph-Louis Lagrange">Joseph-Louis Lagrange</a> in his 1770 paper "<i>Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations</i><span style="padding-left:.15em;">"</span> devoted to solutions of algebraic equations, in which he introduced <a href="/wiki/Resolvent_(Galois_theory)" title="Resolvent (Galois theory)">Lagrange resolvents</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Ruffini" title="Paolo Ruffini">Paolo Ruffini</a> was the first person to develop the theory of <a href="/wiki/Permutation_group" title="Permutation group">permutation groups</a>, and like his predecessors, also in the context of solving algebraic equations.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">Abstract algebra</a> was developed in the 19th century, deriving from the interest in solving equations, initially focusing on what is now called <a href="/wiki/Galois_theory" title="Galois theory">Galois theory</a>, and on <a href="/wiki/Constructible_number" title="Constructible number">constructibility</a> issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Peacock" title="George Peacock">George Peacock</a> was the founder of axiomatic thinking in arithmetic and algebra. <a href="/wiki/Augustus_De_Morgan" title="Augustus De Morgan">Augustus De Morgan</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Relation_algebra" title="Relation algebra">relation algebra</a> in his <i>Syllabus of a Proposed System of Logic</i>. <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs" title="Josiah Willard Gibbs">Josiah Willard Gibbs</a> developed an algebra of vectors in three-dimensional space, and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Cayley" title="Arthur Cayley">Arthur Cayley</a> developed an algebra of matrices (this is a noncommutative algebra).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Some subareas of algebra have the word algebra in their name; <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a> is one example. Others do not: <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">ring theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field theory</a> are examples. In this section, we list some areas of mathematics with the word "algebra" in the name.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Elementary_algebra" title="Elementary algebra">Elementary algebra</a>, the part of algebra that is usually taught in elementary courses of mathematics.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">Abstract algebra</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axiomatization" title="Axiomatization">axiomatically</a> defined and investigated.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Boolean_algebra" title="Boolean algebra">Boolean algebra</a>, a branch of algebra abstracting the computation with the <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth values</a> <i>false</i> and <i>true</i>.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">Homological algebra</a>, the study of algebraic structures that are fundamental to study <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological spaces</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Universal_algebra" title="Universal algebra">Universal algebra</a>, in which properties common to all algebraic structures are studied.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory" title="Algebraic number theory">Algebraic number theory</a>, in which the properties of numbers are studied from an algebraic point of view.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">Algebraic geometry</a>, a branch of geometry, in its primitive form specifying curves and surfaces as solutions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynomial_equation" title="Polynomial equation">polynomial equations</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics" title="Algebraic combinatorics">Algebraic combinatorics</a>, in which algebraic methods are used to study combinatorial questions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Relational_algebra" title="Relational algebra">Relational algebra</a>: a set of <a href="/wiki/Finitary_relation" title="Finitary relation">finitary relations</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">closed</a> under certain operators.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elementary_algebra">Elementary algebra</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elementary_algebra" title="Elementary algebra">Elementary algebra</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Algebraic_equation_notation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="516" data-file-width="883" decoding="async" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Algebraic_equation_notation.svg/220px-Algebraic_equation_notation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Algebraic_equation_notation.svg/330px-Algebraic_equation_notation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Algebraic_equation_notation.svg/440px-Algebraic_equation_notation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Algebraic_equation_notation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Algebraic expression notation:<br/>  1 – power (exponent)<br/>  2 – coefficient<br/>  3 – term<br/>  4 – operator<br/>  5 – constant term<br/>  <i>x</i> <i>y</i> <i>c</i> – variables/constants</div></div></div>
<p>Elementary algebra is the most basic form of algebra. It is taught to students who are presumed to have no knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> beyond the basic principles of <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a>. In arithmetic, only <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a> and their arithmetical operations (such as +, −, ×, ÷) occur. In algebra, numbers are often represented by symbols called <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> (such as <i>a</i>, <i>n</i>, <i>x</i>, <i>y</i> or <i>z</i>). This is useful because:
</p>
<ul><li>It allows the general formulation of arithmetical laws (such as <i>a</i> + <i>b</i> = <i>b</i> + <i>a</i> for all <i>a</i> and <i>b</i>), and thus is the first step to a systematic exploration of the properties of the <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real number system</a>.</li>
<li>It allows the reference to "unknown" numbers, the formulation of <a href="/wiki/Equation" title="Equation">equations</a> and the study of how to solve these. (For instance, "Find a number <i>x</i> such that 3<i>x</i> + 1 = 10" or going a bit further "Find a number <i>x</i> such that <i>ax</i> + <i>b</i> = <i>c</i>". This step leads to the conclusion that it is not the nature of the specific numbers that allow us to solve it, but that of the operations involved.)</li>
<li>It allows the formulation of <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">functional</a> relationships. (For instance, "If you sell <i>x</i> tickets, then your profit will be 3<i>x</i> − 10 dollars, or <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>) = 3<i>x</i> − 10, where <i>f</i> is the function, and <i>x</i> is the number to which the function is applied".)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polynomials">Polynomials</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">Polynomial</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polynomialdeg3.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Polynomialdeg3.svg/180px-Polynomialdeg3.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Polynomialdeg3.svg/270px-Polynomialdeg3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Polynomialdeg3.svg/360px-Polynomialdeg3.svg.png 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polynomialdeg3.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Graph_of_a_function" title="Graph of a function">graph</a> of a polynomial function of degree 3</div></div></div>
<p>A polynomial is an <a href="/wiki/Expression_(mathematics)" title="Expression (mathematics)">expression</a> that is the sum of a finite number of non-zero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summand" title="Summand">terms</a>, each term consisting of the product of a constant and a finite number of <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> raised to whole number powers. For example, <i>x</i><sup>2</sup> + 2<i>x</i> − 3 is a polynomial in the single variable <i>x</i>. A polynomial expression is an expression that may be rewritten as a polynomial, by using commutativity, associativity and distributivity of addition and multiplication. For example, (<i>x</i> − 1)(<i>x</i> + 3) is a polynomial expression, that, properly speaking, is not a polynomial. A polynomial function is a function that is defined by a polynomial, or, equivalently, by a polynomial expression. The two preceding examples define the same polynomial function.
</p><p>Two important and related problems in algebra are the <a href="/wiki/Factorization_of_polynomials" title="Factorization of polynomials">factorization of polynomials</a>, that is, expressing a given polynomial as a product of other polynomials that cannot be factored any further, and the computation of <a href="/wiki/Polynomial_greatest_common_divisor" title="Polynomial greatest common divisor">polynomial greatest common divisors</a>. The example polynomial above can be factored as (<i>x</i> − 1)(<i>x</i> + 3). A related class of problems is finding algebraic expressions for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_of_a_function" title="Root of a function">roots</a> of a polynomial in a single variable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_education" title="Mathematics education">Mathematics education</a></div>
<p>It has been suggested that elementary algebra should be taught to students as young as eleven years old,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> though in recent years it is more common for public lessons to begin at the eighth grade level (≈ 13 y.o. ±) in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, in some US schools, algebra instruction starts in ninth grade.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abstract_algebra">Abstract algebra</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">Abstract algebra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">Algebraic structure</a></div>
<p>Abstract algebra extends the familiar concepts found in elementary algebra and <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a> to more general concepts. Here are the listed fundamental concepts in abstract algebra.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">Sets</a>: Rather than just considering the different types of <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a>, abstract algebra deals with the more general concept of <i>sets</i>: collections of objects called <a href="/wiki/Element_(mathematics)" title="Element (mathematics)">elements</a>. All collections of the familiar types of numbers are sets. Other examples of sets include the set of all two-by-two <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrices</a>, the set of all second-degree <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynomials" title="Polynomials">polynomials</a> (<i>ax</i><sup>2</sup> + <i>bx</i> + <i>c</i>), the set of all two dimensional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometric)" title="Vector (geometric)">vectors</a> of a plane, and the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_groups" title="Finite groups">finite groups</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_group" title="Cyclic group">cyclic groups</a>, which are the groups of integers <a href="/wiki/Modular_arithmetic" title="Modular arithmetic">modulo</a> <i>n</i>. <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">Set theory</a> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> and not technically a branch of algebra.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Binary_operation" title="Binary operation">Binary operations</a>: The notion of <a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">addition</a> (+) is generalized to the notion of <i>binary operation</i> (denoted here by ∗). The notion of binary operation is meaningless without the set on which the operation is defined. For two elements <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> in a set <i>S</i>, <i>a</i> ∗ <i>b</i> is another element in the set; this condition is called <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">closure</a>. <a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">Addition</a> (+), <a href="/wiki/Subtraction" title="Subtraction">subtraction</a> (−), <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">multiplication</a> (×), and <a href="/wiki/Division_(mathematics)" title="Division (mathematics)">division</a> (÷) can be binary operations when defined on different sets, as are addition and multiplication of matrices, vectors, and polynomials.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Identity_element" title="Identity element">Identity elements</a>: The numbers zero and one are generalized to give the notion of an <i>identity element</i> for an operation. Zero is the identity element for addition and one is the identity element for multiplication. For a general binary operator ∗ the identity element <i>e</i> must satisfy <i>a</i> ∗ <i>e</i> = <i>a</i> and <i>e</i> ∗ <i>a</i> = <i>a</i>, and is necessarily unique, if it exists. This holds for addition as <i>a</i> + 0 = <i>a</i> and 0 + <i>a</i> = <i>a</i> and multiplication <i>a</i> × 1 = <i>a</i> and 1 × <i>a</i> = <i>a</i>. Not all sets and operator combinations have an identity element; for example, the set of positive natural numbers (1, 2, 3, ...) has no identity element for addition.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_elements" title="Inverse elements">Inverse elements</a>: The negative numbers give rise to the concept of <i>inverse elements</i>. For addition, the inverse of <i>a</i> is written −<i>a</i>, and for multiplication the inverse is written <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup>. A general two-sided inverse element <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup> satisfies the property that <i>a</i> ∗ <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup> = <i>e</i> and <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup> ∗ <i>a</i> = <i>e</i>, where <i>e</i> is the identity element.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associativity" title="Associativity">Associativity</a>: Addition of integers has a property called associativity. That is, the grouping of the numbers to be added does not affect the sum. For example: <span class="nowrap">(2 + 3) + 4 = 2 + (3 + 4)</span>. In general, this becomes (<i>a</i> ∗ <i>b</i>) ∗ <i>c</i> = <i>a</i> ∗ (<i>b</i> ∗ <i>c</i>). This property is shared by most binary operations, but not subtraction or division or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octonion_multiplication" title="Octonion multiplication">octonion multiplication</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutative_operation" title="Commutative operation">Commutativity</a>: Addition and multiplication of real numbers are both commutative. That is, the order of the numbers does not affect the result. For example: 2 + 3 = 3 + 2. In general, this becomes <i>a</i> ∗ <i>b</i> = <i>b</i> ∗ <i>a</i>. This property does not hold for all binary operations. For example, <a href="/wiki/Matrix_multiplication" title="Matrix multiplication">matrix multiplication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quaternion" title="Quaternion">quaternion multiplication</a> are both non-commutative.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groups">Groups</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">Group (mathematics)</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">Group theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Examples_of_groups" title="Examples of groups">Examples of groups</a></div>
<p>Combining the above concepts gives one of the most important structures in mathematics: a <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a>. A group is a combination of a set <i>S</i> and a single <a href="/wiki/Binary_operation" title="Binary operation">binary operation</a> ∗, defined in any way you choose, but with the following properties:
</p>
<ul><li>An identity element <i>e</i> exists, such that for every member <i>a</i> of <i>S</i>, <i>e</i> ∗ <i>a</i> and <i>a</i> ∗ <i>e</i> are both identical to <i>a</i>.</li>
<li>Every element has an inverse: for every member <i>a</i> of <i>S</i>, there exists a member <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup> such that <i>a</i> ∗ <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup> and <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup> ∗ <i>a</i> are both identical to the identity element.</li>
<li>The operation is associative: if <i>a</i>, <i>b</i> and <i>c</i> are members of <i>S</i>, then (<i>a</i> ∗ <i>b</i>) ∗ <i>c</i> is identical to <i>a</i> ∗ (<i>b</i> ∗ <i>c</i>).</li></ul>
<p>If a group is also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutativity" title="Commutativity">commutative</a> – that is, for any two members <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> of <i>S</i>, <i>a</i> ∗ <i>b</i> is identical to <i>b</i> ∗ <i>a</i> – then the group is said to be <a href="/wiki/Abelian_group" title="Abelian group">abelian</a>.
</p><p>For example, the set of integers under the operation of addition is a group. In this group, the identity element is 0 and the inverse of any element <i>a</i> is its negation, −<i>a</i>. The associativity requirement is met, because for any integers <i>a</i>, <i>b</i> and <i>c</i>, (<i>a</i> + <i>b</i>) + <i>c</i> = <i>a</i> + (<i>b</i> + <i>c</i>)
</p><p>The non-zero <a href="/wiki/Rational_number" title="Rational number">rational numbers</a> form a group under multiplication. Here, the identity element is 1, since 1 × <i>a</i> = <i>a</i> × 1 = <i>a</i> for any rational number <i>a</i>. The inverse of <i>a</i> is <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1050945101">.mw-parser-output .sfrac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .sfrac.tion,.mw-parser-output .sfrac .tion{display:inline-block;vertical-align:-0.5em;font-size:85%;text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .sfrac .num,.mw-parser-output .sfrac .den{display:block;line-height:1em;margin:0 0.1em}.mw-parser-output .sfrac .den{border-top:1px solid}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="sfrac tion" role="math"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den"><i>a</i></span></span>, since <i>a</i> × <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac tion" role="math"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den"><i>a</i></span></span> = 1.
</p><p>The integers under the multiplication operation, however, do not form a group. This is because, in general, the multiplicative inverse of an integer is not an integer. For example, 4 is an integer, but its multiplicative inverse is <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac tion" role="math"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">4</span></span>, which is not an integer.
</p><p>The theory of groups is studied in <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a>. A major result of this theory is the <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups" title="Classification of finite simple groups">classification of finite simple groups</a>, mostly published between about 1955 and 1983, which separates the <a href="/wiki/Finite_set" title="Finite set">finite</a> <a href="/wiki/Simple_group" title="Simple group">simple groups</a> into roughly 30 basic types.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-group" title="Semi-group">Semi-groups</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasi-group" title="Quasi-group">quasi-groups</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monoid" title="Monoid">monoids</a> are <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_structure" title="Algebraic structure">algebraic structures</a> similar to groups, but with less constraints on the operation. They comprise a set and a closed binary operation but do not necessarily satisfy the other conditions. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-group" title="Semi-group">semi-group</a> has an <i>associative</i> binary operation but might not have an identity element. A <a href="/wiki/Monoid" title="Monoid">monoid</a> is a semi-group which does have an identity but might not have an inverse for every element. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasi-group" title="Quasi-group">quasi-group</a> satisfies a requirement that any element can be turned into any other by either a unique left-multiplication or right-multiplication; however, the binary operation might not be associative.
</p><p>All groups are monoids, and all monoids are semi-groups.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rings_and_fields">Rings and fields</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">Ring (mathematics)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">Field (mathematics)</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">Ring theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ring_theory" title="Glossary of ring theory">Glossary of ring theory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_theory_(mathematics)" title="Field theory (mathematics)">Field theory (mathematics)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory" title="Glossary of field theory">Glossary of field theory</a></div>
<p>Groups just have one binary operation. To fully explain the behaviour of the different types of numbers, structures with two operators need to be studied. The most important of these are <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">fields</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Ring_(mathematics)" title="Ring (mathematics)">ring</a> has two binary operations (+) and (×), with × distributive over +. Under the first operator (+) it forms an <i>abelian group</i>. Under the second operator (×) it is associative, but it does not need to have an identity, or inverse, so division is not required. The additive (+) identity element is written as 0 and the additive inverse of <i>a</i> is written as −<i>a</i>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distributivity" title="Distributivity">Distributivity</a> generalises the <i>distributive law</i> for numbers. For the integers <span class="nowrap">(<i>a</i> + <i>b</i>) × <i>c</i> = <i>a</i> × <i>c</i> + <i>b</i> × <i>c</i></span> and <span class="nowrap"><i>c</i> × (<i>a</i> + <i>b</i>) = <i>c</i> × <i>a</i> + <i>c</i> × <i>b</i>,</span> and × is said to be <i>distributive</i> over +.
</p><p>The integers are an example of a ring. The integers have additional properties which make it an <a href="/wiki/Integral_domain" title="Integral domain">integral domain</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field</a> is a <i>ring</i> with the additional property that all the elements excluding 0 form an <i>abelian group</i> under ×. The multiplicative (×) identity is written as 1 and the multiplicative inverse of <i>a</i> is written as <i>a</i><sup>−1</sup>.
</p><p>The rational numbers, the real numbers and the complex numbers are all examples of fields.
</p>
