<p><b>Human migration</b> is the movement of people from one place to another with intentions of settling, permanently or temporarily, at a new location (geographic region). The movement often occurs over long distances and from one <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> to another (external migration), but <a href="/wiki/Internal_migration" title="Internal migration">internal migration</a> (within a single country) is also possible; indeed, this is the dominant form of human migration globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Migration is often associated with better human capital at both individual and household level, and with better access to migration networks, facilitating a possible second move. Age is also important for both work and non-work migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> People may migrate as individuals, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_unit" title="Family unit">family units</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Mass_migration" title="Mass migration">large groups</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There are four major forms of migration: <a href="/wiki/Invasion" title="Invasion">invasion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conquest" title="Conquest">conquest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a>/<a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Persons moving from their home due to <a href="/wiki/Forced_displacement" title="Forced displacement">forced displacement</a> (such as a natural disaster or civil disturbance) may be described as displaced persons or, if remaining in the home country, <a href="/wiki/Internally_displaced_person" title="Internally displaced person">internally-displaced persons</a>. A person who seeks refuge in another country can, if the reason for leaving the home country is political, religious, or another form of persecution, make a formal application to that country where refuge is sought and is then usually described as an <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seeker" title="Asylum seeker">asylum seeker</a>. If this application is successful, this person's legal status becomes <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugee</a>.
</p><p>In contemporary times,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> migration governance has become closely associated with state sovereignty. States retain the power of deciding on the entry and stay of non-nationals because migration directly affects some of the defining elements of a State.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="827" data-file-width="1257" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg/220px-Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg/330px-Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg/440px-Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niger_highway_overloaded_camion_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Niger highway overloaded camion 2007</div></div></div>
<p>Depending on the goal and reason for relocation, people who migrate can be divided into three categories: migrants, refugees, and asylum seekers. Each category is defined broadly as the mixed circumstances that motivate a person to change their location.
</p><p>As such, <i>migrants</i> are traditionally described as persons who change the country of their residence for general reasons and purposes. These purposes may include the search for better job opportunities or healthcare needs. This term is the most generally defined one as anyone changing their geographic location permanently can be considered a migrant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Contrastly, <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee"><i>refugees</i></a> is not defined and described as persons who do not willingly relocate. The reasons for the refugees' migration usually involve war actions within the country or other forms of oppression, coming either from the government or non-governmental sources. Refugees are usually associated with people who must unwillingly relocate as fast as possible; hence, such migrants will likely relocate undocumented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asylum_seekers" title="Asylum seekers">Asylum seekers</a> are associated with persons who also leave their country unwillingly, yet, who also do not do so under oppressing circumstances such as war or death threats. The motivation to leave the country for asylum seekers might involve an unstable economic or political situation or high <a href="/wiki/Crime_statistics" title="Crime statistics">rates of crime</a>. Thus, asylum seekers relocate predominantly to escape the degradation of the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of their lives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">Nomadic</a> movements usually are not regarded as migrations, as the movement is generally <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_human_migration" title="Seasonal human migration">seasonal</a>, there is no intention to settle in the new place, and only a few people have retained this form of lifestyle in modern times. Temporary movement for travel, tourism, pilgrimages, or the commute is also not regarded as migration, in the absence of an intention to live and settle in the visited places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_patterns_and_related_numbers">Migration patterns and related numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Migration patterns and related numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There exist many statistical estimates of worldwide migration patterns.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> has published three editions of its <i>Migration and Remittances Factbook</i>, beginning in 2008, with a second edition appearing in 2011 and a third in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Organisation_for_Migration" title="International Organisation for Migration">International Organisation for Migration</a> (IOM) has published ten editions of the <i><a href="/wiki/World_Migration_Report" title="World Migration Report">World Migration Report</a></i> since 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Statistics_Division" title="United Nations Statistics Division">United Nations Statistics Division</a> also keeps a database on worldwide migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Recent advances in research on migration via the Internet promise better understanding of migration patterns and migration motives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Structurally, there is substantial <a href="/wiki/South%E2%80%93South_cooperation" title="South–South cooperation">South-South</a> and North-North migration; in 2013, 38% of all migrants had migrated from developing countries to other developing countries, while 23% had migrated from high-income <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> countries to other high-income countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> says that "while the North has experienced a higher absolute increase in the migrant stock since 2000 (32 million) compared to the South (25 million), the South recorded a higher growth rate. Between 2000 and 2013, the average annual rate of change of the migrant population in developing regions (2.3%) slightly exceeded that of the developed regions (2.1%)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFrPA_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNFrPA-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Substantial internal migration can also take place within a country, either <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_human_migration" title="Seasonal human migration">seasonal human migration</a> (mainly related to agriculture and tourism to urban places), or shifts of the population into cities (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urbanisation" title="Urbanisation">urbanisation</a>) or out of cities (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburbanisation" title="Suburbanisation">suburbanisation</a>). However, studies of worldwide migration patterns tend to limit their scope to <a href="/wiki/International_migration" title="International migration">international migration</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impacts_of_human_migration">Economic impacts of human migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic impacts of human migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_economy">World economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dorothea_Lange,_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside,_Blythe,_California,_1936.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4474" data-file-width="4586" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dorothea_Lange%2C_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside%2C_Blythe%2C_California%2C_1936.jpg/170px-Dorothea_Lange%2C_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside%2C_Blythe%2C_California%2C_1936.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dorothea_Lange%2C_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside%2C_Blythe%2C_California%2C_1936.jpg/255px-Dorothea_Lange%2C_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside%2C_Blythe%2C_California%2C_1936.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dorothea_Lange%2C_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside%2C_Blythe%2C_California%2C_1936.jpg/340px-Dorothea_Lange%2C_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside%2C_Blythe%2C_California%2C_1936.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dorothea_Lange,_Drought_refugees_from_Oklahoma_camping_by_the_roadside,_Blythe,_California,_1936.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Lange" title="Dorothea Lange">Dorothea Lange</a>, Drought refugees from <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> camping by the roadside, <a href="/wiki/Blythe,_California" title="Blythe, California">Blythe</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, 1936</div></div></div>
<p>The impacts of human migration on the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">world economy</a> have been largely positive. In 2015, migrants, who constituted 3.3% of the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a>, contributed 9.4% of global GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>At a microeconomic level, the value of a human mobility is largely recognized by firms. A 2021 survey by the Boston Consulting Group found that 72% of 850+ executives across several countries and industries believed that migration benefited their countries, and 45% considered globally diverse employees a strategic advantage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Global_Development" title="Center for Global Development">Centre for Global Development</a>, opening all borders could add $78 trillion to the <a href="/wiki/Gross_world_product" title="Gross world product">world GDP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In addition to economic impacts, migrants also make substantial contributions in sociocultural and civic-political life. Sociocultural contributions occur in the following areas of societies: food/cuisine, sport, music, art/culture, ideas and beliefs; civic-political contributions relate to participation in civic duties in the context of accepted authority of the State.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It is in recognition of the importance of these remittances that the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_10" title="Sustainable Development Goal 10">Sustainable Development Goal 10</a> targets to substantially reduce the transaction costs of migrants remittances to less than 3% by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_and_forced_migration">Voluntary and forced migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Voluntary and forced migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Migration is usually divided into <i>voluntary migration</i> and <i>forced migration</i>.
</p><p>The distinction between <i>involuntary</i> (fleeing political conflict or natural disaster) and <i>voluntary migration</i> (economic or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_Migration" title="Labour Migration">labour migration</a>) is difficult to make and partially subjective, as the motivators for migration are often correlated. The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> estimated that, as of 2010, 16.3 million or 7.6% of migrants qualified as refugees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldbankfactbook2011_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldbankfactbook2011-26">[26]</a></sup> This number grew to 19.5 million by 2014 (comprising approximately 7.9% of the total number of migrants, based on the figure recorded in 2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldbankfactbook2016_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldbankfactbook2016-27">[27]</a></sup> At levels of roughly 3 percent the share of migrants among the world population has remained remarkably constant over the last 5 decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_migration">Voluntary migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Voluntary migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Voluntary migration</b> is based on the initiative and the free will of the person and is influenced by a combination of factors: economic, political and social: either in the migrants` country of origin (determinant factors or "push factors") or in the country of destination (attraction factors or "pull factors").
</p><p>"Push-pull factors" are the reasons that push or attract people to a particular place. "Push" factors are the negative aspects(for example wars) of the country of origin, often decisive in people's choice to emigrate. The "pull" factors are the positive aspects of a different country that encourages people to emigrate to seek a better life. For example, the government of <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> periodically gives incentives to people who will migrate to live in villages close to the border with Azerbaijan. This is an implementation of a push strategy, and the reason people don't want to live near the border is security concerns given tensions and hostility because of Azerbaijan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the push-pull factors are opposed, both are sides of the same coin, being equally important. Although specific to forced migration, any other harmful factor can be considered a "push factor" or determinant/trigger factor, such examples being: poor quality of life, lack of jobs, excessive pollution, hunger, drought or natural disasters. Such conditions represent decisive reasons for voluntary migration, the population preferring to migrate in order to prevent financially unfavorable situations or even emotional and physical suffering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forced_migration">Forced migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Forced migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There exist contested definitions of forced migration. However, the editors of a leading scientific journal on the subject, the <i><a href="/wiki/Forced_Migration_Review" title="Forced Migration Review">Forced Migration Review</a></i>, offer the following definition: Forced migration refers to the movements of refugees and internally displaced people (displaced by conflict) as well as people displaced by natural or environmental disasters, chemical or nuclear disasters, famine, or development projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> These different causes of migration leave people with one choice, to move to a new environment. Immigrants leave their beloved homes to seek a life in camps, spontaneous settlement, and countries of asylum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of 2018, there were an estimated 67.2 million forced migrants globally—25.9 million refugees displaced from their countries, and 41.3 million internally displaced persons that had been displaced within their countries for different reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_labor_migration_theories">Contemporary labor migration theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Contemporary labor migration theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerous causes impel migrants to move to another country. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> has increased the demand for workers in order to sustain national economies. Thus one category of <a href="/wiki/Economic_migrant" title="Economic migrant">economic migrants</a> – generally from impoverished developing countries – migrates to obtain sufficient income for survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (October 2018)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
Such migrants often send some of their income homes to family members in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remittances" title="Remittances">economic remittances</a>, which have become an economic staple in a number of developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> People may also move or are forced to move as a result of conflict, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_rights_violations" title="Human rights violations">human-rights violations</a>, of violence, or to escape persecution. In 2013 it was estimated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that around 51.2 million people fell into this category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (October 2018)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Other reasons people may move include to gain access to opportunities and services or to escape extreme weather. This type of movement, usually from rural to urban areas, may be classed as <a href="/wiki/Internal_migration" title="Internal migration">internal migration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (October 2018)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Sociology-cultural and ego-historical factors also play a major role. In North Africa, for example, emigrating to Europe counts as a sign of social prestige. Moreover, many countries were former <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colonies</a>. This means that many have relatives who live legally in the (former) colonial <a href="/wiki/Metropole" title="Metropole">metro pole</a> and who often provide important help for immigrants arriving in that metropole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
Relatives may help with job research and with accommodation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The geographical proximity of Africa to Europe and the long historical ties between Northern and Southern Mediterranean countries also prompt many to migrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Whether a person decides to move to another country depends on the relative skill premier of the source and host countries. One is speaking of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_selection" title="Positive selection">positive selection</a> when the host country shows a higher skill premium than the source country. On the other hand, negative selection occurs when the source country displays a lower skill premium. The relative skill premia define migrants selectivity. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_heaping" title="Age heaping">Age heaping</a> techniques display one method to measure the relative skill premium of a country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of theories attempt to explain the international flow of capital and people from one country to another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_research_contributions_in_the_field_of_migration">Contemporary research contributions in the field of migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Contemporary research contributions in the field of migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recent academic output on migration comprises mainly journal articles. The long-term trend shows a gradual increase in academic publishing on migration, which is likely to be related to the general expansion of academic literature production, and the increased prominence of migration research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
Migration and its research have further changed with the revolution in information and communication technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neoclassical_economic_theory">Neoclassical economic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Neoclassical economic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">Neoclassical economics</a></div>
<p>This migration theory states that the main reason for labour migration is wage difference between two geographic locations. These wage differences are usually linked to geographic labour demand and supply. It can be said that areas with a shortage of labour but an excess of capital have a high relative wage while areas with a high labour supply and a dearth of capital have a low relative wage. Labour tends to flow from low-wage areas to high-wage areas. Often, with this flow of labour comes changes in the sending and the receiving country. Neoclassical economic theory best describes transnational migration because it is not confined by international immigration laws and similar governmental regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_labor_market_theory">Dual labor market theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dual labor market theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dual labour market theory states that pull factors in more developed countries mainly cause migration. This theory assumes that the labour markets in these developed countries consist of two segments: the primary market, which requires high-skilled labour, and the secondary market, which is very labour-intensive, requiring low-skilled workers. This theory assumes that migration from less developed countries into more developed countries results from a pull created by a need for labour in the developed countries in their secondary market. <a href="/wiki/Migrant_worker" title="Migrant worker">Migrant workers</a> are needed to fill the lowest rung of the labour market because the native labourers do not want to do these jobs as they present a lack of mobility. This creates a need for migrant workers. Furthermore, the initial dearth in available labour pushes wages up, making migration even more enticing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_economics_of_labor_migration">New economics of labor migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: New economics of labor migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This theory states that migration flows and patterns can't be explained solely at the level of individual workers and their economic incentives but that wider social entities must also be considered. One such social entity is the household. Migration can be viewed as a result of risk aversion from a household that has insufficient income. In this case, the household needs extra capital that can be achieved through remittances sent back by family members who participate in migrant labour abroad. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remittances" title="Remittances">remittances</a> can also have a broader effect on the economy of the sending country as a whole as they bring in capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-41">[41]</a></sup> Recent research has examined a decline in US interstate migration from 1991 to 2011, theorising that the reduced interstate migration is due to a decline in the geographic specificity of occupations and an increase in workers' ability to learn about other locations before moving there, through both information technology and inexpensive travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Other researchers find that the location-specific nature of housing is more important than moving costs in determining labour reallocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_deprivation_theory">Relative deprivation theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Relative deprivation theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Relative_deprivation" title="Relative deprivation">Relative deprivation</a></div>
<p>Relative deprivation theory states that awareness of the income difference between neighbours or other households in the migrant-sending community is essential in migration. The incentive to migrate is a lot higher in areas with a high level of economic inequality. In the short run, remittances may increase inequality, but in the long run, they may decrease it. There are two stages of migration for workers: first, they invest in human capital formation, and then they try to capitalise on their investments. In this way, successful migrants may use their new capital to provide better schooling for their children and better homes for their families. Successful high-skilled emigrants may serve as an example for neighbours and potential migrants who hope to achieve that level of success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_systems_theory">World systems theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: World systems theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/World-systems_theory" title="World-systems theory">World-systems theory</a> looks at migration from a global perspective. It explains that interaction between different societies can be an important factor in social change. Trade with one country, which causes an economic decline in another, may create incentive to migrate to a country with a more vibrant economy. It can be argued that even after decolonisation, the economic dependence of former colonies remains on mother countries. However, this view of <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">international trade</a> is controversial, and some argue that free trade can reduce migration between developing and developed countries. It can be argued that the developed countries import labour-intensive goods, which causes an increase in the employment of unskilled workers in the less developed countries, decreasing the outflow of migrant workers. Exporting capital-intensive goods from rich countries to developing countries also equalises income and employment conditions, thus slowing migration. In either direction, this theory can be used to explain migration between countries that are geographically far apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_41-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Osmosis_theory">Osmosis theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Osmosis theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based on the <a href="/wiki/History_of_human_migration" title="History of human migration">history of human migration</a>, Djelti (2017a)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> studies the evolution of its natural determinants. According to him, human migration is divided into two main types: simple and complicated. The simple migration is divided, in its turn, into diffusion, stabilisation and concentration periods. During these periods, water availability, adequate climate, security and population density represent the natural determinants of human migration. The complicated migration is characterised by the speedy evolution and the emergence of new sub-determinants, notably earning, unemployment, networks, and migration policies. Osmosis theory (Djelti, 2017b)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> explains analogically human migration by the biophysical phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>. In this respect, the countries are represented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cells" title="Animal cells">animal cells</a>, the borders by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semipermeable_membranes" title="Semipermeable membranes">semipermeable membranes</a> and the humans by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> of water. According to the theory, according to the osmosis phenomenon, humans migrate from countries with less migration pressure to countries with high migration pressure. To measure the latter, the natural determinants of human migration replace the variables of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_principle_of_thermodynamics" title="Second principle of thermodynamics">second principle of thermodynamics</a> used to measure the <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_pressure" title="Osmotic pressure">osmotic pressure</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social-scientific_theories">Social-scientific theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Social-scientific theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_immigration" title="Sociology of immigration">Sociology of immigration</a></div><p>A number of social scientists have examined immigration from a <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> perspective, paying particular attention to how immigration affects and is affected by, matters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(human_categorisation)" title="Race (human categorisation)">race</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnicity</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Social_structure" title="Social structure">social structure</a>. They have produced three main sociological perspectives:
</p><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Symbolic_interactionism" title="Symbolic interactionism">symbolic interactionism</a>, which aims to understand migration via face-to-face interactions on a micro-level</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Social_conflict_theory" title="Social conflict theory">social conflict theory</a>, which examines migration through the prism of competition for <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resources</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Structural_functionalism" title="Structural functionalism">structural functionalism</a> (based on the ideas of <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim" title="Émile Durkheim">Émile Durkheim</a>), which examines the role of migration in fulfilling certain functions within each society, such as the decrease of <a href="/wiki/Anomie" title="Anomie">despair and aimlessness</a> and the consolidation of <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social networks</a></li></ul>
<p>More recently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> as attention has shifted away from countries of destination, sociologists have attempted to understand how <a href="/wiki/Transnationalism" title="Transnationalism">transnationalism</a> allows us to understand the interplay between migrants, their countries of destination, and their countries of origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In this framework, work on social remittances by <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Levitt" title="Peggy Levitt">Peggy Levitt</a> and others has led to a stronger conceptualisation of how migrants affect socio-political processes in their countries of origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Much work also takes place in the field of <a href="/wiki/Social_integration" title="Social integration">integration</a> of migrants into destination-societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_science">Political science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Political science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Political scientists have put forth a number of theoretical frameworks relating to migration, offering different perspectives on processes of <a href="/wiki/International_security" title="International security">security</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The political importance of <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diasporas</a> has also become<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> a growing field of interest, as scholars examine questions of diaspora <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> state-diaspora relations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> out-of-country <a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">voting</a> processes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and states' <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">soft power</a> strategies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In this field, the majority of work has focused on immigration politics, viewing migration from the perspective of the country of destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> With regard to <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a> processes, political scientists have expanded on <a href="/wiki/Albert_O._Hirschman" title="Albert O. Hirschman">Albert Hirschman's</a> framework on '"voice" vs. "exit" to discuss how emigration affects the politics within countries of origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Certain laws of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social science</a> have been proposed to describe human migration. The following was a standard list after <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Georg_Ravenstein" title="Ernst Georg Ravenstein">Ernst Georg Ravenstein</a>'s proposal in the 1880s:
</p>
<ol><li>every migration flow generates a return or counter migration.</li>
<li>the majority of migrants move a short distance.</li>
<li>migrants who move longer distances tend to choose big-city destinations.</li>
<li>urban residents are often less migratory than inhabitants of rural areas.</li>
<li>families are less likely to make international moves than young adults.</li>
<li>most migrants are adults.</li>
<li>large towns grow by migration rather than natural increase.</li>
<li>migration stage by stage (<a href="/wiki/Step_migration" title="Step migration">step migration</a>).</li>
<li>urban, rural difference.</li>
<li>migration and technology.</li>
<li>economic condition.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lee">Lee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Lee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lee's laws divide factors causing migrations into two groups of factors: push and pull factors. Push factors are things that are unfavourable about the area that one lives in, and pull factors are things that attract one to another area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Push factors</b>:

<li>Inadequate conditions</li>
<li>Desertification</li>
<li>Famine or drought</li>
<li>Political fear of persecution</li>
<li>Slavery or forced labour</li>
<li>Poor medical care</li>
<li>Loss of wealth</li>
<li>Natural disasters</li>
<li>Death threats</li>
<li>Desire for more political or religious freedom</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_cycles">Climate cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Climate cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern field of climate history suggests that the successive waves of Eurasian nomadic movement throughout history have had their origins in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_(general_concept)" title="Climate change (general concept)">climatic cycles</a>, which have expanded or contracted pastureland in Central Asia, especially <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and to its west the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altai_mountains" title="Altai mountains">Altai</a>. People were displaced from their home ground by other tribes trying to find land that essential flocks could graze, each group pushing the next further to the south and west, into the highlands of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pannonian_Plain" title="Pannonian Plain">Pannonian Plain</a>, into <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, or southwards, into the rich pastures of China. Bogumil Terminski uses the term "migratory domino effect" to describe this process in the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_People" title="Sea People">Sea People</a> invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Food.2C_sex.2C_security"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Food,_sex,_security">Food, sex, security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Food, sex, security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theory is that migration occurs because individuals search for food, sex and security outside their usual habitation; Idyorough (2008) believes that towns and cities are a creation of the human struggle to obtain food, sex and security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> To produce food, security and reproduction, human beings must, out of necessity, move out of their usual habitation and enter into indispensable social relationships that are cooperative or antagonistic. Human beings also develop the tools and equipment to interact with nature to produce the desired food and security. The improved relationship (cooperative relationships) among human beings and improved technology further conditioned by the push and pull factors all interact together to cause or bring about migration and higher concentration of individuals into towns and cities. The higher the technology of production of food and security and the higher the cooperative relationship among human beings in the production of food and security and the reproduction of the human species, the higher would be the push and pull factors in the migration and concentration of human beings in towns and cities. Countryside, towns and cities do not just exist, but they do so to meet the basic human needs of food, security and the reproduction of the human species. Therefore, migration occurs because individuals search for food, sex and security outside their usual habitation. Social services in the towns and cities are provided to meet these basic needs for human survival and pleasure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_governance">Migration governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Migration governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By their very nature, international migration and displacement are transnational issues concerning the origin and destination States and States through which migrants may travel (often referred to as “transit” States) or in which they are hosted following displacement across national borders. And yet, somewhat paradoxically, the majority of migration governance has historically remained with individual states. Their policies and regulations on migration are typically made at the national level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> For the most part, migration governance has been closely associated with State sovereignty. States retain the power of deciding on the entry and stay of non-nationals because migration directly affects some of the defining elements of a State.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Comparative surveys reveal varying degrees of openness to migrants across countries, considering policies such as visa availability, employment prerequisites, and paths to residency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bilateral and multilateral arrangements are features of migration governance at an international level. There are several global arrangements in the form of international treaties in which States have reached an agreement on the application of human rights and the related responsibilities of States in specific areas. The 1966 <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> and the 1951 <a href="/wiki/Convention_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees">Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees</a> (Refugee Convention) are two significant examples notable for being widely ratified. Other migration conventions have not been so broadly accepted, such as the International Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of Their Families, which still has no traditional countries of destination among its States parties. Beyond this, there have been numerous multilateral and global initiatives, dialogues and processes on migration over several decades. The <a href="/wiki/Global_Compact_for_Migration" title="Global Compact for Migration">Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration</a> (Global Compact for Migration) is another milestone, as the first internationally negotiated statement of objectives for migration governance striking a balance between migrants' rights and the principle of States' sovereignty over their territory. Although it is not legally binding, the Global Compact for Migration was adopted by consensus in December 2018 at a United Nations conference in which more than 150 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Member_States" title="United Nations Member States">United Nations Member States</a> participated and, later that same month, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> (UNGA), by a vote among the Member States of 152 to 5 (with 12 abstentions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
