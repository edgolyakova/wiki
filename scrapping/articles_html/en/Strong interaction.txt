<p>The <b>strong interaction</b> or <b>strong nuclear force</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interaction</a> that confines <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a> into <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadron</a> particles. The strong interaction also binds neutrons and protons to create atomic nuclei, where it is called the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear force</a>.
</p><p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence" title="Mass–energy equivalence">mass</a> of a common <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> is the result of the strong interaction energy; the individual quarks provide only about 1% of the mass of a proton. At the range of 10<sup>−15</sup> m (slightly more than the radius of a <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleon</a>), the strong force is approximately 137 times as strong as <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, 10<sup>6</sup> times as strong as the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>, and 10<sup>38</sup> times as strong as gravitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The strong interaction is observable at two ranges and mediated by two force carriers. On a larger scale (of about 1 to 3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Femtometer" title="Femtometer">femtometer</a>), it is the force (carried by <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a>) that binds <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> (nucleons) together to form the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>. On the smaller scale (less than about 0.8 fm, the radius of a nucleon), it is the force (carried by <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a>) that holds <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a> together to form protons, neutrons, and other <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadron</a> particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup> In the latter context, it is often known as the <b>color force</b>. The strong force inherently has such a high strength that hadrons bound by the strong force can <a href="/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence" title="Mass–energy equivalence">produce new massive particles</a>. Thus, if hadrons are struck by high-energy particles, they give rise to new hadrons instead of emitting freely moving radiation (<a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a>). This property of the strong force is called <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">color confinement</a>, and it prevents the free "emission" of the strong force: instead, in practice, <a href="/wiki/Jet_(particle_physics)" title="Jet (particle physics)">jets</a> of massive particles are produced.
</p><p>In the context of atomic nuclei, the same strong interaction force (that binds quarks within a <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleon</a>) also binds protons and neutrons together to form a nucleus. In this capacity it is called the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear force</a> (or <i>residual strong force</i>). So the residuum from the strong interaction within protons and neutrons also binds nuclei together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup> As such, the residual strong interaction obeys a distance-dependent behavior between nucleons that is quite different from that when it is acting to bind quarks within nucleons. Additionally, distinctions exist in the <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">binding energies</a> of the nuclear force of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> vs <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>. Nuclear fusion accounts for most energy production in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Nuclear fission allows for decay of radioactive elements and <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>, although it is often mediated by the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>. Artificially, the energy associated with the nuclear force is partially released in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a>, both in <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>-based fission weapons and in fusion weapons like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The strong interaction is mediated by the exchange of massless particles called <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a> that act between quarks, <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiquarks</a>, and other gluons. Gluons are thought to interact with quarks and other gluons by way of a type of charge called <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">color charge</a>. Color charge is analogous to electromagnetic charge, but it comes in three types (±red, ±green, and ±blue) rather than one, which results in a different type of force, with different rules of behavior. These rules are detailed in the theory of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a> (QCD), which is the theory of quark–gluon interactions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strong_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before 1971, physicists were uncertain as to how the atomic nucleus was bound together. It was known that the nucleus was composed of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> and that protons possessed positive <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>, while neutrons were electrically neutral. By the understanding of physics at that time, positive charges would repel one another and the positively charged protons should cause the nucleus to fly apart. However, this was never observed. New physics was needed to explain this phenomenon.
</p><p>A stronger attractive force was postulated to explain how the atomic nucleus was bound despite the protons' mutual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_repulsion" title="Electromagnetic repulsion">electromagnetic repulsion</a>. This hypothesized force was called the <i>strong force</i>, which was believed to be a fundamental force that acted on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleons" title="Nucleons">protons and neutrons</a> that make up the nucleus.
</p><p>It was later discovered that protons and neutrons were not fundamental particles, but were made up of constituent particles called <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>. The strong attraction between nucleons was the side-effect of a more fundamental force that bound the quarks together into protons and neutrons. The theory of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a> explains that quarks carry what is called a <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">color charge</a>, although it has no relation to visible color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Quarks with unlike color charge attract one another as a result of the strong interaction, and the particle that mediates this was called the <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluon</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_of_the_strong_force">Behavior of the strong force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strong_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behavior of the strong force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gluon_coupling.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="170" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gluon_coupling.svg/400px-Gluon_coupling.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gluon_coupling.svg/600px-Gluon_coupling.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gluon_coupling.svg/800px-Gluon_coupling.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gluon_coupling.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The fundamental <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(physics)" title="Coupling (physics)">couplings</a> of the strong interaction, from left to right: gluon radiation, gluon splitting and gluon self-coupling.</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>strong</i> is used since the strong interaction is the "strongest" of the four fundamental forces. At a distance of 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Femtometer" title="Femtometer">femtometer</a> (1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Femtometer" title="Femtometer">fm</a> = 10<sup>−15</sup> meters) or less, its strength is around 137 times that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_force" title="Electromagnetic force">electromagnetic force</a>, some 10<sup>6</sup> times as great as that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_force" title="Weak force">weak force</a>, and about 10<sup>38</sup> times that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>.
</p><p>The strong force is described by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a> (QCD), a part of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> of particle physics. Mathematically, QCD is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-abelian_gauge_theory" title="Non-abelian gauge theory">non-Abelian gauge theory</a> based on a local (gauge) <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SU(3)" title="SU(3)">SU(3)</a>.
</p><p>The force carrier particle of the strong interaction is the gluon, a massless <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">gauge boson</a>. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> in electromagnetism, which is neutral, the gluon carries a color charge. Quarks and gluons are the only fundamental particles that carry non-vanishing color charge, and hence they participate in strong interactions only with each other. The strong force is the expression of the gluon interaction with other quark and gluon particles.
</p><p>All quarks and gluons in QCD interact with each other through the strong force. The strength of interaction is parameterized by the strong <a href="/wiki/Coupling_constant" title="Coupling constant">coupling constant</a>. This strength is modified by the gauge color charge of the particle, a <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group-theoretical</a> property.
</p><p>The strong force acts between quarks. Unlike all other forces (electromagnetic, weak, and gravitational), the strong force does not diminish in strength with increasing distance between pairs of quarks. After a limiting distance (about the size of a <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadron</a>) has been reached, it remains at a strength of about 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newtons (N)</a>, no matter how much farther the distance between the quarks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As the separation between the quarks grows, the energy added to the pair creates new pairs of matching quarks between the original two; hence it is impossible to isolate quarks. The explanation is that the amount of work done against a force of 10,000 newtons is enough to create particle–antiparticle pairs within a very short distance of that interaction. The very energy added to the system required to pull two quarks apart would create a pair of new quarks that will pair up with the original ones. In QCD, this phenomenon is called <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">color confinement</a>; as a result only hadrons, not individual free quarks, can be observed. The failure of all experiments that have searched for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_quark" title="Free quark">free quarks</a> is considered to be evidence of this phenomenon.
</p><p>The elementary quark and gluon particles involved in a high energy collision are not directly observable. The interaction produces jets of newly created hadrons that are observable. Those hadrons are created, as a manifestation of mass–energy equivalence, when sufficient energy is deposited into a quark–quark bond, as when a quark in one proton is struck by a very fast quark of another impacting proton during a <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> experiment. However, <a href="/wiki/Quark%E2%80%93gluon_plasma" title="Quark–gluon plasma">quark–gluon plasmas</a> have been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Residual_strong_force">Residual strong force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strong_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Residual strong force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">Nuclear force</a></div>
<p>Contrary to the description above of distance independence, in the post-<a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> universe it is <i>not</i> the case that every quark in the universe attracts every other quark. Color confinement implies that the strong force acts without distance-diminishment only between pairs of quarks, and that in compact collections of bound quarks (<a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>), the net color-charge of the quarks essentially <a href="/wiki/Cancelling_out" title="Cancelling out">cancels out</a>, resulting in a limit of the action of the color-forces: From distances approaching or greater than the radius of a proton, compact collections of color-interacting quarks (hadrons) collectively appear to have effectively no color-charge, or "colorless", and the strong force is therefore nearly absent between those hadrons. However, the cancellation is not quite perfect, and a residual force (described below) remains. This residual force <i>does</i> diminish rapidly with distance, and is thus very short-range (effectively a few femtometers). It manifests as a force between the "colorless" hadrons, and is known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear force</a></i> or <i>residual strong force</i> (and historically as the <i>strong nuclear force</i>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nuclear_Force_anim_smaller.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="337" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="337" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/Nuclear_Force_anim_smaller.gif" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nuclear_Force_anim_smaller.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An animation of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear force</a> (or residual strong force) interaction between a proton and a neutron. The small colored double circles are gluons, which can be seen binding the proton and neutron together. These gluons also hold the quark/antiquark combination called the <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pion</a> together, and thus help transmit a residual part of the strong force even between colorless hadrons. Anticolors are shown as in <a href="/wiki/File:Quark_Anticolors.svg" title="File:Quark Anticolors.svg">this diagram</a>. For a larger version, <a class="external text" href="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Nuclear_Force_anim.gif">click here</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nuclear force acts between <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a>. This "residual strong force", acting indirectly, transmits gluons that form part of the virtual <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion"><i>π</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Rho_meson" title="Rho meson"><i>ρ</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a>, which, in turn, transmit the force between nucleons that holds the nucleus (beyond <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-1" title="Hydrogen-1">protium</a>) together.
</p><p>The residual strong force is thus a minor residuum of the strong force that binds quarks together into protons and neutrons. This same force is much weaker <i>between</i> neutrons and protons, because it is mostly neutralized <i>within</i> them, in the same way that electromagnetic forces between neutral atoms (<a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a>) are much weaker than the electromagnetic forces that hold electrons in association with the nucleus, forming the atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the strong force, the residual strong force diminishes with distance, and does so rapidly. The decrease is approximately as a negative exponential power of distance, though there is no simple expression known for this; see <i><a href="/wiki/Yukawa_potential" title="Yukawa potential">Yukawa potential</a></i>. The rapid decrease with distance of the attractive residual force and the less-rapid decrease of the repulsive electromagnetic force acting between protons within a nucleus, causes the instability of larger atomic nuclei, such as all those with <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic numbers</a> larger than 82 (the element <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>).
</p><p>Although the nuclear force is weaker than the strong interaction itself, it is still highly energetic: transitions produce <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a>. The mass of a nucleus is significantly different from the summed masses of the individual nucleons. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_defect" title="Mass defect">mass defect</a> is due to the potential energy associated with the nuclear force. Differences between mass defects power <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unification">Unification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strong_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The so-called <a href="/wiki/Grand_Unified_Theory" title="Grand Unified Theory">Grand Unified Theories</a> (GUT) aim to describe the strong interaction and the electroweak interaction as aspects of a single force, similarly to how the electromagnetic and <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interactions</a> were unified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glashow%E2%80%93Weinberg%E2%80%93Salam_model" title="Glashow–Weinberg–Salam model">Glashow–Weinberg–Salam model</a> into <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak interaction</a>. The strong interaction has a property called <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_freedom" title="Asymptotic freedom">asymptotic freedom</a>, wherein the strength of the strong force diminishes at higher energies (or temperatures). The theorized energy where its strength becomes equal to the electroweak interaction is the <a href="/wiki/Grand_unification_energy" title="Grand unification energy">grand unification energy</a>. However, no Grand Unified Theory has yet been successfully formulated to describe this process, and Grand Unification remains an <a href="/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_physics" title="List of unsolved problems in physics">unsolved problem in physics</a>.
</p><p>If GUT is correct, after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> and during the <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_epoch" title="Electroweak epoch">electroweak epoch</a> of the universe, the <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak force</a> separated from the strong force. Accordingly, a <a href="/wiki/Grand_unification_epoch" title="Grand unification epoch">grand unification epoch</a> is hypothesized to have existed prior to this.
</p>
