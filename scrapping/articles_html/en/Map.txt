<p>A <b>map</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolic</a> depiction emphasizing relationships between elements of some space, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">objects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">regions</a>, or themes.
</p><p>Many maps are static, fixed to <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or some other durable medium, while others are dynamic or interactive. Although most commonly used to depict <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, maps may represent any <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>, real or fictional, without regard to <a href="/wiki/Context_(language_use)" title="Context (language use)">context</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">scale</a>, such as in <a href="/wiki/Brain_mapping" title="Brain mapping">brain mapping</a>, <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> mapping, or computer network topology mapping. The space being mapped may be two dimensional, such as the surface of the earth, three dimensional, such as the interior of the earth, or even more abstract spaces of any dimension, such as arise in modeling phenomena having many independent variables.
</p><p>Although the earliest maps known are of the heavens, geographic maps of territory have a very long tradition and exist from ancient times. The word "map" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">medieval Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Mappa mundi</i>, wherein <i>mappa</i> meant 'napkin' or 'cloth' and <i>mundi</i> 'the world'. Thus, "map" became a shortened term referring to a two-dimensional representation of the surface of the world.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_cartography" title="History of cartography">History of cartography</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5952" data-file-width="12992" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg/300px-TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg/450px-TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg/600px-TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Tabula_Rogeriana" title="Tabula Rogeriana">Tabula Rogeriana</a></i>, one of the most advanced <a href="/wiki/Early_world_maps" title="Early world maps">early world maps</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Idrisi" title="Muhammad al-Idrisi">Muhammad al-Idrisi</a>'s, 1154</div></div></div>
<p>The history of cartography traces the development of <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a>, or mapmaking technology, in human history. Maps have been one of the most important human inventions for millennia, allowing humans to explain and navigate their way through the world. The earliest surviving maps include <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave paintings</a> and etchings on tusk and stone, followed by extensive maps produced by ancient <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. In their most simple form maps are two dimensional constructs, however since the age of Classical Greece maps have also been projected onto a three-dimensional sphere known as a globe. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercator_Projection" title="Mercator Projection">Mercator Projection</a>, developed by Flemish geographer <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">Gerardus Mercator</a>, was widely used as the standard two-dimensional projection of the earth for world maps until the late 20th century, when more accurate projections were formulated. Mercator was also the first to use and popularise the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Atlas" title="Atlas">atlas</a> as a collection of maps.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3972" data-file-width="5812" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg/220px-Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg/330px-Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg/440px-Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Celestial map by the cartographer <a href="/wiki/Frederik_de_Wit" title="Frederik de Wit">Frederik de Wit</a>,   17th century</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">Cartography</a> or <i>map-making</i> is the study and practice of crafting representations of the Earth upon a flat surface (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_cartography" title="History of cartography">History of cartography</a>), and one who makes maps is called a <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartographer</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Road_map" title="Road map">Road maps</a> are perhaps the most widely used maps today, and form a subset of navigational maps, which also include <a href="/wiki/Aeronautical_chart" title="Aeronautical chart">aeronautical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">nautical charts</a>, railroad network maps, and hiking and bicycling maps. In terms of quantity, the largest number of drawn map sheets is probably made up by local surveys, carried out by <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a>, utilities, tax assessors, emergency services providers, and other local agencies.  Many national surveying projects have been carried out by the military, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a>: a civilian government agency, internationally renowned for its comprehensively detailed work.
</p><p>In addition to location information, maps may also be used to portray <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">contour lines</a> indicating constant values of <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orientation">Orientation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orientation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hereford-Karte.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5355" data-file-width="4606" decoding="async" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Hereford-Karte.jpg/220px-Hereford-Karte.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Hereford-Karte.jpg/330px-Hereford-Karte.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Hereford-Karte.jpg/440px-Hereford-Karte.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hereford-Karte.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i><a href="/wiki/Hereford_Mappa_Mundi" title="Hereford Mappa Mundi">Hereford Mappa Mundi</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Cathedral" title="Hereford Cathedral">Hereford Cathedral</a>, England, circa 1300, a classic "T-O" map with Jerusalem at the center, east toward the top, Europe the bottom left and Africa on the right</div></div></div>
<p>The orientation of a map is the relationship between the directions on the map and the corresponding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compass_direction" title="Compass direction">compass directions</a> in reality. The word "<a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">orient</a>" is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">oriens</i></span>, meaning east. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> many maps, including the <a href="/wiki/T_and_O_map" title="T and O map">T and O maps</a>, were drawn with east at the top (meaning that the direction "up" on the map corresponds to East on the compass). The most common cartographic convention is that north is at the top of a map.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1663" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg/220px-Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg/330px-Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg/440px-Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlas_de_Wit_1698-pl044-Utrecht-KB_PPN_145205088.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of <a href="/wiki/Utrecht" title="Utrecht">Utrecht</a>, Netherlands (1695).</div></div></div>
<p>Maps not oriented with north at the top:
</p>
<ul><li>Maps from non-Western traditions have oriented a variety of ways. Old maps of <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> show the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Imperial_Palace" title="Tokyo Imperial Palace">Japanese imperial palace</a> as the "top", but also at the center, of the map. Labels on the map are oriented in such a way that you cannot read them properly unless you put the imperial palace above your head. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Medieval</a> European <a href="/wiki/T_and_O_map" title="T and O map">T and O maps</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Mappa_Mundi" title="Hereford Mappa Mundi">Hereford Mappa Mundi</a> were centered on <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> with East at the top. Indeed, before the reintroduction of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographia" title="Geographia">Geography</a></i> to Europe around 1400, there was no single convention in the West. <a href="/wiki/Portolan_chart" title="Portolan chart">Portolan charts</a>, for example, are oriented to the shores they describe.</li>
<li>Maps of cities bordering a sea are often conventionally oriented with the sea at the top.</li>
<li>Route and channel maps have traditionally been oriented to the road or waterway they describe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_equidistant_projection" title="Azimuthal equidistant projection">Polar maps</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctic</a> regions are conventionally centered on the pole; the direction North would be toward or away from the center of the map, respectively. Typical maps of the Arctic have 0° meridian toward the bottom of the page; maps of the Antarctic have the 0° meridian toward the top of the page.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South-up_map_orientation" title="South-up map orientation">South-up maps</a> invert the <i>North is up</i> convention by having south at the top. Ancient Africans including in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a> utilized this orientation, as some maps in Brazil do today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dymaxion_map" title="Dymaxion map">Dymaxion maps</a> are based on a projection of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> onto an <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">icosahedron</a>. The resulting triangular pieces may be arranged in any order or orientation.</li>
<li>Using the equator as the edge, the world map of Gott, Vanderbei, and Goldberg is arranged as a pair of disks back-to-back designed to present the least error possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They are designed to be printed as a two-sided flat object that could be held easily for educational purposes.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_and_accuracy">Scale and accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scale and accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">Scale (map)</a></div>
<p>Many maps are drawn to a <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">scale</a> expressed as a <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a>, such as 1:10,000, which means that 1 unit of <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> on the map corresponds to 10,000 of that same unit on the ground. The scale statement can be accurate when the region mapped is small enough for the <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a> of the Earth to be neglected, such as a <a href="/wiki/City_map" title="City map">city map</a>. Mapping larger regions, where the curvature cannot be ignored, requires <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">projections</a> to map from the curved surface of the Earth to the plane. The impossibility of flattening the <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane</a> without distortion means that the map cannot have a constant scale. Rather, on most projections, the best that can be attained is an accurate scale along one or two paths on the projection. Because scale differs everywhere, it can only be measured meaningfully as <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">point scale</a> per location. Most maps strive to keep point scale variation within narrow bounds. Although the scale statement is nominal it is usually accurate enough for most purposes unless the map covers a large fraction of the earth. At the scope of a world map, scale as a single number is practically meaningless throughout most of the map. Instead, it usually refers to the scale along the equator.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1534" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/220px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/330px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/440px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cartogram" title="Cartogram">Cartogram</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> – distorted to show population distributions as of 2008</div></div></div>
<p>Some maps, called <a href="/wiki/Cartogram" title="Cartogram">cartograms</a>, have the scale deliberately distorted to reflect information other than land area or distance. For example, this map (at the right) of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> has been distorted to show population distribution, while the rough shape of the continent is still discernible.
</p><p>Another example of distorted scale is the famous <a href="/wiki/Tube_map" title="Tube map">London Underground map</a>. The basic geographical structure is respected but the tube lines (and the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>) are smoothed to clarify the relationships between stations. Near the center of the map, stations are spaced out more than near the edges of the map.
</p><p>Further inaccuracies may be deliberate. For example, cartographers may simply omit military installations or remove features solely to enhance the clarity of the map. For example, a road map may not show railroads, smaller waterways, or other prominent non-road objects, and even if it does, it may show them less clearly (e.g. dashed or dotted lines/outlines) than the main roads. Known as decluttering, the practice makes the subject matter that the user is interested in easier to read, usually without sacrificing overall accuracy. Software-based maps often allow the user to toggle decluttering between ON, OFF, and AUTO as needed. In AUTO the degree of decluttering is adjusted as the user changes the scale being displayed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projection">Projection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Projection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">Map projection</a></div>
<p>Geographic maps use a <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">projection</a> to translate the three-dimensional real surface of the <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">geoid</a> to a two-dimensional picture. Projection always distorts the surface. There are many ways to apportion the distortion, and so there are many map projections. Which projection to use depends on the purpose of the map. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbology">Symbology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Symbology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Map_symbol" title="Map symbol">Map symbol</a></div>
<p>The various features shown on a map are represented by conventional <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">signs</a> or symbols.  For example, colors can be used to indicate a classification of roads.  Those signs are usually explained in the margin of the map, or on a separately published characteristic sheet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some cartographers prefer to make the map cover practically the entire screen or sheet of paper, leaving no room "outside" the map for information about the map as a whole. These cartographers typically place such information in an otherwise "blank" region "inside" the map—<a href="/wiki/Cartouche_(cartography)" title="Cartouche (cartography)">cartouche</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Page_layout_(cartography)#Legend" title="Page layout (cartography)">map legend</a>, title, <a href="/wiki/Compass_rose" title="Compass rose">compass rose</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_scale" title="Bar scale">bar scale</a>, etc.
In particular, some maps contain smaller "sub-maps" in otherwise blank regions—often one at a much smaller scale showing the whole globe and where the whole map fits on that globe, and a few showing "regions of interest" at a larger scale to show details that wouldn't otherwise fit.
Occasionally sub-maps use the same scale as the large map—a few maps of the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a> include a sub-map to the same scale for each of the two non-contiguous states.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cartographic_design" title="Cartographic design">Cartographic design</a></div>
<p>The design and production of maps is a craft that has developed over thousands of years, from clay tablets to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_information_systems" title="Geographic information systems">Geographic information systems</a>. As a form of <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">Design</a>, particularly closely related to <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">Graphic design</a>, map making incorporates scientific knowledge about how maps are used, integrated with principles of artistic expression, to create an aesthetically attractive product, carries an aura of authority, and functionally serves a particular purpose for an intended audience.
</p><p>Designing a map involves bringing together a number of elements and making a large number of decisions. The elements of design fall into several broad topics, each of which has its own theory, its own research agenda, and its own best practices. That said, there are synergistic effects between these elements, meaning that the overall design process is not just working on each element one at a time, but an iterative feedback process of adjusting each to achieve the desired <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">gestalt</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Map_projections" title="Map projections">Map projections</a>: The foundation of the map is the plane on which it rests (whether paper or screen), but projections are required to flatten the surface of the earth. All projections distort this surface, but the cartographer can be strategic about how and where distortion occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albrecht_2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albrecht_2005-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cartographic_generalization" title="Cartographic generalization"> Generalization</a>: All maps must be drawn at a smaller scale than reality, requiring that the information included on a map be a very small sample of the wealth of information about a place. Generalization is the process of adjusting the level of detail in geographic information to be appropriate for the scale and purpose of a map, through procedures such as selection, simplification, and classification.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Map_symbol" title="Map symbol"> Symbology</a>: Any map visually represents the location and properties of geographic phenomena using map symbols, graphical depictions composed of several <a href="/wiki/Visual_variable" title="Visual variable"> visual variables</a>, such as size, shape, color, and pattern.</li>
<li>Composition: As all of the symbols are brought together, their interactions have major effects on map reading, such as <a href="/wiki/Gestalt_psychology#Prägnanz" title="Gestalt psychology"> grouping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visual_hierarchy" title="Visual hierarchy">Visual hierarchy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Typography_(cartography)" title="Typography (cartography)"> Typography or Labeling</a>: Text serves a number of purposes on the map, especially aiding the recognition of features, but labels must be designed and positioned well to be effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Page_layout_(cartography)" title="Page layout (cartography)"> Layout</a>: The map image must be placed on the page (whether paper, web, or other media), along with related elements, such as the title, legend, additional maps, text, images, and so on. Each of these elements have their own design considerations, as does their integration, which largely follows the principles of <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">Graphic design</a>.</li>
<li>Map type-specific design: Different kinds of maps, especially <a href="/wiki/Thematic_map" title="Thematic map">thematic maps</a>, have their own design needs and best practices.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types<span class="anchor" id="Map_types_and_projections"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Map_types" title="Category:Map types">Category:Map types</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_gravity_map.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="597" data-file-width="1010" decoding="async" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Ocean_gravity_map.gif/220px-Ocean_gravity_map.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Ocean_gravity_map.gif/330px-Ocean_gravity_map.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Ocean_gravity_map.gif/440px-Ocean_gravity_map.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_gravity_map.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of large underwater features. (1995, <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a>)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6982" data-file-width="14999" decoding="async" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png/220px-The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png/330px-The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png/440px-The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_geologic_map_of_the_Moon_at_1-2.5M_scale.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Geological map of the Moon</div></div></div>
<p>Maps of the world or large areas are often either 'political' or 'physical'. The most important purpose of the political map is to show territorial <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a>; the purpose of the physical is to show features of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> such as mountains, soil type, or land use including infrastructures such as roads, railroads, and buildings. <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">Topographic maps</a> show <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">relief</a> with <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">contour lines</a> or shading. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_map" title="Geological map">Geological maps</a> show not only the physical surface, but characteristics of the underlying rock, <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault</a> lines, and subsurface structures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic">Electronic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electronic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">Web page</a>, <a href="/wiki/PDF#Optional_Content_Groups_(layers)" title="PDF">PDF § Optional Content Groups (layers)</a>, <a href="/wiki/MapQuest" title="MapQuest">MapQuest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetMap" title="OpenStreetMap">OpenStreetMap</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Maps" title="Yahoo! Maps">Yahoo! Maps</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Topographic_map_example.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/220px-Topographic_map_example.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/330px-Topographic_map_example.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/440px-Topographic_map_example.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Topographic_map_example.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">USGS</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_raster_graphic" title="Digital raster graphic">digital raster graphic</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>From the last quarter of the 20th century, the indispensable tool of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartographer" title="Cartographer">cartographer</a> has been the computer. Much of cartography, especially at the data-gathering <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">survey</a> level, has been subsumed by <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">Geographic Information Systems</a> (GIS). The functionality of maps has been greatly advanced by technology simplifying the superimposition of spatially located variables onto existing geographical maps. Having local information such as rainfall level, distribution of wildlife, or demographic data integrated within the map allows more efficient analysis and better decision making. In the pre-electronic age such <a href="/wiki/Superimposition" title="Superimposition">superimposition</a> of data led <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Snow_(physician)" title="John Snow (physician)">Dr. John Snow</a> to identify the location of an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>. Today, it is used by agencies of humankind, as diverse as wildlife conservationists and militaries around the world.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="497" decoding="async" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png/220px-Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png/330px-Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png/440px-Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">Relief map</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada" title="Sierra Nevada">Sierra Nevada</a></div></div></div>
<p>Even when GIS is not involved, most cartographers now use a variety of computer graphics programs to generate new maps.
</p><p>Interactive, computerized maps are commercially available, allowing users to <i>zoom in</i> or <i>zoom out</i> (respectively meaning to increase or decrease the scale), sometimes by replacing one map with another of different scale, centered where possible on the same point.  In-car <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_navigation_satellite_system" title="Global navigation satellite system">global navigation satellite systems</a> are computerized maps with route planning and advice facilities that monitor the user's position with the help of satellites. From the computer scientist's point of view, zooming in entails one or a combination of:
</p>
<ol><li>replacing the map by a more detailed one</li>
<li>enlarging the same map without enlarging the <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a>, hence showing more detail by removing less information compared to the less detailed version</li>
<li>enlarging the same map with the pixels enlarged (replaced by rectangles of pixels); no additional detail is shown, but, depending on the quality of one's vision, possibly more detail can be seen; if a computer display does not show adjacent pixels really separate, but overlapping instead (this does not apply for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCD</a>, but may apply for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a>), then replacing a pixel by a rectangle of pixels does show more detail.  A variation of this method is <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a>.</li></ol>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World.pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1275" data-file-width="1650" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/World.pdf/page1-220px-World.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/World.pdf/page1-330px-World.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/World.pdf/page1-440px-World.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World.pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A world map in PDF format.</div></div></div>
<p>For example:
</p>
<ul><li>Typically (2) applies to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portable_Document_Format" title="Portable Document Format">Portable Document Format</a> (PDF) file or other format based on <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">vector graphics</a>. The increase in detail is limited to the information contained in the file: enlargement of a curve may eventually result in a series of standard geometric figures such as straight lines, arcs of circles, or <a href="/wiki/Spline_(mathematics)" title="Spline (mathematics)">splines</a>.</li>
<li>(2) may apply to text and (3) to the outline of a map feature such as a forest or building.</li>
<li>(1) may apply to the text as needed (displaying labels for more features), while (2) applies to the rest of the image.  Text is not necessarily enlarged when zooming in. Similarly, a road represented by a double line may or may not become wider when one zooms in.</li>
<li>The map may also have layers that are partly <a href="/wiki/Raster_graphics" title="Raster graphics">raster graphics</a> and partly <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">vector graphics</a>.  For a single raster graphics image (2) applies until the pixels in the image file correspond to the pixels of the display, thereafter (3) applies.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatic">Climatic<span class="anchor" id="Climate"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The maps that reflect the territorial distribution of <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatic</a> conditions based on the results of long-term observations are called <i>climatic maps</i>. These maps can be compiled both for individual climatic features (temperature, precipitation, humidity) and for combinations of them at the earth's surface and in the upper layers of the atmosphere. Climatic maps show climatic features across a large region and permit values of climatic features to be compared in different parts of the region. When generating the map, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spatial_interpolation" title="Spatial interpolation">spatial interpolation</a> can be used to synthesize values where there are no measurements, under the assumption that conditions change smoothly.
</p><p>Climatic maps generally apply to individual months and the year as a whole, sometimes to the four seasons, to the growing period, and so forth. On maps compiled from the observations of ground meteorological stations, atmospheric pressure is converted to sea level. Air temperature maps are compiled both from the actual values observed on the surface of the earth and from values converted to sea level. The pressure field in the free atmosphere is represented either by maps of the distribution of pressure at different standard altitudes—for example, at every kilometer above sea level—or by maps of baric topography on which altitudes (more precisely geopotentials) of the main isobaric surfaces (for example, 900, 800, and 700 millibars) counted off from sea level are plotted. The temperature, humidity, and wind on aero climatic maps may apply either to standard altitudes or to the main isobaric surfaces.
</p><p>Isolines are drawn on maps of such climatic features as the long-term mean values (of atmospheric pressure, temperature, humidity, total precipitation, and so forth) to connect points with equal values of the feature in question—for example, isobars for pressure, isotherms for temperature, and isohyets for precipitation. Isoamplitudes are drawn on maps of amplitudes (for example, annual amplitudes of air temperature—that is, the differences between the mean temperatures of the warmest and coldest month). Isanomals are drawn on maps of anomalies (for example, deviations of the mean temperature of each place from the mean temperature of the entire latitudinal zone). Isolines of frequency are drawn on maps showing the frequency of a particular phenomenon (for example, the annual number of days with a thunderstorm or snow cover). Isochrones are drawn on maps showing the dates of onset of a given phenomenon (for example, the first frost and appearance or disappearance of the snow cover) or the date of a particular value of a meteorological element in the course of a year (for example, passing of the mean daily air temperature through zero). Isolines of the mean numerical value of wind velocity or isotachs are drawn on wind maps (charts); the wind resultants and directions of prevailing winds are indicated by arrows of different lengths or arrows with different plumes; lines of flow are often drawn. Maps of the zonal and meridional components of wind are frequently compiled for the free atmosphere. Atmospheric pressure and wind are usually combined on climatic maps. Wind roses, curves showing the distribution of other meteorological elements, diagrams of the annual course of elements at individual stations, and the like are also plotted on climatic maps.
</p><p>Maps of climatic regionalization, that is, division of the earth's surface into climatic zones and regions according to some classification of climates, are a special kind of climatic map.
</p><p>Climatic maps are often incorporated into climatic atlases of varying geographic ranges (globe, hemispheres, continents, countries, oceans) or included in comprehensive atlases. Besides general climatic maps, applied climatic maps and atlases have great practical value. Aero climatic maps, aero climatic atlases, and agro climatic maps are the most numerous.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial">Extraterrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maps exist of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, and other cosmological features such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_map" title="Star map">star maps</a>. In addition maps of other bodies such as the Moon and other planets are technically not <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geospatial" title="Geospatial">geo</a></i>graphical maps.
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floor_map" title="Floor map">Floor maps</a> are also spatial but not necessarily geospatial.
</p>

<p>Diagrams such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schematic_diagram" title="Schematic diagram">schematic diagrams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gantt_chart" title="Gantt chart">Gantt charts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treemap" title="Treemap">tree maps</a> display logical relationships between items, rather than geographical relationships. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topological" title="Topological">Topological</a> in nature, only the connectivity is significant. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Underground_map" title="London Underground map">London Underground map</a> and similar subway maps around the world are a common example of these maps.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>General-purpose maps provide many types of information on one map. Most atlas maps, wall maps, and road maps fall into this category. The following are some features that might be shown on general-purpose maps: bodies of water, roads, railway lines, parks, elevations, towns and cities, political boundaries, latitude and longitude, national and provincial parks. These maps give a broad understanding of the location and features of an area.  The reader may gain an understanding of the type of landscape, the location of urban places, and the location of major transportation routes all at once.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_regulation">Legal regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legal regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some countries required that all published maps represent their national claims regarding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Border_dispute" title="Border dispute">border disputes</a>.  For example:
