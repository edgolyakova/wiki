<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_poster.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2200" decoding="async" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/310px-Earth_poster.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/465px-Earth_poster.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/620px-Earth_poster.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_poster.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Geological cross section of Earth, showing its internal structure, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>internal structure of Earth</b> is the solid portion of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, excluding its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>. The structure consists of an outer <a href="/wiki/Silicate_mineral" title="Silicate mineral">silicate</a> solid <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, a highly <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> and solid <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, a liquid <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core" title="Earth's outer core">outer core</a> whose flow generates the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>, and a solid <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_inner_core" title="Earth's inner core">inner core</a>.
</p><p>Scientific understanding of the internal structure of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> is based on observations of <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetry</a>, observations of <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> in <a href="/wiki/Outcrop" title="Outcrop">outcrop</a>, samples brought to the surface from greater depths by <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> or volcanic activity, analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">seismic waves</a> that pass through Earth, measurements of the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">gravitational</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> of Earth, and experiments with crystalline solids at pressures and temperatures characteristic of Earth's deep interior.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_properties">Global properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Global properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Earth#Physical_characteristics" title="Earth">Earth § Physical characteristics</a></div>

<p>The force exerted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_gravity" title="Earth's gravity">Earth's gravity</a> can be used to calculate its <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>. Astronomers can also calculate <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">Earth's mass</a> by observing the motion of orbiting <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>. Earth's average <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> can be determined through gravimetric experiments, which have historically involved <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulums</a>. The mass of Earth is about <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7024600000000000000♠"></span>6<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>24</sup> kg</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA-3">[3]</a></sup> The average density of the Earth is <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7000551499999999999♠"></span>5.515 <a href="/wiki/Gram_per_cubic_centimetre" title="Gram per cubic centimetre">g/cm<sup>3</sup></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Layers">Layers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Layers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure of Earth can be defined in two ways: by mechanical properties such as <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">rheology</a>, or chemically. Mechanically, it can be divided into <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesosphere_(mantle)" title="Mesosphere (mantle)">mesospheric mantle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core" title="Earth's outer core">outer core</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_inner_core" title="Earth's inner core">inner core</a>. Chemically, Earth can be divided into the crust, upper mantle, lower mantle, outer core, and inner core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> The geologic component layers of Earth are at the following depths below the surface:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 146">: 146 </span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crust_and_lithosphere">Crust and lithosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crust and lithosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">Lithosphere</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tectonic_plates_(empty).svg"><img alt="Map of Earth's tectonic plates" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="681" decoding="async" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Tectonic_plates_%28empty%29.svg/300px-Tectonic_plates_%28empty%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Tectonic_plates_%28empty%29.svg/450px-Tectonic_plates_%28empty%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Tectonic_plates_%28empty%29.svg/600px-Tectonic_plates_%28empty%29.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tectonic_plates_(empty).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/List_of_tectonic_plates" title="List of tectonic plates">Earth's major plates</a>, which are:<div class="hlist hlist-separated"><ul><li><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r981673959">.mw-parser-output .legend{page-break-inside:avoid;break-inside:avoid-column}.mw-parser-output .legend-color{display:inline-block;min-width:1.25em;height:1.25em;line-height:1.25;margin:1px 0;text-align:center;border:1px solid black;background-color:transparent;color:black}.mw-parser-output .legend-text{}</style><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#fee6aa; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a></span></li><li><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#fb9a7a; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African Plate</a></span></li><li><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#ac8d7f; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a></span></li><li><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#7fa172; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a></span></li><li><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#8a9dbe; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Plate" title="Antarctic Plate">Antarctic Plate</a></span></li><li><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#fcb482; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian Plate</a></span></li><li><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend nowrap"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#ad82b0; color:black;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">South American Plate</a></span></li></ul></div></div></div></div>
<p>The Earth's crust ranges from 5–70 kilometres (3.1–43.5 mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in depth and is the outermost layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The thin parts are the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>, which underlie the ocean basins (5–10 km) and is <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a>-rich<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rogers_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rogers-8">[8]</a></sup> (dense iron-magnesium <a href="/wiki/Silicate_mineral" title="Silicate mineral">silicate mineral</a> or <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rock</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson1997_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson1997-9">[9]</a></sup> The thicker crust is the <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>, which is less dense<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and is <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic</a>-rich (igneous rocks rich in elements that form <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The rocks of the crust fall into two major categories – <a href="/wiki/Sial" title="Sial">sial</a> (aluminium silicate) and <a href="/wiki/Sima_(geology)" title="Sima (geology)">sima</a> (magnesium silicate).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is estimated that sima starts about 11 km below the <a href="/wiki/Conrad_discontinuity" title="Conrad discontinuity">Conrad discontinuity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kearey_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kearey-11">[11]</a></sup> though the discontinuity is not distinct and can be absent in some continental regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowrie_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lowrie-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Earth's lithosphere consists of the crust and the uppermost <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The crust-mantle boundary occurs as two physically different phenomena. The <a href="/wiki/Mohorovi%C4%8Di%C4%87_discontinuity" title="Mohorovičić discontinuity">Mohorovičić discontinuity</a> is a distinct change of <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">seismic wave</a> velocity. This is caused by a change in the rock's density<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> – Immediately above the Moho, the velocities of primary seismic waves (<a href="/wiki/P_wave" title="P wave">P wave</a>) are consistent with those through <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> (6.7–7.2 km/s), and below they are similar to those through <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">dunite</a> (7.6–8.6 km/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Badescu_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Badescu-15">[15]</a></sup> Second, in oceanic crust, there is a chemical discontinuity between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> cumulates and tectonized <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">harzburgites</a>, which has been observed from deep parts of the oceanic crust that have been <a href="/wiki/Obduction" title="Obduction">obducted</a> onto the continental crust and preserved as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophiolites" title="Ophiolites">ophiolite sequences</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Too technical, a simpler explanation is warranted (August 2022)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Many rocks now making up Earth's crust formed less than 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million_years" title="Million years">million years</a> ago; however, the oldest known mineral grains are about 4.4 <a href="/wiki/Billion_years" title="Billion years">billion years</a> old, indicating that Earth has had a solid crust for at least 4.4 billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mantle">Mantle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mantle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">Earth's mantle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subduction-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="854" data-file-width="1482" decoding="async" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Subduction-en.svg/300px-Subduction-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Subduction-en.svg/450px-Subduction-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Subduction-en.svg/600px-Subduction-en.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subduction-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth's crust and mantle, <a href="/wiki/Mohorovi%C4%8Di%C4%87_discontinuity" title="Mohorovičić discontinuity">Mohorovičić discontinuity</a> between bottom of crust and solid uppermost mantle</div></div></div>
<p>Earth's mantle extends to a depth of 2,890 km, making it the planet's thickest layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The mantle is divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_mantle" title="Upper mantle">upper</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_mantle" title="Lower mantle">lower mantle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> separated by a <a href="/wiki/Transition_zone_(Earth)" title="Transition zone (Earth)">transition zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The lowest part of the mantle next to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core-mantle_boundary" title="Core-mantle boundary">core-mantle boundary</a> is known as the D″ (D-double-prime) layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDoublePron_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDoublePron-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> at the bottom of the mantle is ≈140 G<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a> (1.4 M<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The mantle is composed of <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> rocks richer in iron and magnesium than the overlying crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Although solid, the mantle's extremely hot silicate material can <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">flow</a> over very long timescales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">Convection</a> of the mantle propels the <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">motion of the tectonic plates</a> in the crust. The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_internal_heat_budget" title="Earth's internal heat budget">source of heat</a> that drives this motion is the decay of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotopes" title="Radioactive isotopes">radioactive isotopes</a> in the Earth's crust and mantle combined with the initial heat from the planet's formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to increasing pressure deeper in the mantle, the lower part flows less easily, though chemical changes within the mantle may also be important. The viscosity of the mantle ranges between 10<sup>21</sup> and 10<sup>24</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pascal_second" title="Pascal second">pascal-second</a>, increasing with depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In comparison, the viscosity of water at 25 °C (77 °F) is 0.89 pascal-second<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">pitch</a> is (2.3 ± 0.5)<sup>8</sup> pascal-second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitchdrop_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-pitchdrop-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Core">Core</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Core">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_inner_core" title="Earth's inner core">Earth's inner core</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core" title="Earth's outer core">Earth's outer core</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg"><img alt="A diagram of Earth's geodynamo and magnetic field, which could have been driven in Earth's early history by the crystallization of magnesium oxide, silicon dioxide, and iron(II) oxide. Convection of Earth's outer core is displayed alongside magnetic field lines." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg/220px-Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg/330px-Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg/440px-Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dynamo_Theory_-_Outer_core_convection_and_magnetic_field_geenration.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A diagram of Earth's geodynamo and magnetic field, which could have been driven in Earth's early history by the crystallization of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Earth's outer core is a fluid layer about 2,400 km (1,500 mi) thick and composed of mostly <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> that lies above Earth's solid <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_inner_core" title="Earth's inner core">inner core</a> and below its <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Its outer boundary lies 2,890 km (1,800 mi) beneath Earth's surface. The transition between the inner core and outer core is located approximately 5,150 km (3,200 mi) beneath the Earth's surface. Earth's inner core is the innermost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth" title="Structure of the Earth">geologic layer</a> of the planet <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. It is primarily a solid ball with a radius of about 1,220 km (760 mi), which is about 20% of <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">Earth's radius</a> or 70% of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>'s radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monner2010_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-monner2010-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eng1974_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-eng1974-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The inner core was discovered in 1936 by <a href="/wiki/Inge_Lehmann" title="Inge Lehmann">Inge Lehmann</a> and is generally composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>. Since this layer is able to transmit shear waves (transverse seismic waves), it must be solid. Experimental evidence has at times been inconsistent with current crystal models of the core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Other experimental studies show a discrepancy under high pressure: diamond anvil (static) studies at core pressures yield melting temperatures that are approximately 2000 K below those from shock laser (dynamic) studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The laser studies create plasma,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and the results are suggestive that constraining inner core conditions will depend on whether the inner core is a solid or is a plasma with the density of a solid. This is an area of active research.
</p><p>In early stages of Earth's formation about 4.6 billion years ago, melting would have caused denser substances to sink toward the center in a process called <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">planetary differentiation</a> (see also the <a href="/wiki/Iron_catastrophe" title="Iron catastrophe">iron catastrophe</a>), while less-dense materials would have migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. The core is thus believed to largely be composed of iron (80%), along with <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and one or more light elements, whereas other dense elements, such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, either are too rare to be significant or tend to bind to lighter elements and thus remain in the crust (see <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic materials</a>). Some have argued that the inner core may be in the form of a single iron <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under laboratory conditions a sample of iron–nickel alloy was subjected to the corelike pressures by gripping it in a vise between 2 diamond tips (<a href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell" title="Diamond anvil cell">diamond anvil cell</a>), and then heating to approximately 4000 K. The sample was observed with x-rays, and strongly supported the theory that Earth's inner core was made of giant crystals running north to south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The composition of the Earth bears strong similarities to that of certain <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondrite</a> meteorites, and even to some elements in the outer portion of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Beginning as early as 1940, scientists, including <a href="/wiki/Francis_Birch_(geophysicist)" title="Francis Birch (geophysicist)">Francis Birch</a>, built geophysics upon the premise that Earth is like ordinary chondrites, the most common type of meteorite observed impacting Earth. This ignores the less abundant <a href="/wiki/Enstatite" title="Enstatite">enstatite</a> chondrites, which formed under extremely limited available oxygen, leading to certain normally oxyphile elements existing either partially or wholly in the alloy portion that corresponds to the core of Earth.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">Dynamo theory</a> suggests that convection in the outer core, combined with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>, gives rise to <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>. The solid inner core is too hot to hold a permanent magnetic field (see <a href="/wiki/Curie_temperature" title="Curie temperature">Curie temperature</a>) but probably acts to stabilize the magnetic field generated by the liquid outer core. The average magnetic field in Earth's outer core is estimated to measure 25 Gauss (2.5 mT), 50 times stronger than the magnetic field at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seismology">Seismology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Seismology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">Seismology</a></div>
<p>The layering of Earth has been inferred indirectly using the time of travel of refracted and reflected seismic waves created by earthquakes. The core does not allow shear waves to pass through it, while the speed of travel (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic_velocity" title="Seismic velocity">seismic velocity</a>) is different in other layers. The changes in seismic velocity between different layers causes refraction owing to <a href="/wiki/Snell%27s_law" title="Snell's law">Snell's law</a>, like light bending as it passes through a prism. Likewise, reflections are caused by a large increase in seismic velocity and are similar to light reflecting from a mirror.
</p>
