<p>The <b>performing arts</b> are <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">arts</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> which are performed for an audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They are different from the <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>, which are the use of paint, canvas or various materials to create physical or static <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_objects" title="Art objects">art objects</a>. Performing arts include a range of disciplines which are performed in front of a live audience, including theatre, music, and dance.
</p><p>Theatre, music, dance, <a href="/wiki/Object_manipulation" title="Object manipulation">object manipulation</a>, and other kinds of performances are present in all human cultures. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_music" title="History of music">history of music</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_dance" title="History of dance">dance</a> date to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-historic_times" title="Pre-historic times">pre-historic times</a> whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circus_skills" title="Circus skills">circus skills</a> date to at least <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>. Many performing arts are performed professionally. Performance can be in purpose-built buildings, such as theatres and opera houses, on open air stages at festivals, on stages in tents such as circuses or on the street.
</p><p>Live performances before an audience are a form of entertainment. The development of <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">audio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> recording has allowed for private consumption of the performing arts. The performing arts often aims to express one's emotions and feelings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performers">Performers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Performers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Performing_artists,_Kyoto.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1614" data-file-width="1232" decoding="async" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Performing_artists%2C_Kyoto.jpg/170px-Performing_artists%2C_Kyoto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Performing_artists%2C_Kyoto.jpg/255px-Performing_artists%2C_Kyoto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Performing_artists%2C_Kyoto.jpg/340px-Performing_artists%2C_Kyoto.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Performing_artists,_Kyoto.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Performing artists in Kyoto, Japan</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Performer" and "Performers" redirect here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Performer_(disambiguation)" title="Performer (disambiguation)">Performer (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>Artists who participate in performing arts in front of an audience are called performers. Examples of these include <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">actors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comedian" title="Comedian">comedians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dancers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magic_(illusion)" title="Magic (illusion)">magicians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circus</a> artists, <a href="/wiki/Musician" title="Musician">musicians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Singing" title="Singing">singers</a>. Performing arts are also supported by workers in related fields, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songwriting" title="Songwriting">songwriting</a>, choreography and <a href="/wiki/Stagecraft" title="Stagecraft">stagecraft</a>. Performers often adapt their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_appearance" title="Physical appearance">appearance</a>, such as with <a href="/wiki/Costume" title="Costume">costumes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stage_makeup" title="Stage makeup">stage makeup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stage_lighting" title="Stage lighting">stage lighting</a>, and sound.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:167px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mckenna_Theater_Stage;_SUNY_@_New_Paltz.jpg"><img alt="A picture of a Theatre, a place to showcase performances to audience." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Mckenna_Theater_Stage%3B_SUNY_%40_New_Paltz.jpg/165px-Mckenna_Theater_Stage%3B_SUNY_%40_New_Paltz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Mckenna_Theater_Stage%3B_SUNY_%40_New_Paltz.jpg/248px-Mckenna_Theater_Stage%3B_SUNY_%40_New_Paltz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Mckenna_Theater_Stage%3B_SUNY_%40_New_Paltz.jpg/331px-Mckenna_Theater_Stage%3B_SUNY_%40_New_Paltz.jpg 2x" width="165"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mckenna_Theater_Stage;_SUNY_@_New_Paltz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>McKenna Theatre Stage</div></div></div><p>==Types==
</p><p>Performing arts may include dance, music, opera, theatre and musical theatre, <a href="/wiki/Magic_(illusion)" title="Magic (illusion)">magic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illusion" title="Illusion">illusion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mime" title="Mime">mime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puppetry" title="Puppetry">puppetry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circus_arts" title="Circus arts">circus arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling" title="Professional wrestling">professional wrestling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a>.
</p><p>There is also a specialized form of fine art, in which the artists <i>perform</i> their work live to an audience. This is called performance art. Most performance art also involves some form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_art" title="Plastic art">plastic art</a>, perhaps in the creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Props" title="Props">props</a>. Dance was often referred to as a plastic art during the <a href="/wiki/Modern_dance" title="Modern dance">modern dance</a> era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dance_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dance-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">Theatre</a></div>
<p>Theatre is the branch of performing arts concerned with acting out stories in front of an audience, using a combination of speech, gesture, music, dance, sound, and spectacle. Any one or more of these elements is considered performing arts. In addition to the standard narrative dialogue style of plays, theater takes such forms as plays, musicals, opera, ballet, <a href="/wiki/Illusion" title="Illusion">illusion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mime_artist" title="Mime artist">mime</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Indian_dance" title="Classical Indian dance">classical Indian dance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mummers_Play" title="Mummers Play">mummers' plays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Improvisational_theatre" title="Improvisational theatre">improvisational theatre</a>, comedy, <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomime</a>, and non-conventional or contemporary forms like <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_theatre" title="Postmodern theatre">postmodern theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Postdramatic_theatre" title="Postdramatic theatre">postdramatic theatre</a>, or performance art.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dance">Dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">Dance</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chopiniana_Baku.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1872" data-file-width="2808" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Chopiniana_Baku.jpg/220px-Chopiniana_Baku.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Chopiniana_Baku.jpg/330px-Chopiniana_Baku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Chopiniana_Baku.jpg/440px-Chopiniana_Baku.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chopiniana_Baku.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scene from the ballet <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Sylphides" title="Les Sylphides">Les Sylphides</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>In the context of performing arts, dance generally refers to human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">movement</a>, typically rhythmic and to music, used as a form of audience entertainment in a performance setting. Definitions of what constitutes dance are dependent on <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">social</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesthetic" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artistic" title="Artistic">artistic</a>, and moral constraints and range from functional movement (such as <a href="/wiki/Folk_dance" title="Folk dance">folk dance</a>) to codified, virtuoso techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mackrell_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mackrell-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is one another modern form of dance that emerged in 19th- 20th century with the name of <a href="/wiki/Free_dance" title="Free dance">free dance</a> style. This form of dance was structured to create a harmonious personality which included features such as physical and spiritual freedom. <a href="/wiki/Isadora_Duncan" title="Isadora Duncan">Isadora Duncan</a> was the first female dancer who argued about "woman of future" and developed novel vector of choreography using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nietzsche" title="Nietzsche">Nietzsche</a>'s idea of "supreme mind in free mind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dance is a powerful impulse, but the art of dance is that impulse channeled by skillful performers into something that becomes intensely expressive and that may delight spectators who feel no wish to dance themselves. These two concepts of the art of dance—dance as a powerful impulse and dance as a skillfully choreographed art practiced largely by a professional few—are the two most important connecting ideas running through any consideration of the subject. In dance, the connection between the two concepts is stronger than in some other arts, and neither can exist without the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mackrell_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mackrell-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Choreography" title="Choreography">Choreography</a> is the art of making dances, and the person who practices this art is called a choreographer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">Music</a></div>
<p>Music is an art form which combines <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a>, rhythm, and <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">dynamic</a> to create sound. It can be performed using a variety of instruments and styles and is divided into genres such as <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, pop, and rock, etc. As an art form, music can occur in live or recorded formats, and can be <a href="/wiki/Rehearsal" title="Rehearsal">planned</a> or <a href="/wiki/Musical_improvisation" title="Musical improvisation">improvised.</a>
</p><p>As music is a <a href="/wiki/Proteus" title="Proteus">protean</a> art, it easily coordinates with words for songs as physical movements do in dance. Moreover, it has a capability of shaping human behaviors as it impacts our emotions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sophocles_pushkin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1058" data-file-width="779" decoding="async" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Sophocles_pushkin.jpg/170px-Sophocles_pushkin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Sophocles_pushkin.jpg/255px-Sophocles_pushkin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Sophocles_pushkin.jpg/340px-Sophocles_pushkin.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sophocles_pushkin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_performing_arts">Western performing arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Western performing arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_art_history" title="Western art history">Western art history</a></div><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Antitheatricality" title="Antitheatricality">Antitheatricality</a></div>
<p>Starting in the 6th century BC, the <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical period</a> of performing art began in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, ushered in by the tragic poets such as <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>. These poets wrote plays which, in some cases, incorporated dance (see <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic period</a> began the widespread use of comedy.
</p><p>However, by the 6th century AD, Western performing arts had been largely ended as the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a> began. Between the 9th century and 14th century, performing art in the West was limited to <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> historical enactments and <a href="/wiki/Morality_play" title="Morality play">morality plays</a>, organized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Church</a> in celebration of holy days and other important events.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a></div>
<p>In the 15th century performing arts, along with the arts in general, saw a revival as the Renaissance began in Italy and spread throughout Europe plays, some of which incorporated dance, which were performed and <a href="/wiki/Domenico_da_Piacenza" title="Domenico da Piacenza">Domenico da Piacenza</a> credited with the first use of the term <i>ballo</i> (in <i>De Arte Saltandi et Choreas Ducendi</i>) instead of <i>danza</i> (dance) for his <i>baletti</i> or <i>balli</i>. The term eventually became <i><a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">Ballet</a></i>. The first Ballet <i>per se</i> is thought to be <a href="/wiki/Balthasar_de_Beaujoyeulx" title="Balthasar de Beaujoyeulx">Balthasar de Beaujoyeulx</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ballet_Comique_de_la_Reine" title="Ballet Comique de la Reine">Ballet Comique de la Reine</a> (1581).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1229" data-file-width="1780" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg/240px-Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg/360px-Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg/480px-Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jan_Miel_%E2%80%93_Actors_from_the_Commedia_dell%E2%80%99Arte_on_a_Wagon_in_a_Town_Square.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Commedia_dell%27arte" title="Commedia dell'arte">Commedia dell'arte</a> troupe on a wagon</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Miel" title="Jan Miel">Jan Miel</a>, 1640</div></div></div>
<p>By the mid-16th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commedia_Dell%27arte" title="Commedia Dell'arte">Commedia Dell'arte</a> became popular in Europe, introducing the use of <a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">improvisation</a>. This period also introduced the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabethan_masque&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elizabethan masque (page does not exist)">Elizabethan masque</a>, featuring music, dance and elaborate costumes as well as professional theatrical companies in England. <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s plays in the late 16th century developed from this new class of professional performance.
</p><p>In 1597, the first opera, <a href="/wiki/Dafne" title="Dafne">Dafne</a> was performed and throughout the 17th century, opera would rapidly become the entertainment of choice for the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a> in most of Europe, and eventually for large numbers of people living in cities and towns throughout Europe.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The introduction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proscenium_arch" title="Proscenium arch">proscenium arch</a> in Italy during the 17th century established the traditional theatre form that persists to this day. Meanwhile, in England, the <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritans</a> forbade acting, bringing a halt to performing arts that lasted until 1660. After that, women began to appear in both French and English plays. The French introduced a formal dance instruction in the late 17th century.
</p><p>It is also during this time that the first plays were performed in the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">American Colonies</a>.
</p><p>During the 18th century, the introduction of the popular <a href="/wiki/Comic_opera" title="Comic opera">opera buffa</a> brought opera to the masses as an accessible form of performance. <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Figaro" title="The Marriage of Figaro">The Marriage of Figaro</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">Don Giovanni</a></i> are landmarks of the late 18th century opera.
</p><p>At the turn of the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_movement" title="Romantic movement">Romantic movement</a> ushered in a new era that led first to the spectacles of <a href="/wiki/Grand_opera" title="Grand opera">grand opera</a> and then to the musical dramas of <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Giuseppe Verdi</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Gesamtkunstwerk" title="Gesamtkunstwerk">Gesamtkunstwerk</a></i> (total work of art) of the operas of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> leading directly to the music of the 20th century.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vaslav_Nijinsky,_1912.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="518" data-file-width="362" decoding="async" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Vaslav_Nijinsky%2C_1912.jpg/170px-Vaslav_Nijinsky%2C_1912.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Vaslav_Nijinsky%2C_1912.jpg/255px-Vaslav_Nijinsky%2C_1912.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Vaslav_Nijinsky%2C_1912.jpg/340px-Vaslav_Nijinsky%2C_1912.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vaslav_Nijinsky,_1912.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Vaslav_Nijinsky" title="Vaslav Nijinsky">Vaslav Nijinsky</a> dancing the Faun in <i><a href="/wiki/Afternoon_of_a_Faun_(Nijinsky)" title="Afternoon of a Faun (Nijinsky)">L'après-midi d'un faune</a></i> (1912)</div></div></div>
<p>The 19th century was a period of growth for the performing arts for all social classes, technical advances such as the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gaslight</a> to theatres, <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">burlesque</a>, minstrel dancing, and variety theatre. In ballet, women make great progress in the previously male-dominated art.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Modern_dance" title="Modern dance">Modern dance</a> began in the late 19th century and early 20th century in response to the restrictions of traditional ballet. 
The arrival of <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Diaghilev" title="Sergei Diaghilev">Sergei Diaghilev</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ballets_Russes" title="Ballets Russes">Ballets Russes</a> (1909–1929) revolutionized ballet and the performing arts generally throughout the Western world, most importantly through Diaghilev's emphasis on collaboration, which brought choreographers, dancers, set designers/artists, composers and musicians together to revitalize and revolutionize ballet. It is extremely complex.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Stanislavski" title="Konstantin Stanislavski">Konstantin Stanislavski</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanislavski_System" title="Stanislavski System">"System"</a> revolutionized acting in the early 20th century, and continues to have a major influence on actors of stage and screen to the current day. Both <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">impressionism</a> and modern realism were introduced to the stage during this period.
</p><p>With the invention of the motion picture in the late 19th century by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> and the growth of the <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">motion picture industry in Hollywood</a> in the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> became a dominant performance medium throughout the 20th and 21st centuries.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">Rhythm and blues</a>, a cultural phenomenon of black America, rose to prominence in the early 20th century, influencing a range of later popular music styles internationally.
</p><p>In the 1930s <a href="/wiki/Jean_Rosenthal" title="Jean Rosenthal">Jean Rosenthal</a> introduced what would become modern <a href="/wiki/Stage_lighting" title="Stage lighting">stage lighting</a>, changing the nature of the stage as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadway_musical" title="Broadway musical">Broadway musical</a> became a phenomenon in the United States.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_performing_arts">Eastern performing arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Eastern performing arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The earliest recorded theatrical event dates back to 2000 BC with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_plays" title="Passion plays">passion plays</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>. The story of the god <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a> was performed annually at festivals throughout the civilization, marking the known beginning of a long relationship between theatre and religion.
</p><p>The most popular forms of theater in the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Islamic world</a> were <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">puppet</a> theatre (which included hand puppets, <a href="/wiki/Shadow_play" title="Shadow play">shadow plays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marionette" title="Marionette">marionette</a> productions) and live <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_play" title="Passion play">passion plays</a> known as <i>ta'ziya</i>, where actors re-enact episodes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_history" title="Muslim history">Muslim history</a>. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shia Islamic</a> plays revolved around the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shahid" title="Shahid">shaheed</a></i> (martyrdom) of <a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali</a>'s sons <a href="/wiki/Hasan_ibn_Ali" title="Hasan ibn Ali">Hasan ibn Ali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Husayn_ibn_Ali" title="Husayn ibn Ali">Husayn ibn Ali</a>. Live secular plays were known as <i>akhraja</i>, recorded in medieval <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adab_(behavior)" title="Adab (behavior)">adab</a></i> literature, though they were less common than puppetry and <i>ta'ziya</i> theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Valiollah_Torabi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Valiollah_Torabi.jpg/220px-Valiollah_Torabi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Valiollah_Torabi.jpg/330px-Valiollah_Torabi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Valiollah_Torabi.jpg/440px-Valiollah_Torabi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Valiollah_Torabi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Valiollah Torabi, Iranian naqqāl (storyteller) of Shahnameh.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Persian_theatre" title="Persian theatre">Persian theatre</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> there are other forms of theatrical events such as <i>Naghali</i> or <i>Naqqāli</i> (story telling), <i>ٰRu-Howzi</i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Siah-Bazi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Siah-Bazi (page does not exist)">Siah-Bazi</a></i>, <i>Parde-Khani</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Mareke_giri" title="Mareke giri">Mareke giri</a></i>. Prior to the twentieth century, storytelling was the most recognized form of entertainment, although today, some forms still remain. One form, <i>Naghali,</i> was traditionally performed in coffeehouses where the storytellers, or <i>Naghals (Naqqāls),</i> only recited sections of a story at a time, thus retaining regular cliental. These stories were based on events of historical or religious importance and many referenced poetries from the <a href="/wiki/Shahnameh" title="Shahnameh">Shahnameh</a>. Oftentimes these stories were altered to bond with the atmosphere or mood of the audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theatre_in_India" title="Theatre in India">Theatre in India</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_drama" title="Sanskrit drama">Sanskrit drama</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg/220px-Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg/330px-Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg/440px-Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bharatanatyam_is_a_major_form_of_Indian_classical_dance_that_originated_in_the_state_of_Tamil_Nadu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bharatanatyam" title="Bharatanatyam">Bharatanatyam</a> an Indian classical dance originated in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gotikua_dance.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Gotikua_dance.jpg/220px-Gotikua_dance.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Gotikua_dance.jpg/330px-Gotikua_dance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Gotikua_dance.jpg/440px-Gotikua_dance.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gotikua_dance.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gotikua folk dance is one of the well known performance performed by all boys group dressed in Indian ladies attire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saree" title="Saree">Saree</a></div></div></div>
<p>Folk theatre and dramatics can be traced to the religious ritualism of the <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">Vedic peoples</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2nd_millennium_BC" title="2nd millennium BC">2nd millennium BC</a>. This folk theatre of the misty past was mixed with dance, food, ritualism, plus a depiction of events from daily life. The last element made it the origin of the classical theatre of later times. Many historians, notably D. D. Kosambi, Debiprasad Chattopadhyaya, Adya Rangacharaya, etc. have referred to the prevalence of ritualism amongst <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Aryans" title="Indo-Aryans">Indo-Aryan</a> tribes in which some members of the tribe acted as if they were wild animals, and some others were the hunters. Those who acted as mammals like goats, buffaloes, reindeer, monkeys, etc. were chased by those playing the role of hunters.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bharata_Muni" title="Bharata Muni">Bharata Muni</a> (fl. 5th–2nd century BC) was an ancient Indian writer best known for writing the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natya_Shastra_of_Bharata" title="Natya Shastra of Bharata">Natya Shastra of Bharata</a></i>, a theoretical treatise on Indian performing arts, including theatre, dance, acting, and music, which has been compared to <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Poetics</a></i>. Bharata is often known as the father of Indian theatrical arts. His <i>Natya Shastra</i> seems to be the first attempt to develop the technique or rather art, of drama in a systematic manner. The Natya Shastra tells us not only what is to be portrayed in a drama, but how the portrayal is to be done. Drama, as Bharata Muni says, is the imitation of men and their doings (<i>loka-vritti</i>). As men and their doings have to be respected on the stage, so drama in Sanskrit is also known by the term <i>roopaka</i>, which means portrayal.
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i> can be considered the first recognized plays that originated in India. These epics provided the inspiration to the earliest Indian dramatists and they do it even today. Indian dramatists such as <a href="/wiki/Bh%C4%81sa" title="Bhāsa">Bhāsa</a> in the 2nd century BC wrote plays that were heavily inspired by the <i>Ramayana</i> and <i>Mahabharata</i>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C4%81lid%C4%81sa" title="Kālidāsa">Kālidāsa</a> in the 1st century BC, is arguably considered to be ancient <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>'s greatest dramatist. Three famous romantic plays written by Kālidāsa are the <i><a href="/wiki/M%C4%81lavik%C4%81gnimitram" title="Mālavikāgnimitram">Mālavikāgnimitram</a></i> (<i>Mālavikā and Agnimitra</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Vikram%C5%8Drva%C5%9B%C4%AByam" title="Vikramōrvaśīyam">Vikramōrvaśīyam</a></i> (<i>Pertaining to Vikrama and Urvashi</i>), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Recognition_of_Sakuntala" title="The Recognition of Sakuntala">Abhijñānaśākuntala</a></i> (<i>The Recognition of Shakuntala</i>). The last was inspired by a story in the <i>Mahabharata</i> and is the most famous. It was the first to be translated into English and German. In comparison to Bhāsa, who drew heavily from the epics, Kālidāsa can be considered an original playwright.
</p><p>The next great Indian dramatist was <a href="/wiki/Bhavabhuti" title="Bhavabhuti">Bhavabhuti</a> (c. 7th century). He is said to have written the following three plays: <i>Malati-Madhava</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mahaviracharita" title="Mahaviracharita">Mahaviracharita</a></i> and <i>Uttar Ramacharita</i>. Among these three, the last two cover between them, the entire epic of <i>Ramayana</i>. The powerful Indian emperor <a href="/wiki/Harsha" title="Harsha">Harsha</a> (606–648) is credited with having written three plays: the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Ratnavali" title="Ratnavali">Ratnavali</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priyadarsika" title="Priyadarsika">Priyadarsika</a></i>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/Nagananda" title="Nagananda">Nagananda</a></i>. Many other dramatists followed during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.
</p><p>There were many performing art forms in the southern part of India, Kerala is such a state with different such art forms like <a href="/wiki/Koodiyattam" title="Koodiyattam">Koodiyattam</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nangyarkoothu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nangyarkoothu (page does not exist)">Nangyarkoothu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kathakali" title="Kathakali">Kathakali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chakyar_koothu" title="Chakyar koothu">Chakyar koothu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thirayattam" title="Thirayattam">Thirayattam</a> and there were many prominent artists like <a href="/wiki/Ramanchakyar" title="Ramanchakyar">Painkulam Raman Chakyar</a> and others.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_theatre" title="Chinese theatre">Chinese theatre</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4608" data-file-width="3456" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg/170px-Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg/255px-Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg/340px-Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hand_shadow_drama_3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hand shadow drama, China</div></div></div>
<p>There are references to theatrical entertainments in China as early as 1500 BC during the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>; they often involved music, clowning and acrobatic displays.
</p><p>The Tang dynasty is sometimes known as "The Age of 1000 Entertainments". During this era, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Xuanzong_of_Tang_China" title="Emperor Xuanzong of Tang China">Emperor Xuanzong</a> formed an acting school known as the Children of the <a href="/wiki/Pear_Garden" title="Pear Garden">Pear Garden</a> to produce a form of drama that was primarily musical.
</p><p>During the Han Dynasty, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shadow_puppetry" title="Shadow puppetry">shadow puppetry</a> first emerged as a recognized form of theatre in China. There were two distinct forms of shadow puppetry, Cantonese southern and Pekingese northern. The two styles were differentiated by the method of making the puppets and the positioning of the rods on the puppets, as opposed to the type of play performed by the puppets. Both styles generally performed plays depicting great adventure and fantasy, rarely was this very stylized form of theatre used for political propaganda. Cantonese shadow puppets were the larger of the two. They were built using thick leather that created more substantial shadows. Symbolic color was also very prevalent; a black face represented honesty, a red one bravery. The rods used to control Cantonese puppets were attached perpendicular to the puppets' heads. Thus, they were not seen by the audience when the shadow was created. Pekingese puppets were more delicate and smaller. They were created out of thin, translucent leather usually taken from the belly of a donkey. They were painted with vibrant paints, thus they cast a very colorful shadow. The thin rods that controlled their movements were attached to a leather collar at the neck of the puppet. The rods ran parallel to the bodies of the puppet then turned at a ninety-degree angle to connect to the neck. While these rods were visible when the shadow was cast, they laid outside the shadow of the puppet; thus, they did not interfere with the appearance of the figure. The rods attached at the necks to facilitate the use of multiple heads with one body. When the heads were not being used, they were stored in a muslin book or fabric lined box. The heads were always removed at night. This was in keeping with the old superstition that if left intact, the puppets would come to life at night. Some puppeteers went so far as to store the heads in one book and the bodies in another, to further reduce the possibility of reanimating puppets. Shadow puppetry is said to have reached its highest point of artistic development in the 11th century before becoming a tool of the government.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>, there were many popular plays involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrobatic" title="Acrobatic">acrobatics</a> and music. These developed in the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> into a more sophisticated form with a four- or five-act structure. Yuan drama spread across China and diversified into numerous regional forms, the best known of which is Beijing Opera, which is still popular today.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ramakien" title="Ramakien">Ramakien</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG/220px-WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG/330px-WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG/440px-WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WatPhraKeaw_Ramayana_Chariot.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hanuman" title="Hanuman">Hanuman</a> on his chariot, a scene from the <a href="/wiki/Ramakien" title="Ramakien">Ramakien</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wat_Phra_Kaew" title="Wat Phra Kaew">Wat Phra Kaew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a></div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, it has been a tradition from the Middle Ages to stage plays based on plots drawn from Indian epics. In particular, the theatrical version of Thailand's national epic <i><a href="/wiki/Ramakien" title="Ramakien">Ramakien</a></i>, a version of the Indian <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i>, remains popular in Thailand even today.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cambodia">Cambodia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cambodia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, inscriptions dating back to the 6th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">AD</a> indicates evidence of dancers at a local temple and using puppetry for religious plays. At the ancient capital <a href="/wiki/Angkor_Wat" title="Angkor Wat">Angkor Wat</a>, stories from the Indian epics <i>Ramayana</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i> have been carved on the walls of temples and palaces. Similar reliefs are found at <a href="/wiki/Borobudur" title="Borobudur">Borobudur</a> in Indonesia.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performing_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, the famous <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a> poem <a href="/wiki/Ibong_Adarna" title="Ibong Adarna">Ibong Adarna</a>, originally titled <i>"Korido at Buhay na Pinagdaanan ng Tatlong Prinsipeng Magkakapatid na anak nina Haring Fernando at Reyna Valeriana sa Kahariang Berbania"</i> (English: "<a href="/wiki/Corrido" title="Corrido">Corrido</a> and Life Lived by the Three Princes, children of King Fernando and Queen Valeriana in the Kingdom of Berbania") from the 16th century was written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_la_Cruz_(writer)" title="José de la Cruz (writer)">José de la Cruz</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_era" title="Spanish era">Spanish era</a>. Aside from theatrical performances, different films were produced by different film studios/ television productions. The first produced "Ang Ibong Adarna" film was produced by <a href="/wiki/LVN_Pictures" title="LVN Pictures">LVN Pictures</a>, the biggest film studio in the history of the Philippines.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Florante_at_Laura" title="Florante at Laura">Florante at Laura</a> is an <i>"<a href="/wiki/Awit_(poem)" title="Awit (poem)">awit</a>"</i> or a poem consisting of 12-syllable quatrains with the full title <i>"Pinagdaanang Buhay ni Florante at ni Laura sa Kahariang Albanya"</i> (English: "The History of Florante and Laura in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Albania_(medieval)" title="Kingdom of Albania (medieval)">Kingdom of Albania</a>") was written by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Balagtas" title="Francisco Balagtas">Francisco Balagtas</a> in 1838 during his imprisonment dedicated to his sweetheart Maria Asuncuion Rivera (nicknamed "M.A.R.", referenced to as "Selya"). The poem has a special part entitled "<a href="/wiki/Kay_Selya" title="Kay Selya">Kay Selya</a>" (English: "For Celia") specially dedicated for Rivera.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine" title="Philippine">Philippine</a>'s national hero, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Rizal" title="José Rizal">José Rizal</a> who is also a novelist, created the two famous poems in the Philippines, <i><a href="/wiki/Noli_Me_T%C3%A1ngere_(novel)" title="Noli Me Tángere (novel)">Noli Me Tángere</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "Touch me not", with an acute accent added on the final word in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">Spanish orthography</a>) (1887) that describes perceived inequities of the Spanish Catholic friars and the ruling government and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Filibusterismo" title="El Filibusterismo">El Filibusterismo</a></i> (translations: The filibusterism; The Subversive or The Subversion, as in the Locsín English translation, are also possible translations, also known by its alternative English title The Reign of Greed) (1891). The novel's dark theme departs dramatically from the previous novel's hopeful and romantic atmosphere, signifying Ibarra's resort to solving his country's issues through violent means, after his previous attempt in reforming the country's system made no effect and seemed impossible with the corrupt attitude of the Spaniards toward the Filipinos. These novels were written during the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Colonization_of_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Colonization of the Philippines (page does not exist)">colonization of the Philippines</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>.
</p><p>All of these literary pieces were under the curriculum of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=K-12_Program_for_Junior_High_Schools&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="K-12 Program for Junior High Schools (page does not exist)">K-12 Program for Junior High Schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibong_Adarna" title="Ibong Adarna">Ibong Adarna</a> is under the Grade 7 Curriculum; <a href="/wiki/Florante_at_Laura" title="Florante at Laura">Florante at Laura</a> (Grade 8); <i>Noli Me Tángere</i> (Grade 9); and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Filibusterismo" title="El Filibusterismo">El Filibusterismo</a> (Grade 10).
</p>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Noh" title="Noh">Noh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bunraku" title="Bunraku">Bunraku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">Kabuki</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Butoh" title="Butoh">Butoh</a></div>
<p>During the 14th century, there were small companies of actors in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> who performed short, sometimes vulgar comedies. A director of one of these companies, Kan'ami (1333–1384), had a son, <a href="/wiki/Zeami_Motokiyo" title="Zeami Motokiyo">Zeami Motokiyo</a> (1363–1443), who was considered one of the finest child actors in Japan. When Kan'ami's company performed for <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshimitsu" title="Ashikaga Yoshimitsu">Ashikaga Yoshimitsu</a> (1358–1408), the <span title="Japanese-language romanization"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Shōgun">shōgun</a></i></span> of Japan, he implored Zeami to have a court education for his arts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After Zeami succeeded his father, he continued to perform and adapt his style into what is today <a href="/wiki/Noh" title="Noh">Noh</a>. A mixture of <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomime</a> and vocal acrobatics, the Noh style of theatre has become one of Japan's most refined forms of theatrical performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Japan, after a long period of civil wars and political disarray, was unified and at peace primarily due to <span title="Japanese-language romanization"><i lang="ja-Latn">shōgun</i></span> <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu" title="Tokugawa Ieyasu">Tokugawa Ieyasu</a> (1600–1668). However, alarmed at the increasing numbers of Christians within the country due to the proselytizing efforts of Christian missionaries, he cut off contact from Japan to Europe and China and outlawed Christianity. When peace did come, a flourish of cultural influence and growing merchant class demanded its own entertainment. The first form of theatre to flourish was Ningyō jōruri (commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Bunraku" title="Bunraku">Bunraku</a>). The founder of and main contributor to Ningyō jōruri, <a href="/wiki/Chikamatsu_Monzaemon" title="Chikamatsu Monzaemon">Chikamatsu Monzaemon</a> (1653–1725), turned his form of theatre into a true art form. Ningyō jōruri is a highly stylized form of theatre using puppets, today about <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020198016">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">1</span>⁄<span class="den">3rd</span></span> the size of a human. The men who control the puppets train their entire lives to become master puppeteers, when they can then operate the puppet's head and right arm and choose to show their faces during the performance. The other puppeteers, controlling the less important limbs of the puppet, cover themselves and their faces in a black suit, to imply their invisibility. The dialogue is handled by a single person, who uses varied tones of voice and speaking manners to simulate different characters. Chikamatsu wrote thousands of plays during his lifetime, most of which are still used today.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">Kabuki</a> began shortly after Bunraku, legend has it by an actress named Okuni, who lived around the end of the 16th century. Most of kabuki's material came from Noh and Bunraku, and its erratic dance-type movements are also an effect of Bunraku. However, kabuki is less formal and more distant than Noh, yet very popular among the Japanese public. Actors are trained in many varied things including dancing, singing, pantomime, and even acrobatics. Kabuki was first performed by young girls, then by young boys, and by the end of the 16th century, kabuki companies consisted of all men. The men who portrayed women on stage were specifically trained to elicit the essence of a woman in their subtle movements and gestures.
</p>

<p>Oftentimes, Melanesian dance exhibits a cultural theme of masculinity where leadership and a unique skill set are important for sharing with the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-11">[11]</a></sup> These dances demonstrate the soldiery of a man; however they can also represent profitability such as encouraging conflict resolutions or healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The costumes of impersonating dancers incorporate large masks and unhuman-like characteristics that act to imitate mythical figures. The music can also act as a voice for these magical personas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
