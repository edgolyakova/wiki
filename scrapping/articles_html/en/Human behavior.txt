<p><b>Human behavior</b> is the potential and expressed capacity (<a href="/wiki/Energy_(psychological)" title="Energy (psychological)">mentally</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_activity" title="Physical activity">physically</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_actions" title="Social actions">socially</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individuals</a> or groups to respond to internal and external <a href="/wiki/Stimulation" title="Stimulation">stimuli</a> throughout their life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Behavior is driven by genetic and environmental factors that affect an individual. Behavior is also driven, in part, by <a href="/wiki/Thought" title="Thought">thoughts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feeling" title="Feeling">feelings</a>, which provide insight into individual <a href="/wiki/Psyche_(psychology)" title="Psyche (psychology)">psyche</a>, revealing such things as <a href="/wiki/Attitude_(psychology)" title="Attitude (psychology)">attitudes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_(personal_and_cultural)" title="Value (personal and cultural)">values</a>. Human behavior is shaped by <a href="/wiki/Trait_theory" title="Trait theory">psychological traits</a>, as personality types vary from person to person, producing different actions and behavior.
</p><p>Social behavior accounts for actions directed at others. It is concerned with the considerable influence of <a href="/wiki/Social_relation" title="Social relation">social interaction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">interpersonal relationships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Conflict_(process)" title="Conflict (process)">conflict</a>. Some behaviors are common while others are unusual. The acceptability of behavior depends upon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_norms" title="Social norms">social norms</a> and is regulated by various means of <a href="/wiki/Social_control" title="Social control">social control</a>. Social norms also <a href="/wiki/Operant_conditioning" title="Operant conditioning">condition</a> behavior, whereby humans are <a href="/wiki/Peer_pressure" title="Peer pressure">pressured</a> into following certain rules and displaying certain behaviors that are deemed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_acceptance" title="Social acceptance">acceptable</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_deviance" title="Social deviance">unacceptable</a> depending on the given society or culture.
</p><p>Cognitive behavior accounts for actions of obtaining and using <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>. It is concerned with how information is learned and passed on, as well as creative application of knowledge and personal beliefs such as <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>. Physiological behavior accounts for actions to maintain the body. It is concerned with basic bodily functions as well as measures taken to maintain health. Economic behavior accounts for actions regarding the development, organization, and use of materials as well as other forms of <a href="/wiki/Work_(human_activity)" title="Work (human activity)">work</a>. Ecological behavior accounts for actions involving the ecosystem. It is concerned with how humans interact with other organisms and how the environment shapes human behavior.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Study">Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_ethology" title="Human ethology">Human ethology</a></div>
<p>Human behavior is studied by the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>, which include <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethology</a>, and their various branches and schools of thought. The study of human behavior includes how the human mind <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> and how the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a> controls behavior. The <a href="/wiki/Nature_versus_nurture" title="Nature versus nurture">nature versus nurture</a> debate considers how behavior is affected by genetic and environmental factors. <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind" title="Philosophy of mind">Philosophy of mind</a> considers aspects such as <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mind%E2%80%93body_problem" title="Mind–body problem">mind–body problem</a>, and malleability of human behavior. The study of human behavior sometimes receives public attention due to its intersection with cultural issues, including <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexuality</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">social inequality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Twin_study" title="Twin study">Twin studies</a> are a common method by which human behavior is studied. <a href="/wiki/Twin" title="Twin">Twins</a> with identical <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a> can be compared to isolate genetic and environmental factors in behavior. Lifestyle, susceptibility to disease, and unhealthy behaviors have been identified to have both genetic and environmental indicators through twin studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_behavior">Social behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">Sociology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing,_%22ojigi%22_(hand-colored_albumen_print,_Japan).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6051" data-file-width="4191" decoding="async" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing%2C_%22ojigi%22_%28hand-colored_albumen_print%2C_Japan%29.jpg/170px-Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing%2C_%22ojigi%22_%28hand-colored_albumen_print%2C_Japan%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing%2C_%22ojigi%22_%28hand-colored_albumen_print%2C_Japan%29.jpg/255px-Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing%2C_%22ojigi%22_%28hand-colored_albumen_print%2C_Japan%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing%2C_%22ojigi%22_%28hand-colored_albumen_print%2C_Japan%29.jpg/340px-Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing%2C_%22ojigi%22_%28hand-colored_albumen_print%2C_Japan%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Circa_1880_Two_women_bowing,_%22ojigi%22_(hand-colored_albumen_print,_Japan).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Women bowing in Japan. (c. 1880)</div></div></div>
<p>Human social behavior is the behavior that considers other humans, including communication and cooperation. It is highly complex and structured, based on advanced <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_mind" title="Theory of mind">theory of mind</a> that allows humans to attribute thoughts and actions to one another. Through social behavior, humans have developed <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> distinct from other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Human social behavior is governed by a combination of biological factors that affect all humans and cultural factors that change depending on upbringing and societal norms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuck20071–5_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuck20071–5-6">[6]</a></sup> Human communication is based heavily on <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, typically through <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a> or <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nonverbal_communication" title="Nonverbal communication">Nonverbal communication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paralanguage" title="Paralanguage">paralanguage</a> can modify the meaning of communications by demonstrating ideas and intent through physical and vocal behaviors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuck200710–14_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuck200710–14-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_norms">Social norms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social norms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Human behavior in a society is governed by <a href="/wiki/Social_norm" title="Social norm">social norms</a>. Social norms are unwritten expectations that members of society have for one another. These norms are ingrained in the particular culture that they emerge from, and humans often follow them unconsciously or without deliberation. These norms affect every aspect of life in human society, including <a href="/wiki/Decorum" title="Decorum">decorum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Right_to_property" title="Right to property">property rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contractual agreement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(philosophy)" title="Meaning (philosophy)">meaning</a>. Many norms facilitate coordination between members of society and prove mutually beneficial, such as norms regarding communication and agreements. Norms are enforced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_pressure" title="Social pressure">social pressure</a>, and individuals that violate social norms risk <a href="/wiki/Social_exclusion" title="Social exclusion">social exclusion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Systems of <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> are used to guide human behavior to determine what is moral. Humans are distinct from other animals in the use of ethical systems to determine behavior. Ethical behavior is human behavior that takes into consideration how actions will affect others and whether behaviors will be optimal for others. What constitutes ethical behavior is determined by the individual <a href="/wiki/Value_judgment" title="Value judgment">value judgments</a> of the person and the collective social norms regarding right and wrong. Value judgments are intrinsic to people of all cultures, though the specific systems used to evaluate them may vary. These systems may be derived from <a href="/wiki/Divine_law" title="Divine law">divine law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_law" title="Natural law">natural law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_authority" title="Civil authority">civil authority</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>, or a combination of these and other principles. <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">Altruism</a> is an associated behavior in which humans consider the welfare of others equally or preferentially to their own. While other animals engage in biological altruism, ethical altruism is unique to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deviance_(sociology)" title="Deviance (sociology)">Deviance</a> is behavior that violates social norms. As social norms vary between individuals and cultures, the nature and severity of a deviant act is subjective. What is considered deviant by a society may also change over time as new social norms are developed. Deviance is punished by other individuals through <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">social stigma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Censure" title="Censure">censure</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a>. Many deviant actions are recognized as <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crimes</a> and punished through a system of <a href="/wiki/Criminal_justice" title="Criminal justice">criminal justice</a>. Deviant actions may be punished to prevent harm to others, to maintain a particular worldview and way of life, or to enforce principles of morality and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decency" title="Decency">decency</a>. Cultures also attribute positive value to certain physical traits, causing individuals that do not have these traits to be seen as deviant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpersonal_relationships">Interpersonal relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interpersonal relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">Interpersonal relationship</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak,_Alaska,_1929,_Edward_S._Curtis_(restored).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2872" data-file-width="3751" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak%2C_Alaska%2C_1929%2C_Edward_S._Curtis_%28restored%29.jpg/220px-Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak%2C_Alaska%2C_1929%2C_Edward_S._Curtis_%28restored%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak%2C_Alaska%2C_1929%2C_Edward_S._Curtis_%28restored%29.jpg/330px-Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak%2C_Alaska%2C_1929%2C_Edward_S._Curtis_%28restored%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak%2C_Alaska%2C_1929%2C_Edward_S._Curtis_%28restored%29.jpg/440px-Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak%2C_Alaska%2C_1929%2C_Edward_S._Curtis_%28restored%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inupiat_Family_from_Noatak,_Alaska,_1929,_Edward_S._Curtis_(restored).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A family in <a href="/wiki/Noatak,_Alaska" title="Noatak, Alaska">Noatak</a>, Alaska. (1929)</div></div></div>
<p>Interpersonal relationships can be evaluated by the specific choices and emotions between two individuals, or they can be evaluated by the broader societal context of how such a relationship is expected to function. Relationships are developed through communication, which creates intimacy, expresses emotions, and develops identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuck200710–14_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuck200710–14-7">[7]</a></sup> An individual's interpersonal relationships form a <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">social group</a> in which individuals all communicate and socialize with one another, and these social groups are connected by additional relationships. Human social behavior is affected not only by individual relationships, but also by how behaviors in one relationship may affect others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuck2007107_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuck2007107-11">[11]</a></sup> Individuals that actively seek out social interactions are <a href="/wiki/Extraversion_and_introversion" title="Extraversion and introversion">extraverts</a>, and those that do not are introverts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Romance_(love)" title="Romance (love)">Romantic love</a> is a significant <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_attraction" title="Interpersonal attraction">interpersonal attraction</a> toward another. Its nature varies by culture, but it is often contingent on gender, occurring in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Sexual_attraction" title="Sexual attraction">sexual attraction</a> and being either <a href="/wiki/Heterosexuality" title="Heterosexuality">heterosexual</a> or <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexual</a>. It takes different forms and is associated with many individual emotions. Many cultures place a higher emphasis on romantic love than other forms of interpersonal attraction. <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">Marriage</a> is a union between two people, though whether it is associated with romantic love is dependent on the culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuck200756–60_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuck200756–60-13">[13]</a></sup> Individuals that are closely related by <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a> form a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>. There are many variations on family structures that may include parents and children as well as <a href="/wiki/Stepchild" title="Stepchild">stepchildren</a> or extended relatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuck2007121–125_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuck2007121–125-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_conflict">Politics and conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Politics and conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">Political science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theories_of_political_behavior" title="Theories of political behavior">Theories of political behavior</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Conflict_(process)" title="Conflict (process)">Conflict (process)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:5tavaszi_08_n.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="558" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/5tavaszi_08_n.jpg/220px-5tavaszi_08_n.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/5tavaszi_08_n.jpg/330px-5tavaszi_08_n.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/5tavaszi_08_n.jpg/440px-5tavaszi_08_n.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:5tavaszi_08_n.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A depiction of men fighting in the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Kom%C3%A1rom_(1849)" title="First Battle of Komárom (1849)">First Battle of Komárom</a>. (1849)</div></div></div>
<p>When humans make decisions as a group, they engage in politics. Humans have evolved to engage in behaviors of <a href="/wiki/Self-interest" title="Self-interest">self-interest</a>, but this also includes behaviors that facilitate cooperation rather than conflict in collective settings. Individuals will often form <a href="/wiki/In-group_and_out-group" title="In-group and out-group">in-group and out-group</a> perceptions, through which individuals cooperate with the in-group and compete with the out-group. This causes behaviors such as unconsciously conforming, passively obeying authority, taking pleasure in the misfortune of opponents, initiating hostility toward out-group members, artificially creating out-groups when none exist, and punishing those that do not comply with the standards of the in-group. These behaviors lead to the creation of <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political systems</a> that enforce in-group standards and norms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>When humans oppose one another, it creates conflict. It may occur when the involved parties have a <a href="/wiki/Disagreement" title="Disagreement">disagreement</a> of opinion, when one party obstructs the goals of another, or when parties experience negative emotions such as <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a> toward one another. Conflicts purely of disagreement are often resolved through communication or <a href="/wiki/Negotiation" title="Negotiation">negotiation</a>, but incorporation of emotional or obstructive aspects can escalate conflict. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpersonal_conflict" title="Interpersonal conflict">Interpersonal conflict</a> is that between specific individuals or groups of individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Social_conflict" title="Social conflict">Social conflict</a> is that between different social groups or demographics. This form of conflict often takes place when groups in society are marginalized, do not have the resources they desire, wish to instigate social change, or wish to resist social change. Significant social conflict can cause <a href="/wiki/Civil_disorder" title="Civil disorder">civil disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_conflict" title="International conflict">International conflict</a> is that between nations or governments. It may be solved through <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomacy</a> or <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_behavior">Cognitive behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cognitive behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">Cognition</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Andriyaka_mk_(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="852" decoding="async" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Andriyaka_mk_%281%29.jpg/220px-Andriyaka_mk_%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Andriyaka_mk_%281%29.jpg/330px-Andriyaka_mk_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Andriyaka_mk_%281%29.jpg/440px-Andriyaka_mk_%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andriyaka_mk_(1).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>People being taught to paint in <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">Volgograd</a>, Russia. (2013)</div></div></div>
<p>Human cognition is distinct from that of other animals. This is derived from biological traits of human cognition, but also from shared <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> and development passed down culturally. Humans are able to learn from one another due to advanced theory of mind that allows knowledge to be obtained through <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>. The use of language allows humans to directly pass knowledge to one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a> has <a href="/wiki/Neuroplasticity" title="Neuroplasticity">neuroplasticity</a>, allowing it to modify its features in response to new experiences. This facilitates <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a> in humans and leads to behaviors of <a href="/wiki/Practice_(learning_method)" title="Practice (learning method)">practice</a>, allowing the development of new skills in individual humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans engage in <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">inferences</a> with a limited amount of information. Most human reasoning is done automatically without conscious effort on the part of the individual. Reasoning is carried out by making generalizations from past experiences and applying them to new circumstances. Learned knowledge is acquired to make more accurate inferences about the subject. <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">Deductive reasoning</a> infers conclusions that are true based on <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logical</a> premises, while <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">inductive reasoning</a> infers what conclusions are likely to be true based on context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">Emotion</a> is a cognitive experience innate to humans. Basic emotions such as <a href="/wiki/Joy" title="Joy">joy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mental_distress" title="Mental distress">distress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">fear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surprise_(emotion)" title="Surprise (emotion)">surprise</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Disgust" title="Disgust">disgust</a> are common to all cultures, though social norms regarding the expression of emotion may vary. Other emotions come from higher cognition, such as <a href="/wiki/Love" title="Love">love</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(emotion)" title="Guilt (emotion)">guilt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shame" title="Shame">shame</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embarrassment" title="Embarrassment">embarrassment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pride" title="Pride">pride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Envy" title="Envy">envy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jealousy" title="Jealousy">jealousy</a>. These emotions develop over time rather than instantly and are more strongly influenced by cultural factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEvans20031–21_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvans20031–21-21">[21]</a></sup> Emotions are influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_information" title="Sensory information">sensory information</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mood_(psychology)" title="Mood (psychology)">moods</a> of <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sadness" title="Sadness">sadness</a>. Humans typically maintain a standard level of happiness or sadness determined by health and social relationships, though positive and negative events have short-term influences on mood. Humans often seek to improve the moods of one another through <a href="/wiki/Consolation" title="Consolation">consolation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Complaining" title="Complaining">venting</a>. Humans can also self-regulate mood through <a href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">exercise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEvans200347–_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvans200347–-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">Creativity</a> is the use of previous ideas or resources to produce something original. It allows for <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a>, adaptation to change, learning new information, and novel problem solving. Expression of creativity also supports <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>. Creativity includes personal creativity, in which a person presents new ideas <a href="/wiki/Authenticity_(philosophy)" title="Authenticity (philosophy)">authentically</a>, but it can also be expanded to social creativity, in which a community or society produces and recognizes ideas collectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Creativity is applied in typical human life to solve problems as they occur. It also leads humans to carry out <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>. Individuals engaging in advanced creative work typically have specialized knowledge in that field, and humans draw on this knowledge to develop novel ideas. In art, creativity is used to develop new artistic works, such as <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual art</a> or <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>. In science, those with knowledge in a particular scientific field can use <a href="/wiki/Trial_and_error" title="Trial and error">trial and error</a> to develop theories that more accurately explain phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_behavior" title="Religious behavior">Religious behavior</a> is a set of traditions that are followed based on the teachings of a religious belief system. The nature of religious behavior varies depending on the specific religious traditions. Most religious traditions involve variations of telling <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myths</a>, practicing <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">rituals</a>, making certain things <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>, adopting <a href="/wiki/Religious_symbol" title="Religious symbol">symbolism</a>, determining morality, experiencing <a href="/wiki/Altered_state_of_consciousness" title="Altered state of consciousness">altered states of consciousness</a>, and believing in <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> beings. Religious behavior is often demanding and has high time, energy, and material costs, and it conflicts with <a href="/wiki/Rational_choice_theory" title="Rational choice theory">rational choice</a> models of human behavior, though it does provide community-related benefits. Anthropologists offer competing theories as to why humans adopted religious behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Religious behavior is heavily influenced by social factors, and group involvement is significant in the development of an individual's religious behavior. Social structures such as <a href="/wiki/Religious_organization" title="Religious organization">religious organizations</a> or family units allow the sharing and coordination of religious behavior. These social connections reinforce the cognitive behaviors associated with religion, encouraging <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodoxy</a> and commitment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> According to a <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a> report, 54% of adults around the world state that religion is very important in their lives as of 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pew1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-pew1-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_behavior">Physiological behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Physiological behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Psychophysiology" title="Psychophysiology">Psychophysiology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg/170px-Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg/255px-Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg/340px-Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boy_eating_a_mango.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A boy eating in <a href="/wiki/Harare" title="Harare">Harare</a>, Zimbabwe. (2017)</div></div></div>
<p>Humans undergo many behaviors common to animals to support the processes of the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a>. Humans eat <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> to obtain <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>. These foods may be chosen for their nutritional value, but they may also be <a href="/wiki/Hedonic_hunger" title="Hedonic hunger">eaten for pleasure</a>. Eating often follows a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_preparation" title="Food preparation">food preparation</a> process to make it more enjoyable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Humans dispose of excess food through <a href="/wiki/Human_waste" title="Human waste">waste</a>. Excrement is often treated as taboo, particularly in developed and urban communities where <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> is more widely available and excrement has no value as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Humans also regularly engage in <a href="/wiki/Sleep" title="Sleep">sleep</a>, based on homeostatic and circadian factors. The <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a> causes humans to require sleep at a regular pattern and is typically calibrated to the day-night cycle and sleep-wake habits. Homeostasis is also be maintained, causing longer sleep longer after periods of <a href="/wiki/Sleep_deprivation" title="Sleep deprivation">sleep deprivation</a>. The human <a href="/wiki/Sleep_cycle" title="Sleep cycle">sleep cycle</a> takes place over 90 minutes, and it repeats 3-5 times during normal sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also unique behaviors that humans undergo to maintain physical health. Humans have developed <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> to prevent and treat illnesses. In industrialized nations, eating habits that favor better nutrition, hygienic behaviors that promote <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a>, medical treatment to eradicate diseases, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> significantly improve human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Humans can also engage in <a href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">exercise</a> beyond that required for survival to maintain health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Humans engage in <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a> to limit exposure to <a href="/wiki/Dirt" title="Dirt">dirt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. Some of these behaviors are adaptive while others are learned. Basic behaviors of disgust evolved as an adaptation to prevent contact with sources of pathogens, resulting in a biological aversion to <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Body_fluid" title="Body fluid">body fluids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotten_food" title="Rotten food">rotten food</a>, and animals that are commonly <a href="/wiki/Disease_vector" title="Disease vector">disease vectors</a>. <a href="/wiki/Personal_grooming" title="Personal grooming">Personal grooming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disposal_of_human_corpses" title="Disposal of human corpses">disposal of human corpses</a>, use of <a href="/wiki/Sewerage" title="Sewerage">sewerage</a>, and use of <a href="/wiki/Cleaning_agent" title="Cleaning agent">cleaning agents</a> are hygienic behaviors common to most human societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans are <a href="/wiki/Bipedalism" title="Bipedalism">bipedal</a> and move by <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a>. Human walking corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Bipedal_gait_cycle" title="Bipedal gait cycle">bipedal gait cycle</a>, which involves alternating heel contact and toe off with the ground and slight elevation and rotation of the <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a>. Balance while walking learned during the first 7–9 years of life, and individual humans develop unique <a href="/wiki/Gait" title="Gait">gaits</a> while learning to displace weight, adjust <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>, and correspond neural control with movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Humans can achieve higher speed by <a href="/wiki/Running" title="Running">running</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Endurance_running_hypothesis" title="Endurance running hypothesis">endurance running hypothesis</a> proposes that humans can outpace most other animals over long distances through running, though human running causes a higher rate of energy exertion. The human body self-regulates through <a href="/wiki/Perspiration" title="Perspiration">perspiration</a> during periods of exertion, allowing humans more endurance than other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The human <a href="/wiki/Hand" title="Hand">hand</a> is <a href="/wiki/Prehensility" title="Prehensility">prehensile</a> and capable of <a href="/wiki/Grasp" title="Grasp">grasping</a> objects and applying force with control over the hand's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dexterity" title="Dexterity">dexterity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grip_strength" title="Grip strength">grip strength</a>. This allows the use of complex <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a> by humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_behavior">Economic behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economic behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_economics" title="Behavioral economics">Behavioral economics</a></div>
<p>Humans engage in predictable behaviors when considering economic decisions, and these behaviors may or may not be <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rational</a>. Like all animals, humans make basic decisions through <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93benefit_analysis" title="Cost–benefit analysis">cost–benefit analysis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Risk%E2%80%93return_spectrum" title="Risk–return spectrum">risk–return spectrum</a>, though humans are able to contemplate these decisions more thoroughly. Human economic decision making is often <a href="/wiki/Reference_dependence" title="Reference dependence">reference dependent</a>, in which options are weighed in reference to the status quo rather than absolute gains and losses. Humans are also <a href="/wiki/Loss_aversion" title="Loss aversion">loss averse</a>, fearing loss rather than seeking gain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Advanced economic behavior developed in humans after the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> and the development of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. These developments led to a sustainable supply of resources that allowed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specialization_of_labor" title="Specialization of labor">specialization</a> in more complex societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeff198524–33_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeff198524–33-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Work_(human_activity)" title="Work (human activity)">Work (human activity)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2082" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg/220px-Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg/330px-Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg/440px-Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boskapssk%C3%B6tsel_i_ladug%C3%A5rden._Kvinna_sitter_och_mj%C3%B6lkar_en_mager_ko_medan_barfota_flicka_mockar_-_Nordiska_museet_-_NMA.0036603.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Women tending to farm animals in Mangskogs, Sweden. (1911)</div></div></div>
<p>The nature of human work is defined by the complexity of society. The simplest societies are <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribes</a> that work primarily for sustenance as <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>. In this sense, work is not a distinct activity but a constant that makes up all parts of life, as all members of the society must work consistently to stay alive. More advanced societies developed after the Neolithic Revolution, emphasizing work in agricultural and <a href="/wiki/Pastoralism" title="Pastoralism">pastoral</a> settings. In these societies, production is increased, ending the need for constant work and allowing some individuals to specialize and work in areas outside of food-production. This also created non-laborious work, as increasing occupational complexity required some individuals to specialize in technical knowledge and administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeff198524–33_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeff198524–33-38">[38]</a></sup> Laborious work in these societies has variously been carried out by slaves, serfs, peasants, and guild craftsmen. The nature of work changed significantly during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Factory_system" title="Factory system">factory system</a> was developed for use by industrializing nations. In addition to further increasing general quality of life, this development changed the dynamic of work. Under the factory system, workers increasingly collaborate with others, employers serve as authority figures during work hours, and forced labor is largely eradicated. Further changes occur in <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial societies</a> where technological advance makes industries obsolete, replacing them with <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_industries" title="Service industries">service industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeff198541–46_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeff198541–46-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans approach work differently based on both physical and personal attributes, and some work with more effectiveness and commitment than others. Some find work to contribute to personal fulfillment, while others work only out of necessity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeff19852_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeff19852-40">[40]</a></sup> Work can also serve as an identity, with individuals identifying themselves based on their occupation. <a href="/wiki/Work_motivation" title="Work motivation">Work motivation</a> is complex, both contributing to and subtracting from various human needs. The primary motivation for work is for material gain, which takes the form of <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> in modern societies. It may also serve to create self-esteem and personal worth, provide activity, gain respect, and express creativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeff1985142–153_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeff1985142–153-41">[41]</a></sup> Modern work is typically categorized as laborious or <a href="/wiki/Blue-collar_worker" title="Blue-collar worker">blue-collar work</a> and non-laborious or <a href="/wiki/White-collar_worker" title="White-collar worker">white-collar work</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeff198579–80_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeff198579–80-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leisure">Leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Leisure" title="Leisure">Leisure</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="298" data-file-width="460" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg/220px-Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg/330px-Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg/440px-Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amateur_Football_in_Kilkenny-Ireland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Men playing <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kilkenny" title="Kilkenny">Kilkenny</a>, Ireland. (2007)</div></div></div>
<p>Leisure is activity or lack of activity that takes place outside of work. It provides relaxation, <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a>, and improved quality of life for individuals. Casual leisure behaviors provide short-term gratification, but they do not provide long-term gratification or personal identity. These include <a href="/wiki/Play_(activity)" title="Play (activity)">play</a>, relaxation, casual social interaction, <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">volunteering</a>, passive entertainment, active entertainment, and sensory stimulation. Passive entertainment is typically derived from <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>, which may include written works or <a href="/wiki/Digital_media" title="Digital media">digital media</a>. Active entertainment involves games in which individuals participate. Sensory stimulation is immediate gratification from behaviors such as eating or sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Serious leisure behaviors involve non-professional pursuit of arts and sciences, the development of <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobbies</a>, or career volunteering in an area of expertise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Leisure can be beneficial for physical and mental health. It may be used to seek temporary relief from <a href="/wiki/Psychological_stress" title="Psychological stress">psychological stress</a>, to produce positive emotions, or to facilitate social interaction. Leisure can also facilitate health risks and negative emotions caused by <a href="/wiki/Boredom" title="Boredom">boredom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">high-risk behavior</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption">Consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consumer_behaviour" title="Consumer behaviour">Consumer behaviour</a></div>
<p>Humans operate as <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> that obtain and use goods. All production is ultimately designed for <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>, and consumers adapt their behavior based on the availability of production. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_consumption" title="Mass consumption">Mass consumption</a> began during the Industrial Revolution, caused by the development of new technologies that allowed for increased production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-46">[46]</a></sup> Many factors affect a consumer's decision to purchase goods through trade. They may consider the nature of the product, its associated cost, the convenience of purchase, and the nature of <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> around the product. Cultural factors may influence this decision, as different cultures value different things, and <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subcultures</a> within these cultures may have distinct priorities as buyers. <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">Social class</a>, including wealth, education, and occupation may affect one's purchasing behavior. A consumer's interpersonal relationships and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reference_group" title="Reference group">reference groups</a> may also influence purchasing behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_behavior">Ecological behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ecological behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_ecology" title="Human ecology">Human ecology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rural_life_in_Gilandeh,_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="1050" decoding="async" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Rural_life_in_Gilandeh%2C_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg/220px-Rural_life_in_Gilandeh%2C_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Rural_life_in_Gilandeh%2C_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg/330px-Rural_life_in_Gilandeh%2C_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Rural_life_in_Gilandeh%2C_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg/440px-Rural_life_in_Gilandeh%2C_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rural_life_in_Gilandeh,_Kharajgil_-_15_April_2018_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A girl with lambs in <a href="/wiki/Gilandeh,_Kharajgil" title="Gilandeh, Kharajgil">Gilandeh</a>, Iran. (2018)</div></div></div>
<p>Like all living things, humans live in <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> and interact with other organisms. Human behavior is affected by the environment in which a human lives, and environments are affected by human habitation. Humans have also developed man-made ecosystems such as <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_land" title="Agricultural land">agricultural land</a>. Geography and <a href="/wiki/Landscape_ecology" title="Landscape ecology">landscape ecology</a> determine how humans are distributed within an ecosystem, both naturally and through planned <a href="/wiki/Urban_morphology" title="Urban morphology">urban morphology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans exercise control over the animals that live within their environment. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_animal" title="Domesticated animal">Domesticated animals</a> are trained and cared for by humans. Humans can develop social and emotional bonds with animals in their care. <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">Pets</a> are kept for companionship within human homes, including <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> that have been bred for domestication over many centuries. <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">Livestock</a> animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, are kept on agricultural land to produce <a href="/wiki/Animal_product" title="Animal product">animal products</a>. Domesticated animals are also kept in laboratories for <a href="/wiki/Animal_testing" title="Animal testing">animal testing</a>. Non-domesticated animals are sometimes kept in <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoo" title="Zoo">zoos</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors">Factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Human behavior is influenced by biological and cultural elements. The <a href="/wiki/Structure_and_agency" title="Structure and agency">structure and agency</a> debate considers whether human behavior is predominantly led by individual human impulses or by external structural forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-46">[46]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral_genetics" title="Behavioral genetics">Behavioral genetics</a> considers how human behavior is affected by inherited traits. Though genes do not guarantee certain behaviors, certain traits can be inherited that make individuals more likely to engage in certain behaviors or express certain personalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> An individual's environment can also affect behavior, often in conjunction with genetic factors. An individual's <a href="/wiki/Personality" title="Personality">personality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attitude_(psychology)" title="Attitude (psychology)">attitudes</a> affect how behaviors are expressed, formed in conjunction by genetic and environmental factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Age">Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While specific traits of one's personality, <a href="/wiki/Temperament" title="Temperament">temperament</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> may be more consistent, other behaviors change as one moves between life stages—i.e., from birth through <a href="/wiki/Adolescence" title="Adolescence">adolescence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adult" title="Adult">adulthood</a>, and, for example, <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parenthood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Retirement" title="Retirement">retirement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">Infants</a> are limited in their ability to interpret their surroundings shortly after birth. <a href="/wiki/Object_permanence" title="Object permanence">Object permanence</a> and understanding of <a href="/wiki/Motion" title="Motion">motion</a> typically develop within the first six months of an infant's life, though the specific cognitive processes are not understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010234–235_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010234–235-52">[52]</a></sup> The ability to mentally categorize different concepts and objects that they perceive also develops within the first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010264–265_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010264–265-53">[53]</a></sup> Infants are quickly able to discern their body from their surroundings and often take interest in their own limbs or actions they cause by two months of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010337–340_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010337–340-54">[54]</a></sup> Infants practice <a href="/wiki/Imitation" title="Imitation">imitation</a> of other individuals to engage socially and learn new behaviors. In young infants, this involves imitating <a href="/wiki/Facial_expression" title="Facial expression">facial expressions</a>, and imitation of tool use takes place within the first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010346–347_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010346–347-55">[55]</a></sup> Communication develops over the first year, and infants begin using <a href="/wiki/Gesture" title="Gesture">gestures</a> to communicate intention around nine to ten months of age. Verbal communication develops more gradually, taking form during the second year of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010398–399_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBremnerWachs2010398–399-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adolescents" title="Adolescents">Adolescents</a> undergo changes in behavior caused by <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a> and the associated changes in <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> production. Production of <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a> increases <a href="/wiki/Sensation_seeking" title="Sensation seeking">sensation seeking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reward_system" title="Reward system">sensitivity to rewards</a> in adolescents as well as <a href="/wiki/Aggression" title="Aggression">aggression</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risk-taking" title="Risk-taking">risk-taking</a> in adolescent boys. Production of <a href="/wiki/Estradiol" title="Estradiol">estradiol</a> causes similar risk-taking behavior among adolescent girls. The new hormones cause changes in emotional processing that allow for close friendships, stronger motivations and intentions, and <a href="/wiki/Adolescent_sexuality" title="Adolescent sexuality">adolescent sexuality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Adolescents undergo social changes on a large scale, developing a <a href="/wiki/Self-concept" title="Self-concept">self-concept</a> and making autonomous decisions independently of adults. They typically become more aware of social norms and social cues than children, causing an increase in <a href="/wiki/Self-consciousness" title="Self-consciousness">self-consciousness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adolescent_egocentrism" title="Adolescent egocentrism">adolescent egocentrism</a> that guides behavior in social settings throughout adolescence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disability">Disability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_behavior&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Disability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Physical_disability" title="Physical disability">Physical disabilities</a> can prevent individuals from engaging in typical human behavior or necessitate alternative behaviors. Accommodations and <a href="/wiki/Accessibility" title="Accessibility">accessibility</a> are often made available for individuals with physical disabilities in developed nations, including health care, <a href="/wiki/Assistive_technology" title="Assistive technology">assistive technology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vocational_rehabilitation" title="Vocational rehabilitation">vocational services</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Severe disabilities are associated with increased leisure time but also with a lower satisfaction in the quality of leisure time. Productivity and health both commonly undergo long term decline following the onset of a severe disability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mental_disability" title="Mental disability">Mental disabilities</a> are those that directly affect cognitive and social behavior. Common <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">mental disorders</a> include <a href="/wiki/Mood_disorder" title="Mood disorder">mood disorders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anxiety_disorder" title="Anxiety disorder">anxiety disorders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personality_disorder" title="Personality disorder">personality disorders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Substance_dependence" title="Substance dependence">substance dependence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
