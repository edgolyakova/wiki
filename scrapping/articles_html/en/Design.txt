<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Designing">Designing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Designing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>People who produce designs are called <i><a href="/wiki/Designer" title="Designer">designers</a></i>. The term 'designer' generally refers to someone who works professionally in one of the various design areas. Within the professions, the word 'designer' is generally qualified by the area of practice (so one may be, for example, a <a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">fashion designer</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Product_design" title="Product design">product designer</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Web_design" title="Web design">web designer</a>, or an <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">interior designer</a>), but it can also designate others such as architects and engineers (see below: Types of designing). A designer's sequence of activities to produce a design is called a design process, using <a href="/wiki/Design_thinking" title="Design thinking">design thinking</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Design_methods" title="Design methods">design methods</a>. The process of creating a design can be brief (a quick sketch) or lengthy and complicated, involving considerable research, negotiation, reflection, <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_model" title="Conceptual model">modeling</a>, interactive adjustment, and re-design.
</p><p>Designing is also a widespread activity outside of the professions, done by more people than just those formally recognised as designers. In his influential book <i>The Sciences of the Artificial</i> the interdisciplinary scientist <a href="/wiki/Herbert_A._Simon" title="Herbert A. Simon">Herbert A. Simon</a> proposed that "Everyone designs who devises courses of action aimed at changing existing situations into preferred ones".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> And according to the design researcher <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Cross" title="Nigel Cross">Nigel Cross</a> "Everyone can – and does – design", and “Design ability is something that everyone has, to some extent, because it is embedded in our brains as a natural cognitive function”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_design">History of design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Design_history" title="Design history">Design history</a></div>
<p>Study of the history of design is complicated by varying interpretations of what constitutes ‘designing’. Many design historians, such as <a href="/wiki/John_Heskett" title="John Heskett">John Heskett</a>, start with the Industrial Revolution and the development of mass production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Others subscribe to conceptions of design that include pre-industrial objects and artefacts, beginning their narratives of design in prehistorical times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huppatz_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huppatz-5">[5]</a></sup> Originally situated within <a href="/wiki/Art_history" title="Art history">art history</a>, the historical development of the discipline of design history coalesced in the 1970s, as interested academics worked to recognize design as a separate and legitimate target for historical research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Early influential design historians include German-British art historian <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Pevsner" title="Nikolaus Pevsner">Nikolaus Pevsner</a> and Swiss historian and architecture critic <a href="/wiki/Sigfried_Giedion" title="Sigfried Giedion">Sigfried Giedion</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_education">Design education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Design education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Institutions for design education date back to the nineteenth century. The <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_National_Academy_of_Craft_and_Art_Industry" title="Norwegian National Academy of Craft and Art Industry">Norwegian National Academy of Craft and Art Industry</a> was founded in 1818, followed by the United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Government School of Design</a> (1837), <a href="/wiki/Konstfack" title="Konstfack">Konstfack</a> in Sweden (1844), and <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_School_of_Design" title="Rhode Island School of Design">Rhode Island School of Design</a> in the United States (1877). The German art and design school <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a>, founded in 1919, greatly influenced modern design education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Design_education" title="Design education">Design education</a> covers the teaching of theory, knowledge and values in the design of products, services and environments, and focusses on the development of both particular and general skills for designing. It is primarily orientated to preparing students for professional design practice, and based around project work and studio or <a href="/wiki/Atelier" title="Atelier">atelier</a> teaching methods.
</p><p>There are also broader forms of higher education in <a href="/wiki/Design_studies" title="Design studies">design studies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Design_thinking" title="Design thinking">design thinking</a>, and design also features as a part of general education, for example within <a href="/wiki/Design_and_Technology" title="Design and Technology">Design and Technology</a>. The development of design in general education in the 1970s led to a need to identify fundamental aspects of ‘designerly’ ways of knowing, thinking and acting, and hence to the establishment of design as a distinct <a href="/wiki/Academic_discipline" title="Academic discipline">discipline</a> of study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_process">Design process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Design process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Substantial disagreement exists concerning how designers in many fields, whether amateur or professional, alone or in teams, produce designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Design researchers Dorst and Dijkhuis acknowledge that "there are many ways of describing design processes", and compare and contrast two dominant but different views of the design process: as a rational problem solving process and as a process of reflection-in-action. They suggested that these two paradigms "represent two fundamentally different ways of looking at the world –  positivism and constructionism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The paradigms may reflect differing views of how designing <i>should be</i> done and how it <i>actually is</i> done, and they both have a variety of names. The problem-solving view has been called "the rational model",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brooks_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks-11">[11]</a></sup> "technical rationality"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schön_1983_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schön_1983-12">[12]</a></sup> and "the reason-centric perspective".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph_2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph_2010-13">[13]</a></sup> The alternative view has been called "reflection-in-action",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schön_1983_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schön_1983-12">[12]</a></sup> "co-evolution",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DC_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DC-14">[14]</a></sup> and "the action-centric perspective".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph_2010_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph_2010-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rational_model">Rational model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rational model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rational model was independently developed by Herbert A. Simon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simon-16">[16]</a></sup> an American scientist, and two German engineering design theorists, Gerhard Pahl and Wolfgang Beitz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It posits that:
</p>
<ol><li>Designers attempt to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimization_(mathematics)" title="Optimization (mathematics)">optimize</a> a design candidate for known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constraint_optimization" title="Constraint optimization">constraints</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goal" title="Goal">objectives</a>.</li>
<li>The design process is plan-driven.</li>
<li>The design process is understood in terms of a discrete sequence of stages.</li></ol>
<p>The rational model is based on a <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalist philosophy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brooks_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks-11">[11]</a></sup> and underlies the <a href="/wiki/Waterfall_model" title="Waterfall model">waterfall model</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Royce_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Royce-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Systems_development_life_cycle" title="Systems development life cycle">systems development life cycle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourque_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bourque-19">[19]</a></sup> and much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_design" title="Engineering design">engineering design</a> literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> According to the rationalist philosophy, design is informed by research and knowledge in a predictable and controlled manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Typical stages consistent with the rational model include the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Pre-production design
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Design_brief" title="Design brief">Design brief</a> – initial statement of intended outcome</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Analysis" title="Analysis">Analysis</a> – analysis of design goals</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">Research</a> – investigating similar design solutions in the field or related topics</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specification" title="Specification">Specification</a> – specifying requirements of a design solution for a product (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_design_specification" title="Product design specification">product design specification</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> or service.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">Problem solving</a> – <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conceptualize" title="wikt:conceptualize">conceptualizing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Document" title="Document">documenting</a> design solutions</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Presentation" title="Presentation">Presentation</a> – presenting design solutions</li></ul></li>
<li>Design during production
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_development" title="Product development">Development</a> – continuation and improvement of a designed solution</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Product_testing" title="Product testing">Product testing</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i> testing of a designed solution</li></ul></li>
<li>Post-production design feedback for future designs
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">Implementation</a> – introducing the designed solution into the environment</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evaluation" title="Evaluation">Evaluation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logical_consequence" title="Logical consequence">conclusion</a> – summary of process and results, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructive_criticism" title="Constructive criticism">constructive criticism</a> and suggestions for future improvements</li></ul></li>
<li>Redesign – any or all stages in the design process repeated (with corrections made) at any time before, during, or after production.</li></ul>
<p>Each stage has many associated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_practices" title="Best practices">best practices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_rational_model">Criticism of the rational model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism of the rational model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The rational model has been widely criticized on two primary grounds:
</p>
<ol><li>Designers do not work this way – extensive empirical evidence has demonstrated that designers do not act as the rational model suggests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schön_1983_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schön_1983-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph_2010_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph_2010-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cross_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cross-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Unrealistic assumptions – goals are often unknown when a design project begins, and the requirements and constraints continue to change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brooks_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Action-centric_model">Action-centric model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Action-centric model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The action-centric perspective is a label given to a collection of interrelated concepts, which are antithetical to the rational model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph_2010_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph_2010-13">[13]</a></sup> It posits that:
</p>
<ol><li>Designers use <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a> to generate design candidates.</li>
<li>The design process is <a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">improvised</a>.</li>
<li>No universal sequence of stages is apparent – analysis, design and implementation are contemporary and inextricably linked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph_2010_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph_2010-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>The action-centric perspective is based on an <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricist philosophy</a> and broadly consistent with the <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile approach</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beck_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beck-27">[27]</a></sup> and methodical development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Truex_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Truex-28">[28]</a></sup> Substantial empirical evidence supports the veracity of this perspective in describing the actions of real designers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cross_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cross-25">[25]</a></sup> Like the rational model, the action-centric model sees design as informed by research and knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>At least two views of design activity are consistent with the action-centric perspective. Both involve these three basic activities:
</p>
<ul><li>In the <a href="/wiki/Reflective_practice" title="Reflective practice">reflection-in-action paradigm</a>, designers alternate between "<a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)" title="Framing (social sciences)">framing</a>", "making moves", and "evaluating moves". "Framing" refers to conceptualizing the problem, i.e., defining goals and objectives. A "move" is a tentative design decision. The evaluation process may lead to further moves in the design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schön_1983_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schön_1983-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the sensemaking–coevolution–implementation framework, designers alternate between its three titular activities. <a href="/wiki/Sensemaking_(information_science)" title="Sensemaking (information science)">Sensemaking</a> includes both framing and evaluating moves. Implementation is the process of constructing the design object. Coevolution is "the process where the design agent simultaneously refines its mental picture of the design object based on its mental picture of the context, and vice versa".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph_2010_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph_2010-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The concept of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_cycle" title="Design cycle">design cycle</a> is understood as a circular time structure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which may start with the thinking of an idea, then expressing it by the use of visual or verbal means of communication (design tools), the sharing and perceiving of the expressed idea, and finally starting a new cycle with the critical rethinking of the perceived idea. Anderson points out that this concept emphasizes the importance of the means of expression, which at the same time are means of perception of any design ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophies">Philosophies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philosophies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_design" title="Philosophy of design">Philosophy of design</a> is the study of definitions of design, and the assumptions, foundations, and implications of design. There are also many informal 'philosophies' for guiding design such as personal values or preferred approaches.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches_to_design">Approaches to design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Approaches to design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of these values and approaches include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Critical_design" title="Critical design">Critical design</a> uses designed artifacts as an embodied critique or commentary on existing values, morals, and practices in a culture.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecological_design" title="Ecological design">Ecological design</a> is a design approach that prioritizes the consideration of the environmental impacts of a product or service, over its whole lifecycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Participatory_design" title="Participatory design">Participatory design</a> (originally co-operative design, now often co-design) is the practice of collective creativity to design, attempting to actively involve all stakeholders (e.g. employees, partners, customers, citizens, end-users) in the design process to help ensure the result meets their needs and is usable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>Scientific design refers to industrialised design based on scientific knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Science can be used to study the effects and need for a potential or existing product in general and to design products that are based on scientific knowledge. For instance, a scientific design of <a href="/wiki/Face_masks_during_the_COVID-19_pandemic" title="Face masks during the COVID-19 pandemic">face masks for COVID-19 mitigation</a> may be based on investigations of filtration performance, mitigation performance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> thermal comfort, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodegradability" title="Biodegradability">biodegradability</a> and flow resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Service_design" title="Service design">Service design</a> designing or organizing the experience around a product and the service associated with a product's use.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sociotechnical_system" title="Sociotechnical system">Sociotechnical system</a> design, a philosophy and tools for participative designing of work arrangements and supporting processes – for organizational purpose, quality, safety, economics, and customer requirements in core work processes, the quality of peoples experience at work, and the needs of society</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transgenerational_design" title="Transgenerational design">Transgenerational design</a>, the practice of making products and environments compatible with those physical and sensory impairments associated with human aging and which limit major activities of daily living.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/User-centered_design" title="User-centered design">User-centered design</a>, which focuses on the needs, wants, and limitations of the end-user of the designed artifact. One aspect of user-centered design is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomics</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_the_arts">Relationship with the arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relationship with the arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The boundaries between art and design are blurry, largely due to a range of applications both for the term 'art' and the term 'design'. <a href="/wiki/Applied_arts" title="Applied arts">Applied arts</a> can include <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">fashion design</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Decorative_arts" title="Decorative arts">decorative arts</a> which traditionally includes craft objects. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphic_art" title="Graphic art">graphic arts</a> (2D image making that ranges from photography to illustration), the distinction is often made between <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">fine art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_art" title="Commercial art">commercial art</a>, based on the context within which the work is produced and how it is traded.
</p>
