<p><b>Mansa Musa</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">منسا موسى</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>: </small><span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">Mansā Mūsā</i></span>; <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><abbr title="reigned">r.</abbr> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1312</span> – <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1337</span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup>) was the ninth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mansa_(title)" title="Mansa (title)">mansa</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Mali_Empire" title="Mali Empire">Mali Empire</a>, which reached its territorial peak during his reign. Musa is known for his wealth and gift-giving, and has sometimes been called <a href="/wiki/List_of_wealthiest_historical_figures" title="List of wealthiest historical figures">the wealthiest person in history</a>. His riches came from the mining of significant gold and salt deposits in the Mali Empire, along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ivory_trade" title="Ivory trade">ivory trade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the time of Musa's ascension to the throne, Mali in large part consisted of the territory of the former <a href="/wiki/Ghana_Empire" title="Ghana Empire">Ghana Empire</a>, which Mali had conquered. The <a href="/wiki/Mali_Empire" title="Mali Empire">Mali Empire</a> consisted of land that is now part of <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a>, and the modern state of <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>.
</p><p>Musa went on <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">hajj</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> in 1324, traveling with an enormous entourage and a vast supply of gold. En route, he spent time in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>, where his lavish gift-giving is said to have noticeably affected the value of gold in Egypt and garnered the attention of the wider Muslim world.
</p><p>Musa expanded the borders of the Mali Empire, in particular incorporating the cities of <a href="/wiki/Gao" title="Gao">Gao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> into its territory. He sought closer ties with the rest of the Muslim world, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate" title="Mamluk Sultanate">Mamluk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marinid_Sultanate" title="Marinid Sultanate">Marinid Sultanates</a>. He recruited scholars from the wider Muslim world to travel to Mali, such as the Andalusian poet <a href="/wiki/Al-Sahili" title="Al-Sahili">Abu Ishaq al-Sahili</a>, and helped establish Timbuktu as a center of Islamic learning. His reign is associated with numerous construction projects, including part of <a href="/wiki/Djinguereber_Mosque" title="Djinguereber Mosque">Djinguereber Mosque</a> in Timbuktu. Musa's reign is often regarded as the zenith of Mali's power and prestige.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_titles">Name and titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name and titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mansa Musa's personal name was Musa (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">موسى</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>: </small><span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">Mūsā</i></span>), the Arabic form of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Source needed for connection to Moses (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mansa_(title)" title="Mansa (title)">Mansa</a></i>, 'ruler'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> or 'king'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Manding_languages" title="Manding languages">Mandé</a>, was the title of the ruler of the Mali Empire. It has also been translated as "conqueror" and "priest-king".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> In oral tradition and the <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu_Chronicles" title="Timbuktu Chronicles">Timbuktu Chronicles</a>, Musa is known as Kanku Musa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[b]</a></sup> In Mandé tradition, it was common for one's name to be prefixed by their mother's name, so the name Kanku Musa means "Musa, son of Kanku", although it is unclear if the genealogy implied is literal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gomez_2018_109_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gomez_2018_109-16">[14]</a></sup> He is also called <a href="/wiki/Hajji" title="Hajji">Hidji</a> Mansa Musa in oral tradition in reference to his hajj.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENiane1959_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENiane1959-17">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Al-Yafii gave Musa's name as Musa ibn Abi Bakr ibn Abi al-Aswad (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">موسى بن أبي بكر بن أبي الأسود</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>: </small><span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">Mūsā ibn Abī Bakr ibn Abī al-Aswad</i></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019115–116_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019115–116-18">[16]</a></sup> and Ibn Hajar gave Musa's name as Musa ibn Abi Bakr Salim al-Takruri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000358_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000358-19">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Songhai language, rulers of Mali such as Musa were known as the Mali-koi, <i>koi</i> being a title that conveyed authority over a region: in other words, the "ruler of Mali".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_sources">Historical sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of what is known about Musa comes from Arabic sources written after his hajj, especially the writings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Umari" title="Al-Umari">Al-Umari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a>. While in Cairo during his hajj, Musa befriended officials such as Ibn Amir Hajib, who learned about him and his country from him and later passed on that information to historians such as Al-Umari.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAl-UmariChapter_10_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAl-UmariChapter_10-21">[19]</a></sup> Additional information comes from two 17th-century manuscripts written in <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Tarikh_al-fattash" title="Tarikh al-fattash">Tarikh Ibn al-Mukhtar</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[c]</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Tarikh_al-Sudan" title="Tarikh al-Sudan">Tarikh al-Sudan</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez201892–93_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez201892–93-24">[21]</a></sup> Oral tradition, as performed by the <i>jeliw</i> (<small><abbr title="singular form">sg.</abbr></small> <i>jeli</i>), also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Griots" title="Griots">griots</a>, includes relatively little information about Musa compared to some other parts of the history of Mali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez201892–93_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez201892–93-24">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lineage_and_accession_to_the_throne">Lineage and accession to the throne</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lineage and accession to the throne">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

2px 3px;font-size:87%">Genealogy of the mansas of the Mali Empire up to Magha II (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><abbr title="died">d.</abbr> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1389</span></span>), based on Levtzion's interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963-25">[22]</a></sup> Numbered individuals reigned as <i>mansa</i>; the numbers indicate the order in which they reigned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[d]</a></sup></p></div>
<p>Musa's father was named Faga Leye<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Niane_1959_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Niane_1959-12">[11]</a></sup> and his mother may have been named Kanku.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[g]</a></sup> Faga Leye was the son of <a href="/wiki/Mande_Bori" title="Mande Bori">Abu Bakr</a>, a brother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunjata" title="Sunjata">Sunjata</a>, the first mansa of the Mali Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Niane_1959_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Niane_1959-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[h]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a>, who visited Mali during the reign of Musa's brother Sulayman, said that Musa's grandfather was named Sariq Jata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000295_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000295-33">[25]</a></sup> Sariq Jata may be another name for Sunjata, who was actually Musa's great-uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000416_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000416-34">[26]</a></sup> The date of Musa's birth is unknown, but he still appeared to be a young man in 1324.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[27]</a></sup> The <i>Tarikh al-fattash</i> claims that Musa accidentally killed Kanku at some point prior to his hajj.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gomez_2018_109_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gomez_2018_109-16">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Musa ascended to power in the early 1300s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[i]</a></sup> under unclear circumstances. According to Musa's own account, his predecessor as mansa of Mali, presumably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohammed_ibn_Gao" title="Mohammed ibn Gao">Muhammad ibn Qu</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[30]</a></sup> launched <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_voyage_of_the_predecessor_of_Mansa_Musa" title="Atlantic voyage of the predecessor of Mansa Musa">two expeditions</a> to explore the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> (200 ships for the first exploratory mission and 2,000 ships for the second). The mansa led the second expedition himself, and appointed Musa as his deputy to rule the empire until he returned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[31]</a></sup> When he did not return, Musa was crowned as mansa himself, marking a transfer of the line of succession from the descendants of Sunjata to the descendants of his brother Abu Bakr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[32]</a></sup> Some modern historians have cast doubt on Musa's version of events, suggesting he may have deposed his predecessor and devised the story about the voyage to explain how he took power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[34]</a></sup> Nonetheless, the possibility of such a voyage has been taken seriously by several historians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018101_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018101-44">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevisseLabib1984666_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevisseLabib1984666-45">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThornton201213_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThornton201213-46">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <i>Tarikh al-Fattash</i>, Musa had a wife named Inari Konte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBühnen199412_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBühnen199412-47">[38]</a></sup> Her <i>jamu</i> (clan name) Konte is shared with both Sunjata's mother Sogolon Konte and his arch-enemy <a href="/wiki/Soumaoro_Kant%C3%A9" title="Soumaoro Kanté">Sumanguru Konte</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBühnen199412–13_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBühnen199412–13-48">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_reign">Early reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musa was a young man when he became <i>mansa</i>, possibly in his early twenties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018104_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018104-49">[40]</a></sup> Given the grandeur of his subsequent <i>hajj</i>, it is likely that Musa spent much of his early reign preparing for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018105_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018105-50">[41]</a></sup> Among these preparations would likely have been raids to capture and enslave people from neighboring lands, as Musa's entourage would include many thousands of enslaved people; the historian Michael Gomez estimates that Mali may have captured over 6,000 people per year for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018107_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018107-51">[42]</a></sup> Perhaps because of this, Musa's early reign was spent in continuous military conflict with neighboring non-Muslim societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018107_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018107-51">[42]</a></sup> In 1324, while in Cairo, Musa said that he had conquered 24 cities and their surrounding districts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilgrimage_to_Mecca">Pilgrimage to Mecca</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pilgrimage to Mecca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musa was a <a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslim</a>, and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrimage_to_Mecca" title="Pilgrimage to Mecca">pilgrimage to Mecca</a>, also known as hajj, made him well known across <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">Northern Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. To Musa, Islam was "an entry into the cultured world of the Eastern Mediterranean".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin110_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin110-53">[44]</a></sup> He would have spent much time fostering the growth of the religion within his empire.
</p><p>When Musa departed Mali for the <i>hajj</i>, he left his son Muhammad to rule in his absence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[45]</a></sup>
Musa made his pilgrimage between 1324 and 1325 spanning 2,700 miles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez20184_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez20184-55">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakewell_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bakewell-57">[48]</a></sup> His procession reportedly included 60,000 men, all wearing <a href="/wiki/Brocade" title="Brocade">brocade</a> and Persian silk, including 12,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave" title="Slave">slaves</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[49]</a></sup> who each carried 1.8 kg (4 lb) of gold bars, and heralds dressed in silks, who bore gold staffs, organized horses, and handled bags. Musa provided all necessities for the procession, feeding the entire company of men and animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin110_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin110-53">[44]</a></sup> Those animals included 80 <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camels</a> which each carried 23–136 kg (50–300 lb) of gold dust. Musa gave the gold to the poor he met along his route. Musa not only gave to the cities he passed on the way to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>, but also traded gold for souvenirs. It was reported that he built a <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosque</a> every Friday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_1972_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bell_1972-36">[28]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shihab_al-Din_al-%27Umari" title="Shihab al-Din al-'Umari">Al-Umari</a> who visited Cairo shortly after Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca, noted that it was "a lavish display of power, wealth, and unprecedented by its size and pageantry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[50]</a></sup> Musa made a major point of showing off his nation's wealth.
</p><p>Musa and his entourage arrived at the outskirts of Cairo in July 1324. They camped for three days by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyramids_of_Giza" title="Pyramids of Giza">Pyramids of Giza</a>, before crossing the Nile into Cairo on 19 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[j]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000355_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000355-61">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018114,_117_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018114,_117-62">[52]</a></sup> While in Cairo, Musa met with the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate" title="Mamluk Sultanate">Mamluk</a> sultan <a href="/wiki/Al-Nasir_Muhammad" title="Al-Nasir Muhammad">al-Nasir Muhammad</a>, whose reign had already seen one <i>mansa</i>, <a href="/wiki/Mansa_Sakura" title="Mansa Sakura">Sakura</a>, make the hajj. Al-Nasir expected Musa to prostrate himself before him, which Musa initially refused to do. When he did finally bow, he said he was doing so for God alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018116_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018116-63">[53]</a></sup> Despite this initial awkwardness, the two rulers got along well, and exchanged gifts. Musa and his entourage gave and spent freely while in Cairo. Musa stayed in the Qarafa district of Cairo, and befriended its governor, Ibn Amir Hajib, who learned much about Mali from him. Musa stayed in Cairo for three months, departing on 18 October<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[k]</a></sup> with the official caravan to Mecca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000355_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000355-61">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019111_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019111-65">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Musa's generosity continued as he traveled onwards to Mecca, and he gave gifts to fellow pilgrims and the people of Medina and Mecca. While in Mecca, conflict broke out between a group of Malian pilgrims and a group of Turkic pilgrims in the <a href="/wiki/Masjid_al-Haram" title="Masjid al-Haram">Masjid al-Haram</a>. Swords were drawn, but before the situation escalated further, Musa persuaded his men to back down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019115–122_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019115–122-66">[55]</a></sup> Musa and his entourage lingered in Mecca after the last day of the hajj. Traveling separately from the main caravan, their return journey to Cairo was struck by catastrophe. By the time they reached Suez, many of the Malian pilgrims had died of cold, starvation, or bandit raids, and they had lost many of their supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018118_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018118-67">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019122–129_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019122–129-68">[57]</a></sup> Having run out of money, Musa and his entourage were forced to borrow money and re-sell much of what they had purchased while in Cairo before the hajj, and Musa went into debt to several merchants, such as Siraj al-Din. However, Al-Nasir Muhammad returned Musa's earlier show of generosity with gifts of his own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018118–120_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018118–120-69">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>On his return journey, Musa met the Andalusian poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Ishaq_al-Sahili" title="Abu Ishaq al-Sahili">Abu Ishaq al-Sahili</a>, whose eloquence and knowledge of jurisprudence impressed him, and whom he convinced to travel with him to Mali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHunwick199060–61_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHunwick199060–61-70">[59]</a></sup> Other scholars whom Musa brought to Mali included <a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> <a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">jurists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Tarikh_al-Sudan" title="Tarikh al-Sudan">Tarikh al-Sudan</a></i>, the cities of <a href="/wiki/Gao" title="Gao">Gao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> submitted to Musa's rule as he traveled through on his return to Mali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[61]</a></sup> According to one account given by Ibn Khaldun, Musa's general Saghmanja conquered Gao. The other account claims that Gao had been conquered during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Mansa_Sakura" title="Mansa Sakura">Mansa Sakura</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[62]</a></sup> Both of these accounts may be true, as Mali's control of Gao may have been weak, requiring powerful mansas to reassert their authority periodically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzion197375_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzion197375-74">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_reign">Later reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_in_Mali">Construction in Mali</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Construction in Mali">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Musa embarked on a large building program, raising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosques" title="Mosques">mosques</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrasah" title="Madrasah">madrasas</a> in Timbuktu and Gao. Most notably, the ancient center of learning <a href="/wiki/Sankore_Madrasah" title="Sankore Madrasah">Sankore Madrasah</a> (or University of Sankore) was constructed during his reign.
</p><p>In Niani, Musa built the Hall of Audience, a building communicating by an interior door to the royal palace. It was "an admirable Monument", surmounted by a dome and adorned with <a href="/wiki/Arabesque" title="Arabesque">arabesques</a> of striking colours. The wooden window frames of an upper storey were plated with silver foil; those of a lower storey with gold. Like the Great Mosque, a contemporaneous and grandiose structure in Timbuktu, the Hall was built of cut stone.
</p><p>During this period, there was an advanced level of urban living in the major centers of Mali. Sergio Domian, an Italian scholar of art and architecture, wrote of this period: "Thus was laid the foundation of an urban civilization. At the height of its power, Mali had at least 400 cities, and the interior of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Niger_Delta" title="Inner Niger Delta">Niger Delta</a> was very densely populated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Djingareiber_cour.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Djingareiber_cour.jpg/220px-Djingareiber_cour.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Djingareiber_cour.jpg/330px-Djingareiber_cour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Djingareiber_cour.jpg/440px-Djingareiber_cour.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Djingareiber_cour.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Djinguereber_Mosque" title="Djinguereber Mosque">Djinguereber Mosque</a>, commissioned by Mansa Musa in 1327</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_education">Economy and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is recorded that Mansa Musa traveled through the cities of <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gao" title="Gao">Gao</a> on his way to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, and made them a part of his empire when he returned around 1325. He brought architects from <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, a region in Spain, and Cairo to build his grand palace in Timbuktu and the great <a href="/wiki/Djinguereber_Mosque" title="Djinguereber Mosque">Djinguereber Mosque</a> that still stands today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Timbuktu soon became the center of trade, culture, and Islam; markets brought in merchants from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hausaland" title="Hausaland">Hausaland</a>, Egypt, and other African kingdoms, a university was founded in the city (as well as in the Malian cities of <a href="/wiki/Djenn%C3%A9" title="Djenné">Djenné</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9gou" title="Ségou">Ségou</a>), and Islam was spread through the markets and university, making Timbuktu a new area for Islamic scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[66]</a></sup> News of the Malian empire's city of wealth even traveled across the Mediterranean to southern Europe, where traders from <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> soon added Timbuktu to their maps to trade manufactured goods for gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Sankore" title="University of Sankore">University of Sankore</a> in Timbuktu was restaffed under Musa's reign with jurists, astronomers, and mathematicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[68]</a></sup> The university became a center of learning and culture, drawing Muslim scholars from around Africa and the Middle East to Timbuktu.
</p><p>In 1330, the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Mossi_Kingdoms" title="Mossi Kingdoms">Mossi</a> invaded and conquered the city of Timbuktu. Gao had already been captured by Musa's general, and Musa quickly regained Timbuktu, built a rampart and stone fort, and placed a standing army to protect the city from future invaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>While Musa's palace has since vanished, the university and mosque still stand in Timbuktu today.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Mali_Empire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2250" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/The_Mali_Empire.jpg/220px-The_Mali_Empire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/The_Mali_Empire.jpg/330px-The_Mali_Empire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/The_Mali_Empire.jpg/440px-The_Mali_Empire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Mali_Empire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Mali Empire at the time of Mansa Musa's death</div></div></div>
<p>The date of Mansa Musa's death is not certain. Using the reign lengths reported by Ibn Khaldun to calculate back from the death of Mansa Suleyman in 1360, Musa would have died in 1332.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963349_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963349-81">[70]</a></sup> However, Ibn Khaldun also reports that Musa sent an envoy to congratulate <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_Ali_ibn_Othman" title="Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn Othman">Abu al-Hasan Ali</a> for his conquest of <a href="/wiki/Tlemcen" title="Tlemcen">Tlemcen</a>, which took place in May 1337, but by the time Abu al-Hasan sent an envoy in response, Musa had died and Suleyman was on the throne, suggesting Musa died in 1337.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963350_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963350-82">[71]</a></sup> In contrast, al-Umari, writing twelve years after Musa's hajj, in approximately 1337,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000252,_413_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000252,_413-83">[72]</a></sup> claimed that Musa returned to Mali intending to abdicate and return to live in Mecca but died before he could do so,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000268_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000268-84">[73]</a></sup> suggesting he died even earlier than 1332.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1972224_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1972224-85">[74]</a></sup> It is possible that it was actually Musa's son Maghan who congratulated Abu al-Hasan, or Maghan who received Abu al-Hasan's envoy after Musa's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1972225–226_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1972225–226-86">[75]</a></sup> The latter possibility is corroborated by Ibn Khaldun calling Suleyman Musa's son in that passage, suggesting he may have confused Musa's brother Suleyman with Musa's son Maghan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1972225_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1972225-87">[76]</a></sup> Alternatively, it is possible that the four-year reign Ibn Khaldun credits Maghan with actually referred to his ruling Mali while Musa was away on the hajj, and he only reigned briefly in his own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1972226–227_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1972226–227-88">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nehemia_Levtzion" title="Nehemia Levtzion">Nehemia Levtzion</a> regarded 1337 as the most likely date,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963350_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzion1963350-82">[71]</a></sup> which has been accepted by other scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESapong20162_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESapong20162-89">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018145_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018145-90">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musa's hajj has been regarded as the most illustrious moment in the history of West Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[80]</a></sup> Musa's reign is commonly regarded as Mali's golden age, but this perception may be the result of his reign being the best recorded by Arabic sources, rather than him necessarily being the wealthiest and most powerful mansa of Mali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECanós-Donnay2019_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECanós-Donnay2019-92">[81]</a></sup> The territory of the Mali Empire was at its height during the reigns of Musa and his brother Sulayman, and covered the Sudan-Sahel region of West Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENiane1984152_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENiane1984152-93">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>Musa is less renowned in Mandé oral tradition as performed by the <i>jeliw</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[83]</a></sup> He is criticized for being unfaithful to tradition, and some of the <i>jeliw</i> regard Musa as having wasted Mali's wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENiane1984_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENiane1984-95">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMohamud2019_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMohamud2019-96">[85]</a></sup> However, some aspects of Musa appear to have been incorporated into a figure in Mandé oral tradition known as Fajigi, which translates as "father of hope".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConrad1992152_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConrad1992152-97">[86]</a></sup> Fajigi is remembered as having traveled to Mecca to retrieve ceremonial objects known as <i>boliw</i>, which feature in Mandé traditional religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConrad1992152_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConrad1992152-97">[86]</a></sup> As Fajigi, Musa is sometimes conflated with a figure in oral tradition named Fakoli, who is best known as Sunjata's top general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConrad1992153_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConrad1992153-98">[87]</a></sup> The figure of Fajigi combines both Islam and traditional beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConrad1992152_97-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConrad1992152-97">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>The name "Musa" has become virtually synonymous with pilgrimage in Mandé tradition, such that other figures who are remembered as going on a pilgrimage, such as Fakoli, are also called Musa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConrad1992153–154_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConrad1992153–154-99">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth">Wealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mansa Musa is renowned for his wealth and generosity. Online articles in the 21st century have claimed that Mansa Musa was <a href="/wiki/List_of_wealthiest_historical_figures" title="List of wealthiest historical figures">the richest person of all time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019106_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019106-100">[89]</a></sup> This claim is often sourced to an article in <i><a href="/wiki/CelebrityNetWorth" title="CelebrityNetWorth">CelebrityNetWorth</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019106_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019106-100">[89]</a></sup> which claims that Musa's wealth was the equivalent of <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>400 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[90]</a></sup> <i>CelebrityNetWorth</i> has been criticized for the unreliability of its estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2018_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2018-102">[91]</a></sup> Historians such as Hadrien Collet have argued that Musa's wealth is impossible to accurately calculate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019106_100-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019106-100">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMohamud2019_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMohamud2019-96">[85]</a></sup> Contemporary Arabic sources may have been trying to express that Musa had more gold than they thought possible, rather than trying to give an exact number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson2015b_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson2015b-103">[92]</a></sup> Furthermore, it is difficult to meaningfully compare the wealth of historical figures such as Mansa Musa, due to the difficulty of separating the personal wealth of a monarch from the wealth of the state and the difficulty of comparing wealth in highly different societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson2015a_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson2015a-104">[93]</a></sup> Musa may have brought as much as 18 tons of gold on his hajj,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018106_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018106-105">[94]</a></sup> equal in value to over US$957 million in 2022.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[95]</a></sup> Musa himself further promoted the appearance of having vast, inexhaustible wealth by spreading rumors that gold grew like a plant in his kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGomez2018121_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGomez2018121-107">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to some Arabic writers, Musa's gift-giving caused a depreciation in the value of gold in Egypt. Al-Umari said that, before Musa's arrival, a <i><a href="/wiki/Mithqal" title="Mithqal">mithqal</a></i> of gold was worth 25 silver <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirhams" title="Dirhams">dirhams</a></i>, but that it dropped to less than 22 <i>dirhams</i> afterward and did not go above that number for at least twelve years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000271_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000271-108">[97]</a></sup> Though this has been described as having "wrecked" Egypt's economy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMohamud2019_96-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMohamud2019-96">[85]</a></sup> the historian Warren Schultz has argued that this was well within normal fluctuations in the value of gold in Mamluk Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchultz2006_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchultz2006-109">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Character">Character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mansa_Musa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arabic writers, such as Ibn Battuta and Abdallah ibn Asad al-Yafii, praised Musa's generosity, virtue, and intelligence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000295_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000295-33">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollet2019115–116_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollet2019115–116-18">[16]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun said that he "was an upright man and a great king, and tales of his justice are still told."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000334_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevtzionHopkins2000334-110">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
