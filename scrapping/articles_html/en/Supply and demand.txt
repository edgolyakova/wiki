<p>In <a href="/wiki/Microeconomics" title="Microeconomics">microeconomics</a>, <b>supply and demand</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Economic_model" title="Economic model">economic model</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Price_determination" title="Price determination">price determination</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a>. It postulates that, <a href="/wiki/Ceteris_paribus" title="Ceteris paribus">holding all else equal</a>, in a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_competition" title="Perfect competition">competitive market</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Unit_price" title="Unit price">unit price</a> for a particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">good</a>, or other traded item such as labor or liquid financial assets, will vary until it settles at a point where the quantity demanded (at the current price) will equal the quantity supplied (at the current price), resulting in an <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">economic equilibrium</a> for price and quantity transacted. It forms the theoretical basis of modern economics.  
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomics</a>, as well, the <a href="/wiki/AD%E2%80%93AS_model" title="AD–AS model">aggregate demand-aggregate supply model</a> has been used to depict how the quantity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_GDP" title="Real GDP">total output</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_price_level" title="Aggregate price level">aggregate price level</a> may be determined in equilibrium.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphical_representations">Graphical representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Graphical representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supply_schedule">Supply schedule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Supply schedule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A supply schedule, depicted graphically as a supply curve, is a table that shows the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity supplied by producers. Under the assumption of <a href="/wiki/Perfect_competition" title="Perfect competition">perfect competition</a>, supply is determined by <a href="/wiki/Marginal_cost" title="Marginal cost">marginal cost</a>: firms will produce additional output as long as the cost of producing an extra unit is less than the market price they receive.
</p><p>A rise in the cost of raw materials would decrease supply, shifting the supply curve to the left because at each possible price a smaller quantity would be supplied. One may also think of this as a shift up in the supply curve, because the price must rise for producers to supply a given quantity.  A fall in production costs would increase supply, shifting the supply curve to the right and down. 
</p><p>Mathematically, a supply curve is represented by a  supply function, giving the quantity supplied as a function of its price and as many other variables as desired to better explain quantity supplied. The two most common specifications are linear supply, e.g.,  the slanted line

<p>By its very nature, the concept of a supply curve assumes that firms are <i>perfect competitors</i>, having no influence over the market price. This is because each point on the supply curve answers the question, "If this firm is <i>faced with</i> this potential price, how much output will it sell?" If a firm has market power—in violation of the <i>perfect competitor</i> model—its decision on how much output to bring to market influences the market price. Thus the firm is not "faced with" any given price,  and a more complicated model, e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oligopoly" title="Oligopoly">oligopoly</a> or <a href="/wiki/Product_differentiation" title="Product differentiation">differentiated-product</a> model, should be used.
</p><p>Economists distinguish between the supply curve of an individual firm and the market supply curve.  The market supply curve shows the total quantity supplied by all firms, so it is the sum of the quantities supplied by all suppliers at each potential price  (that is, the individual firms' supply curves are added horizontally).
</p><p>Economists  distinguish between  short-run and   long-run  supply curve.  <i>Short run</i>  refers to a time period during which one or more inputs are fixed (typically <a href="/wiki/Physical_capital" title="Physical capital">physical capital</a>), and the number of firms in the industry is also fixed (if it is a market supply curve).  <i>Long run</i> refers to a time period during which new firms enter or existing firms exit  and all inputs can be adjusted fully to any price change.   Long-run supply curves are flatter than short-run counterparts (with quantity more sensitive to price, more elastic supply).
</p><p>Common determinants of supply are:
</p>
<ol><li>Prices of inputs, including wages</li>
<li>The technology used, <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">Productivity</a></li>
<li>Firms' expectations about future prices</li>
<li>Number of suppliers (for a market supply curve)</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demand_schedule">Demand schedule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demand schedule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A demand schedule, depicted graphically as a <a href="/wiki/Demand_curve" title="Demand curve">demand curve</a>, represents the amount of a certain
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goods_(economics)" title="Goods (economics)">good</a> that buyers are willing and able to purchase at various prices, assuming all other determinants of demand are held constant, such as income, tastes and preferences, and the prices of <a href="/wiki/Substitute_good" title="Substitute good">substitute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Complementary_good" title="Complementary good">complementary goods</a>.   According to the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_demand" title="Law of demand">law of demand</a>, the demand curve is always downward-sloping, meaning that as the price decreases, consumers will buy more of the good.
</p><p>Mathematically, a demand curve is represented by a demand function, giving the quantity demanded as a function of its price and as many other variables as desired to better explain  quantity demanded. The two most common specifications are linear demand, e.g.,  the slanted line

<p>Note that really a demand curve should be drawn with price on the horizontal <i>x</i>-axis, since it is the independent variable. Instead, price is put on the vertical, <i>f(x)</i> <i>y</i>-axis as a matter of unfortunate historical convention.
</p><p>Just as the supply curve parallels the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_cost" title="Marginal cost">marginal cost</a> curve,  the demand curve parallels <a href="/wiki/Marginal_utility" title="Marginal utility">marginal utility</a>, measured in dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Consumers will be willing to buy a given quantity of a good, at a given price, if the marginal utility of additional consumption is equal to the <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a> determined by the price, that is, the marginal utility of alternative consumption choices. The demand schedule is defined as the <i>willingness</i> and <i>ability</i> of a consumer to purchase a given product at a certain time.
</p><p>The demand curve is generally downward-sloping, but for some goods it is upward-sloping. Two such types of goods have been given definitions and names that are in common use: <a href="/wiki/Veblen_good" title="Veblen good">Veblen goods</a>, goods which because of fashion or  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signalling" title="Signalling">signalling</a>  are  more attractive at higher prices, and <a href="/wiki/Giffen_good" title="Giffen good">Giffen goods</a>, which, by virtue of being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inferior_goods" title="Inferior goods">inferior goods</a> that absorb a large part of a consumer's income (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staples</a> such as the classic example of potatoes in Ireland), may see an increase in quantity demanded when the price rises. The reason the law of demand is violated for Giffen goods is that the rise in the price of the good has a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Income_effect" title="Income effect">income effect</a>, sharply reducing the purchasing power of the consumer so that he switches away from luxury goods to the Giffen good, e.g., when the price of potatoes rises, the Irish peasant can no longer afford meat and eats more potatoes to cover for the lost calories.
</p><p>As with the supply curve, by its very nature  the concept of a demand curve requires that the purchaser be a perfect competitor—that is, that the purchaser have no influence over the market price.  This is true because each point on the demand curve answers the question, "If buyers are <i>faced with</i> this potential price, how much of the product will they purchase?" But, if  a buyer has market power (that is, the amount  he buys influences the price),  he is not "faced with" any given price, and  we must use a more complicated model, of <a href="/wiki/Monopsony" title="Monopsony">monopsony</a>.
</p><p>As with supply curves, economists distinguish between the demand curve for an individual and the demand curve for a market.  The market demand curve is obtained by adding the  quantities from the individual demand curves at each price.
</p><p>Common determinants of demand are:
</p>
<ol><li>Income</li>
<li>Tastes and preferences</li>
<li>Prices of related goods and services</li>
<li>Consumers' expectations about future prices and incomes</li>
<li>Number of potential consumers</li>
<li>Advertising</li></ol>

<p>Since supply and demand can be considered as <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">functions</a> of price they have a natural graphical representation. Demand curves were first drawn by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustin_Cournot" title="Augustin Cournot">Augustin Cournot</a> in his <i>Recherches sur les Principes Mathématiques de la Théorie des Richesses</i> (1838) – see <a href="/wiki/Cournot_competition" title="Cournot competition">Cournot competition</a>. Supply curves were added by <a href="/wiki/Fleeming_Jenkin" title="Fleeming Jenkin">Fleeming Jenkin</a> in <i>The Graphical Representation of the Laws of Supply and Demand...</i> of 1870. Both sorts of curve were popularised by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Marshall" title="Alfred Marshall">Alfred Marshall</a> who, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Economics_(Marshall_book)" title="Principles of Economics (Marshall book)">Principles of Economics</a></i> (1890), chose to represent price – normally the independent variable – by the vertical axis; a practice which remains common.
</p><p>If supply or demand is a function of other variables besides price, it may be represented by a family of curves (with a change in the other variables constituting a shift between curves) or by a surface in a higher dimensional space.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microeconomics">Microeconomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microeconomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:165px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fig5_Supply_and_demand_curves.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="203" data-file-width="221" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Fig5_Supply_and_demand_curves.jpg/163px-Fig5_Supply_and_demand_curves.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/Fig5_Supply_and_demand_curves.jpg 1.5x" width="163"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fig5_Supply_and_demand_curves.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 3: Supply and Demand</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equilibrium">Equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally speaking,  an equilibrium is defined to be the price-quantity pair where the quantity demanded is equal to the quantity supplied. It is represented by the intersection of the demand and supply curves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The analysis of various equilibria is a fundamental aspect of <a href="/wiki/Microeconomics" title="Microeconomics">microeconomics</a>:
</p><p><b>Market equilibrium</b>: A situation in a market when the price is such that the quantity demanded by consumers is correctly balanced by the quantity that firms wish to supply. In this situation, the market clears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Changes in market equilibrium</b>:
Practical uses of supply and demand analysis often center on the different variables that change equilibrium price and quantity, represented as shifts in the respective curves. <a href="/wiki/Comparative_statics" title="Comparative statics">Comparative statics</a> of such a shift traces the effects from the initial equilibrium to the new equilibrium.
</p><p><b>Demand curve shifts</b>:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demand_curve" title="Demand curve">Demand curve</a></div>
<p>When consumers increase the quantity demanded <i>at a given price</i>, it is referred to as an <i>increase in demand</i>. Increased demand can be represented on the graph as the curve being shifted to the right. At each price point, a greater quantity is demanded, as from the initial curve <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i><sub>1</sub></span> to the new curve <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i><sub>2</sub></span>. In the diagram, this raises the equilibrium price from <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i><sub>1</sub></span> to the higher <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i><sub>2</sub></span>. This raises the equilibrium quantity from <span class="texhtml"><i>Q</i><sub>1</sub></span> to the higher <span class="texhtml"><i>Q</i><sub>2</sub></span>.  (A movement along the curve is described as a "change in the quantity demanded" to distinguish it from a "change in demand," that is, a shift of the curve.) The <i>increase</i> in demand has caused an increase in (equilibrium) quantity.  The increase in demand could come from changing tastes and fashions, incomes, price changes in complementary and substitute goods, market expectations, and number of buyers. This would cause the entire demand curve to shift changing the equilibrium price and quantity.  Note in the diagram that the shift of the demand curve, by causing a new equilibrium price to emerge, resulted in <i>movement along</i> the supply curve from the point <span class="texhtml">(<i>Q</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>P</i><sub>1</sub>)</span> to the point <span class="texhtml">(<i>Q</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>P</i><sub>2</sub>)</span>.
</p><p>If the <i>demand decreases</i>, then the opposite happens: a shift of the curve to the left. If the demand starts at <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i><sub>2</sub></span>, and <i>decreases</i> to <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i><sub>1</sub></span>, the equilibrium price will decrease, and the equilibrium quantity will also decrease. The quantity supplied at each price is the same as before the demand shift, reflecting the fact that the supply curve has not shifted; but the equilibrium quantity and price are different as a result of the change (shift) in demand.
</p><p><b>Supply curve shifts</b>:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supply_(economics)" title="Supply (economics)">Supply (economics)</a></div>
<p>When technological progress occurs, the supply curve shifts.  For example, assume that someone invents a better way of growing wheat so that the cost of growing a given quantity of wheat decreases.   Otherwise stated, producers will be willing to supply more wheat at every price and this shifts the supply curve <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub></span> outward, to <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i><sub>2</sub></span>—an <i>increase in supply</i>. This increase in supply causes the equilibrium price to decrease from <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i><sub>1</sub></span> to <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i><sub>2</sub></span>. The equilibrium quantity increases from <span class="texhtml"><i>Q</i><sub>1</sub></span> to <span class="texhtml"><i>Q</i><sub>2</sub></span> as consumers move along the demand curve to the new lower price. As a result of a supply curve shift, the price and the quantity move in opposite directions.  If the quantity supplied <i>decreases</i>, the opposite happens. If the supply curve starts at <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i><sub>2</sub></span>, and shifts leftward to <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub></span>, the equilibrium price will increase and the equilibrium quantity will decrease as consumers move along the demand curve to the new higher price and associated lower quantity demanded. The quantity demanded at each price is the same as before the supply shift, reflecting the fact that the demand curve has not shifted. But due to the change (shift) in supply, the equilibrium quantity and price have changed.
</p><p>The movement of the supply curve in response to a change in a non-price determinant of supply is caused by a change in the y-intercept, the constant term of the supply equation. The supply curve shifts up and down the y axis as non-price determinants of demand change.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_equilibrium">Partial equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Partial equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Partial_equilibrium" title="Partial equilibrium">Partial equilibrium</a></div>
<p>Partial equilibrium, as the name suggests, takes into consideration only a part of the market to attain equilibrium.
</p><p>Jain proposes (attributed to <a href="/wiki/George_Stigler" title="George Stigler">George Stigler</a>): "A partial equilibrium is one which is based on only a restricted range of data, a standard example is price of a single product, the prices of all other products being held fixed during the analysis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The supply-and-demand model is a <b>partial equilibrium</b> model of <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">economic equilibrium</a>, where the clearance on the <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> of some specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> is obtained independently from prices and quantities in other markets. In other words, the prices of all <a href="/wiki/Substitute_good" title="Substitute good">substitutes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complement_good" title="Complement good">complements</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> levels of <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> are constant. This makes analysis much simpler than in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_equilibrium" title="General equilibrium">general equilibrium</a> model which includes an entire economy.
</p><p>Here the dynamic process is that prices adjust until supply equals demand. It is a powerfully simple technique that allows one to study <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">equilibrium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pareto_Efficiency" title="Pareto Efficiency">efficiency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comparative_statics" title="Comparative statics">comparative statics</a>.  The stringency of the simplifying assumptions inherent in this approach makes the model considerably more tractable, but may produce results which, while seemingly precise, do not effectively model real world economic
phenomena.
</p><p>Partial equilibrium analysis examines the effects of policy action in creating equilibrium only in that particular sector or market which is directly affected, ignoring its effect in any other market or industry assuming that they being small will have little impact if any.
</p><p>Hence this analysis is considered to be useful in constricted markets.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Walras" title="Léon Walras">Léon Walras</a> first formalized the idea of a one-period economic equilibrium of the general economic system, but it was French economist <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Augustin_Cournot" title="Antoine Augustin Cournot">Antoine Augustin Cournot</a> and English political economist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Marshall" title="Alfred Marshall">Alfred Marshall</a> who developed tractable models to analyze an economic system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_markets">Other markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The model of supply and demand also applies to various specialty markets.
</p><p>The model is commonly applied to <a href="/wiki/Wage" title="Wage">wages</a>, in the market for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" title="Labour (economics)">labor</a>. The typical roles of supplier and demander are reversed. The suppliers are individuals, who try to sell their labor for the highest price. The demanders of labor are businesses, which try to buy the type of labor they need at the lowest price. The equilibrium price for a certain type of labor is the wage rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, economist Steve Fleetwood revisited the empirical reality of supply and demand curves in labor markets and concluded that the evidence is "at best inconclusive and at worst casts doubt on their existence." For instance, he cites Kaufman and Hotchkiss (2006): "For adult men, nearly all studies find the labour supply curve to be negatively sloped or backward bending."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In both <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian</a> economics, the <a href="/wiki/Money_market" title="Money market">money market</a> is analyzed as a supply-and-demand system with <a href="/wiki/Interest#Market_interest_rates" title="Interest">interest rates</a> being the price. The <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> may be a vertical supply curve, if the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> of a country chooses to use <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> to fix its value regardless of the interest rate; in this case the money supply is totally inelastic. On the other hand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the money supply curve is a horizontal line if the central bank is targeting a fixed interest rate and ignoring the value of the money supply; in this case the money supply curve is perfectly elastic. The demand for money intersects with the money supply to determine the interest rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RitterSilberUdell_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-RitterSilberUdell-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to some studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the laws of supply and demand are applicable not only to the business relationships of people, but to the behaviour of social animals and to all living things that interact on the biological markets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> in scarce resource environments.
</p><p>The model of supply and demand accurately describes the characteristic of metabolic systems: specifically, it explains how <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_enzyme" title="Regulatory enzyme">feedback inhibition</a> allows metabolic pathways to respond to the demand for a metabolic intermediates while minimizing effects due to variation in the supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_estimation">Empirical estimation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Empirical estimation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Demand and supply relations in a market can be statistically estimated from price, quantity, and other <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> with sufficient information in the model.  This can be done with <i><a href="/wiki/System_of_linear_equations" title="System of linear equations">simultaneous-equation</a> methods of estimation</i> in <a href="/wiki/Econometrics" title="Econometrics">econometrics</a>.  Such methods allow solving for the model-relevant "structural coefficients," the estimated algebraic counterparts of the theory. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identification_(parameter)" title="Identification (parameter)">Parameter identification problem</a></i> is a common issue in "structural estimation." Typically, data on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exogenous_variable" title="Exogenous variable">exogenous</a> variables (that is, variables other than price and quantity, both of which are <a href="/wiki/Exogenous_and_endogenous_variables" title="Exogenous and endogenous variables">endogenous</a> variables) are needed to perform such an estimation.  An alternative to "structural estimation" is <a href="/wiki/Reduced_form" title="Reduced form">reduced-form</a> estimation, which regresses each of the endogenous variables on the respective exogenous variables.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Macroeconomic_uses">Macroeconomic uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Macroeconomic uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Demand and supply have also been generalized to explain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomic</a> variables in a <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_GDP" title="Real GDP">quantity of total output</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_price_level" title="Aggregate price level">aggregate price level</a>.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD-AS_model" title="AD-AS model">aggregate demand-aggregate supply model</a> may be the most direct application of supply and demand to macroeconomics, but other macroeconomic models also use supply and demand. Compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microeconomic" title="Microeconomic">microeconomic</a> uses of demand and supply, different (and more controversial) theoretical considerations apply to such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomic</a> counterparts as <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_supply" title="Aggregate supply">aggregate supply</a>. Demand and supply are also used in macroeconomic theory to relate <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> and money demand to <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rates</a>, and to relate labor supply and labor demand to wage rates.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 256th couplet of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tirukkural" title="Tirukkural">Tirukkural</a>, which was composed at least 2000 years ago, says that "if people do not consume a product or service, then there will not be anybody to supply that product or service for the sake of price".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Hamid S. Hosseini, the power of supply and demand was understood to some extent by several early Muslim scholars, such as fourteenth-century Syrian scholar <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Taymiyyah" title="Ibn Taymiyyah">Ibn Taymiyyah</a>, who wrote: "If desire for goods increases while its availability decreases, its price rises. On the other hand, if availability of the good increases and the desire for it decreases, the price comes down."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosseini_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hosseini-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1023981488">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .rquote{width:auto!important;float:none!important}}</style><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote rquote" style="float: right; width: 33%;"><p>If desire for goods increases while its availability decreases, its price rises. On the other hand, if availability of the good increases and the desire for it decreases, the price comes down.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Taymiyyah" title="Ibn Taymiyyah">Ibn Taymiyyah</a>, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosseini_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hosseini-14">[14]</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AdamSmith.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2173" data-file-width="1456" decoding="async" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/AdamSmith.jpg/150px-AdamSmith.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/AdamSmith.jpg/225px-AdamSmith.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/AdamSmith.jpg/300px-AdamSmith.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AdamSmith.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adam Smith</div></div></div>
<p>Shifting focus to the English etymology of the expression, it has been confirmed that the phrase 'supply and demand' was not used by English economics writers until after the end of the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> 
In <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>'s 1691 work <i>Some Considerations on the Consequences of the Lowering of Interest and the Raising of the Value of Money</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Locke alluded to the idea of supply and demand, however, he failed to accurately label it as such and thus, he fell short in coining the phrase and conveying its true significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groenewegen_P._2008_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Groenewegen_P._2008-17">[17]</a></sup>  Locke wrote: “The price of any commodity rises or falls by the proportion of the number of buyer and sellers” and “that which regulates the price... [of goods] is nothing else but their quantity in proportion to [the] Vent.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groenewegen_P._2008_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Groenewegen_P._2008-17">[17]</a></sup>  Locke's terminology drew criticism from John Law. Law argued that,"The Prices of Goods are not according to the quantity in proportion to the Vent, but in proportion to the Demand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> From Law the demand part of the phrase was given its proper title and it began to circulate among "prominent authorities" in the 1730s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groenewegen_P._2008_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Groenewegen_P._2008-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1755, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Hutcheson_(philosopher)" title="Francis Hutcheson (philosopher)">Francis Hutcheson</a>, in his <i>A System of Moral Philosophy</i>, furthered development toward the phrase by stipulating that, "the prices of goods depend on these two jointly, the Demand... and the Difficulty of acquiring."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groenewegen_P._2008_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Groenewegen_P._2008-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was not until 1767 that the phrase "supply and demand" was first used by Scottish writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Denham-Steuart" title="James Denham-Steuart">James Denham-Steuart</a> in his <i>Inquiry into the Principles of Political Economy.</i> He originated the use of this phrase by effectively combining "supply" and "demand" together in a number of different occasions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Price_determination" title="Price determination">price determination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Competitor_analysis" title="Competitor analysis">competitive analysis</a>. In Steuart's chapter entitled "Of Demand", he argues that "The nature of Demand is to encourage industry; and when it is regularly made, the effect of it is, that the supply for the most part is found to be in proportion to it, and then the demand is simple". It is presumably from this chapter that the idea spread to other authors and economic thinkers. <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> used the phrase after Steuart in his 1776 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i>. In <i>The Wealth of Nations</i>, Smith asserted that the supply price was fixed but that its "merit" (value) would decrease as its "scarcity" increased, this idea by Smith was later named the law of demand. In 1803, Thomas Robert Malthus used the phrase "supply and demand" twenty times in the second edition of the <i>Essay on Population</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groenewegen_P._2008_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Groenewegen_P._2008-17">[17]</a></sup> 
And <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">David Ricardo</a> in his 1817 work, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principles_of_Political_Economy_and_Taxation" title="Principles of Political Economy and Taxation">Principles of Political Economy and Taxation</a></i>, titled one chapter, "On the Influence of Demand and Supply on Price".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphrey_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humphrey-19">[19]</a></sup> In <i>Principles of Political Economy and Taxation</i>, Ricardo more rigorously laid down the idea of the assumptions that were used to build his ideas of supply and demand. In 1838, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Augustin_Cournot" title="Antoine Augustin Cournot">Antoine Augustin Cournot</a> developed a mathematical model of supply and demand in his <i>Researches into the Mathematical Principles of Wealth</i>, it included diagrams. It is important to note  that the use of the phrase was still rare and only a few examples of more than 20 uses in a single work have been identified by the end of the second decade of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groenewegen_P._2008_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Groenewegen_P._2008-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the late 19th century the marginalist school of thought emerged. The main innovators of this approach were <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_Jevons" title="William Stanley Jevons">Stanley Jevons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Menger" title="Carl Menger">Carl Menger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Walras" title="Léon Walras">Léon Walras</a>. The key idea was that the price was set by the subjective value of a good at the margin. This was a substantial change from Adam Smith's thoughts on determining the supply price.
</p><p>In his 1870 essay "On the Graphical Representation of Supply and Demand", <a href="/wiki/Fleeming_Jenkin" title="Fleeming Jenkin">Fleeming Jenkin</a> in the course of "introduc[ing] the diagrammatic method into the English economic literature"  published the first drawing of supply and demand curves in English,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Comparative_statics" title="Comparative statics">comparative statics</a> from a shift of supply or demand and application to the labor market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The model was further developed and popularized by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Marshall" title="Alfred Marshall">Alfred Marshall</a> in the 1890 textbook <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principles_of_Economics_(Marshall)" title="Principles of Economics (Marshall)">Principles of Economics</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphrey_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Humphrey-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supply_and_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hans_Albert" title="Hans Albert">Hans Albert</a> has argued that the <a href="/wiki/Ceteris_paribus" title="Ceteris paribus">ceteris paribus</a> conditions of the marginalist theory rendered the theory itself an empty tautology and completely closed to experimental testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In essence, he argues, the supply and demand curves (theoretical functions which express the quantity of a product which would be offered or requested for a given price) are purely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological" title="Ontological">ontological</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Piero_Sraffa" title="Piero Sraffa">Piero Sraffa</a>'s critique focused on the inconsistency (except in implausible circumstances) of partial equilibrium analysis and the rationale for the upward slope of the supply curve in a market for a produced consumption good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The notability of Sraffa's critique is also demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Samuelson" title="Paul Samuelson">Paul Samuelson</a>'s comments and engagements with it over many years, for example:
</p>
<dl><dd>What a cleaned-up version of Sraffa (1926) establishes is how <i>nearly empty</i> are <i>all</i> of Marshall's partial equilibrium boxes. To a logical purist of Wittgenstein and Sraffa class, the <i>Marshallian partial</i> equilibrium box of <i>constant</i> cost is even more empty than the box of <i>increasing</i> cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Keynesians" title="Post-Keynesians">Post-Keynesians</a> criticize the supply and demand model for failing to explain the prevalence of <a href="/wiki/Administered_prices" title="Administered prices">administered prices</a>, in which retail prices are set by firms, primarily based on a mark-up over normal average unit costs, and aren't responsive to changes in demand up to capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some economists criticize the conventional supply and demand theory for failing to explain or anticipate asset bubbles that can arise from a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback loop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Conventional supply and demand theory assumes that expectations of consumers do not change as a consequence of price changes. In scenarios such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_housing_bubble" title="United States housing bubble">United States housing bubble</a>, an initial price change of an asset can increase the expectations of investors, making the asset more lucrative and contributing to further price increases until market sentiment changes, which creates a positive feedback loop and an asset bubble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Asset bubbles cannot be understood in the conventional supply and demand framework because the conventional system assumes a price change will be self-correcting and the system will snap back to equilibrium.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Cockshott" title="Paul Cockshott">Paul Cockshott</a>'s critique focuses on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unfalsifiability" title="Unfalsifiability">unfalsifiability</a> of the neoclassical model.
