<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Year</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

</p>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Time of one planet's orbit around a star</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif/330px-Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif/495px-Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif/660px-Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_system_orrery_inner_planets.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An animation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_Solar_System" title="Inner Solar System">inner Solar System</a> planets' orbit around the Sun. The duration of the year is the time taken to go around the Sun.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>year</b> or <b>annus</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> of a planetary body, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, moving in <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">its orbit</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. Due to the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a>, the course of a year sees the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>, marked by change in <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>, the hours of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a>, and, consequently, <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_fertility" title="Soil fertility">soil fertility</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpolar_climate" title="Subpolar climate">subpolar</a> regions around the planet, four seasons are generally recognized: <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> regions, several geographical sectors do not present defined seasons; but in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics#Seasons_and_climate" title="Tropics">seasonal tropics</a>, the annual <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry seasons</a> are recognized and tracked.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Calendar_year" title="Calendar year">calendar year</a> is an approximation of the number of days of the Earth's orbital period, as counted in a given <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendar</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, or modern calendar, presents its calendar year to be either a <a href="/wiki/Common_year" title="Common year">common year</a> of 365 days or a <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">leap year</a> of 366 days, as do the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendars</a>. For the Gregorian calendar, the average length of the calendar year (the mean year) across the complete leap cycle of 400 years is 365.2425 days (97 out of 400 years are leap years). 
</p><p>In English, the <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_time" title="Unit of time">unit of time</a> for year is commonly abbreviated as "y" or "yr". The symbol "a" is more common in scientific literature, though its exact duration may be inconsistent.
In astronomy, the <a href="/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy)" title="Julian year (astronomy)">Julian year</a> is a unit of time defined as 365.25 <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a> of exactly 86,400 <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">seconds</a> (<a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a>), totalling exactly 31,557,600 seconds in the Julian astronomical year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <i>year</i> is also used for periods loosely associated with, but not identical to, the calendar or astronomical year, such as the <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_year" title="Seasonal year">seasonal year</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Academic_year" title="Academic year">academic year</a>, etc. Similarly, <i>year</i> can mean the orbital period of any <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>; for example, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_year" title="Martian year">Martian year</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venusian</a> year are examples of the time a planet takes to transit one complete orbit. The term can also be used in reference to any long period or cycle, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Year" title="Great Year">Great Year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>English <i>year</i> (via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Saxon_dialect_(Old_English)" title="West Saxon dialect (Old English)">West Saxon</a> <i>ġēar</i> (<span class="IPA" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/jɛar/</span>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglian_dialects" title="Anglian dialects">Anglian</a> <i>ġēr</i>) continues <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> <i>*jǣran</i> (<i>*j<a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language#ē₁_and_ē₂" title="Proto-Germanic language">ē₁</a>ran</i>). Cognates are <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Jahr</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>jār</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>ár</i> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothic</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%F0%90%8C%BE%F0%90%8C%B4%F0%90%8D%82" title="wikt:𐌾𐌴𐍂">jer</a></i>, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">Proto-Indo-European</a> noun <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:nowrap;" title="Proto-Indo-European language"><span lang="ine"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/yeh%E2%82%81-" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/yeh₁-">*yeh₁r-om</a></span></span></i> "year, season". Cognates also descended from the same Proto-Indo-European noun (with variation in suffix <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablaut" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Avestan" title="Avestan">Avestan</a> <i>yārǝ</i> "year", <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">ὥρα</span></span> (<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><i lang="grc-Latn">hṓra</i></span>) "year, season, period of time" (whence "<a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hour</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a> <i>jarŭ</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>hornus</i> "of this year". 
</p><p>Latin <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annus" title="Annus">annus</a></i></span> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_declension" title="2nd declension">2nd declension</a> masculine noun; <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">annum</i></span> is the <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative singular</a>; <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">annī</i></span> is genitive singular and nominative plural; <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">annō</i></span> the dative and ablative singular) is from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">PIE</a> noun <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:nowrap;" title="Proto-Indo-European language"><span lang="ine">*h₂et-no-</span></span></i>, which also yielded Gothic <i>aþn</i> "year" (only the dative plural <i>aþnam</i> is attested).
</p><p>Although most languages treat the word as thematic <i>*yeh₁r-o-</i>, there is evidence for an original derivation with an <i>*-r/n</i> suffix, <i>*yeh₁-ro-</i>. Both Indo-European words for year, <i>*yeh₁-ro-</i> and <i>*h₂et-no-</i>, would then be derived from verbal roots meaning "to go, move", <i>*h₁ey-</i> and <i>*h₂et-</i>, respectively (compare <a href="/wiki/Vedic_Sanskrit" title="Vedic Sanskrit">Vedic Sanskrit</a> <i>éti</i> "goes", <i>atasi</i> "thou goest, wanderest"). A number of English words are derived from Latin <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">annus</i></span>, such as <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/annual" title="wikt:annual">annual</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/annuity" title="wikt:annuity">annuity</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anniversary" title="wikt:anniversary">anniversary</a>, etc.; <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/per_annum" title="wikt:per annum">per annum</a></i> means "each year", <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">annō Dominī</a></i></span> means "in the year of the Lord".
</p><p>The Greek word for "year", <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">ἔτος</span></span>, is cognate with Latin <i>vetus</i> "old", from the PIE word <i>*wetos-</i> "year", also preserved in this meaning in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i><span title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration"><i lang="sa-Latn">vat-sa-ras</i></span></i> "year" and <i><span title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration"><i lang="sa-Latn">vat-sa-</i></span></i> "yearling (calf)", the latter also reflected in Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vitulus" title="wikt:vitulus">vitulus</a></i> "bull calf", English <i>wether</i> "ram" (Old English <i>weðer</i>, Gothic <i>wiþrus</i> "lamb").
</p><p>In some languages, it is common to count years by referencing to one season, as in "summers", or "winters", or "harvests". Examples include Chinese <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B9%B4" title="wikt:年">年</a> "year", originally <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A7%82" title="wikt:秂">秂</a>, an ideographic compound of a person carrying a bundle of wheat denoting "harvest". Slavic besides <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/god%D1%8A" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/godъ">godŭ</a></i> "time period; year" uses <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/l%C4%9Bto" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/lěto">lěto</a></i> "summer; year".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intercalation">Intercalation</span></h2>
<p>Astronomical years do not have an <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> number of days or lunar months. Any calendar that follows an astronomical year must have a system of <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">intercalation</a> such as leap years.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Julian_calendar">Julian calendar</span></h3>
<p>In the Julian calendar, the average (mean) length of a year is 365.25 days. In a non-leap year, there are 365 days, in a leap year there are 366 days. A leap year occurs every fourth year, or leap year, during which a leap day is <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)#Solar_calendars" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">intercalated</a> into the month of February. The name "Leap Day" is applied to the added day.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Revised_Julian_calendar" title="Revised Julian calendar">Revised Julian calendar</a>, proposed in 1923 and used in some <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Churches</a>,
has 218 leap years every 900 years, for the average (mean) year length of <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365242222200000♠"></span>365.242<span style="margin-left:.25em;">2222</span></span> days, close to the length of the mean tropical year, <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365242190000000♠"></span>365.242<span style="margin-left:.25em;">19</span></span> days (relative error of 9·10<sup>−8</sup>).
In the year 2800 CE, the Gregorian and Revised Julian calendars will begin to differ by one calendar day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gregorian_calendar">Gregorian calendar</span></h3>
<p>The Gregorian calendar attempts to cause the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northward_equinox" title="Northward equinox">northward equinox</a> to fall on or shortly before March 21 and hence it follows the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">northward equinox year</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Because 97 out of 400 years are leap years, the mean length of the Gregorian calendar year is <span style="white-space:nowrap">365.242<span style="margin-left:0.25em">5</span></span> days; with a relative error below one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> (8·10<sup>−7</sup>) relative to the current length of the mean <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical year</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap">365.242<span style="margin-left:0.25em">19</span></span> days) and even closer to the current <i>March equinox year</i> of <span style="white-space:nowrap">365.242<span style="margin-left:0.25em">374</span></span> days that it aims to match. It is estimated that by the year 4000 CE, the northward equinox will fall back by one day in the Gregorian calendar,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> not because of this difference, but due to the slowing of the Earth's rotation and the associated lengthening of the day.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_calendars">Other calendars</span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">Lunisolar calendar</a></div>
<p>Historically, lunisolar calendars intercalated entire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leap_month" title="Leap month">leap months</a> on an observational basis. Lunisolar calendars have mostly fallen out of use except for liturgical reasons (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">Hindu calendars</a>).
</p><p>A modern adaptation of the historical <a href="/wiki/Jalali_calendar" title="Jalali calendar">Jalali calendar</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Hijri_calendar" title="Solar Hijri calendar">Solar Hijri calendar</a> (1925), is a purely <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">solar calendar</a> with an irregular pattern of leap days based on observation (or astronomical computation), aiming to place new year (<a href="/wiki/Nowruz" title="Nowruz">Nowruz</a>) on the day of <a href="/wiki/March_equinox" title="March equinox">vernal equinox</a> (for the time zone of <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>), as opposed to using an algorithmic system of leap years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Year_numbering">Year numbering</span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Calendar_era" title="Calendar era">calendar era</a> assigns a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_number_(linguistics)" title="Cardinal number (linguistics)">cardinal number</a> to each sequential year, using a reference event in the past (called the <a href="/wiki/Epoch" title="Epoch">epoch</a>) as the beginning of the era.
</p><p>The Gregorian calendar era is the world's most widely used <a href="/wiki/Civil_calendar" title="Civil calendar">civil calendar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its epoch is a <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Exiguus#Anno_Domini" title="Dionysius Exiguus">6th century estimate</a> of the date birth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth" title="Jesus of Nazareth">Jesus of Nazareth</a>. Two notations are used to indicate year numbering in the Gregorian calendar: the Christian "<a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">Anno Domini</a>" (meaning "in the year of the Lord"), abbreviated AD; and "<a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">Common Era</a>", abbreviated CE, preferred by many of other faiths and none.  Year numbers are based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_counting" title="Inclusive counting">inclusive counting</a>, so that there is no "year zero".  Years before the epoch are abbreviated BC for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ">Before Christ</a> or BCE for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_the_Common_Era" title="Before the Common Era">Before the Common Era</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_year_numbering" title="Astronomical year numbering">Astronomical year numbering</a>, positive numbers indicate years AD/CE, the number <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/0_(year)" title="0 (year)">0</a> designates 1 BC/BCE, −1 designates 2 BC/BCE, and so on.
</p><p>Other eras include that of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a>, <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ab_Urbe_Condita" title="Ab Urbe Condita">Ab Urbe Condita</a></i></span> ("from the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">the city</a>), abbreviated AUC; <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Anno_Mundi" title="Anno Mundi">Anno Mundi</a></i></span> ("year of the world"), used for the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a> and abbreviated AM; and the Japanese emperor eras described above. The Islamic <a href="/wiki/Hijri_year" title="Hijri year">Hijri year</a>, (year of the <a href="/wiki/Hijrah" title="Hijrah">Hijrah</a>, <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anno_Hegirae" title="Anno Hegirae">Anno Hegirae</a></i></span> abbreviated AH), is a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendar</a> of twelve <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">lunar months</a> and thus is shorter than a solar year.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pragmatic_divisions">Pragmatic divisions</span></h2>
<p>Financial and scientific calculations often use a <a href="/wiki/365-day_calendar" title="365-day calendar">365-day calendar</a> to simplify daily rates.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiscal_year">Fiscal year</span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> or financial year is a 12-month period used for calculating annual financial statements in businesses and other organizations. In many jurisdictions, regulations regarding accounting require such reports once per twelve months, but do not require that the twelve months constitute a calendar year.
</p><p>For example, in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> the fiscal year runs from April 1; in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> it runs from April 1 for purposes of corporation tax and government financial statements, but from April 6 for purposes of personal taxation and payment of state benefits; in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> it runs from July 1; while in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> the fiscal year of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> runs from October 1.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_year">Academic year</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Academic_year" title="Academic year">Academic year</a></div>
<p>An academic year is the annual period during which a student attends an <a href="/wiki/Educational_institution" title="Educational institution">educational institution</a>. The academic year may be divided into <a href="/wiki/Academic_term" title="Academic term">academic terms</a>, such as semesters or quarters. The school year in many countries starts in August or September and ends in May, June or July. In Israel the academic year begins around October or November, aligned with the second month of the Hebrew calendar.
</p><p>Some schools in the UK, Canada and the United States divide the academic year into <i>three</i> roughly equal-length terms (called <i>trimesters</i> or <i>quarters</i> in the United States), roughly coinciding with autumn, winter, and spring. At some, a shortened summer session, sometimes considered part of the regular academic year, is attended by students on a voluntary or elective basis. Other schools break the year into <i>two</i> main semesters, a first (typically August through December) and a second semester (January through May). Each of these main semesters may be split in half by mid-term exams, and each of the halves is referred to as a <i>quarter</i> (or <i>term</i> in some countries). There may also be a voluntary summer session and/or a short January session.
</p><p>Some other schools, including some in the United States, have <i>four</i> marking periods. Some schools in the United States, notably <a href="/wiki/Boston_Latin_School" title="Boston Latin School">Boston Latin School</a>, may divide the year into <i>five or more</i> marking periods. Some state in defense of this that there is perhaps a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_correlation" title="Positive correlation">positive correlation</a> between report frequency and academic achievement.
</p><p>There are typically 180 days of teaching each year in schools in the US, excluding weekends and breaks, while there are 190 days for pupils in state schools in Canada, New Zealand and the United Kingdom, and 200 for pupils in Australia.
</p><p>In India the academic year normally starts from June 1 and ends on May 31. Though schools start closing from mid-March, the actual academic closure is on May 31 and in Nepal it starts from July 15.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Schools and universities in Australia typically have academic years that roughly align with the calendar year (i.e., starting in February or March and ending in October to December), as the southern hemisphere experiences summer from December to February.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_years">Astronomical years</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_month" title="Astronomical month">Astronomical month</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Julian_year">Julian year</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy)" title="Julian year (astronomy)">Julian year (astronomy)</a></div>
<p>The Julian year, as used in astronomy and other sciences, is a time unit defined as exactly 365.25 days of 86,400 <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">SI seconds</a> each ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephemeris_day" title="Ephemeris day">ephemeris days</a>"). This is the normal meaning of the unit "year" used in various scientific contexts. The Julian century of <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7004365250000000000♠"></span>36<span style="margin-left:.25em;">525</span></span> ephemeris days and the Julian millennium of <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7005365250000000000♠"></span>365<span style="margin-left:.25em;">250</span></span> ephemeris days are used in astronomical calculations. Fundamentally, expressing a time interval in Julian years is a way to precisely specify an amount of time (not how many "real" years), for long time intervals where stating the number of ephemeris days would be unwieldy and unintuitive. By convention, the Julian year is used in the computation of the distance covered by a <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-year</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Unified_Code_for_Units_of_Measure" title="Unified Code for Units of Measure">Unified Code for Units of Measure</a> (but not according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Physics" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Physics">International Union of Pure and Applied Physics</a> or the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Geological_Sciences" title="International Union of Geological Sciences">International Union of Geological Sciences</a>, see below), the symbol <i>a</i> (without subscript) always refers to the Julian year, <i>a<sub>j</sub></i>, of exactly <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7007315576000000000♠"></span>31<span style="margin-left:.25em;">557</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">600</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">seconds</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>365.25 d × <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7004864000000000000♠"></span>86<span style="margin-left:.25em;">400</span></span> s = 1 a = 1 a<sub>j</sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7001315576000000000♠"></span>31.5576</span> <a href="/wiki/Second#SI_multiples" title="Second">Ms</a></dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="#SI_prefix_multipliers">SI multiplier prefixes</a> may be applied to it to form "ka", "Ma", etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCUM_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCUM-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Sidereal.2C_tropical.2C_and_anomalistic_years"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sidereal,_tropical,_and_anomalistic_years">Sidereal, tropical, and anomalistic years</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">Sidereal year</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">Tropical year</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">The relations among these are considered more fully in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">Axial precession (astronomy)</a>.</div>
<p>Each of these three years can be loosely called an <i>astronomical year</i>.
</p><p>The sidereal year is the time taken for the Earth to complete one revolution of its <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, as measured against a fixed frame of reference (such as the fixed stars, Latin <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">sidera</i></span>, singular <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">sidus</i></span>). Its average duration is <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365256363004000♠"></span>365.256<span style="margin-left:.25em;">363</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">004</span></span> days (365 d 6 h 9 min 9.76 s) (at the epoch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J2000.0" title="J2000.0">J2000.0</a> = January 1, 2000, 12:00:00 <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_Time" title="Terrestrial Time">TT</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today the mean tropical year is defined as the period of time for the mean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_longitude" title="Ecliptic longitude">ecliptic longitude</a> of the Sun to increase by 360 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Since the Sun's ecliptic longitude is measured with respect to the equinox,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the tropical year comprises a complete cycle of the seasons and is the basis of <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">solar calendars</a> such as the internationally used <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>. The modern definition of mean tropical year differs from the actual time between passages of, e.g., the northward equinox, by a minute or two, for several reasons explained below. Because of the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">axial precession</a>, this year is about 20 minutes shorter than the sidereal year. The mean tropical year is approximately 365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, 45 seconds, using the modern definition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> ( = 365.24219 d × 86 400 s). The length of the tropical year varies a bit over thousands of years because the rate of axial precession is not constant.
</p><p><span class="anchor" id="Anomalistic"></span>The anomalistic year is the time taken for the Earth to complete one revolution with respect to its <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apsides</a>. The orbit of the Earth is elliptical; the extreme points, called apsides, are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a>, where the Earth is closest to the Sun (January 5, 07:48 UT in 2020), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a>, where the Earth is farthest from the Sun (July 4, 11:35 UT in 2020). The anomalistic year is usually defined as the time between perihelion passages. Its average duration is 365.259636 days (365 d 6 h 13 min 52.6 s) (at the epoch J2011.0).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Draconic_year">Draconic year <span class="anchor" id="Draconitic_year"></span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draconic_period" title="Draconic period">Draconic period</a></div>
<p>The draconic year, draconitic year, eclipse year, or ecliptic year is the time taken for the Sun (as seen from the Earth) to complete one revolution with respect to the same <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">lunar node</a> (a point where the Moon's orbit intersects the ecliptic). The year is associated with <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">eclipses</a>: these occur only when both the Sun and the Moon are near these nodes; so eclipses occur within about a month of every half eclipse year. Hence there are two <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_season" title="Eclipse season">eclipse seasons</a> every eclipse year. The average duration of the eclipse year is
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002346620075883000♠"></span>346.620<span style="margin-left:.25em;">075</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">883</span></span> days (346 d 14 h 52 min 54 s) (at the epoch J2000.0).</dd></dl>
<p>This term is sometimes erroneously used for the draconic or nodal period of <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">lunar precession</a>, that is the period of a complete revolution of the Moon's ascending node around the ecliptic: <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7001186128159320000♠"></span>18.612<span style="margin-left:.25em;">815</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">932</span></span> Julian years (<span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7003679833101900000♠"></span>6<span style="margin-left:.25em;">798</span>.331<span style="margin-left:.25em;">019</span></span> days; at the epoch J2000.0).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_moon_cycle">Full moon cycle</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_moon_cycle" title="Full moon cycle">full moon cycle</a> is the time for the Sun (as seen from the Earth) to complete one revolution with respect to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> of the Moon's orbit. This period is associated with the apparent size of the <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a>, and also with the varying duration of the <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">synodic month</a>. The duration of one full moon cycle is:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002411784430290000♠"></span>411.784<span style="margin-left:.25em;">430</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">29</span></span> days (411 days 18 hours 49 minutes 35 seconds) (at the epoch J2000.0).</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_year">Lunar year</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_year" title="Lunar year">lunar year</a> comprises twelve full cycles of the phases of the Moon, as seen from Earth. It has a duration of approximately 354.37 days. <a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a> use this for celebrating their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_holidays" title="Muslim holidays">Eids</a> and for marking the start of the fasting month of <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a>. A Muslim calendar year is based on the lunar cycle. The <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Jewish calendar</a> is also essentially lunar, except that an intercalary lunar month is added once every two or three years, in order to keep the calendar synchronized with the solar cycle as well. Thus, a lunar year on the Jewish (Hebrew) calendar consists of either twelve or thirteen lunar months.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vague_year">Vague year</span></h3>
<p>The vague year, from <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">annus vagus</i></span> or wandering year, is an integral approximation to the year equaling 365 days, which wanders in relation to more exact years. Typically the vague year is divided into 12 <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/schematic" title="wikt:schematic">schematic</a> months of 30 days each plus 5 <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">epagomenal</a> days. The vague year was used in the calendars of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_calendar" title="Ethiopian calendar">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_calendar" title="Egyptian calendar">Ancient Egypt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_calendar" title="Iranian calendar">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_calendar" title="Armenian calendar">Armenia</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_calendars" title="Mesoamerican calendars">Mesoamerica</a> among the <a href="/wiki/Aztec_calendar" title="Aztec calendar">Aztecs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haab%27" title="Haab'">Maya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is still used by many Zoroastrian communities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heliacal_year">Heliacal year</span></h3>
<p>A heliacal year is the interval between the <a href="/wiki/Heliacal_rising" title="Heliacal rising">heliacal risings</a> of a star. It differs from the <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">sidereal year</a> for stars away from the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> due mainly to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_of_the_equinoxes" title="Precession of the equinoxes">precession of the equinoxes</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sothic_year">Sothic year</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sothic_cycle" title="Sothic cycle">Sothic year</a> is the interval between heliacal risings of the star <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a>. It is currently less than the <a href="#Sidereal,_tropical,_and_anomalistic_years">sidereal year</a> and its duration is very close to the Julian year of 365.25 days.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaussian_year">Gaussian year</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_year" title="Gaussian year">Gaussian year</a> is the sidereal year for a planet of negligible mass (relative to the Sun) and unperturbed by other planets that is governed by the <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_gravitational_constant" title="Gaussian gravitational constant">Gaussian gravitational constant</a>. Such a planet would be slightly closer to the Sun than Earth's mean distance. Its length is:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365256898300000♠"></span>365.256<span style="margin-left:.25em;">8983</span></span> days (365 d 6 h 9 min 56 s).</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Besselian_year">Besselian year</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Besselian_epoch#Besselian_years" title="Besselian epoch">Besselian year</a> is a tropical year that starts when the (fictitious) mean Sun reaches an ecliptic longitude of 280°. This is currently on or close to January 1. It is named after the 19th-century German astronomer and mathematician <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel" title="Friedrich Bessel">Friedrich Bessel</a>. The following equation can be used to compute the current Besselian epoch (in years):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>B = 1900.0 + (Julian date<sub>TT</sub> − <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7006241502031352000♠"></span>2<span style="margin-left:.25em;">415</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">020</span>.313<span style="margin-left:.25em;">52</span></span>) / <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365242198781000♠"></span>365.242<span style="margin-left:.25em;">198</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">781</span></span></dd></dl>
<p>The TT subscript indicates that for this formula, the Julian date should use the <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_Time" title="Terrestrial Time">Terrestrial Time</a> scale, or its predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris_time" title="Ephemeris time">ephemeris time</a>.
</p>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Length_of_day_(astronometry)" title="Length of day (astronometry)">Length of day (astronometry)</a></div>
<p>The exact length of an astronomical year changes over time.
</p>
<ul><li>The positions of the equinox and solstice points with respect to the apsides of Earth's orbit change: the equinoxes and solstices move westward relative to the stars because of <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precession</a>, and the apsides move in the other direction because of the long-term effects of gravitational pull by the other planets. Since the speed of the Earth varies according to its position in its orbit as measured from its perihelion, Earth's speed when in a solstice or equinox point changes over time: if such a point moves toward perihelion, the interval between two passages decreases a little from year to year; if the point moves towards aphelion, that period increases a little from year to year. So a "tropical year" measured from one passage of the northward ("vernal") equinox to the next, differs from the one measured between passages of the southward ("autumnal") equinox. The average over the full orbit does not change because of this, so the length of the average tropical year does not change because of this second-order effect.</li>
<li>Each planet's movement is perturbed by the gravity of every other planet. This leads to short-term fluctuations in its speed, and therefore its period from year to year. Moreover, it causes long-term changes in its orbit, and therefore also long-term changes in these periods.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_drag" title="Tidal drag">Tidal drag</a> between the Earth and the Moon and Sun increases the length of the day and of the month (by transferring angular momentum from the rotation of the Earth to the revolution of the Moon); since the apparent mean solar day is the unit with which we measure the length of the year in civil life, the length of the year appears to decrease. The rotation rate of the Earth is also changed by factors such as <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>.</li></ul>

<p>An average Gregorian year may be said to be 365.2425 <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a> (52.1775 <a href="/wiki/Week" title="Week">weeks</a>, and if an hour is defined as one twenty-fourth of a day, <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7003876582000000000♠"></span>8<span style="margin-left:.25em;">765</span>.82</span> <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hours</a>, <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7005525949199999999♠"></span>525<span style="margin-left:.25em;">949</span>.2</span> <a href="/wiki/Minute" title="Minute">minutes</a> or <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7007315569520000000♠"></span>31<span style="margin-left:.25em;">556</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">952</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">seconds</a>). Note however that in absolute time the average Gregorian year does not exist, because each period of 400 years is longer (by more than 1000 seconds) than the preceding one as the rotation of the earth slows. For this calendar, a common year is 365 days (<span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7003876000000000000♠"></span>8760</span> hours, <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7005525600000000000♠"></span>525<span style="margin-left:.25em;">600</span></span> minutes or <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7007315360000000000♠"></span>31<span style="margin-left:.25em;">536</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> seconds), and a leap year is 366 days (<span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7003878400000000000♠"></span>8784</span> hours, <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7005527040000000000♠"></span>527<span style="margin-left:.25em;">040</span></span> minutes or <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7007316224000000000♠"></span>31<span style="margin-left:.25em;">622</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">400</span></span> seconds). The 400-year civil cycle of the Gregorian calendar has <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7005146097000000000♠"></span>146<span style="margin-left:.25em;">097</span></span> days and hence exactly <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7004208710000000000♠"></span>20<span style="margin-left:.25em;">871</span></span> weeks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_astronomical_years">Greater astronomical years</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equinoctial_cycle">Equinoctial cycle</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Year" title="Great Year">Great Year</a>, or equinoctial cycle, corresponds to a complete revolution of the equinoxes around the ecliptic. Its length is about 25,700 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galactic_year">Galactic year</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Galactic_year" title="Galactic year">Galactic year</a> is the time it takes Earth's <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> to revolve once around the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">Galactic Center</a>. It comprises roughly 230 million Earth years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_year">Seasonal year</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_year" title="Seasonal year">Seasonal year</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">Effect of sun angle on climate</a></div>
<p>A seasonal year is the time between successive recurrences of a seasonal event such as the flooding of a river, the migration of a species of bird, the flowering of a species of plant, the first frost, or the first scheduled game of a certain sport. All of these events can have wide variations of more than a <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">month</a> from year to year.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols_and_abbreviations">Symbols and abbreviations <span class="anchor" id="Symbols"></span><span class="anchor" id="Abbreviations"></span></span></h2>
<p>A common symbol for the year as a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_time" title="Unit of time">unit of time</a> is "a", taken from the Latin word <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">annus</i></span>. 
For example, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) <i>Guide for the Use of the International System of Units (SI)</i> supports the symbol "a" as the unit of time for a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In English, the abbreviations "y" or "yr" are more commonly used in non-scientific literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-How-22">[22]</a></sup> In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_sciences" title="Earth sciences">Earth sciences</a> branches (<a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>), "<a href="/wiki/Kyr" title="Kyr">kyr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">myr</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byr" title="Byr">byr</a>" (thousands, millions, and billions of years, respectively) and similar abbreviations are used to denote intervals of time remote from the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGUStyle_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGUStyle-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strat_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strat-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Unified_Code_for_Units_of_Measure" title="Unified Code for Units of Measure">Unified Code for Units of Measure</a> (UCUM) disambiguates the varying symbologies of ISO 1000, ISO 2955 and ANSI X3.50 by using:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCUM_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCUM-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>a<sub>t</sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365242190000000♠"></span>365.242<span style="margin-left:.25em;">19</span></span> days for the mean tropical year;</dd>
<dd>a<sub>j</sub> = 365.25 days for the mean Julian year;</dd>
<dd>a<sub>g</sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365242500000000♠"></span>365.2425</span> days for the mean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_year" title="Gregorian year">Gregorian year</a>;</dd></dl>
<p>In the UCUM, the symbol "a", without any qualifier, equals 1 a<sub>j</sub>.
The UCUM also minimizes confusion with <i><a href="/wiki/Hectare#Are" title="Hectare">are</a></i>, a unit of area, by using the abbreviation "ar".
</p><p>Since 1989, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) recognizes the symbol "a" rather than "yr" for a year, notes the different kinds of year, and recommends adopting the Julian year of 365.25 days, unless otherwise specified (IAU <i>Style Manual</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAU-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1987, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Physics" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Physics">International Union of Pure and Applied Physics</a> (IUPAP) notes "a" as the general symbol for the time unit year (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAP_Red_Book" title="IUPAP Red Book">IUPAP <i>Red Book</i></a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
Since 1993, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_Green_Book" title="IUPAC Green Book"><i>Green Book</i></a> also uses the same symbol "a", notes the difference between Gregorian year and Julian year, and adopts the former (a=365.2425 days),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> also noted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_Gold_Book" title="IUPAC Gold Book">IUPAC <i>Gold Book</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gold_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, the IUPAC and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Geological_Sciences" title="International Union of Geological Sciences">International Union of Geological Sciences</a> jointly recommended defining the "annus", with symbol "a", as the length of the tropical year in the year 2000:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>a = <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7007315569254450000♠"></span>31<span style="margin-left:.25em;">556</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">925</span>.445</span> seconds (approximately <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002365242192650000♠"></span>365.242<span style="margin-left:.25em;">192</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">65</span></span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephemeris_day" title="Ephemeris day">ephemeris days</a>)</dd></dl>
<p>This differs from the above definition of 365.25 days by about 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a>. The joint document says that definitions such as the Julian year "bear an inherent, pre-programmed obsolescence because of the variability of Earth's orbital movement", but then proposes using the length of the tropical year as of 2000 AD (specified down to the millisecond), which suffers from the same problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biever-war_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biever-war-31">[31]</a></sup> (The tropical year oscillates with time by more than a minute.)
</p><p>The notation has proved controversial as it conflicts with an earlier convention among geoscientists to use "a" specifically for "years ago" (e.g. 1 Ma for 1 million years ago), and "y" or "yr" for a one-year time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biever-war_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biever-war-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iupac2011_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-iupac2011-32">[32]</a></sup>
However, this historical practice does not comply with the NIST <i>Guide</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIST-21">[21]</a></sup> considering the unacceptability of mixing information concerning the <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">physical quantity</a> being measured (in this case, time intervals or points in time) with the units and also the unnaceptability of using abbreviations for units.
Furthermore, according to the <a href="/wiki/UK_Metric_Association" title="UK Metric Association">UK Metric Association</a> (UKMA), language-independent symbols are more universally understood (UKMA <i>Style guide</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_Metric_Association_2017_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK_Metric_Association_2017-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SI_prefix_multipliers">SI prefix multipliers</span></h3>
<p>For the following, there are alternative forms that elide the consecutive vowels, such as <i>kilannus</i>, <i>megannus</i>, etc. The exponents and exponential notations are typically used for calculating and in displaying calculations, and for conserving space, as in tables of data.
</p>
<ul><li><span class="anchor" id="Kiloannus"></span><span class="anchor" id="Kiloannus"></span> ka (for <i>kiloannus</i>) – a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_time" title="Unit of time">unit of time</a> equal to one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thousand" title="Thousand">thousand</a> or 10<sup>3</sup> years, also known as a <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and calendar uses. The prefix multiplier "ka" is typically used in geology, paleontology, and <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">periods</a>, where a non−<a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> technique such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> dating, <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium-thorium_dating" title="Uranium-thorium dating">uranium-thorium dating</a> or <a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varve</a> analysis is used as the primary method for age determination. If age is determined primarily by <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a>, then the age should be expressed in either radiocarbon or calendar (calibrated) years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">Before Present</a>.</li>
<li><span class="anchor" id="Megaannus"></span><span class="anchor" id="Megaannus"></span> Ma (for <i>megaannus</i>) – a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_time" title="Unit of time">unit of time</a> equal to one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million" title="Million">million</a> or 10<sup>6</sup> years. The suffix "Ma" is commonly used in scientific disciplines such as geology, paleontology, and <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">celestial mechanics</a>. In astronomical applications, the year used is the Julian year of precisely 365.25 days. In geology and paleontology, the year is not so precise and varies depending on the author.</li>
<li><span class="anchor" id="Gigaannus"></span><span class="anchor" id="Gigaannus"></span> Ga (for <i>gigaannus</i>) – a unit of time equal to one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> or 10<sup>9</sup> years. "Ga" is commonly used in scientific disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmology</a> and geology to signify extremely long time periods in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_earth" title="History of the earth">the formation of the Earth</a> occurred approximately 4.54 Ga (4.54 billion years) ago and the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a> is approximately 13.8 Ga.</li>
<li><span class="anchor" id="Teraannus"></span><span class="anchor" id="Teraannus"></span> Ta (for <i>teraannus</i>) – a unit of time equal to one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000000_(number)" title="1000000000000 (number)">trillion</a> or 10<sup>12</sup> years. "Ta" is an extremely long unit of time, about 70 times as long as the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a>. It is the same order of magnitude as the expected life span of a small <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a>.</li>
<li><span class="anchor" id="Petaannus"></span><span class="anchor" id="Petaannus"></span> Pa (for <i>petaannus</i>) – a unit of time equal to one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10%5E15" title="10^15">quadrillion</a> or 10<sup>15</sup> years. The <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclide</a> <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium-113</a> is about 8 Pa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  This symbol coincides with that for the <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascal</a> without a multiplier prefix, though both are infrequently used and context will normally be sufficient to distinguish time from pressure values.</li>
<li><span class="anchor" id="Exaannus"></span><span class="anchor" id="Exaannus"></span> Ea (for <i>exaannus</i>) – a unit of time equal to one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quintillion" title="Quintillion">quintillion</a> or 10<sup>18</sup> years. The half-life of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten-180</a> is 1.8 Ea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Abbreviations_for_.22years_ago.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Abbreviations_for_"years_ago"'>Abbreviations for "years ago"</span></h3>
<p><span class="anchor" id="Abbreviations_yr_and_ya"></span><span class="anchor" id="years_ago"></span><span class="anchor" id="kya"></span><span class="anchor" id="Mya"></span><span class="anchor" id="Bya"></span>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">Before Present</a></div>
<p>In geology and paleontology, a distinction sometimes is made between abbreviation "yr" for <i>years</i> and "ya" for <i>years ago</i>, combined with prefixes for thousand, million, or billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGUStyle_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-AGUStyle-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASC_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASC-37">[37]</a></sup> In archaeology, dealing with more recent periods, normally expressed dates, e.g. "10,000 BC", may be used as a more traditional form than <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">Before Present</a> ("BP").

</p>

<li id="cite_note-NASC-37"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-NASC_37-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1067248974" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><cite class="citation journal cs1" id="CITEREFNorth_American_Commission_on_Stratigraphic_Nomenclature">North American Commission on Stratigraphic Nomenclature. <a class="external text" href="http://ngmdb.usgs.gov/Info/NACSN/Code2/code2.html#Article13" rel="nofollow">"North American Stratigraphic Code (Article 13 (c))"</a>. <q>(c) Convention and abbreviations. – The age of a stratigraphic unit or the time of a geologic event, as commonly determined by numerical dating or by reference to a calibrated time-scale, may be expressed in years before the present. The unit of time is the modern year as presently recognized worldwide.  Recommended (but not mandatory) abbreviations for such ages are SI (International System of Units) multipliers coupled with "a" for annus: ka, Ma, and Ga for kilo-annus (10<sup>3</sup> years), Mega-annus (10<sup>6</sup> years), and Giga-annus (10<sup>9</sup> years), respectively. Use of these terms after the age value follows the convention established in the field of C-14 dating. The "present" refers to AD 1950, and such qualifiers as "ago" or "before the present" are omitted after the value because measurement of the duration from the present to the past is implicit in the designation. In contrast, the duration of a remote interval of geologic time, as a number of years, should not be expressed by the same symbols. Abbreviations for numbers of years, without reference to the present, are informal (e.g., y or yr for years; my, m.y., or m.yr. for millions of years; and so forth, as preference dictates).
