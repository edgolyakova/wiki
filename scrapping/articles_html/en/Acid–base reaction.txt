</tr><tr><td class="sidebar-navbar"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1063604349">.mw-parser-output .navbar{display:inline;font-size:88%;font-weight:normal}.mw-parser-output .navbar-collapse{float:left;text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .navbar-boxtext{word-spacing:0}.mw-parser-output .navbar ul{display:inline-block;white-space:nowrap;line-height:inherit}.mw-parser-output .navbar-brackets::before{margin-right:-0.125em;content:"[ "}.mw-parser-output .navbar-brackets::after{margin-left:-0.125em;content:" ]"}.mw-parser-output .navbar li{word-spacing:-0.125em}.mw-parser-output .navbar a>span,.mw-parser-output .navbar a>abbr{text-decoration:inherit}.mw-parser-output .navbar-mini abbr{font-variant:small-caps;border-bottom:none;text-decoration:none;cursor:inherit}.mw-parser-output .navbar-ct-full{font-size:114%;margin:0 7em}.mw-parser-output .navbar-ct-mini{font-size:114%;margin:0 4em}</style><div class="navbar plainlinks hlist navbar-mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Acids_and_bases" title="Template:Acids and bases"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Acids_and_bases" title="Template talk:Acids and bases"><abbr title="Discuss this template">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Acids_and_bases&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>An <b>acid–base reaction</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> that occurs between an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>. It can be used to determine <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. Several <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theoretical</a> frameworks provide alternative conceptions of the reaction mechanisms and their application in solving related problems; these are called the acid–base theories, for example, <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory">Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory</a>.
</p><p>Their importance becomes apparent in analyzing acid–base reactions for gaseous or liquid species, or when acid or base character may be somewhat less apparent. The first of these concepts was provided by the French <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, around 1776.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lavoisier_1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lavoisier_1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is important to think of the acid-base reaction models as theories that complement each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> For example, the current Lewis model has the broadest definition of what an acid and base are, with the Brønsted-Lowry theory being a subset of what acids and bases are, and the Arrhenius theory being the most restrictive.
</p>

<h2><span id="Acid.E2.80.93base_definitions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid–base_definitions">Acid–base definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Acid–base definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_development">Historic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept of an acid-base reaction was first proposed in 1754 by <a href="/wiki/Guillaume-Fran%C3%A7ois_Rouelle" title="Guillaume-François Rouelle">Guillaume-François Rouelle</a>, who introduced the word "<a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>" into chemistry to mean a substance which reacts with an acid to give it solid form (as a salt). Bases are mostly bitter in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Lavoisier.27s_oxygen_theory_of_acids"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lavoisier's_oxygen_theory_of_acids">Lavoisier's oxygen theory of acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lavoisier's oxygen theory of acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first scientific concept of acids and bases was provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lavoisier" title="Lavoisier">Lavoisier</a> in around 1776. Since Lavoisier's knowledge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">strong acids</a> was mainly restricted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxoacid" title="Oxoacid">oxoacids</a>, such as <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></a></span> (nitric acid) and <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></a></span> (sulfuric acid), which tend to contain central atoms in high <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> surrounded by oxygen, and since he was not aware of the true composition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrohalic_acid" title="Hydrohalic acid">hydrohalic acids</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">HF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">HCl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bromide" title="Hydrogen bromide">HBr</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_iodide" title="Hydrogen iodide">HI</a>), he defined acids in terms of their containing <i><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a></i>, which in fact he named from Greek words meaning "acid-former" (from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ὀξύς (<i>oxys</i>) meaning "acid" or "sharp" and γεινομαι (<i>geinomai</i>) meaning "engender"). The Lavoisier definition held for over 30 years, until the 1810 article and subsequent lectures by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Sir Humphry Davy</a> in which he proved the lack of oxygen in <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_telluride" title="Hydrogen telluride">H<sub>2</sub>Te</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrohalic_acid" title="Hydrohalic acid">hydrohalic acids</a>. However, Davy failed to develop a new theory, concluding that "acidity does not depend upon any particular elementary substance, but upon peculiar arrangement of various substances".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-4">[4]</a></sup> One notable modification of oxygen theory was provided by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a>, who stated that acids are oxides of nonmetals while bases are oxides of metals.
</p>
<h4><span id="Liebig.27s_hydrogen_theory_of_acids"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Liebig's_hydrogen_theory_of_acids">Liebig's hydrogen theory of acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Liebig's hydrogen theory of acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1838, <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a> proposed that an acid is a hydrogen-containing compound whose hydrogen can be replaced by a metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liebig_1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-liebig_1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyers_156_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-meyers_156-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This redefinition was based on his extensive work on the chemical composition of <a href="/wiki/Organic_acid" title="Organic acid">organic acids</a>, finishing the doctrinal shift from oxygen-based acids to hydrogen-based acids started by Davy. Liebig's definition, while completely empirical, remained in use for almost 50 years until the adoption of the Arrhenius definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liebig_2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-liebig_2-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrhenius_definition">Arrhenius definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arrhenius definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="anchor" id="Arrhenius_theory"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arrhenius2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="479" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Arrhenius2.jpg/130px-Arrhenius2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Arrhenius2.jpg/195px-Arrhenius2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Arrhenius2.jpg/260px-Arrhenius2.jpg 2x" width="130"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arrhenius2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a></div></div></div>
<p>The first modern definition of acids and bases in molecular terms was devised by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miessler_154_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-miessler_154-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A hydrogen theory of acids, it followed from his 1884 work with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Ostwald">Friedrich Wilhelm Ostwald</a> in establishing the presence of ions in <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a> and led to Arrhenius receiving the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 1903.
</p><p>As defined by Arrhenius:
</p>
<ul><li><i>an Arrhenius acid</i> is a substance that <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociates</a> in water to form hydrogen ions (H<sup>+</sup>);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miessler_165_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-miessler_165-11">[11]</a></sup> that is, an acid increases the concentration of H<sup>+</sup> ions in an aqueous solution.</li></ul>
<p>This causes the <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a> of water, or the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> (H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>) ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[note 1]</a></sup> Thus, in modern times, the symbol H<sup>+</sup> is interpreted as a shorthand for H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>, because it is now known that a bare proton does not exist as a free species in aqueous solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acids,_Bases,_&amp;_Salts_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Acids,_Bases,_&amp;_Salts-15">[14]</a></sup> This is the species which is measured by <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">pH indicators</a> to measure the acidity or basicity of a solution. 
</p>
<ul><li><i>an Arrhenius base is a substance that dissociates in water to form hydroxide (OH<sup>−</sup>) ions; that is, a base increases the concentration of OH<sup>−</sup> ions in an aqueous solution."</i></li></ul>
<p>The Arrhenius definitions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> are restricted to aqueous solutions and are not valid for most non-aqueous solutions, and refer to the concentration of the solvent ions. Under this definition, pure H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> and HCl dissolved in toluene are not acidic, and molten NaOH and solutions of calcium amide in liquid ammonia are not alkaline. This led to the development of the Brønsted-Lowry theory and subsequent Lewis theory to account for these <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">non-aqueous</a> exceptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reaction of an acid with a base is called a <a href="/wiki/Neutralization_(chemistry)" title="Neutralization (chemistry)">neutralization</a> reaction. The products of this reaction are a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> and water.
</p>
<dl><dd>acid + base → salt + water</dd></dl>
<p>In this traditional representation an acid–base neutralization reaction is formulated as a <a href="/wiki/Salt_metathesis_reaction" title="Salt metathesis reaction">double-replacement reaction</a>. For example, the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, HCl, with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, NaOH, solutions produces a solution of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, NaCl, and some additional water molecules.
</p>
<dl><dd>HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The modifier (<a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aq</a>) in this equation was implied by Arrhenius, rather than included explicitly. It indicates that the substances are dissolved in water. Though all three substances, HCl, NaOH and NaCl are capable of existing as pure compounds, in <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solutions</a> they are fully dissociated into the aquated ions H<sup>+</sup>, Cl<sup>−</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup> and OH<sup>−</sup>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Example:_Baking_Powder">Example: Baking Powder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Example: Baking Powder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When combined with water, the sodium bicarbonate and acid salts react to produce gaseous <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. 
Whether commercially or domestically prepared, the principles behind baking powder formulations remain the same. The acid-base reaction can be generically represented as shown:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>NaHCO<sub>3</sub>  +  H<sup>+</sup>   →   Na<sup>+</sup>  +  CO<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The real reactions are more complicated because the acids are complicated. For example, starting with baking soda and monocalcium phosphate, the reaction produces carbon dioxide by the following <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KO_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-KO-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>14 NaHCO<sub>3</sub>  +  5 Ca(H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>    →   14 CO<sub>2</sub>  +  Ca<sub>5</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>OH  + 7 Na<sub>2</sub>HPO<sub>4</sub>  +  13 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="896" decoding="async" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png/220px-Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png/330px-Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png/440px-Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calcium_dihydrogen_phosphate.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Monocalcium_phosphate" title="Monocalcium phosphate">Monocalcium phosphate</a> ("MCP") is a common acid component in domestic baking powders.</div></div></div>
<p>A typical formulation (by weight) could call for 30% sodium bicarbonate, 5–12% <a href="/wiki/Monocalcium_phosphate" title="Monocalcium phosphate">monocalcium phosphate</a>, and 21–26% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminium_sulfate" title="Sodium aluminium sulfate">sodium aluminium sulfate</a>.  
Alternately, a commercial baking powder might use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_acid_pyrophosphate" title="Sodium acid pyrophosphate">sodium acid pyrophosphate</a> as one of the two acidic components instead of sodium aluminium sulfate.
Another typical acid in such formulations is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cream_of_tartar" title="Cream of tartar">cream of tartar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K</a><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C<sub>4</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H<sub>5</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>6</sub></a>), a derivative of <a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">tartaric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KO_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-KO-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Brønsted–Lowry definition, formulated in 1923, independently by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Nicolaus_Br%C3%B8nsted" title="Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted">Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted</a> in Denmark and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lowry" title="Martin Lowry">Martin Lowry</a> in England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> is based upon the idea of <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a> of bases through the <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonation</a> of acids – that is, the ability of acids to "donate" hydrogen ions (H<sup>+</sup>)—otherwise known as <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">protons</a>—to bases, which "accept" them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miessler_167_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-miessler_167-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[note 2]</a></sup>
</p><p>An acid–base reaction is, thus, the removal of a hydrogen ion from the acid and its addition to the base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayden_1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clayden_1-23">[21]</a></sup> The removal of a hydrogen ion from an acid produces its <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate base</a></i>, which is the acid with a hydrogen ion removed. The reception of a proton by a base produces its <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate acid</a></i>, which is the base with a hydrogen ion added.
</p><p>Unlike the previous definitions, the Brønsted–Lowry definition does not refer to the formation of salt and solvent, but instead to the formation of <i>conjugate acids</i> and <i>conjugate bases</i>, produced by the transfer of a proton from the acid to the base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miessler_165_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-miessler_165-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miessler_167_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-miessler_167-21">[20]</a></sup> In this approach, acids and bases are fundamentally different in behavior from salts, which are seen as electrolytes, subject to the theories of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Debye" title="Peter Debye">Debye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lars_Onsager" title="Lars Onsager">Onsager</a>, and others. An acid and a base react not to produce a salt and a solvent, but to form a new acid and a new base. The concept of neutralization is thus absent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-4">[4]</a></sup> Brønsted–Lowry acid–base behavior is formally independent of any solvent, making it more all-encompassing than the Arrhenius model. The calculation of <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> under the Arrhenius model depended on alkalis (bases) dissolving in water (<a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>). The Brønsted–Lowry model expanded what could be <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> tested using insoluble and soluble solutions (gas, liquid, solid).
</p><p>The general formula for acid–base reactions according to the Brønsted–Lowry definition is:
</p>
<dl><dd>HA + B → BH<sup>+</sup> + A<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>where HA represents the acid, B represents the base, BH<sup>+</sup> represents the conjugate acid of B, and A<sup>−</sup> represents the conjugate base of HA.
</p><p>For example, a Brønsted-Lowry model for the dissociation of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> (HCl) in <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a> would be the following:
</p>
<dl><dd>HCl + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> + Cl<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The removal of H<sup>+</sup> from the HCl produces the <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> ion, Cl<sup>−</sup>, the conjugate base of the acid. The addition of H<sup>+</sup> to the H<sub>2</sub>O (acting as a base) forms the <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion, H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>, the conjugate acid of the base.
</p><p>Water is <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoteric</a>—that is, it can act as both an acid and a base. The Brønsted-Lowry model explains this, showing the dissociation of water into low concentrations of hydronium and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> ions:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> + OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>This equation is demonstrated in the image below:
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png"><img alt="Bronsted lowry 3d diagram.png" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="2148" decoding="async" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png/600px-Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png/900px-Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png/1200px-Bronsted_lowry_3d_diagram.png 2x" width="600"/></a></div></div>
<p>Here, one molecule of water acts as an acid, donating an H<sup>+</sup> and forming the conjugate base, OH<sup>−</sup>, and a second molecule of water acts as a base, accepting the H<sup>+</sup> ion and forming the conjugate acid, H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>.
</p><p>As an example of water acting as an acid, consider an aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>, C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>N.
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>N + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ [C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NH]<sup>+</sup> + OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>In this example, a water molecule is split into a hydrogen ion, which is donated to a pyridine molecule, and a hydroxide ion.
</p><p>In the Brønsted-Lowry model, the solvent does not necessarily have to be water, as is required by the <a href="#Arrhenius_definition">Arrhenius Acid-Base model</a>. For example, consider what happens when <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, CH<sub>3</sub>COOH, dissolves in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_ammonia" title="Liquid ammonia">liquid ammonia</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>COOH</span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>COO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>An H<sup>+</sup> ion is removed from acetic acid, forming its conjugate base, the <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> ion, CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>−</sup>. The addition of an H<sup>+</sup> ion to an ammonia molecule of the solvent creates its conjugate acid, the ammonium ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>The Brønsted–Lowry model calls hydrogen-containing substances (like HCl) acids. Thus, some substances, which many chemists considered to be acids, such as SO<sub>3</sub> or BCl<sub>3</sub>, are excluded from this classification due to lack of hydrogen. <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis" title="Gilbert N. Lewis">Gilbert N. Lewis</a> wrote in 1938, "To restrict the group of acids to those substances that contain hydrogen interferes as seriously with the systematic understanding of chemistry as would the restriction of the term <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> to substances containing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-4">[4]</a></sup> Furthermore, KOH and KNH<sub>2</sub> are not considered Brønsted bases, but rather salts containing the bases OH<sup>−</sup> and <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lewis_definition">Lewis definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lewis definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Lewis_acids_and_bases" title="Lewis acids and bases">Lewis acids and bases</a></div>
<p>The hydrogen requirement of Arrhenius and Brønsted–Lowry was removed by the Lewis definition of acid–base reactions, devised by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis" title="Gilbert N. Lewis">Gilbert N. Lewis</a> in 1923,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_1_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis_1-24">[22]</a></sup> in the same year as Brønsted–Lowry, but it was not elaborated by him until 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-4">[4]</a></sup> Instead of defining acid–base reactions in terms of protons or other bonded substances, the Lewis definition defines a base (referred to as a <i>Lewis base</i>) to be a compound that can donate an <i><a href="/wiki/Electron_pair" title="Electron pair">electron pair</a></i>, and an acid (a <i>Lewis acid</i>) to be a compound that can receive this electron pair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis_2-25">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a>, BF<sub>3</sub> is a typical Lewis acid. It can accept a pair of electrons as it has a vacancy in its <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> ion has a full octet and can donate a pair of electrons. Thus
</p>
<dl><dd>BF<sub>3</sub> + F<sup>−</sup> → <span class="chemf nowrap">BF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>is a typical Lewis acid, Lewis base reaction. All compounds of <a href="/wiki/Boron_group" title="Boron group">group 13</a> elements with a formula AX<sub>3</sub> can behave as Lewis acids. Similarly, compounds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_15" title="Group 15">group 15</a> elements with a formula DY<sub>3</sub>, such as <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>, NR<sub>3</sub>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphine" title="Phosphine">phosphines</a>, PR<sub>3</sub>, can behave as Lewis bases. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adducts" title="Adducts">Adducts</a> between them have the formula X<sub>3</sub>A←DY<sub>3</sub> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative_covalent_bond" title="Dative covalent bond">dative covalent bond</a>, shown symbolically as ←, between the atoms A (acceptor) and D (donor). Compounds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_16" title="Group 16">group 16</a> with a formula DX<sub>2</sub> may also act as Lewis bases; in this way, a compound like an <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a>, R<sub>2</sub>O, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thioether" title="Thioether">thioether</a>, R<sub>2</sub>S, can act as a Lewis base. The Lewis definition is not limited to these examples. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> acts as a Lewis base when it forms an adduct with boron trifluoride, of formula F<sub>3</sub>B←CO.
</p><p>Adducts involving metal ions are referred to as co-ordination compounds; each ligand donates a pair of electrons to the metal ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-lewis_2-25">[23]</a></sup> The reaction
</p>
<dl><dd>[Ag(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup> + 2NH<sub>3</sub> → [Ag(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]<sup>+</sup> + 4H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>can be seen as an acid–base reaction in which a stronger base (ammonia) replaces a weaker one (water)
</p><p>The Lewis and Brønsted–Lowry definitions are consistent with each other since the reaction
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sup>+</sup> + OH<sup>−</sup> ⇌ H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>is an acid–base reaction in both theories.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent_system_definition">Solvent system definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Solvent system definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the limitations of the Arrhenius definition is its reliance on water solutions. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Curtis_Franklin" title="Edward Curtis Franklin">Edward Curtis Franklin</a> studied the acid–base reactions in liquid ammonia in 1905 and pointed out the similarities to the water-based Arrhenius theory. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Fredrick_Ottomar_Germann" title="Albert Fredrick Ottomar Germann">Albert F.O. Germann</a>, working with liquid <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">COCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, formulated the solvent-based theory in 1925, thereby generalizing the Arrhenius definition to cover aprotic solvents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Germann pointed out that in many solutions, there are ions in equilibrium with the neutral solvent molecules:
</p>
<ul><li>solvonium ions: a generic name for positive ions. (The term <i>solvonium</i> has replaced the older term <i><a href="/wiki/Lyonium_ion" title="Lyonium ion">lyonium ions</a></i>: positive ions formed by protonation of solvent molecules.)</li>
<li>solvate ions: a generic name for negative ions. (The term <i>solvate</i> has replaced the older term <i><a href="/wiki/Lyate_ion" title="Lyate ion">lyate ions</a></i>: negative ions formed by deprotonation of solvent molecules.)</li></ul>
<p>For example, water and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> undergo such dissociation into <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metal_amides#Alkali_metal_amides" title="Metal amides">amide</a>, respectively:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">OH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd>
<dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Some aprotic systems also undergo such dissociation, such as <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">dinitrogen tetroxide</a> into <a href="/wiki/Nitrosonium" title="Nitrosonium">nitrosonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antimony_trichloride" title="Antimony trichloride">antimony trichloride</a> into dichloroantimonium and tetrachloroantimonate, and phosgene into chlorocarboxonium and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd>

<p>A solute that causes an increase in the concentration of the solvonium ions and a decrease in the concentration of solvate ions is defined as an <i>acid</i>. A solute that causes an increase in the concentration of the solvate ions and a decrease in the concentration of the solvonium ions is defined as a <i>base</i>.
</p><p>Thus, in liquid ammonia, <span class="chemf nowrap">KNH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (supplying <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) is a strong base, and <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> (supplying <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) is a strong acid. On the other hand, solvent system theory has been criticized as being too general to be useful. Also, it has been thought that there is something intrinsically acidic about hydrogen compounds, a property not shared by non-hydrogenic solvonium salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review1940_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-review1940-4">[4]</a></sup>

<h3><span id="Lux.E2.80.93Flood_definition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lux–Flood_definition">Lux–Flood definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lux–Flood definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This acid–base theory was a revival of the oxygen theory of acids and bases proposed by German chemist <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Lux" title="Hermann Lux">Hermann Lux</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lux_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-lux-28">[26]</a></sup> in 1939, further improved by <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A5kon_Flood" title="Håkon Flood">Håkon Flood</a> circa 1947<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-flood-29">[27]</a></sup> and is still used in modern <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">molten salts</a>. This definition describes an acid as an oxide ion (<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) acceptor and a base as an oxide ion donor. For example:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drago_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-drago-30">[28]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Usanovich_definition">Usanovich definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Usanovich definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

This defined the concept of <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> (oxidation-reduction) as a special case of acid–base reactions

<h2><span id="Rationalizing_the_strength_of_Lewis_acid.E2.80.93base_interactions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationalizing_the_strength_of_Lewis_acid–base_interactions">Rationalizing the strength of Lewis acid–base interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rationalizing the strength of Lewis acid–base interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HSAB_theory">HSAB theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: HSAB theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">HSAB theory</a></div>
<p>In 1963, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Pearson" title="Ralph Pearson">Ralph Pearson</a> proposed a qualitative concept known as the <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">Hard and Soft Acids and Bases principle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearson_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-32">[30]</a></sup> later made quantitative with help of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Parr" title="Robert Parr">Robert Parr</a> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abshardess_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-abshardess-33">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> 'Hard' applies to species that are small, have high charge states, and are weakly polarizable. 'Soft' applies to species that are large, have low charge states and are strongly polarizable. Acids and bases interact, and the most stable interactions are hard–hard and soft–soft. This theory has found use in organic and inorganic chemistry.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ECW_model">ECW model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: ECW model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/ECW_model" title="ECW model">ECW model</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/ECW_model" title="ECW model">ECW model</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Russell_S._Drago" title="Russell S. Drago">Russell S. Drago</a> is a quantitative model that describes and predicts the strength of Lewis acid base interactions, −Δ<i>H</i>. The model assigned <i>E</i> and <i>C</i> parameters to many Lewis acids and bases. Each acid is characterized by an <i>E</i><sub>A</sub> and a <i>C</i><sub>A</sub>. Each base is likewise characterized by its own <i>E</i><sub>B</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>B</sub>. The <i>E</i> and <i>C</i> parameters refer, respectively, to the electrostatic and covalent contributions to the strength of the bonds that the acid and base will form. The equation is
</p>
<dl><dd>−Δ<i>H</i> = <i>E</i><sub>A</sub><i>E</i><sub>B</sub> + <i>C</i><sub>A</sub><i>C</i><sub>B</sub> + <i>W</i></dd></dl>
<p>The <i>W</i> term represents a constant energy contribution for acid–base reaction such as the cleavage of a dimeric acid or base. The equation predicts reversal of acids and base strengths. The graphical presentations of the equation show that there is no single order of Lewis base strengths or Lewis acid strengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Acid.E2.80.93base_equilibrium"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid–base_equilibrium">Acid–base equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Acid–base equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">Acid dissociation constant</a></div>
<p>The reaction of a strong acid with a strong base is essentially a quantitative reaction. For example,
</p>
<dl><dd>HCl<sub>(aq)</sub> + Na(OH)<sub>(aq)</sub> → H<sub>2</sub>O + NaCl<sub>(aq)</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In this reaction both the sodium and chloride ions are spectators as the neutralization reaction,
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sup>+</sup> + OH<sup>−</sup> → H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>does not involve them. With weak bases addition of acid is not quantitative because a solution of a weak base is a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solution</a>. A solution of a weak acid is also a buffer solution. When a weak acid reacts with a weak base an equilibrium mixture is produced. For example, <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, written as AH, can react with a hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">HPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.
</p>
<dl><dd>AH + <span class="chemf nowrap">HPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> ⇌ A<sup>−</sup> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>The equilibrium constant for this reaction can be derived from the acid dissociation constants of adenine and of the dihydrogen phosphate ion.
</p>
<dl><dd>[A<sup>−</sup>] [H<sup>+</sup>] = <i>K</i><sub>a1</sub>[AH]</dd>
<dd>[<span class="chemf nowrap">HPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>] [H<sup>+</sup>] = <i>K</i><sub>a2</sub>[<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>]</dd></dl>
<p>The notation [X] signifies "concentration of X". When these two equations are combined by eliminating the hydrogen ion concentration, an expression for the equilibrium constant, <i>K</i> is obtained.
</p>

<h2><span id="Acid.E2.80.93alkali_reaction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid–alkali_reaction">Acid–alkali reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Acid–alkali reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neutralization_(chemistry)" title="Neutralization (chemistry)">Neutralization (chemistry)</a></div>
<p>An acid–alkali reaction is a special case of an acid–base reaction, where the base used is also an <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a>. When an acid reacts with an alkali salt (a metal hydroxide), the product is a metal <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> and water. Acid–alkali reactions are also <a href="/wiki/Neutralization_(chemistry)" title="Neutralization (chemistry)">neutralization</a> reactions.
</p><p>In general, acid–alkali reactions can be simplified to
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide"><span class="chemf nowrap">OH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub></a> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub> → <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)"><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></a></dd></dl>
<p>by omitting <a href="/wiki/Spectator_ion" title="Spectator ion">spectator ions</a>.
</p><p>Acids are in general pure substances that contain <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">hydrogen cations</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) or cause them to be produced in solutions. Hydrochloric acid (<span class="chemf nowrap">HCl</span>) and sulfuric acid (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) are common examples. In water, these break apart into ions:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">HCl</span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub> + <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub></dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub> + <span class="chemf nowrap">HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The alkali breaks apart in water, yielding dissolved hydroxide ions:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">NaOH</span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub> + <span class="chemf nowrap">OH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span><sub>(aq)</sub></dd></dl>
