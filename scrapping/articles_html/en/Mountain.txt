<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Mountain</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Large and tall landform</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mountain_(disambiguation)" title="Mountain (disambiguation)">Mountain (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg/330px-Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg/495px-Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg/660px-Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>, Earth's highest mountain</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>mountain</b> is an elevated portion of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>, generally with steep sides that show significant exposed <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a>. Although definitions vary, a mountain may differ from a <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> in having a limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summit_(topography)" title="Summit (topography)">summit</a> area, and is usually higher than a <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hill</a>, typically rising at least 300 metres (1,000 feet) above the surrounding land. A few mountains are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monadnock" title="Monadnock">isolated summits</a>, but most occur in <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain ranges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agi-1997_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-agi-1997-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mountain_formation" title="Mountain formation">Mountains are formed</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic forces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agi-1997_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-agi-1997-1">[1]</a></sup> which act on time scales of up to tens of millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levin-2010-83_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-levin-2010-83-2">[2]</a></sup> Once mountain building ceases, mountains are slowly leveled through the action of <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Slump_(geology)" title="Slump (geology)">slumping</a> and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Mass_wasting" title="Mass wasting">mass wasting</a>, as well as through <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> by <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>High elevations on mountains produce <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">colder climates</a> than at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> at similar latitude. These colder climates strongly affect the <a href="/wiki/Montane_ecosystems" title="Montane ecosystems">ecosystems</a> of mountains: different elevations have different plants and animals. Because of the less hospitable terrain and climate, mountains tend to be used less for agriculture and more for resource extraction, such as <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>, along with recreation, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_climbing" title="Mountain climbing">mountain climbing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>.
</p><p>The highest mountain on Earth is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, whose summit is 8,850 m (29,035 ft) above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a>. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_mountains_in_the_Solar_System" title="List of tallest mountains in the Solar System">highest known mountain</a> on any planet in the Solar System is <a href="/wiki/Olympus_Mons" title="Olympus Mons">Olympus Mons</a> on Mars at 21,171 m (69,459 ft).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg/220px-Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg/330px-Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg/440px-Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Elbrus_May_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Elbrus" title="Mount Elbrus">Mount Elbrus</a>, the highest mountain in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Puncakjaya.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Puncakjaya.jpg/220px-Puncakjaya.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Puncakjaya.jpg/330px-Puncakjaya.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Puncakjaya.jpg/440px-Puncakjaya.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Puncakjaya.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Puncak_Jaya" title="Puncak Jaya">Puncak Jaya</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, the highest mountain in <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a></div></div></div>
<p>There is no universally accepted definition of a mountain. Elevation, volume, relief, steepness, spacing and continuity have been used as criteria for defining a mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerrard1990_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerrard1990-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> a mountain is defined as "a natural elevation of the earth surface rising more or less abruptly from the surrounding level and attaining an altitude which, relatively to the adjacent elevation, is impressive or notable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerrard1990_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gerrard1990-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Whether a landform is called a mountain may depend on local usage. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Scott_(Oklahoma)" title="Mount Scott (Oklahoma)">Mount Scott</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Lawton,_Oklahoma" title="Lawton, Oklahoma">Lawton, Oklahoma</a>, USA, is only 251 m (823 ft) from its base to its highest point. Whittow's <i>Dictionary of Physical Geography</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whittow_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-whittow-5">[5]</a></sup> states "Some authorities regard eminences above 600 metres (1,969 ft) as mountains, those below being referred to as hills."
</p><p>In the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland, a mountain is usually defined as any summit at least 2,000 feet (610 m) high,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which accords with the official UK government's definition that a mountain, for the purposes of access, is a summit of 2,000 feet (610 m) or higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In addition, some definitions also include a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographical_prominence" title="Topographical prominence">topographical prominence</a> requirement, such as that the mountain rises 300 metres (984 ft) above the surrounding terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agi-1997_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-agi-1997-1">[1]</a></sup> At one time the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Board_on_Geographic_Names" title="U.S. Board on Geographic Names">U.S. Board on Geographic Names</a> defined a mountain as being 1,000 feet (305 m) or taller,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but has abandoned the definition since the 1970s. Any similar landform lower than this height was considered a hill. However, today, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> (USGS) concludes that these terms do not have technical definitions in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Environmental_Programme" title="UN Environmental Programme">UN Environmental Programme</a>'s definition of "mountainous environment" includes any of the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 74">: 74 </span></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Class 1: Elevation greater than 4,500 m (14,764 ft).</li>
<li>Class 2: Elevation between 3,500 m (11,483 ft) and 4,500 m (14,764 ft).</li>
<li>Class 3: Elevation between 2,500 m (8,202 ft) and 3,500 m (11,483 ft).</li>
<li>Class 4: Elevation between 1,500 m (4,921 ft) and 2,500 m (8,202 ft), with a slope greater than 2 degrees.</li>
<li>Class 5: Elevation between 1,000 m (3,281 ft) and 1,500 m (4,921 ft), with a slope greater than 5 degrees and/or 300 m (984 ft) elevation range within 7 km (4.3 mi).</li>
<li>Class 6: Elevation between 300 m (984 ft) and 1,000 m (3,281 ft), with a 300 m (984 ft) elevation range within 7 km (4.3 mi).</li>
<li>Class 7: Isolated inner basins and plateaus less than 25 km<sup>2</sup> (9.7 sq mi) in area that are completely surrounded by Class 1 to 6 mountains, but do not themselves meet criteria for Class 1 to 6 mountains.</li></ul>
<p>Using these definitions, mountains cover 33% of Eurasia, 19% of South America, 24% of North America, and 14% of Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 14">: 14 </span></sup> As a whole, 24% of the Earth's land mass is mountainous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panos_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-panos-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mountain_formation" title="Mountain formation">Mountain formation</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountain_types" title="List of mountain types">List of mountain types</a></div>
<p>There are three main types of mountains: <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fold_mountain" title="Fold mountain">fold</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fault-block_mountain" title="Fault-block mountain">block</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-literacy_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-literacy-12">[12]</a></sup> All three types are formed from <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>: when portions of the Earth's crust move, crumple, and dive. Compressional forces, <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">uplift</a> and intrusion of <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous matter</a> forces surface rock upward, creating a landform higher than the surrounding features. The height of the feature makes it either a hill or, if higher and steeper, a mountain. Major mountains tend to occur in long linear arcs, indicating tectonic plate boundaries and activity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">Volcano</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg/220px-080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg/330px-080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg/440px-080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:080103_hakkai_fuji.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji" title="Mount Fuji">Fuji volcano</a></div></div></div>
<p>Volcanoes are formed when <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">a plate is pushed below another plate</a>, or at a <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butz_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butz-13">[13]</a></sup> At a depth of around 100 km, melting occurs in rock above the slab (due to the addition of water), and forms <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> that reaches the surface. When the magma reaches the surface, it often builds a volcanic mountain, such as a <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">shield volcano</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerrard1990_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gerrard1990-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 194">: 194 </span></sup> Examples of volcanoes include <a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji" title="Mount Fuji">Mount Fuji</a> in Japan and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> in the Philippines. The magma does not have to reach the surface in order to create a mountain: magma that solidifies below ground can still form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dome_mountain" title="Dome mountain">dome mountains</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Mountain" title="Navajo Mountain">Navajo Mountain</a> in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fold_mountains">Fold mountains</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fold_mountains" title="Fold mountains">Fold mountains</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="870" data-file-width="1400" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg/220px-Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg/330px-Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg/440px-Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lewis_overthrust_fault_nh10f.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of mountains that developed on a <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">fold</a> that has been <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrust</a></div></div></div>
<p>Fold mountains occur when two plates collide: shortening occurs along thrust faults and the crust is overthickened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatcher_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatcher-15">[15]</a></sup> Since the less dense <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> "floats" on the denser <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> rocks beneath, the weight of any crustal material forced upward to form hills, <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateaus</a> or mountains must be <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">balanced by the buoyancy force</a> of a much greater volume forced downward into the mantle. Thus the continental crust is normally much thicker under mountains, compared to lower lying areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Rock can <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">fold</a> either symmetrically or asymmetrically. The upfolds are <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticlines</a> and the downfolds are <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">synclines</a>: in asymmetric folding there may also be recumbent and overturned folds. The <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Mountains" title="Balkan Mountains">Balkan Mountains</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Jura_Mountains" title="Jura Mountains">Jura Mountains</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> are examples of fold mountains.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Block_mountains">Block mountains</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_mountains" title="Block mountains">Block mountains</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg"><img alt="the highest summit of Pirin" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg/220px-Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg/330px-Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg/440px-Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vihren_Pirin_IMG_8898.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pirin" title="Pirin">Pirin Mountain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, part of the fault-block <a href="/wiki/Rila" title="Rila">Rila</a>-<a href="/wiki/Rhodope_Mountains" title="Rhodope Mountains">Rhodope</a> <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">massif</a></div></div></div>
<p>Block mountains are caused by <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faults</a> in the crust: a plane where rocks have moved past each other. When rocks on one side of a fault rise relative to the other, it can form a mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan-19">[19]</a></sup> The uplifted blocks are block mountains or <a href="/wiki/Horst_(geology)" title="Horst (geology)">horsts</a>. The intervening dropped blocks are termed <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">graben</a>: these can be small or form extensive <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> systems. This form of <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a> can be seen in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vosges_Mountains" title="Vosges Mountains">Vosges</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhine_graben" title="Rhine graben">Rhine</a> valley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Basin_and_Range_Province" title="Basin and Range Province">Basin and Range Province</a> of Western North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevin2010474–478_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevin2010474–478-22">[22]</a></sup> These areas often occur when the regional stress is extensional and the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> is thinned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevin2010474–478_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevin2010474–478-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erosion">Erosion</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">Erosion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="311" data-file-width="478" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg/220px-Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg/330px-Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg/440px-Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Slide_Mountain_Catskills.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catskills" title="Catskills">Catskills</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upstate_New_York" title="Upstate New York">Upstate New York</a> represent an eroded <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>During and following uplift, mountains are subjected to the agents of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> (water, wind, ice, and gravity) which gradually wear the uplifted area down. Erosion causes the surface of mountains to be younger than the rocks that form the mountains themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraknoi2004_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fraknoi2004-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 160">: 160 </span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">Glacial processes</a> produce characteristic landforms, such as <a href="/wiki/Pyramidal_peak" title="Pyramidal peak">pyramidal peaks</a>, knife-edge <a href="/wiki/Ar%C3%AAte" title="Arête">arêtes</a>, and bowl-shaped <a href="/wiki/Cirque" title="Cirque">cirques</a> that can contain lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thornbury-1969_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-thornbury-1969-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">Plateau</a> mountains, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catskills" title="Catskills">Catskills</a>, are formed from the erosion of an uplifted plateau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">Alpine climate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="726" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg/220px-Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg/330px-Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg/440px-Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ural_mountains_3_448122223_93fa978a6d_b.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">northern Urals</a> at high latitude and elevation have an alpine climate and barren ground.</div></div></div>
<p>Climate in the mountains becomes colder at high <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevations</a>, due to an interaction between <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. Sunlight in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> hits the ground and heats it. The ground then heats the air at the surface. If <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> were the only way to transfer heat from the ground to space, the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> of gases in the atmosphere would keep the ground at roughly 333 K (60 °C; 140 °F), and the temperature would decay exponentially with height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodywilson_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-goodywilson-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, when air is hot, it tends to expand, which lowers its density. Thus, hot air tends to rise and transfer heat upward. This is the process of <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. Convection comes to equilibrium when a parcel of air at a given altitude has the same density as its surroundings. Air is a poor conductor of heat, so a parcel of air will rise and fall without exchanging heat. This is known as an <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a>, which has a characteristic pressure-temperature dependence. As the pressure gets lower, the temperature decreases. The rate of decrease of temperature with elevation is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">adiabatic lapse rate</a>, which is approximately 9.8 °C per kilometre (or 5.4 °F (3.0 °C) per 1000 feet) of altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodywilson_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-goodywilson-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The presence of water in the atmosphere complicates the process of convection. Water vapor contains latent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a>. As air rises and cools, it eventually becomes <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">saturated</a> and cannot hold its quantity of water vapor. The water vapor condenses (forming <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>), and releases heat, which changes the lapse rate from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Dry adiabatic lapse rate">dry adiabatic lapse rate</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moist_adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Moist adiabatic lapse rate">moist adiabatic lapse rate</a> (5.5 °C per kilometre or 3 °F (1.7 °C) per 1000 feet)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
The actual lapse rate can vary by altitude and by location.
Therefore, moving up 100 metres on a mountain is roughly equivalent to moving 80 kilometres (45 miles or 0.75° of <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>) towards the nearest pole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 15">: 15 </span></sup> This relationship is only approximate, however, since local factors such as proximity to oceans (such as the Arctic Ocean) can drastically modify the climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As the altitude increases, the main form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> becomes <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> and the winds increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 12">: 12 </span></sup>
</p><p>The effect of the climate on the ecology at an elevation can be largely captured through a combination of amount of precipitation, and the <a href="/wiki/Holdridge_life_zones" title="Holdridge life zones">biotemperature</a>, as described by <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Holdridge" title="Leslie Holdridge">Leslie Holdridge</a> in 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Biotemperature is the mean temperature; all temperatures below 0 °C (32 °F) are considered to be 0 °C. When the temperature is below 0 °C, plants are <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormant</a>, so the exact temperature is unimportant. The peaks of mountains with permanent snow can have a biotemperature below 1.5 °C (34.7 °F).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_ecology" title="Montane ecology">Montane ecology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GlarusAlps.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GlarusAlps.jpg/220px-GlarusAlps.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GlarusAlps.jpg/330px-GlarusAlps.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GlarusAlps.jpg/440px-GlarusAlps.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GlarusAlps.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An alpine <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">mire</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a></div></div></div>
<p>The colder climate on mountains affects the plants and animals residing on mountains. A particular set of plants and animals tend to be adapted to a relatively narrow range of climate. Thus, ecosystems tend to lie along elevation bands of roughly constant climate. This is called <a href="/wiki/Altitudinal_zonation" title="Altitudinal zonation">altitudinal zonation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
In regions with dry climates, the tendency of mountains to have higher precipitation as well as lower temperatures also provides for varying conditions, which enhances zonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merriam_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some plants and animals found in altitudinal zones tend to become isolated since the conditions above and below a particular zone will be inhospitable and thus constrain their movements or <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal</a>. These isolated ecological systems are known as <a href="/wiki/Sky_island" title="Sky island">sky islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Altitudinal zones tend to follow a typical pattern. At the highest elevations, trees cannot grow, and whatever life may be present will be of the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> type, resembling <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merriam_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-31">[31]</a></sup> Just below the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a>, one may find <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subalpine" title="Subalpine">subalpine</a> forests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">needleleaf</a> trees, which can withstand cold, dry conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Below that, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_forest" title="Montane forest">montane forests</a> grow. In the temperate portions of the earth, those forests tend to be needleleaf trees, while in the tropics, they can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadleaf_tree" title="Broadleaf tree">broadleaf trees</a> growing in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountains_and_humans">Mountains and humans</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_highest_cities_in_the_world" title="List of highest cities in the world">List of highest cities in the world</a></div>
<p>The highest known permanently tolerable altitude is at 5,950 metres (19,520 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highestHabitation_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-highestHabitation-34">[34]</a></sup> At very high altitudes, the decreasing atmospheric pressure means that less oxygen is available for breathing, and there is less protection against solar radiation (<a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup> Above 8,000 metres (26,000 ft) elevation, there is not enough oxygen to support human life. This is sometimes referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Death_zone" title="Death zone">death zone</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-35">[35]</a></sup> The summits of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> and <a href="/wiki/K2" title="K2">K2</a> are in the death zone.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_societies_and_economies">Mountain societies and economies</span></h3>
<p>Mountains are generally less preferable for <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> habitation than lowlands, because of harsh weather and little level ground suitable for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. While 7% of the land area of Earth is above 2,500 metres (8,200 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 14">: 14 </span></sup> only 140 million people live above that altitude<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and only 20-30 million people above 3,000 metres (9,800 ft) elevation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> About half of mountain dwellers live in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, Central Asia, and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panos_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-panos-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Paz_Skyline.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/La_Paz_Skyline.jpg/220px-La_Paz_Skyline.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/La_Paz_Skyline.jpg/330px-La_Paz_Skyline.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/La_Paz_Skyline.jpg/440px-La_Paz_Skyline.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Paz_Skyline.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The city of La Paz reaches up to 4,000 metres (13,000 ft) in elevation.</div></div></div>
<p>With limited access to infrastructure, only a handful of human communities exist above 4,000 metres (13,000 ft) of elevation. Many are small and have heavily specialized economies, often relying on industries such as agriculture, mining, and tourism. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>An example of such a specialized town is <a href="/wiki/La_Rinconada,_Peru" title="La Rinconada, Peru">La Rinconada, Peru</a>, a gold-mining town and the highest elevation human habitation at 5,100 metres (16,700 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A counterexample is <a href="/wiki/El_Alto" title="El Alto">El Alto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, at 4,150 metres (13,620 ft), which has a highly diverse service and manufacturing economy and a population of nearly 1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditional mountain societies rely on agriculture, with higher risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_failure" title="Crop failure">crop failure</a> than at lower elevations. Minerals often occur in mountains, with <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> being an important component of the economics of some montane societies. More recently, <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> supports mountain communities, with some intensive development around attractions such as <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ski_resorts" title="Ski resorts">ski resorts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 17">: 17 </span></sup> About 80% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_people" title="Mountain people">mountain people</a> live below the poverty line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panos_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-panos-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the world's <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> are fed from mountain sources, with snow acting as a storage mechanism for downstream users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth2002_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 22">: 22 </span></sup> More than half of humanity depends on mountains for water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">geopolitics</a> mountains are often seen as preferable "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_boundaries" title="Natural boundaries">natural boundaries</a>" between polities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountaineering">Mountaineering</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">Mountaineering</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1437" decoding="async" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg/170px-Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg/255px-Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg/340px-Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ortler_Ascent_-_South_Tyrol.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mountaineers climbing in <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol" title="South Tyrol">South Tyrol</a></div></div></div>
<p>Mountain climbing, or alpinism is the <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobby</a> or <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">profession</a> of hiking, skiing, and climbing mountains. While mountaineering began as attempts to reach the highest point of unclimbed big mountains it has branched into specializations that address different aspects of the mountain and consists of three areas: rock-craft, snow-craft and skiing, depending on whether the route chosen is over <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>. All require experience, athletic ability, and technical knowledge of the terrain to maintain safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedom_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-freedom-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountains_as_sacred_places">Mountains as sacred places</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sacred_mountains" title="Sacred mountains">Sacred mountains</a></div>
<p>Mountains often play a significant role in <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>. There are for example a number of sacred mountains within <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Olympus" title="Mount Olympus">Mount Olympus</a> which was held to be the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Olympians" title="Twelve Olympians">home of the gods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In Japanese culture, the 3,776.24 m (12,389 ft) volcano of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji" title="Mount Fuji">Mount Fuji</a> is also held to be sacred with tens of thousands of Japanese ascending it each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kailash" title="Mount Kailash">Mount Kailash</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="Tibet Autonomous Region">Tibet Autonomous Region</a> of China, is considered to be sacred in four religions: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bon" title="Bon">Bon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimages</a> are made up the 952 metres (3,123 ft) <a href="/wiki/Mount_Brandon" title="Mount Brandon">Mount Brandon</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">Irish Catholics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> peak of <a href="/wiki/Nanda_Devi" title="Nanda Devi">Nanda Devi</a> is associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> goddesses Nanda and Sunanda;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> it has been off-limits to climbers since 1983. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ararat" title="Mount Ararat">Mount Ararat</a> is a sacred mountain, as it is believed to be the landing place of <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">Noah's Ark</a>. In Europe and especially in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Summit_cross" title="Summit cross">summit crosses</a> are often erected on the tops of prominent mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eppacher_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eppacher-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Superlatives">Superlatives</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains" title="List of highest mountains">List of highest mountains</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo,_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo%2C_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg/220px-Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo%2C_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo%2C_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg/330px-Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo%2C_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo%2C_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg/440px-Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo%2C_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volc%C3%A1n_Chimborazo,_%22El_Taita_Chimborazo%22.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chimborazo_(volcano)" title="Chimborazo (volcano)">Chimborazo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. The point on Earth's surface farthest from its centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_'Highest'_Spot_on_Earth_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_'Highest'_Spot_on_Earth-51">[51]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Heights of mountains are typically measured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" title="Above sea level">above sea level</a>. Using this metric, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> is the highest mountain on Earth, at 8,848 metres (29,029 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> There are at least 100 mountains with heights of over 7,200 metres (23,622 ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" title="Above sea level">above sea level</a>, all of which are located in central and southern Asia. The highest mountains above sea level are generally not the highest above the surrounding terrain. There is no precise definition of surrounding base, but <a href="/wiki/Denali" title="Denali">Denali</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helman_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-helman-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanga_Parbat" title="Nanga Parbat">Nanga Parbat</a> are possible candidates for the tallest mountain on land by this measure. The bases of mountain islands are below sea level, and given this consideration <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">Mauna Kea</a> (4,207 m (13,802 ft) above sea level) is the world's tallest mountain and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>, rising about 10,203 m (33,474 ft) from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The highest mountains are not generally the most voluminous. <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a> (4,169 m or 13,678 ft) is the largest mountain on Earth in terms of base area (about 2,000 sq mi or 5,200 km<sup>2</sup>) and volume (about 18,000 cu mi or 75,000 km<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a> is the largest non-shield volcano in terms of both base area (245 sq mi or 635 km<sup>2</sup>) and volume (1,150 cu mi or 4,793 km<sup>3</sup>).  <a href="/wiki/Mount_Logan" title="Mount Logan">Mount Logan</a> is the largest non-volcanic mountain in base area (120 sq mi or 311 km<sup>2</sup>).
</p><p>The highest mountains above sea level are also not those with peaks farthest from the centre of the Earth, because the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">figure of the Earth</a> is not spherical. Sea level closer to the equator is several miles farther from the centre of the Earth. The summit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chimborazo_(volcano)" title="Chimborazo (volcano)">Chimborazo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>'s tallest mountain, is usually considered to be the farthest point from the Earth's centre, although the southern summit of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>'s tallest mountain, <a href="/wiki/Huascar%C3%A1n" title="Huascarán">Huascarán</a>, is another contender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Both have elevations above sea level more than 2 kilometres (6,600 ft) less than that of Everest.
</p>
