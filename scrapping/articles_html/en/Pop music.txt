<p><b>Pop music</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genre_(music)" title="Genre (music)">genre</a> of <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a> that originated in its modern form during the mid-1950s in the United States and the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup> The terms <i>popular music</i> and <i>pop music</i> are often used interchangeably, although the former describes all music that is popular and includes many disparate styles. During the 1950s and 1960s, pop music encompassed <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> and the youth-oriented styles it influenced. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_Music" title="Rock Music">Rock</a></i> and <i>pop</i> music remained roughly synonymous until the late 1960s, after which <i>pop</i> became associated with music that was more commercial, ephemeral, and accessible.
</p><p>Although much of the music that appears on <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">record charts</a> is considered to be pop music, the genre is distinguished from chart music. Identifying factors usually include repeated choruses and <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a>, short to medium-length songs written in a basic format (often the <a href="/wiki/Verse%E2%80%93chorus_form" title="Verse–chorus form">verse-chorus structure</a>), and rhythms or tempos that can be easily danced to. Much pop music also borrows elements from other styles such as rock, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_contemporary" title="Urban contemporary">R&amp;B/Hip-Hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_music" title="Latin music">Latin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_and_etymology">Definitions and etymology<span class="anchor" id="Etymology"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Hatch" title="David Hatch">David Hatch</a> and Stephen Millward describe pop music as "a body of music which is distinguishable from popular, jazz, and folk music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
According to <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>, pop music is "professional music which draws upon both folk music and fine arts music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pc1-3">[3]</a></sup>
David Boyle, a music researcher, states pop music as any type of music that a person has been exposed to by the mass media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Most individuals think that pop music is just the <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">singles charts</a> and not the sum of all chart music. The music charts contain songs from a variety of sources, including <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Novelty_song" title="Novelty song">novelty songs</a>. As a genre, pop music is seen to exist and develop separately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Therefore, the term "pop music" may be used to describe a distinct genre, designed to appeal to all, often characterized as "instant singles-based music aimed at teenagers" in contrast to rock music as "album-based music for adults".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pop music continuously evolves along with the term's definition. According to music writer Bill Lamb, popular music is defined as "the music since industrialization in the 1800s that is most in line with the tastes and interests of the urban middle class."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What_Is_Pop_Music_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-What_Is_Pop_Music-13">[13]</a></sup> The term "pop song" was first used in 1926, in the sense of a piece of music "having popular appeal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED-14">[14]</a></sup> Hatch and Millward indicate that many events in the history of recording in the 1920s can be seen as the birth of the modern pop music industry, including in <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hillbilly_music" title="Hillbilly music">hillbilly music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="683" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg/220px-Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg/330px-Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg/440px-Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Oxford Dictionary of Music</i> states that the term "pop" refers to music performed by such artists as  <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a> (pictured here in a 2006 performance).</div></div></div>
<p>According to the website of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Grove_Dictionary_of_Music_and_Musicians" title="The New Grove Dictionary of Music and Musicians">The New Grove Dictionary of Music and Musicians</a></i>, the term "pop music" "originated in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> in the mid-1950s as a description for <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> and the new youth music styles that it influenced", this is however unsubstantiated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>The Oxford Dictionary of Music</i> states that while pop's "earlier meaning meant concerts appealing to a wide audience [...] since the late 1950s, however, pop has had the special meaning of non-classical mus[ic], usually in the form of songs, performed by such artists as <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a>, etc."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>Grove Music Online</i> also states that "[...] in the early 1960s, [the term] 'pop music' competed terminologically with <a href="/wiki/Beat_music" title="Beat music">beat music</a> [in England], while in the US its coverage overlapped (as it still does) with that of 'rock and roll'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>
From about 1967, the term "pop music" was increasingly used in opposition to the term <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a>, a division that gave generic significance to both terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gloag983_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gloag983-17">[17]</a></sup> While rock aspired to <a href="/wiki/Authenticity_(philosophy)" title="Authenticity (philosophy)">authenticity</a> and an expansion of the possibilities of popular music,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gloag983_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gloag983-17">[17]</a></sup> pop was more commercial, ephemeral, and accessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner2003_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner2003-18">[18]</a></sup> According to British musicologist <a href="/wiki/Simon_Frith" title="Simon Frith">Simon Frith</a>, pop music is produced "as a matter of <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">enterprise</a> not art", and is "designed to appeal to everyone" but "doesn't come from any particular place or mark off any particular taste". Frith adds that it is "not driven by any significant ambition except profit and commercial reward [...] and, in musical terms, it is essentially conservative". It is, "provided from on high (by record companies, radio programmers, and concert promoters) rather than being made from below ... Pop is not a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DIY" title="DIY">do-it-yourself</a> music but is professionally produced and packaged".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup> </p><div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>According to Frith, characteristics of pop music include an aim of appealing to a general audience, rather than to a particular sub-culture or ideology, and an emphasis on craftsmanship rather than formal "artistic" qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup> Besides, Frith also offers three identifying characteristics of pop music: light entertainment, commercial imperatives, and personal identification. Pop music grew out of a light entertainment/ easy listening tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Pop music is more conservative than other music genres such as folk, blues, country, and tradition. Many pop songs do not contain themes of resistance, opposition, or political themes, rather focusing more on love and relationships.  Therefore, pop music does not challenge its audiences socially, and does not cause political activism. Frith also said the main purpose of pop music is to create revenue. It is not a medium of free articulation of the people. Instead, pop music seeks to supply the nature of personal desire and achieve the instant empathy with cliche personalities, stereotypes, and melodrama that appeals to listeners. It is mostly about how much revenue pop music makes for record companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Music scholar Timothy Warner said pop music typically has an emphasis on recording, production, and technology, rather than live performance; a tendency to reflect existing trends rather than <a href="/wiki/Progressive_music" title="Progressive music">progressive developments</a>; and seeks to encourage dancing or uses dance-oriented rhythms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner2003_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Warner2003-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main medium of pop music is the song, often between two and a half and three and a half minutes in length, generally marked by a consistent and noticeable <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic element</a>, a mainstream style and a simple traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_structure_(popular_music)" title="Song structure (popular music)">structure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The structure of many popular songs is that of a verse and a chorus, the chorus serving as the portion of the track that is designed to stick in the ear through simple repetition both musically and lyrically. The chorus is often where the music builds towards and is often preceded by "the drop" where the bass and drum parts "drop out".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Common variants include the verse-chorus form and the <a href="/wiki/Thirty-two-bar_form" title="Thirty-two-bar form">thirty-two-bar form</a>, with a focus on <a href="/wiki/Melody" title="Melody">melodies</a> and catchy <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">chorus</a> that contrasts melodically, rhythmically and <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmonically</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verse_(music)" title="Verse (music)">verse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The beat and the melodies tend to be simple, with limited harmonic accompaniment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The lyrics of modern pop songs typically focus on simple themes – often love and romantic relationships – although there are notable exceptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmony_(music)" title="Harmony (music)">Harmony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chord_progression" title="Chord progression">chord progressions</a> in pop music are often "that of <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a> European <a href="/wiki/Tonality" title="Tonality">tonality</a>, only more simple-minded."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Clichés include the <a href="/wiki/Close_and_open_harmony" title="Close and open harmony">barbershop quartet-style harmony</a> (i.e. ii – V – I) and <a href="/wiki/Blues_scale" title="Blues scale">blues scale</a>-influenced harmony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> There was a lessening of the influence of traditional views of the <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_fifths" title="Circle of fifths">circle of fifths</a> between the mid-1950s and the late 1970s, including less predominance for the <a href="/wiki/Dominant_(music)" title="Dominant (music)">dominant</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatonic_function" title="Diatonic function">function</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_influence">Development and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_and_media">Technology and media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology and media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1685" data-file-width="1350" decoding="async" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg/170px-Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg/255px-Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg/340px-Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bing_Crosby_1930s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a> was one of the first artists to be nicknamed "King of Pop" or "King of Popular Music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (September 2020)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div></div></div>
<p>In the 1940s, improved <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> design allowed a more intimate singing style and, ten or twenty years later, inexpensive and more durable 45 rpm records for singles "revolutionized the manner in which pop has been disseminated", which helped to move pop music to "a record/radio/film star system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buckley_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley-31">[31]</a></sup> Another technological change was the widespread availability of television in the 1950s with televised performances, forcing "pop stars had to have a visual presence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buckley_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley-31">[31]</a></sup> In the 1960s, the introduction of inexpensive, portable <a href="/wiki/Transistor_radio" title="Transistor radio">transistor radios</a> meant that teenagers in the developed world could listen to music outside of the home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buckley_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley-31">[31]</a></sup> By the early 1980s, the promotion of pop music had been greatly affected by the rise of music television channels like <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>, which "favoured those artists such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madonna" title="Madonna">Madonna</a> who had a strong visual appeal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buckley_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-track_recording" title="Multi-track recording">Multi-track recording</a> (from the 1960s) and <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">digital sampling</a> (from the 1980s) have also been utilized as methods for the creation and elaboration of pop music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup> During the mid-1960s, pop music made repeated forays into new sounds, styles, and techniques that inspired public discourse among its listeners. The word "progressive" was frequently used, and it was thought that every song and single was to be a "progression" from the last.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">Music critic</a> <a href="/wiki/Simon_Reynolds" title="Simon Reynolds">Simon Reynolds</a> writes that beginning with 1967, a divide would exist between "progressive" pop and "mass/chart" pop, a separation which was "also, broadly, one between boys and girls, middle-class and working-class."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The latter half of the 20th-century included a large-scale trend in American culture in which the boundaries between art and pop music were increasingly blurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Between 1950 and 1970, there was a debate of pop versus art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMLoss2015_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMLoss2015-35">[35]</a></sup> Since then, certain music publications have embraced the music's legitimacy, a trend referred to as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poptimism" title="Poptimism">poptimism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMLoss2015_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-PMLoss2015-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stylistic_evolution">Stylistic evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stylistic evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Beatles_in_America.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2526" data-file-width="3578" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/The_Beatles_in_America.JPG/220px-The_Beatles_in_America.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/The_Beatles_in_America.JPG/330px-The_Beatles_in_America.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/The_Beatles_in_America.JPG/440px-The_Beatles_in_America.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Beatles_in_America.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 1960s <a href="/wiki/British_Invasion" title="British Invasion">British Invasion</a> marked a period when the US charts were inundated with British acts such as <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a> (pictured 1964).</div></div></div>
<p>Throughout its development, pop music has absorbed influences from other genres of popular music. Early pop music drew on the <a href="/wiki/Sentimental_ballad" title="Sentimental ballad">sentimental ballad</a> for its form, gained its use of vocal harmonies from <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul music</a>, instrumentation from <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a>, orchestration from <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, tempo from <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a>, backing from <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a>, rhythmic elements from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hip-hop_music" title="Hip-hop music">hip-hop music</a>, and spoken passages from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rap_music" title="Rap music">rap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (February 2017)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2016, a <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_Reports" title="Scientific Reports">Scientific Reports</a></i> study that examined over 464,000 recordings of popular music recorded between 1955 and 2010 found that, compared to 1960s pop music, contemporary pop music uses a smaller variety of pitch progressions, greater average volume,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> less diverse instrumentation and recording techniques, and less timbral variety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matson_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matson-37">[37]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s John Matson reported that this "seems to support the popular anecdotal observation that pop music of yore was "better", or at least more varied, than today's top-40 stuff". However, he also noted that the study may not have been entirely representative of pop in each generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matson_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matson-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s,  the majority of mainstream pop music fell in two categories: guitar, drum and bass groups or singers backed by a traditional orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Since early in the decade, it was common for pop producers, songwriters, and engineers to freely experiment with musical form, <a href="/wiki/Orchestration" title="Orchestration">orchestration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reverb_effect" title="Reverb effect">unnatural reverb</a>, and other sound effects. Some of the best known examples are <a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector" title="Phil Spector">Phil Spector</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Wall_of_Sound" title="Wall of Sound">Wall of Sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Meek" title="Joe Meek">Joe Meek</a>'s use of homemade electronic sound effects for acts like <a href="/wiki/The_Tornados" title="The Tornados">the Tornados</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> At the same time, pop music on radio and in both American and British film moved away from refined <a href="/wiki/Tin_Pan_Alley" title="Tin Pan Alley">Tin Pan Alley</a> to more eccentric songwriting and incorporated reverb-drenched rock guitar, symphonic strings, and horns played by groups of properly arranged and rehearsed studio musicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  A 2019 study held by <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> in which 643 participants had to rank how familiar a pop song is to them, songs from the 1960s turned out to be the most memorable, significantly more than songs from recent years 2000 to 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_pop" title="Progressive pop">progressive pop</a> of the late 1960s, performers were typically unable to decide on the artistic content of their music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Assisted by the mid-1960s economic boom, record labels began investing in artists, giving them the freedom to experiment, and offering them limited control over their content and marketing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202-43">[43]</a></sup> This situation declined after the late 1970s and would not reemerge until the rise of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">Indie pop</a>, which developed in the late 1970s, marked another departure from the glamour of contemporary pop music, with guitar bands formed on the then-novel premise that one could record and release their own music without having to procure a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Record_contract" title="Record contract">record contract</a> from a major label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1980s are commonly remembered for an increase in the use of <a href="/wiki/Digital_recording" title="Digital recording">digital recording</a>, associated with the usage of <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizers</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Synth-pop" title="Synth-pop">synth-pop</a> music and other <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a> genres featuring non-traditional instruments increasing in popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> By 2014, pop music worldwide had been permeated by <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christgau_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-46">[46]</a></sup> In 2018, researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Irvine" title="University of California, Irvine">University of California, Irvine</a>, concluded that pop music has become 'sadder' since the 1980s. The elements of <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a> and brightness have eventually been replaced with electronic beats making pop music more 'sad yet danceable'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_spread_and_crosspollination">International spread and crosspollination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International spread and crosspollination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Pop music has been dominated by the American and (from the mid-1960s) British <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">music industries</a>, whose influence has made pop music something of an international monoculture, but most regions and countries have their own form of pop music, sometimes producing local versions of wider trends, and lending them local characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Some of these trends (for example <a href="/wiki/Europop" title="Europop">Europop</a>) have had a significant impact on the development of the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firth2002_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2002-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1062260506">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:22%; border-width: 1px; font-size: 89%;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>The story of pop music is largely the story of the intertwining pop culture of the United States and the United Kingdom in the postwar era.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="right-aligned" style=""> — <a href="/wiki/Bob_Stanley_(musician)" title="Bob Stanley (musician)">Bob Stanley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christgau_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-46">[46]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>According to <i>Grove Music Online</i>, "Western-derived pop styles, whether coexisting with or marginalizing distinctively local genres, have spread throughout the world and have come to constitute stylistic common denominators in global commercial music cultures".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PManuel_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-PManuel-51">[51]</a></sup> Some non-Western countries, such as Japan, have developed a thriving pop music industry, most of which is devoted to Western-style pop. Japan has for several years produced a greater quantity of music than everywhere except the US.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="vague (April 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PManuel_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-PManuel-51">[51]</a></sup> The spread of Western-style pop music has been interpreted variously as representing processes of Americanization, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Homogenization" title="Radio Homogenization">homogenization</a>, modernization, creative appropriation, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_imperialism" title="Cultural imperialism">cultural imperialism</a>, or a more general process of <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PManuel_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-PManuel-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the pop music styles that developed alongside other music styles is <a href="/wiki/Latin_pop" title="Latin pop">Latin pop</a>, which rose in popularity in the US during the 1950s with early rock and roll success <a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Valens" title="Ritchie Valens">Ritchie Valens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strachwitz_Frontera_Collection_2017_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strachwitz_Frontera_Collection_2017-52">[52]</a></sup> Later, as <a href="/wiki/Los_Lobos" title="Los Lobos">Los Lobos</a> garnered major <a href="/wiki/Chicano_rock" title="Chicano rock">Chicano rock</a> popularity during the 1970s and 1980s, musician <a href="/wiki/Selena" title="Selena">Selena</a> saw large-scale pop music presence as the 1980s and 1990s progressed, along with crossover appeal with fans of <a href="/wiki/Tejano_music" title="Tejano music">Tejano music</a> pioneers <a href="/wiki/Lydia_Mendoza" title="Lydia Mendoza">Lydia Mendoza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Joe_(singer)" title="Little Joe (singer)">Little Joe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucero_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lucero-53">[53]</a></sup> With later <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Hispanic and Latino Americans</a> seeing success within pop music charts, 1990s pop successes stayed popular in both their original genres and in broader pop music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Latin pop hit singles, such as "<a href="/wiki/Macarena" title="Macarena">Macarena</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Los_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Los del Río">Los del Río</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Despacito" title="Despacito">Despacito</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Luis_Fonsi" title="Luis Fonsi">Luis Fonsi</a>, have seen record-breaking success on worldwide pop music charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Villafañe_2017_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Villafañe_2017-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the beginning of the 2000s, the trends that dominated during the late 1990s still continued, but the music industry started to change as people began to download music from the internet. People were able to discover genres and artists that were outside of the mainstream and propel them to fame, but at the same time smaller artists had a harder time making a living because their music was being pirated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Popular artists were <a href="/wiki/Avril_Lavigne" title="Avril Lavigne">Avril Lavigne</a>, <a href="/wiki/NSYNC" title="NSYNC">NSYNC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">Christina Aguilera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Destiny%27s_Child" title="Destiny's Child">Destiny's Child</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Britney_Spears" title="Britney Spears">Britney Spears</a>. Pop music often came from many different genres, with each genre in turn influencing the next one, blurring the lines between them and making them less distinct. This change was epitomized in the album <a href="/wiki/FutureSex/LoveSounds" title="FutureSex/LoveSounds">FutureSex/LoveSounds</a>, which under the influence of producer <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a>, mixed the sounds of <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the year 2010, pop music impacted by dance music came to be dominant on the charts. Instead of the radio setting the trends, it was now the club. "The new bubble is all the collective clubs around the world. Radio is just doing its best to keep up," said Will.i.am. at the beginning of the decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Songs that talked of escapism through partying became the most popular, influenced by the impulse to forget the economic troubles that had taken over the world after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">2008 crash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The most popular artists of this era were <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Swift" title="Taylor Swift">Taylor Swift</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Black_Eyed_Peas" title="The Black Eyed Peas">the Black Eyed Peas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Katy_Perry" title="Katy Perry">Katy Perry</a>.
</p>
