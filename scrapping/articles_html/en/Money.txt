<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Money_(disambiguation)" title="Money (disambiguation)">Money (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p><b>Money</b> is any item or verifiable record that is generally accepted as <a href="/wiki/Payment" title="Payment">payment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> and repayment of <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debts</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxes" title="Taxes">taxes</a>, in a particular country or socio-economic context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The primary functions which distinguish money are as a <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">unit of account</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Store_of_value" title="Store of value">store of value</a> and sometimes, a <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_deferred_payment" title="Standard of deferred payment">standard of deferred payment</a>.
</p><p>Money was historically an <a href="/wiki/Emergence#Money" title="Emergence">emergent market phenomenon</a> that possess intrinsic value as a <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity</a>; nearly all contemporary money systems are based on unbacked <a href="/wiki/Fiat_money" title="Fiat money">fiat money</a> without <a href="/wiki/Use_value" title="Use value">use value</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-4">[4]</a></sup> Its value is consequently derived by social convention, having been declared by a <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> or regulatory entity to be <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>; that is, it must be accepted as a form of payment within the boundaries of the country, for "all debts, public and private", in the case of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a>. Contexts which erode public confidence, such as the circulation of <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_money" title="Counterfeit money">counterfeit money</a> or domestic <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a>, can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_money" title="Good money">good money</a> to lose its value.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> of a country comprises all <a href="/wiki/Currency_in_circulation" title="Currency in circulation">currency in circulation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknotes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a> currently issued) and, depending on the particular definition used, one or more types of <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">bank money</a> (the balances held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transactional_account" title="Transactional account">checking accounts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savings_account" title="Savings account">savings accounts</a>, and other types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_accounts" title="Bank accounts">bank accounts</a>). Bank money, whose value exists on the books of financial institutions and can be converted into physical notes or used for cashless payment, forms by far the largest part of <a href="/wiki/Broad_money" title="Broad money">broad money</a> in developed countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word money derives from the Latin word <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">moneta</i></span><span class="lang-comment" style="font-style: normal; display: none; color: #33aa33; margin-left: 0.3em;">code: lat promoted to code: la </span> with the meaning "coin" via French <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">monnaie</i></span>. The Latin word is believed to originate from a temple of <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a>, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitoline" title="Capitoline">Capitoline</a>, one of Rome's seven hills. In the ancient world, Juno was often associated with money. The temple of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juno_Moneta" title="Juno Moneta">Juno Moneta</a> at Rome was the place where the mint of Ancient Rome was located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The name "Juno" may have derived from the Etruscan goddess <a href="/wiki/Uni_(mythology)" title="Uni (mythology)">Uni</a> (which means "the one", "unique", "unit", "union", "united") and "Moneta" either from the Latin word "monere" (remind, warn, or instruct) or the Greek word "moneres" (alone, unique).
</p><p>In the Western world a prevalent term for coin-money has been <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/specie" title="wikt:specie">specie</a></i>, stemming from Latin <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">in specie</i></span><span class="lang-comment" style="font-style: normal; display: none; color: #33aa33; margin-left: 0.3em;">code: lat promoted to code: la </span>, meaning 'in kind'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_money" title="History of money">History of money</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BMC_06.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="202" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/BMC_06.jpg/220px-BMC_06.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/BMC_06.jpg/330px-BMC_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/BMC_06.jpg/440px-BMC_06.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BMC_06.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 640 BC one-third <a href="/wiki/Stater" title="Stater">stater</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrum" title="Electrum">electrum</a> coin from <a href="/wiki/Lydia#First_coinage" title="Lydia">Lydia</a></div></div></div>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Barter" title="Barter">barter</a>-like methods may date back to at least 100,000 years ago, though there is no evidence of a society or economy that relied primarily on barter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Instead, non-monetary societies operated largely along the principles of <a href="/wiki/Gift_economy" title="Gift economy">gift economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> When barter did in fact occur, it was usually between either complete strangers or potential enemies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graeber,_David_pp._153-154_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graeber,_David_pp._153-154-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many cultures around the world eventually developed the use of <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a>. The Mesopotamian <a href="/wiki/Shekel" title="Shekel">shekel</a> was a unit of weight, and relied on the mass of something like 160 <a href="/wiki/Grain_(unit)" title="Grain (unit)">grains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The first usage of the term came from <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> circa 3000 BC. Societies in the Americas, Asia, Africa and Australia used <a href="/wiki/Shell_money" title="Shell money">shell money</a> – often, the shells of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowry" title="Cowry">cowry</a> (<i>Cypraea moneta L.</i> or <i>C. annulus L.</i>). According to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lydians" title="Lydians">Lydians</a> were the first people to introduce the use of <a href="/wiki/Gold_coin" title="Gold coin">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silver_coin" title="Silver coin">silver coins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is thought by modern scholars that these first stamped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coins" title="Coins">coins</a> were minted around 650 to 600 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jiao_zi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="308" decoding="async" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jiao_zi.jpg/170px-Jiao_zi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jiao_zi.jpg/255px-Jiao_zi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/Jiao_zi.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jiao_zi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Song Dynasty <i>Jiaozi</i>, the world's earliest paper money</div></div></div>
<p>The system of <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a> eventually evolved into a system of <a href="/wiki/Representative_money" title="Representative money">representative money</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This occurred because gold and silver merchants or banks would issue receipts to their depositors – redeemable for the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a> deposited. Eventually, these receipts became generally accepted as a means of payment and were used as money. Paper money or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banknotes" title="Banknotes">banknotes</a> were first used in China during the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>. These banknotes, known as "<a href="/wiki/Jiaozi_(currency)" title="Jiaozi (currency)">jiaozi</a>", evolved from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promissory_notes" title="Promissory notes">promissory notes</a> that had been used since the 7th century. However, they did not displace commodity money and were used alongside coins. In the 13th century, paper money became known in Europe through the accounts of travellers, such as <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_of_Rubruck" title="William of Rubruck">William of Rubruck</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Marco Polo's account of paper money during the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> is the subject of a chapter of his book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Travels_of_Marco_Polo" title="The Travels of Marco Polo">The Travels of Marco Polo</a></i>, titled "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Travels_of_Marco_Polo/Book_2/Chapter_24" title="s:The Travels of Marco Polo/Book 2/Chapter 24">How the Great Kaan Causeth the Bark of Trees, Made Into Something Like Paper, to Pass for Money All Over his Country</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marco_Polo_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marco_Polo-19">[19]</a></sup> Banknotes were first issued in Europe by <a href="/wiki/Stockholms_Banco" title="Stockholms Banco">Stockholms Banco</a> in 1661 and were again also used alongside coins. The <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Monetary_system" title="Monetary system">monetary system</a> where the medium of exchange are paper notes that are convertible into pre-set, fixed quantities of gold, replaced the use of gold coins as currency in the 17th–19th centuries in Europe. These gold standard notes were made <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>, and redemption into gold coins was discouraged. By the beginning of the 20th century, almost all countries had adopted the gold standard, backing their legal tender notes with fixed amounts of gold.
</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_Conference" title="Bretton Woods Conference">Bretton Woods Conference</a>, most countries adopted fiat currencies that were fixed to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">U.S. dollar</a>. The U.S. dollar was in turn fixed to gold. In 1971 the U.S. government suspended the convertibility of the dollar to gold. After this many countries de-pegged their currencies from the U.S. dollar, and most of the world's currencies became unbacked by anything except the governments' fiat of legal tender and the ability to convert the money into goods via payment. According to proponents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Money_Theory" title="Modern Money Theory">modern money theory</a>, fiat money is also backed by taxes. By imposing taxes, states create demand for the currency they issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In <i>Money and the Mechanism of Exchange (1875)</i>, <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_Jevons" title="William Stanley Jevons">William Stanley Jevons</a> famously analyzed money in terms of four functions: a <i><a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a></i>, a <i>common measure of value</i> (or <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">unit of account</a>), a <i>standard of value</i> (or <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_deferred_payment" title="Standard of deferred payment">standard of deferred payment</a>), and a <i><a href="/wiki/Store_of_value" title="Store of value">store of value</a></i>. By 1919, Jevons's four functions of money were summarized in the <a href="/wiki/Couplet" title="Couplet">couplet</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Money's a matter of functions four,</dd>
<dd>A Medium, a Measure, a Standard, a Store.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_milnes_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-a_milnes-21">[21]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>This couplet would later become widely popular in macroeconomics textbooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dwivedi_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dwivedi-22">[22]</a></sup> Most modern textbooks now list only three functions, that of <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">unit of account</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Store_of_value" title="Store of value">store of value</a>, not considering a standard of deferred payment as a distinguished function, but rather subsuming it in the others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krugman_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-krugman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abel_bernanke_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-abel_bernanke-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>There have been many historical disputes regarding the combination of money's functions, some arguing that they need more separation and that a single unit is insufficient to deal with them all. One of these arguments is that the role of money as a <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a> conflicts with its role as a <a href="/wiki/Store_of_value" title="Store of value">store of value</a>: its role as a store of value requires holding it without spending, whereas its role as a medium of exchange requires it to circulate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greco_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-greco-25">[25]</a></sup> Others argue that storing of value is just deferral of the exchange, but does not diminish the fact that money is a medium of exchange that can be transported both across space and time. The term "financial capital" is a more general and inclusive term for all liquid instruments, whether or not they are a uniformly recognized tender.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medium_of_exchange">Medium of exchange</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">Medium of exchange</a></div>
<p>When money is used to intermediate the exchange of goods and services, it is performing a function as a <i>medium of exchange</i>. It thereby avoids the inefficiencies of a barter system, such as the inability to permanently ensure "<a href="/wiki/Coincidence_of_wants" title="Coincidence of wants">coincidence of wants</a>". For example, between two parties in a barter system, one party may not have or make the item that the other wants, indicating the non-existence of the coincidence of wants. Having a medium of exchange can alleviate this issue because the former can have the freedom to spend time on other items, instead of being burdened to only serve the needs of the latter. Meanwhile, the latter can use the medium of exchange to seek for a party that can provide them with the item they want.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measure_of_value">Measure of value</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">Unit of account</a></div>
<p>A <i>unit of account</i> (in economics)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> is a standard numerical monetary unit of measurement of the market value of goods, services, and other transactions. Also known as a "measure" or "standard" of relative worth and deferred payment, a unit of account is a necessary prerequisite for the formulation of commercial agreements that involve debt.
</p><p>Money acts as a standard measure and a common denomination of trade. It is thus a basis for quoting and bargaining of prices. It is necessary for developing efficient accounting systems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_of_deferred_payment">Standard of deferred payment</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_deferred_payment" title="Standard of deferred payment">Standard of deferred payment</a></div>
<p>While <i>standard of deferred payment</i> is distinguished by some texts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greco_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-greco-25">[25]</a></sup> particularly older ones, other texts subsume this under other functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krugman_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-krugman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abel_bernanke_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-abel_bernanke-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Specify which other functions are sometimes said to subsume this function, and in that way. (December 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A "standard of deferred payment" is an accepted way to settle a <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a> – a unit in which debts are denominated, and the status of money as <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>, in those jurisdictions which have this concept, states that it may function for the discharge of debts. When debts are denominated in money, the real value of debts may change due to inflation and <a href="/wiki/Deflation" title="Deflation">deflation</a>, and for sovereign and international debts via <a href="/wiki/Debasement" title="Debasement">debasement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">devaluation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Store_of_value">Store of value</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Store_of_value" title="Store of value">Store of value</a></div>
<p>To act as a <i>store of value</i>, money must be able to be reliably saved, stored, and retrieved – and be predictably usable as a medium of exchange when it is retrieved. The value of the money must also remain stable over time. Some have argued that inflation, by reducing the value of money, diminishes the ability of the money to function as a store of value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mankiw_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-mankiw-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span></h2>
<p>According to Desjardins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desjardins_infographic_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-desjardins_infographic-27">[27]</a></sup> "many economists and experts in the field agree" that the properties of money are that it is a medium of exchange, a unit of account, and a store of value.
To fulfill these various functions, he states that money must be:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desjardins_infographic_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-desjardins_infographic-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Fungibility" title="Fungibility">Fungible</a>: its individual units must be capable of mutual substitution (i.e., interchangeability).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Durability" title="Durability">Durable</a>: able to withstand repeated use.</li>
<li>Divisible: divisible to small units.</li>
<li>Portable: easily carried and transported.</li>
<li>Acceptable: everyone must accept the money as payment</li>
<li>Limited in supply: its supply in circulation must be limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desjardins_infographic_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-desjardins_infographic-27">[27]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Money_supply">Money supply</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">Money supply</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MB,_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="306" data-file-width="630" decoding="async" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/MB%2C_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png/330px-MB%2C_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/MB%2C_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png/495px-MB%2C_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/58/MB%2C_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MB,_M1_and_M2_aggregates_from_1981_to_2012.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Money Base, M1 and M2 in the U.S. from 1981 to 2012</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="786" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg/220px-RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg/330px-RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg/440px-RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RIAN_archive_978776_Printing_paper_money_at_Goznak_factory_in_Perm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Printing paper money at a printing press in <a href="/wiki/Perm,_Russia" title="Perm, Russia">Perm</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pengar_-_2019.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="999" data-file-width="1044" decoding="async" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Pengar_-_2019.jpg/220px-Pengar_-_2019.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Pengar_-_2019.jpg/330px-Pengar_-_2019.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Pengar_-_2019.jpg/440px-Pengar_-_2019.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pengar_-_2019.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A person counts a bundle of different <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> banknotes.</div></div></div>
<p>In economics, money is any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instrument_(finance)" title="Instrument (finance)">financial instrument</a> that can fulfill the functions of money (detailed above). These financial instruments together are collectively referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> of an economy. In other words, the money supply is the number of financial instruments within a specific economy available for purchasing goods or services. Since the money supply consists of various financial instruments (usually currency, demand deposits, and various other types of deposits), the amount of money in an economy is measured by adding together these financial instruments creating a <i>monetary aggregate</i>.
</p><p>Economists employ different ways to measure the stock of money or money supply, reflected in different types of monetary aggregates, using a categorization system that focuses on the <a href="/wiki/Market_liquidity" title="Market liquidity">liquidity</a> of the financial instrument used as money. The most commonly used monetary aggregates (or types of money) are conventionally designated M1, M2, and M3. These are successively larger aggregate categories: M1 is currency (coins and bills) plus <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">demand deposits</a> (such as checking accounts); M2 is M1 plus <a href="/wiki/Savings_account" title="Savings account">savings accounts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time_deposit" title="Time deposit">time deposits</a> under $100,000; M3 is M2 plus larger time deposits and similar institutional accounts. M1 includes only the most liquid financial instruments, and M3 relatively illiquid instruments. The precise definition of M1, M2, etc. may be different in different countries.
</p><p>Another measure of money, M0, is also used; unlike the other measures, it does not represent actual <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power" title="Purchasing power">purchasing power</a> by firms and households in the economy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> M0 is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_money" title="Base money">base money</a>, or the amount of money actually issued by the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> of a country. It is measured as currency plus deposits of banks and other institutions at the central bank. M0 is also the only money that can satisfy the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserve_requirements" title="Reserve requirements">reserve requirements</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_banks" title="Commercial banks">commercial banks</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_money">Creation of money</span></h3>
<p>In current economic systems, money is created by two procedures:
</p><p><b>Legal tender</b>, or <b>narrow money</b> (M0) is the cash created by a Central Bank by minting coins and printing banknotes.
</p><p><b>Bank money</b>, or <b>broad money</b> (M1/M2) is the money created by private banks through the recording of loans as deposits of borrowing clients, with partial support indicated by the <i>cash ratio</i>. Currently, bank money is created as electronic money.
</p><p>In most countries, the majority of money is mostly created as M1/M2 by commercial banks making loans. Contrary to some popular misconceptions, banks do not act simply as intermediaries, lending out deposits that savers place with them, and do not depend on central bank money (M0) to create new loans and deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Market_liquidity">Market liquidity</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Market_liquidity" title="Market liquidity">Market liquidity</a></div>
<p>"Market liquidity" describes how easily an item can be traded for another item, or into the common currency within an economy. Money is the most liquid asset because it is universally recognized and accepted as a common currency. In this way, money gives consumers the <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">freedom</a> to trade goods and services easily without having to barter.
</p><p>Liquid financial instruments are easily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tradable" title="Tradable">tradable</a> and have low <a href="/wiki/Transaction_cost" title="Transaction cost">transaction costs</a>. There should be no (or minimal) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bid/offer_spread" title="Bid/offer spread">spread</a> between the prices to buy and sell the instrument being used as money.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commodity">Commodity</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">Commodity money</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="836" data-file-width="836" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg/170px-1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg/255px-1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg/340px-1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1914_Sydney_Half_Sovereign_-_St._George.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1914 British <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_(British_coin)" title="Sovereign (British coin)">gold sovereign</a></div></div></div>
<p>Many items have been used as <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a> such as naturally scarce <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conch_shell" title="Conch shell">conch shells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, beads, etc., as well as many other things that are thought of as having <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_theory_of_value" title="Intrinsic theory of value">value</a>. Commodity money value comes from the commodity out of which it is made. The commodity itself constitutes the money, and the money is the commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mises_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mises-29">[29]</a></sup> Examples of commodities that have been used as mediums of exchange include gold, silver, copper, rice, <a href="/wiki/Wampum" title="Wampum">Wampum</a>, salt, peppercorns, large stones, decorated belts, shells, alcohol, cigarettes, cannabis, candy, etc. These items were sometimes used in a metric of perceived value in conjunction with one another, in various commodity valuation or <a href="/wiki/Price_system" title="Price system">price system</a> economies. The use of commodity money is similar to barter, but a commodity money provides a simple and automatic <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">unit of account</a> for the commodity which is being used as money. Although some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_coins" title="Gold coins">gold coins</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Krugerrand" title="Krugerrand">Krugerrand</a> are considered <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>, there is no record of their face value on either side of the coin. The rationale for this is that emphasis is laid on their direct link to the prevailing value of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millesimal_fineness" title="Millesimal fineness">fine gold</a> content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/American_Gold_Eagle" title="American Gold Eagle">American Eagles</a> are imprinted with their gold content and legal tender <a href="/wiki/Face_value" title="Face value">face value</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usmint.gov_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-usmint.gov-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Representative">Representative</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Representative_money" title="Representative money">Representative money</a></div>
<p>In 1875, the British economist <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_Jevons" title="William Stanley Jevons">William Stanley Jevons</a> described the money used at the time as "<a href="/wiki/Representative_money" title="Representative money">representative money</a>". Representative money is money that consists of <a href="/wiki/Token_coin" title="Token coin">token coins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_money" title="Paper money">paper money</a> or other physical tokens such as certificates, that can be reliably exchanged for a fixed quantity of a commodity such as gold or silver. The value of representative money stands in direct and fixed relation to the commodity that backs it, while not itself being composed of that commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jevons_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jevons-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiat">Fiat</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fiat_money" title="Fiat money">Fiat money</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1881" data-file-width="1890" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png/170px-2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png/255px-2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png/340px-2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2006_AEGold_Proof_Rev.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gold coins are an example of legal tender that are traded for their intrinsic value, rather than their face value.</div></div></div>
<p>Fiat money or fiat currency is money whose value is not derived from any intrinsic value or guarantee that it can be converted into a valuable commodity (such as gold). Instead, it has value only by government order (fiat). Usually, the government declares the fiat currency (typically notes and coins from a central bank, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve System</a> in the U.S.) to be <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>, making it unlawful not to accept the fiat currency as a means of repayment for all debts, public and private.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www-personal.umich_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-www-personal.umich-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlackHenry_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-BlackHenry-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullion_coins" title="Bullion coins">bullion coins</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Gold_Nugget" title="Australian Gold Nugget">Australian Gold Nugget</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Gold_Eagle" title="American Gold Eagle">American Eagle</a> are legal tender, however, they trade based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_price" title="Market price">market price</a> of the metal content as a <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a>, rather than their legal tender <a href="/wiki/Face_value" title="Face value">face value</a> (which is usually only a small fraction of their bullion value).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usmint.gov_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-usmint.gov-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fiat money, if physically represented in the form of currency (paper or coins), can be accidentally damaged or destroyed. However, fiat money has an advantage over representative or commodity money, in that the same laws that created the money can also define rules for its replacement in case of damage or destruction. For example, the U.S. government will replace mutilated <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Note" title="Federal Reserve Note">Federal Reserve Notes</a> (U.S. fiat money) if at least half of the physical note can be reconstructed, or if it can be otherwise proven to have been destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> By contrast, commodity money that has been lost or destroyed cannot be recovered.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coinage">Coinage</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">Coin</a></div>
<p>These factors led to the shift of the store of value being the metal itself: at first silver, then both silver and gold, and at one point there was bronze as well. Now we have copper coins and other non-precious metals as coins. Metals were mined, weighed, and stamped into coins. This was to assure the individual taking the coin that he was getting a certain known weight of precious metal. Coins could be counterfeited, but they also created a new <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">unit of account</a>, which helped lead to banking. <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_principle" title="Archimedes' principle">Archimedes' principle</a> provided the next link: coins could now be easily tested for their <a href="/wiki/Fineness" title="Fineness">fine</a> weight of the metal, and thus the value of a coin could be determined, even if it had been shaved, debased or otherwise tampered with (see <a href="/wiki/Numismatics" title="Numismatics">Numismatics</a>).
</p><p>In most major economies using coinage, copper, silver, and gold formed three tiers of coins. Gold coins were used for large purchases, payment of the military, and backing of state activities. Silver coins were used for midsized transactions, and as a unit of account for taxes, dues, contracts, and fealty, while copper coins represented the coinage of common transaction. This system had been used in ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_coinage" title="Indian coinage">India</a> since the time of the <a href="/wiki/Mahajanapadas" title="Mahajanapadas">Mahajanapadas</a>. In Europe, this system worked through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> period because there was virtually no new gold, silver, or copper introduced through mining or conquest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus the overall ratios of the three coinages remained roughly equivalent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paper">Paper</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">Banknote</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hui_zi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="473" data-file-width="325" decoding="async" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Hui_zi.jpg/170px-Hui_zi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Hui_zi.jpg/255px-Hui_zi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6a/Hui_zi.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hui_zi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Huizi_(currency)" title="Huizi (currency)">Huizi currency</a>, issued in 1160</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">premodern China</a>, the need for credit and for circulating a medium that was less of a burden than exchanging thousands of <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">copper coins</a> led to the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_money" title="Paper money">paper money</a>, commonly known today as "banknote"s. This economic phenomenon was a slow and gradual process that took place from the late <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> (618–907) into the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279). It began as a means for merchants to exchange heavy coinage for <a href="/wiki/Receipt" title="Receipt">receipts</a> of deposit issued as <a href="/wiki/Promissory_note" title="Promissory note">promissory notes</a> from shops of wholesalers, notes that were valid for temporary use in a small regional territory. In the 10th century, the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> government began circulating these notes amongst the traders in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monopolized" title="Monopolized">monopolized</a> salt industry. The Song government granted several shops the sole right to issue banknotes, and in the early 12th century the government finally took over these shops to produce state-issued currency. Yet the banknotes issued were still regionally valid and temporary; it was not until the mid 13th century that a standard and uniform government issue of paper money was made into an acceptable nationwide currency. The already widespread methods of <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock printing</a> and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pi_Sheng" title="Pi Sheng">Pi Sheng</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a> printing by the 11th century was the impetus for the massive production of paper money in premodern China.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Money_poster.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Money_poster.JPG/220px-Money_poster.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Money_poster.JPG/330px-Money_poster.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Money_poster.JPG/440px-Money_poster.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Money_poster.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paper money from different countries</div></div></div>
<p>At around the same time in the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Islamic world</a>, a vigorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monetary_economy" title="Monetary economy">monetary economy</a> was created during the 7th–12th centuries on the basis of the expanding levels of circulation of a stable high-value currency (the <a href="/wiki/Dinar" title="Dinar">dinar</a>). Innovations introduced by economists, traders and merchants of the Muslim world include the earliest uses of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banaji_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Banaji-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cheque" title="Cheque">cheques</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savings_account" title="Savings account">savings accounts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transactional_account" title="Transactional account">transactional accounts</a>, loaning, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trusts" title="Trusts">trusts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rates</a>, the transfer of credit and <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labib_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Labib-38">[38]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banking_institution" title="Banking institution">banking institutions</a> for loans and <a href="/wiki/Deposit_account" title="Deposit account">deposits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labib_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Labib-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (September 2019)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In Europe, paper money was first introduced in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> in 1661. Sweden was rich in copper, thus, because of copper's low value, extraordinarily big coins (often weighing several kilograms) had to be made. The advantages of paper currency were numerous: it reduced transport of gold and silver, and thus lowered the risks; it made loaning gold or silver at interest easier since the specie (gold or silver) never left the possession of the lender until someone else redeemed the note; it allowed for a division of currency into credit and specie backed forms. It enabled the sale of <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_stock_companies" title="Joint stock companies">joint stock companies</a>, and the redemption of those <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shares" title="Shares">shares</a> in the paper.
</p><p>However, these advantages are held within their disadvantages. First, since a note has no intrinsic value, there was nothing to stop issuing authorities from printing more of it than they had specie to back it with. Second, because it increased the money supply, it increased inflationary pressures, a fact observed by <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> in the 18th century. The result is that paper money would often lead to an inflationary bubble, which could collapse if people began demanding hard money, causing the demand for paper notes to fall to zero. The printing of paper money was also associated with wars, and financing of wars, and therefore regarded as part of maintaining a <a href="/wiki/Standing_army" title="Standing army">standing army</a>. For these reasons, paper currency was held in suspicion and hostility in Europe and America. It was also addictive since the speculative profits of trade and capital creation were quite large. Major nations established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mint_(coin)" title="Mint (coin)">mints</a> to print money and mint coins, and branches of their treasury to collect taxes and hold gold and silver stock.
</p><p>At this time both silver and gold were considered <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>, and accepted by governments for taxes. However, the <a href="/wiki/Gresham%27s_law" title="Gresham's law">instability in the ratio</a> between the two grew over the 19th century, with the increase both in the supply of these metals, particularly silver, and of trade. This is called <a href="/wiki/Bimetallism" title="Bimetallism">bimetallism</a> and the attempt to create a bimetallic standard where both gold and silver backed currency remained in circulation occupied the efforts of inflationists. Governments at this point could use currency as an instrument of policy, printing paper currency such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Note" title="United States Note">United States greenback</a>, to pay for military expenditures. They could also set the terms at which they would redeem notes for specie, by limiting the amount of purchase, or the minimum amount that could be redeemed.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Billets_de_5000.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="931" data-file-width="598" decoding="async" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Billets_de_5000.jpg/170px-Billets_de_5000.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Billets_de_5000.jpg/255px-Billets_de_5000.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Billets_de_5000.jpg/340px-Billets_de_5000.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Billets_de_5000.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Banknotes of different currencies with a face value of 5000</div></div></div>
<p>By 1900, most of the industrializing nations were on some form of a gold standard, with paper notes and silver coins constituting the circulating medium. Private banks and governments across the world followed <a href="/wiki/Gresham%27s_law" title="Gresham's law">Gresham's law</a>: keeping gold and silver paid but paying out in notes. This did not happen all around the world at the same time, but occurred sporadically, generally in times of war or financial crisis, beginning in the early part of the 20th century and continuing across the world until the late 20th century, when the regime of floating fiat currencies came into force. One of the last countries to break away from the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a> was the United States in 1971.
</p><p>No country anywhere in the world today has an enforceable gold standard or <a href="/wiki/Silver_standard" title="Silver standard">silver standard</a> currency system.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_bank">Commercial bank</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">Demand deposit</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sweet_success.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1662" data-file-width="2485" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Sweet_success.jpg/220px-Sweet_success.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Sweet_success.jpg/330px-Sweet_success.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Sweet_success.jpg/440px-Sweet_success.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sweet_success.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A check, used as a means of converting funds in a <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">demand deposit</a> to cash</div></div></div>
<p>Commercial bank money or <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">demand deposits</a> are claims against financial institutions that can be used for the purchase of goods and services. A demand deposit account is an account from which funds can be withdrawn at any time by check or <a href="/wiki/Cash" title="Cash">cash</a> withdrawal without giving the bank or financial institution any prior notice. Banks have the legal obligation to return funds held in demand deposits immediately upon demand (or 'at call'). Demand deposit withdrawals can be performed in person, via checks or bank drafts, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automatic_teller_machine" title="Automatic teller machine">automatic teller machines</a> (ATMs), or through <a href="/wiki/Online_banking" title="Online banking">online banking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commercial bank money is created through <a href="/wiki/Fractional-reserve_banking" title="Fractional-reserve banking">fractional-reserve banking</a>, the banking practise where banks keep only a fraction of their <a href="/wiki/Demand_deposit" title="Demand deposit">deposits</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bank_reserves" title="Bank reserves">reserve</a> (as cash and other highly liquid assets) and lend out the remainder, while maintaining the simultaneous obligation to redeem all these deposits upon demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2014)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Commercial bank money differs from commodity and fiat money in two ways: firstly it is non-physical, as its existence is only reflected in the account ledgers of banks and other financial institutions, and secondly, there is some element of risk that the claim will not be fulfilled if the financial institution becomes insolvent. The process of fractional-reserve banking has a cumulative effect of <a href="/wiki/Money_creation" title="Money creation">money creation</a> by commercial banks, as it expands the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> (cash and demand deposits) beyond what it would otherwise be. Because of the prevalence of fractional reserve banking, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_(economics)" title="M2 (economics)">broad money supply</a> of most countries is a multiple (greater than 1) of the amount of <a href="/wiki/Monetary_base" title="Monetary base">base money</a> created by the country's <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a>. That multiple (called the <a href="/wiki/Money_multiplier" title="Money multiplier">money multiplier</a>) is determined by the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_requirement" title="Reserve requirement">reserve requirement</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Financial_ratio" title="Financial ratio">financial ratio</a> requirements imposed by financial regulators.
</p><p>The money supply of a country is usually held to be the total amount of currency in circulation plus the total value of checking and savings deposits in the commercial banks in the country. In modern economies, relatively little of the money supply is in physical currency. For example, in December 2010 in the U.S., of the $8853.4 billion in broad money supply (M2), only $915.7 billion (about 10%) consisted of physical coins and paper money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_or_electronic">Digital or electronic</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_money" title="Digital money">Digital money</a></div>
<p>The development of computer technology in the second part of the twentieth century allowed money to be represented digitally. By 1990, in the United States all money transferred between its central bank and commercial banks was in electronic form. By the 2000s most money existed as <a href="/wiki/Digital_currency" title="Digital currency">digital currency</a> in bank databases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 2012, by number of transaction, 20 to 58 percent of transactions were electronic (dependent on country).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Non-national digital currencies were developed in the early 2000s. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Flooz.com" title="Flooz.com">Flooz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beenz.com" title="Beenz.com">Beenz</a> had gained momentum before the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">Dot-com bubble</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Not much innovation occurred until the conception of <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">Bitcoin</a> in 2008, which introduced the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency" title="Cryptocurrency">cryptocurrency</a> – a decentralised trustless currency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monetary_policy">Monetary policy</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">Monetary policy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USCurrency_Federal_Reserve.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="416" data-file-width="255" decoding="async" height="359" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/USCurrency_Federal_Reserve.jpg/220px-USCurrency_Federal_Reserve.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/63/USCurrency_Federal_Reserve.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USCurrency_Federal_Reserve.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US dollar banknotes</div></div></div>
<p>When gold and silver are used as money, the money supply can grow only if the supply of these metals is increased by mining. This rate of increase will accelerate during periods of <a href="/wiki/Gold_rush" title="Gold rush">gold rushes</a> and discoveries, such as when Columbus traveled to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> and brought back gold and silver to Spain, or when gold was <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">discovered in California in 1848</a>. This causes inflation, as the value of gold goes down. However, if the rate of <a href="/wiki/Gold_mining" title="Gold mining">gold mining</a> cannot keep up with the growth of the economy, gold becomes relatively more valuable, and prices (denominated in gold) will drop, causing deflation. Deflation was the more typical situation for over a century when gold and paper money backed by gold were used as money in the 18th and 19th centuries.
</p><p>Modern-day monetary systems are based on fiat money and are no longer tied to the value of gold. The control of the amount of money in the economy is known as monetary policy. Monetary policy is the process by which a government, central bank, or <a href="/wiki/Monetary_authority" title="Monetary authority">monetary authority</a> manages the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> to achieve specific goals. Usually, the goal of monetary policy is to accommodate economic growth in an environment of stable prices. For example, it is clearly stated in the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Act" title="Federal Reserve Act">Federal Reserve Act</a> that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Governors" title="Board of Governors">Board of Governors</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Open_Market_Committee" title="Federal Open Market Committee">Federal Open Market Committee</a> should seek "to promote effectively the goals of maximum employment, stable prices, and moderate long-term interest rates."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>A failed monetary policy can have significant detrimental effects on an economy and the society that depends on it. These include <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stagflation" title="Stagflation">stagflation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a>, high unemployment, shortages of imported goods, inability to export goods, and even total monetary collapse and the adoption of a much less efficient barter economy. This happened in Russia, for instance, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union_(1985-1991)" title="History of the Soviet Union (1985-1991)">fall of the Soviet Union</a>.
</p><p>Governments and central banks have taken both regulatory and <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> approaches to monetary policy. Some of the tools used to control the money supply include:
</p>
<ul><li>changing the <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rate</a> at which the central bank loans money to (or borrows money from) the commercial banks</li>
<li>currency purchases or sales</li>
<li>increasing or lowering <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">government borrowing</a></li>
<li>increasing or lowering <a href="/wiki/Government_spending" title="Government spending">government spending</a></li>
<li>manipulation of <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rates</a></li>
<li>raising or lowering bank reserve requirements</li>
<li>regulation or prohibition of <a href="/wiki/Private_currency" title="Private currency">private currencies</a></li>
<li>taxation or tax breaks on imports or exports of capital into a country</li></ul>
<p>In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve" title="Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve</a> is responsible for controlling the money supply, while in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_area" title="Euro area">Euro area</a> the respective institution is the <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">European Central Bank</a>. Other central banks with a significant impact on global finances are the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Japan" title="Bank of Japan">Bank of Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Bank_of_China" title="People's Bank of China">People's Bank of China</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a>.
</p><p>For many years much of monetary policy was influenced by an <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic theory</a> known as monetarism. <a href="/wiki/Monetarism" title="Monetarism">Monetarism</a> is an economic theory which argues that management of the money supply should be the primary means of regulating economic activity. The stability of the demand for money prior to the 1980s was a key finding of <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Schwartz" title="Anna Schwartz">Anna Schwartz</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> supported by the work of <a href="/wiki/David_Laidler" title="David Laidler">David Laidler</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and many others. The nature of the demand for money changed during the 1980s owing to technical, institutional, and legal factors<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the influence of monetarism has since decreased.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locality">Locality</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="889" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg/240px-FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg/360px-FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg/480px-FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FIN-10m-1980-anv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._K._Paasikivi" title="J. K. Paasikivi">J. K. Paasikivi</a> illustrated in a former Finnish <a href="/wiki/Finnish_markka" title="Finnish markka">10 mark</a> banknote from 1980</div></div></div>
<p>The definition of money says it is money only "in a particular country or socio-economic context".  In general, communities only use a single measure of value, which can be identified in the prices of goods listed for sale.  There might be multiple media of exchange, which can be observed by what is given to purchase goods ("medium of exchange"), etc.  In most countries, the government acts to encourage a particular forms of money, such as requiring it for taxes and punishing <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a>.
</p><p>Some places do maintain two or more currencies, particularly in border towns or high-travel areas. Shops in these locations might list prices and accept payment in multiple currencies.  Otherwise, foreign currency is treated as a <a href="/wiki/Financial_asset" title="Financial asset">financial asset</a> in the local market.  Foreign currency is commonly bought or sold on <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange markets</a> by travelers and traders.
</p><p>Communities can change the money they use, which is known as <a href="/wiki/Currency_substitution" title="Currency substitution">currency substitution</a>.  This can happen intentionally, when a government issues a new currency.  For example, when Brazil moved from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_cruzeiro_(disambiguation)" title="Brazilian cruzeiro (disambiguation)">Brazilian cruzeiro</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_real" title="Brazilian real">Brazilian real</a>.  It can also happen spontaneously, when the people refuse to accept a currency experiencing <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a> (even if its use is encouraged by the government).
</p><p>The money used by a community can change on a smaller scale. This can come through innovation, such as the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Cheque" title="Cheque">cheques (checks)</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gresham%27s_law" title="Gresham's law">Gresham's law</a> says that "bad money drives out good".  That is, when buying a good, a person is more likely to pass on less-desirable items that qualify as "money" and hold on to more valuable ones.  For example, coins with less silver in them (but which are still valid coins) are more likely to circulate in the community.  This may effectively change the money used by a community.
</p><p>The money used by a community does not have to be a currency issued by a government.  A famous example of community adopting a new form of money is prisoners-of-war using cigarettes to trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_crimes">Financial crimes</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counterfeiting">Counterfeiting</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_money" title="Counterfeit money">Counterfeit money</a></div>
<p>Counterfeit money is imitation currency produced without the legal sanction of the state or government. Producing or using counterfeit money is a form of fraud or forgery. Counterfeiting is almost as old as money itself. Plated copies (known as <a href="/wiki/Fourr%C3%A9e" title="Fourrée">Fourrées</a>) have been found of <a href="/wiki/Lydia#First_coinage" title="Lydia">Lydian coins</a> which are thought to be among the first western coins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Historically, objects that were difficult to counterfeit (e.g. shells, rare stones, precious metals) were often chosen as money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Before the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banknotes" title="Banknotes">paper money</a>, the most prevalent method of counterfeiting involved mixing base metals with pure gold or silver. A form of counterfeiting is the production of documents by legitimate printers in response to fraudulent instructions. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">Nazis</a> forged British pounds and American dollars. Today some of the finest counterfeit banknotes are called <i><a href="/wiki/Superdollar" title="Superdollar">Superdollars</a></i> because of their high quality and likeness to the real U.S. dollar. There has been significant counterfeiting of <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a> banknotes and coins since the launch of the currency in 2002, but considerably less than for the U.S. dollar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Money_laundering">Money laundering</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">Money laundering</a></div>
<p>Money laundering is the process in which the proceeds of crime are transformed into ostensibly legitimate money or other assets. However, in several legal and regulatory systems the term money laundering has become <a href="/wiki/Conflation" title="Conflation">conflated</a> with other forms of financial crime, and sometimes used more generally to include misuse of the financial system (involving things such as securities, <a href="/wiki/Digital_currency" title="Digital currency">digital currencies</a>, credit cards, and traditional currency), including <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_financing" title="Terrorism financing">terrorism financing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tax_evasion" title="Tax evasion">tax evasion</a>, and evading of <a href="/wiki/International_sanctions" title="International sanctions">international sanctions</a>.
</p>

The two main third parties whose promises we accept are the government and the banks ... money ... is not backed by anything physical, and instead relies on trust.
