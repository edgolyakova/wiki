<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Wheel</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Circular component rotating on an axle</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Wheel_(disambiguation)" title="Wheel (disambiguation)">Wheel (disambiguation)</a> and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Wheels_(disambiguation)" title="Wheels (disambiguation)">Wheels (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roue_primitive.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="963" data-file-width="914" decoding="async" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Roue_primitive.png/220px-Roue_primitive.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Roue_primitive.png/330px-Roue_primitive.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Roue_primitive.png/440px-Roue_primitive.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roue_primitive.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An early wheel made of a solid piece of wood</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>wheel</b> is a circular component that is intended to rotate on an <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearing</a>. The wheel is one of the key components of the <a href="/wiki/Wheel_and_axle" title="Wheel and axle">wheel and axle</a> which is one of the <a href="/wiki/Simple_machine" title="Simple machine">six simple machines</a>. Wheels, in conjunction with axles, allow heavy objects to be moved easily facilitating movement or transportation while supporting a load, or performing labor in machines. Wheels are also used for other purposes, such as a <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_wheel" title="Ship's wheel">ship's wheel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steering_wheel" title="Steering wheel">steering wheel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel" title="Potter's wheel">potter's wheel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a>.
</p><p>Common examples are found in <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> applications. A wheel reduces <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> by facilitating motion by <a href="/wiki/Rolling" title="Rolling">rolling</a> together with the use of <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axles</a>. In order for wheels to rotate, a <a href="/wiki/Moment_(physics)" title="Moment (physics)">moment</a> needs to be applied to the wheel about its axis, either by way of gravity or by the application of another external force or <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a>. Using the wheel, <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerians</a> invented a device that spins clay as a potter shapes it into the desired object.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wheel" title="wikt:wheel">wheel</a></i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word <span title="Old English (ca. 450-1100)-language text"><i lang="ang">hwēol</i></span>, from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a> <span title="Proto-Germanic-language text">*<i lang="gem">hwehwlaz</i></span>, from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <span title="Proto-Indo-European-language text">*<i lang="ine">k<sup>w</sup>ék<sup>w</sup>los</i></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> an extended form of the root <span title="Proto-Indo-European-language text">*<i lang="ine">k<sup>w</sup>el-</i></span> "to revolve, move around".
Cognates within Indo-European include <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> <span title="Icelandic-language text"><i lang="is">hjól</i></span> "wheel, tyre", <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">κύκλος</span></span> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization"><i lang="grc-Latn">kúklos</i></span>, and <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <span title="Sanskrit-language romanization"><i lang="sa-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Chakra" title="Chakra">chakra</a></i></span>, the last two both meaning "circle" or "wheel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The place and time of the invention of the wheel remains unclear, because the oldest hints do not guarantee the existence of real wheeled transport, or are dated with too much scatter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a> civilization is credited with the invention of the wheel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, unlike other breakthrough inventions, the wheel cannot be attributed to a single nor several inventors. Evidence of early usage of wheeled carts has been found across the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It is not known whether Chinese and Europeans invented the wheel independently or not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thought_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-thought-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The invention of the solid wooden disk wheel falls into the late <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>, and may be seen in conjunction with other technological advances that gave rise to the early <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. This implies the passage of several wheelless millennia even after the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">invention of agriculture</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aceramic_Neolithic" title="Aceramic Neolithic">Aceramic Neolithic</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>4500–3300 BCE (<a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Copper Age</a>): invention of the <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel" title="Potter's wheel">potter's wheel</a>; earliest solid wooden wheels (disks with a hole for the axle); earliest wheeled vehicles; <a href="/wiki/Domestication_of_the_horse" title="Domestication of the horse">domestication of the horse</a></li>
<li>3300–2200 BCE (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a>)</li>
<li>2200–1550 BCE (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age" title="Middle Bronze Age">Middle Bronze Age</a>): invention of the <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spoked</a> wheel and the <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariot</a></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_(oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3448" data-file-width="4504" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_%28oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered%29.jpg/220px-Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_%28oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_%28oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered%29.jpg/330px-Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_%28oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_%28oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered%29.jpg/440px-Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_%28oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel_with_axle_(oldest_wooden_wheel_yet_discovered).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana_Marshes_Wheel" title="Ljubljana Marshes Wheel">Ljubljana Marshes Wheel</a> with axle is the oldest wooden wheel yet discovered dating to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_Age" title="Copper Age">Copper Age</a> (c. 3,130 BCE)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halaf" title="Halaf">Halaf</a> culture of 6500–5100 BCE is sometimes credited with the earliest depiction of a wheeled vehicle, but this is doubtful as there is no evidence of Halafians using either wheeled vehicles or even pottery wheels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Precursors of pottery wheels, known as "tournettes" or "slow wheels", were known in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> by the 5th millennium BCE. One of the earliest examples was discovered at Tepe Pardis, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, and dated to 5200–4700 BCE. These were made of stone or clay and secured to the ground with a peg in the center, but required significant effort to turn. True potter's wheels, which are freely-spinning and have a <a href="/wiki/Wheel_and_axle" title="Wheel and axle">wheel and axle</a> mechanism, were developed in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>) by 4200–4000 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The oldest surviving example, which was found in <a href="/wiki/Ur" title="Ur">Ur</a> (modern day <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>), dates to approximately 3100 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moorey1994_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moorey1994-11">[11]</a></sup> Wheels of uncertain dates have also been found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" title="Indus Valley civilization">Indus Valley civilization</a>, a 4th millennium BCE civilization covering areas of present-day India and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The oldest indirect evidence of wheeled movement was found in the form of miniature clay wheels north of the Black Sea before 4000 BCE. From the middle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4th_millennium_BCE" title="4th millennium BCE">4th millennium BCE</a> onward, the evidence is condensed throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in the form of toy cars, depictions, or ruts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, depictions of wheeled <a href="/wiki/Wagon" title="Wagon">wagons</a> found on <a href="/wiki/Clay_tablet" title="Clay tablet">clay tablet</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictographs" title="Pictographs">pictographs</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Uruk#Eanna_district" title="Uruk">Eanna district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uruk" title="Uruk">Uruk</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> civilization are dated to c. 3500–3350 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the second half of the 4th millennium BCE, evidence of wheeled vehicles appeared near-simultaneously in the Northern (<a href="/wiki/Maykop_culture" title="Maykop culture">Maykop culture</a>) and South <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cucuteni-Trypillian_culture" title="Cucuteni-Trypillian culture">Cucuteni-Trypillian culture</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ur_chariot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1356" data-file-width="2660" decoding="async" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Ur_chariot.jpg/220px-Ur_chariot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Ur_chariot.jpg/330px-Ur_chariot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Ur_chariot.jpg/440px-Ur_chariot.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ur_chariot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A depiction of an <a href="/wiki/Onager" title="Onager">onager</a>-drawn cart on the <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> "War" panel of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_Ur" title="Standard of Ur">Standard of Ur</a> (c. 2500 BCE)</div></div></div>
<p>Depictions of a wheeled vehicle appeared between 3631 and 3380 BCE in the <a href="/wiki/Bronocice_pot" title="Bronocice pot">Bronocice clay pot</a> excavated in a <a href="/wiki/Funnelbeaker_culture" title="Funnelbeaker culture">Funnelbeaker culture</a> settlement in southern <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In nearby <a href="/wiki/Zwierzyniec_(Krak%C3%B3w)" title="Zwierzyniec (Kraków)">Olszanica</a>, a 2.2 m wide door was constructed for wagon entry; this barn was 40 m long with three doors, dated to 5000 B.C.E—7000 years old, and belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Linear_Pottery_culture" title="Linear Pottery culture">Linear Pottery culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Surviving evidence of a wheel-axle combination, from Stare Gmajne near Ljubljana in Slovenia (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ljubljana_Marshes_Wooden_Wheel" title="Ljubljana Marshes Wooden Wheel">Ljubljana Marshes Wooden Wheel</a>), is dated within two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_deviations" title="Standard deviations">standard deviations</a> to 3340–3030 BCE, the axle to 3360–3045 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Two types of early Neolithic European wheel and axle are known; a <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_pile_dwellings_around_the_Alps" title="Prehistoric pile dwellings around the Alps">circumalpine</a> type of wagon construction (the wheel and axle rotate together, as in Ljubljana Marshes Wheel), and that of the <a href="/wiki/Baden_culture" title="Baden culture">Baden culture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> (axle does not rotate). They both are dated to c. 3200–3000 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Some historians believe that there was a diffusion of the wheeled vehicle from the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> to Europe around the mid-4th millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_(2507526057).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_%282507526057%29.jpg/220px-India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_%282507526057%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_%282507526057%29.jpg/330px-India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_%282507526057%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_%282507526057%29.jpg/440px-India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_%282507526057%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:India_-_Kanchipuram_-_023_-_chariot_unveiled_for_upcoming_festival_(2507526057).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solid wheels on a heavy <a href="/wiki/Temple_car" title="Temple car">temple car</a>, contrasted with the lighter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wire-spoked_wheel" title="Wire-spoked wheel">wire-spoked wheels</a> of the black <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(bicycle)" title="Roadster (bicycle)">roadster bicycle</a> in the foreground</div></div></div>
<p>Early wheels were simple wooden disks with a hole for the axle. Some of the earliest wheels were made from horizontal slices of tree trunks. Because of the uneven structure of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, a wheel made from a horizontal slice of a tree trunk will tend to be inferior to one made from rounded pieces of longitudinal boards.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spoked</a> wheel was invented more recently and allowed the construction of lighter and swifter vehicles. The earliest known examples of wooden spoked wheels are in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Sintashta_culture" title="Sintashta culture">Sintashta culture</a>, dating to c. 2000 BCE (<a href="/wiki/Krivoye_Lake" title="Krivoye Lake">Krivoye Lake</a>). Soon after this, horse cultures of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region used horse-drawn spoked-wheel war <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chariots" title="Chariots">chariots</a> for the greater part of three centuries. They moved deep into the Greek peninsula where they joined with the existing Mediterranean peoples to give rise, eventually, to classical Greece after the breaking of <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> dominance and consolidations led by pre-classical <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celt" title="Celt">Celtic</a> chariots introduced an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> rim around the wheel in the 1st millennium BCE.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, wheel tracks dating to around 2200 BCE have been found at Pingliangtai, a site of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longshan_Culture" title="Longshan Culture">Longshan Culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Similar tracks were also found at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yanshi" title="Yanshi">Yanshi</a>, a city of the <a href="/wiki/Erlitou_culture" title="Erlitou culture">Erlitou culture</a>, dating to around 1700 BCE. The earliest evidence of spoked wheels in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> comes from <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, in the form of two wheel hubs from a site dated between 2000 and 1500 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Britain, a large wooden wheel, measuring about 1 m (3.3 ft) in diameter, was uncovered at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Must_Farm" title="Must Farm">Must Farm</a> site in East Anglia in 2016. The specimen, dating from 1,100 to 800 BCE, represents the most complete and earliest of its type found in Britain. The wheel's hub is also present. A horse's spine found nearby suggests the wheel may have been part of a horse-drawn cart. The wheel was found in a settlement built on stilts over wetland, indicating that the settlement had some sort of link to dry land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMF2016_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCMF2016-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1515" data-file-width="1662" decoding="async" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg/220px-Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg/330px-Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg/440px-Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Remojadas_Wheeled_Figurine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A figurine featuring the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>'s independently invented wheel. Among the places where wheeled toys were found, <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a> is the only one where the wheel was never put to practical use before the 16th century.</div></div></div>
<p>Although large-scale use of wheels did not occur in <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">the Americas prior to European contact</a>, numerous small wheeled artifacts, identified as children's toys, have been found in Mexican archeological sites, some dating to approximately 1500 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Some argue that the primary obstacle to large-scale development of the wheel in the Americas was the absence of domesticated large animals that could be used to pull wheeled carriages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The closest relative of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> present in Americas in pre-Columbian times, the <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">American bison</a>, is difficult to domesticate and was never domesticated by Native Americans; several horse species existed until about 12,000 years ago, but ultimately became extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The only large animal that was domesticated in the Western hemisphere, the <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llama</a>, a pack animal, was not physically suited to use as a draft animal to pull wheeled vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and use of the llama did not spread far beyond the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> by the time of the arrival of Europeans.
</p><p>On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamericans</a> never developed the <a href="/wiki/Wheelbarrow" title="Wheelbarrow">wheelbarrow</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel" title="Potter's wheel">potter's wheel</a>, nor any other practical object with a wheel or wheels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.com_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.com-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith-28">[28]</a></sup> Although present in a number of toys, very similar to those found throughout the world and still made for children today ("pull toys"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.com_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.com-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith-28">[28]</a></sup> the wheel was never put into practical use in Mesoamerica before the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.com_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.com-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-smith-28">[28]</a></sup> Possibly the closest the Mayas came to the utilitarian wheel is the <a href="/wiki/Spindle_whorl" title="Spindle whorl">spindle whorl</a>, and some scholars believe that these toys were originally made with spindle whorls and spindle sticks as "wheels" and "axes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-smith-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">Aboriginal Australians</a> traditionally used circular discs rolled along the ground for use as target practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubians</a> from after about 400 BCE used wheels for spinning <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> and as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_wheels" title="Water wheels">water wheels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It is thought that Nubian waterwheels may have been ox-driven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It is also known that Nubians used horse-drawn chariots imported from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Starting from the 18th century in West Africa, wheeled vehicles were mostly used for ceremonial purposes in places like <a href="/wiki/Dahomey" title="Dahomey">Dahomey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law_wheel_Africa_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Law_wheel_Africa-33">[33]</a></sup> The wheel was barely used for transportation, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> well into the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law_wheel_Africa_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Law_wheel_Africa-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TricycleAntique.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/TricycleAntique.jpg/220px-TricycleAntique.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/TricycleAntique.jpg/330px-TricycleAntique.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/TricycleAntique.jpg/440px-TricycleAntique.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TricycleAntique.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three spoked wheels on an antique <a href="/wiki/Tricycle" title="Tricycle">tricycle</a></div></div></div><p>The spoked wheel was in continued use without major modification until the 1870s, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wire-spoked_wheel" title="Wire-spoked wheel">wire-spoked wheels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic_tire" title="Pneumatic tire">pneumatic tires</a> were invented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Pneumatic tires can greatly reduce rolling resistance and improve comfort.  Wire spokes are under tension, not compression, making it possible for the wheel to be both stiff and light. Early radially-spoked wire wheels gave rise to tangentially-spoked wire wheels, which were widely used on cars into the late 20th century. Cast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloy_wheels" title="Alloy wheels">alloy wheels</a> are now more commonly used; forged alloy wheels are used when weight is critical.
</p><p>The invention of the wheel has also been important for <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> in general, important applications including the <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheel</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cogwheel" title="Cogwheel">cogwheel</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Antikythera_mechanism" title="Antikythera mechanism">antikythera mechanism</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Spinning_wheel" title="Spinning wheel">spinning wheel</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">astrolabe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Torquetum" title="Torquetum">torquetum</a>. More modern descendants of the wheel include the <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscope</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanics_and_function">Mechanics and function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanics and function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This section is about the application to transport. For the simple machine, see <a href="/wiki/Wheel_and_axle" title="Wheel and axle">Wheel and axle</a>.</div>
<p>A wheeled vehicle requires much less work to move than simply dragging the same weight. The low resistance to motion is explained by the fact that the <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">frictional work</a> done is no longer at the surface that the vehicle is traversing, but in the <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a>. In the simplest and oldest case the bearing is just a round hole through which the axle passes (a "<a href="/wiki/Plain_bearing" title="Plain bearing">plain bearing</a>").  Even with a plain bearing, the frictional work is greatly reduced because:
</p>
<ul><li>The normal force at the sliding interface is same as with simple dragging.</li>
<li>The sliding distance is reduced for a given distance of travel.</li>
<li>The coefficient of friction at the interface is usually lower.</li></ul>
<p>Example:
</p>
<ul><li>If a 100 kg object is dragged for 10 m along a surface with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_friction" title="Coefficient of friction">coefficient of friction</a> <i>μ</i> = 0.5, the <a href="/wiki/Normal_force" title="Normal force">normal force</a> is 981 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">N</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">work</a> done (required <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>) is (work=force x distance) 981 × 0.5 × 10 = 4905 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a>.</li>
<li>Now give the object 4 wheels. The normal force between the 4 wheels and axles is the same (in total) 981 N. Assume, for wood, <i>μ</i> = 0.25, and say the wheel <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> is 1000 mm and axle diameter is 50 mm. So while the object still moves 10 m the sliding frictional surfaces only slide over each other a distance of 0.5 m. The work done is 981 × 0.25 × 0.5 = 123 joules; the work done has reduced to 1/40 of that of dragging.</li></ul>
<p>Additional energy is lost from the wheel-to-road interface. This is termed <a href="/wiki/Rolling_resistance" title="Rolling resistance">rolling resistance</a> which is predominantly a deformation loss. It depends on the nature of the ground, of the material of the wheel, its inflation in the case of a tire, the net torque exerted by the eventual engine, and many other factors.
</p><p>A wheel can also offer advantages in traversing irregular surfaces if the wheel radius is sufficiently large compared to the irregularities.
</p><p>The wheel alone is not a machine, but when attached to an <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> in conjunction with bearing, it forms the <a href="/wiki/Wheel_and_axle" title="Wheel and axle">wheel and axle</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Simple_machine" title="Simple machine">simple machines</a>. A driven wheel is an example of a wheel and axle. Wheels pre-date driven wheels by about 6000 years, themselves an evolution of using round logs as rollers to move a heavy load—a practice going back in pre-history so far that it has not been dated.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rim">Rim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rim_(wheel)" title="Rim (wheel)">Rim (wheel)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="2213" decoding="async" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg/220px-AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg/330px-AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg/440px-AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AluminumWheel_spoke_design.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminum</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy_wheel" title="Alloy wheel">alloy wheel</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>rim</b> is the "outer edge of a wheel, holding the tire".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It makes up the outer circular design of the wheel on which the inside edge of the <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tire</a> is mounted on vehicles such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>. For example, on a <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_wheel" title="Bicycle wheel">bicycle wheel</a> the <a href="/wiki/Rim_(wheel)" title="Rim (wheel)">rim</a> is a large hoop attached to the outer ends of the spokes of the wheel that holds the tire and tube.
</p><p>In the 1st millennium BCE an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> rim was introduced around the wooden wheels of <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariots</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hub">Hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hub is the center of the wheel, and typically houses a <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearing</a>, and is where the spokes meet.
</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubless_wheel" title="Hubless wheel">hubless wheel</a> (also known as a rim-rider or centerless wheel) is a type of wheel with no center <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hub" title="wikt:hub">hub</a>. More specifically, the hub is actually almost as big as the wheel itself. The <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> is hollow, following the wheel at very close <a href="/wiki/Engineering_tolerance" title="Engineering tolerance">tolerances</a>.
</p>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spoke</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wheel_Iran.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="275" decoding="async" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Wheel_Iran.jpg/220px-Wheel_Iran.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Wheel_Iran.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wheel_Iran.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A spoked wheel on display at The <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Iran" title="National Museum of Iran">National Museum of Iran</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>. The wheel is dated to the late 2nd millennium BCE and was excavated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choqa_Zanbil" title="Choqa Zanbil">Choqa Zanbil</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>A <i>spoke</i> is one of some number of rods radiating from the center of a wheel (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycle_hub" title="Bicycle hub">hub</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> connects), connecting the hub with the round traction surface. The term originally referred to portions of a log which had been split lengthwise into four or six sections. The radial members of a wagon wheel were made by carving a spoke (from a log) into their finished shape. A <a href="/wiki/Spokeshave" title="Spokeshave">spokeshave</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a> originally developed for this purpose. Eventually, the term spoke was more commonly applied to the finished product of the <a href="/wiki/Wheelwright" title="Wheelwright">wheelwright</a>'s work, than to the materials used.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wire">Wire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wire_wheel" title="Wire wheel">wire wheel</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rim_(wheel)" title="Rim (wheel)">rims</a> of <i>wire wheels</i> (or "wire spoked wheels") are connected to their hubs by wire <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spokes</a>. Although these <a href="/wiki/Wire" title="Wire">wires</a> are generally stiffer than a typical <a href="/wiki/Wire_rope" title="Wire rope">wire rope</a>, they function mechanically the same as <a href="/wiki/Tension_(physics)" title="Tension (physics)">tensioned</a> flexible wires, keeping the rim true while supporting applied loads.
</p><p>Wire wheels are used on most <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycles</a> and still used on many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycles" title="Motorcycles">motorcycles</a>. They were invented by aeronautical engineer <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a> and first used in bicycles by <a href="/wiki/James_Starley" title="James Starley">James Starley</a>. A process of assembling wire wheels is described as <a href="/wiki/Wheelbuilding" title="Wheelbuilding">wheelbuilding</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Tire.2FTyre"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tire/Tyre">Tire/Tyre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tire/Tyre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tire</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycle_tire" title="Motorcycle tire">motorcycle tire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_tire" title="Bicycle tire">bicycle tire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BMW_tire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3804" data-file-width="3852" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BMW_tire.jpg/220px-BMW_tire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BMW_tire.jpg/330px-BMW_tire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BMW_tire.jpg/440px-BMW_tire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BMW_tire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A wheel with car tire made by <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> company</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>tire</b> (in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian English</a>) or <b>tyre</b> (in some <a href="/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="English in the Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth Nations</a> such as UK, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>) is a ring-shaped covering that fits around a wheel <a href="/wiki/Rim_(wheel)" title="Rim (wheel)">rim</a> to protect it and enable better vehicle performance by providing a flexible cushion that absorbs shock while keeping the wheel in close contact with the ground. The word itself may be derived from the word "tie", which refers to the outer steel ring part of a wooden cart wheel that ties the wood segments together (see <a href="#Etymology">Etymology</a> above).
</p><p>The fundamental materials of modern tires are <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">synthetic rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">natural rubber</a>, fabric, and wire, along with other compound chemicals. They consist of a tread and a body. The tread provides <a href="/wiki/Traction_(engineering)" title="Traction (engineering)">traction</a> while the body ensures support. Before rubber was invented, the first versions of tires were simply bands of metal that fitted around wooden wheels to prevent wear and tear. Today, the vast majority of tires are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic" title="Pneumatic">pneumatic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflatable_structure" title="Inflatable structure">inflatable structures</a>, comprising a doughnut-shaped body of cords and wires encased in rubber and generally filled with compressed air to form an inflatable cushion. Pneumatic tires are used on many types of vehicles, such as <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycles" title="Motorcycles">motorcycles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">trucks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_equipment_(construction)" title="Heavy equipment (construction)">earthmovers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protruding_or_covering_attachments">Protruding or covering attachments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Protruding or covering attachments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Extreme <a href="/wiki/Off-roading" title="Off-roading">off-road</a> conditions have resulted in the invention of several types of wheel cover, which may be constructed as removable attachments or as permanent covers. Wheels like this are no longer necessarily round, or have panels that make the ground-contact area flat.
</p><p>Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Snow_chains" title="Snow chains">Snow chains</a> - Specially designed chain assemblies that wrap around the tire to provide increased grip, designed for deep snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dreadnaught_wheel" title="Dreadnaught wheel">Dreadnaught wheel</a> - A type of permanently attached hinged panels for general extreme off-road use. These are not connected directly to the wheels, but to each other.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pedrail_wheel" title="Pedrail wheel">Pedrail wheel</a> - A system of rails that holds panels that hold the vehicle. These do not necessarily have to be built as a circle (wheel) and are thus also a form of <a href="/wiki/Continuous_track" title="Continuous track">Continuous track</a>.</li>
<li>A version of the above examples (name unknown to the writer) was commonly used on heavy <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Specific examples: <a href="/wiki/Cannone_da_149/35_A" title="Cannone da 149/35 A">Cannone da 149/35 A</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bertha_(howitzer)" title="Big Bertha (howitzer)">Big Bertha</a>. These were panels that were connected to each other by multiple hinges and could be installed over a contemporary wheel.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Continuous_track" title="Continuous track">Continuous track</a> - A system of linked and hinged chains/panels that cover multiple wheels in a way that allows the vehicles mass to be distributed across the space between wheels that are positioned in front of / behind other wheels.</li>
<li>"Tire totes" - A bag designed to cover a tire to improve traction in deep snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Truck and bus wheels may block (stop rotating) under certain circumstances, such as brake system failure. To help detect this, they sometimes feature "wheel rotation indicators": colored strips of plastic attached to the rim and protruding out from it, such that they can be seen by the driver in the <a href="/wiki/Side-view_mirror" title="Side-view mirror">side-view mirrors</a>. These devices were invented and patented in 1998 by a Canadian truck shop owner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">Symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="798" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg/300px-Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg/450px-Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg/600px-Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Wheel_of_time" title="Wheel of time">wheel of time</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The wheel has also become a strong cultural and spiritual metaphor for a cycle or regular repetition (see <a href="/wiki/Chakra" title="Chakra">chakra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yin_and_yang" title="Yin and yang">Yin and Yang</a> among others). As such and because of the difficult terrain, wheeled vehicles were forbidden in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Tibet" title="History of Tibet">old Tibet</a>. The wheel in ancient <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> is seen as a symbol of health and strength and utilized by some villages as a tool to predict future health and success. The <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> of the wheel is indicator of one's future health. The <a href="/wiki/Kalachakra" title="Kalachakra">Kalachakra</a> or wheel of time is also a subject in some forms of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Dharmachakra" title="Dharmachakra">dharmachakra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Winged_wheel" title="Winged wheel">winged wheel</a> is a symbol of progress, seen in many contexts including the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Panama" title="Coat of arms of Panama">coat of arms of Panama</a>, the logo of the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_Highway_Patrol" title="Ohio State Highway Patrol">Ohio State Highway Patrol</a> and the <a href="/wiki/State_Railway_of_Thailand" title="State Railway of Thailand">State Railway of Thailand</a>. The wheel is also the prominent figure on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_India" title="Flag of India">flag of India</a>. The wheel in this case represents law (<a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a>). It also appears in the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Romani_people" title="Flag of the Romani people">flag of the Romani people</a>, hinting to their nomadic history and their Indian origins.
</p><p>
The introduction of spoked (<a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariot</a>) wheels in the Middle Bronze Age appears to have carried somewhat of a prestige. The <a href="/wiki/Sun_cross" title="Sun cross">sun cross</a> appears to have a significance in <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_religion_(disambiguation)" title="Bronze Age religion (disambiguation)">Bronze Age religion</a>, replacing the earlier concept of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_barge" title="Solar barge">solar barge</a> with the more 'modern' and technologically advanced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_chariot" title="Solar chariot">solar chariot</a>. The wheel was also a solar symbol for the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>In modern usage, the 'invention of the wheel' can be considered as a symbol of one of the first technologies of early civilization, alongside farming and metalwork, and thus be used as a benchmark to grade the level of societal progress.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Paganism" title="Modern Paganism">Neopagans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wiccans</a> have adopted the <a href="/wiki/Wheel_of_the_Year" title="Wheel of the Year">Wheel of the Year</a> into their religious practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
