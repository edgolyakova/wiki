<p>A <b>spice</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bark_(botany)" title="Bark (botany)">bark</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> substance primarily used for <a href="/wiki/Flavoring" title="Flavoring">flavoring</a> or <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">coloring</a> food. Spices are distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a>, which are the leaves, flowers, or stems of plants used for <a href="/wiki/Flavoring" title="Flavoring">flavoring</a> or as a <a href="/wiki/Garnish_(food)" title="Garnish (food)">garnish</a>. Spices are sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_rite" title="Sacred rite">religious rituals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfume</a> production. For example, <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a> is commonly used as an ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Aroma_compound" title="Aroma compound">fragrance</a> manufacturing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>A spice may be available in several forms: fresh, whole dried, or pre-ground dried. Generally, spices are dried. Spices may be ground into a powder for convenience. A whole dried spice has the longest shelf life, so it can be purchased and stored in larger amounts, making it cheaper on a per-serving basis. A fresh spice, such as <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a>, is usually more flavorful than its dried form, but fresh spices are more expensive and have a much shorter shelf life. Some spices are not always available either fresh or whole, for example <a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">turmeric</a>, and often must be purchased in ground form. Small seeds, such as fennel and mustard seeds, are often used both whole and in powder form.
</p><p>Although health benefits are often claimed for spices, there is currently not enough research conducted to prove these benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> contributes to 75% of global spice production. This is reflected culturally through their <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">cuisine</a>; historically, the <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a> developed throughout the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and the Middle East. Europe's demand for spices was among the economic and cultural factors that encouraged exploration in the early modern period.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>spice</i> originated in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup> which came from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> words <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">espece</i></span><i>, <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">espis(c)e</i></span>,</i> and <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">espis(c)e</i></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[4]</a></sup> According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Middle_English_Dictionary" title="Middle English Dictionary">Middle English Dictionary</a></i>, the Old French words came from <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-French</a> <i>spece</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[4]</a></sup> according to <a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam Webster</a>, the Old-French words came from Anglo-French <i>espece,</i> and <i>espis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup> Both publications agree that the Anglo-French words derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>species</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <span title="Middle English (1100-1500)-language text"><i lang="enm">spice</i></span> had its first known use as a noun in the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">Spice trade</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The spice trade developed throughout the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sidebotham2019_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sidebotham2019-5">[5]</a></sup> by at earliest 2000 BCE with <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> with herbs and pepper. The Egyptians used herbs for <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_funerary_practices" title="Ancient Egyptian funerary practices">mummification</a> and their demand for exotic spices and herbs helped stimulate world trade. By 1000 BCE, medical systems based upon herbs could be found in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. Early uses were connected with magic, medicine, religion, tradition, and preservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCp14_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABCp14-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Clove" title="Clove">Cloves</a> were used in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> by 1700 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[note 1]</a></sup> The ancient Indian <a href="/wiki/Indian_epic_poetry" title="Indian epic poetry">epic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a> mentions cloves. The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> had cloves in the 1st century CE, as <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> wrote about them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duke_2002_p._7_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duke_2002_p._7-11">[10]</a></sup> The earliest written records of spices come from ancient Egyptian, Chinese, and Indian cultures. The <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a> from early Egypt dating from 1550 <small>B.C.E.</small> describes some eight hundred different medicinal remedies and numerous medicinal procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Historians believe that <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a>, which originates from the <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda Islands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, was introduced to Europe in the 6th century BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indonesian merchants traveled around China, India, the Middle East, and the east coast of Africa. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> merchants facilitated the routes through the Middle East and India. This resulted in the Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_city" title="Port city">port city</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> being the main trading center for spices. The most important discovery prior to the European spice trade was the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> winds (40 CE). Sailing from Eastern spice cultivators to Western European consumers gradually replaced the land-locked spice routes once facilitated by the Middle East Arab caravans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCp14_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABCp14-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Spices were prominent enough in the ancient world that they are mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genesis_(Old_Testament)" title="Genesis (Old Testament)">Genesis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_(Hebrew_Bible)" title="Joseph (Hebrew Bible)">Joseph</a> was sold into slavery by his brothers to spice merchants. In <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manna" title="Manna">manna</a> is described as being similar to coriander in appearance. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_of_Solomon" title="Song of Solomon">Song of Solomon</a>, the male narrator compares his beloved to many saffron, cinnamon, and other spices.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1382" data-file-width="2252" decoding="async" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg/220px-Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg/330px-Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg/440px-Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Le_livre_des_merveilles_de_Marco_Polo-pepper.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"The Mullus" harvesting pepper. Illustration from a French edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Travels_of_Marco_Polo" title="The Travels of Marco Polo">The Travels of Marco Polo</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Spices were among the most demanded and expensive products available in Europe in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>,<sup><a href="/wiki/Spice#cite_note-5" title="Spice">[5]</a></sup> the most common being <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a> (and the cheaper alternative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinnamomum_aromaticum" title="Cinnamomum aromaticum">cassia</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cumin" title="Cumin">cumin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloves" title="Cloves">cloves</a>. Given medieval medicine's main theory of <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">humorism</a>, spices and herbs were indispensable to balance "humors" in food,<sup><a href="/wiki/Spice#cite_note-6" title="Spice">[6]</a></sup> a daily basis for good health at a time of recurrent <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemics</a>. In addition to being desired by those using <a href="/wiki/Medieval_medicine_of_Western_Europe" title="Medieval medicine of Western Europe">medieval medicine</a>, the European elite also craved spices in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. An example of the European aristocracy's demand for spice comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Aragon" title="King of Aragon">King of Aragon</a>, who invested substantial resources into bringing back spices to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in the 12th century. He was specifically looking for spices to put in <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, and was not alone among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Monarchs" title="European Monarchs">European monarchs</a> at the time to have such a desire for spice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Spices were all imported from plantations in Asia and Africa, which made them expensive. From the 8th until the 15th century, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a> held a monopoly on spice trade with the Middle East, using this position to dominate the neighboring Italian <a href="/wiki/Maritime_republics" title="Maritime republics">maritime republics</a> and city-states. The trade made the region rich. It has been estimated that around 1,000  tons of pepper and 1,000  tons of the other common spices were imported into Western Europe each year during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>. The value of these goods was the equivalent of a yearly supply of grain for 1.5 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> The most exclusive was <a href="/wiki/Saffron" title="Saffron">saffron</a>, used as much for its vivid yellow-red color as for its flavor. Spices that have now fallen into obscurity in European cuisine include <a href="/wiki/Aframomum_melegueta" title="Aframomum melegueta">grains of paradise</a>, a relative of <a href="/wiki/Cardamom" title="Cardamom">cardamom</a> which mostly replaced pepper in late medieval north French cooking, <a href="/wiki/Long_pepper" title="Long pepper">long pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">mace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spikenard" title="Spikenard">spikenard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galangal" title="Galangal">galangal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubeb" title="Cubeb">cubeb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> were interested in seeking new routes to trade in spices and other valuable products from Asia. The control of trade routes and the spice-producing regions were the main reasons that <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> navigator <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> sailed to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in 1499.<sup><a href="/wiki/Spice#cite_note-8" title="Spice">[8]</a></sup> When da Gama discovered the pepper market in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, he was able to secure peppers for a much cheaper price than the ones demanded by <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[13]</a></sup> At around the same time, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> returned from the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. He described to <a href="/wiki/Investor" title="Investor">investors</a> new spices available there.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Another source of competition in the spice trade during the 15th and 16th century was the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa" title="Republic of Ragusa">Ragusans</a> from the maritime republic of <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a> in southern Croatia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> The military prowess of <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> (1453–1515) allowed the Portuguese to take control of the sea routes to India. In 1506, he took the island of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a> in the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and, in 1507, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ormuz" title="Ormuz">Ormuz</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. Since becoming the <a href="/wiki/Viceroy" title="Viceroy">viceroy</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indies" title="Indies">Indies</a>, he took <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a> in India in 1510, and <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malay_peninsula" title="Malay peninsula">Malay peninsula</a> in 1511. The Portuguese could now trade directly with <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Siam</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Maluku Islands</a>.
</p><p>With the discovery of the New World came new spices, including <a href="/wiki/Allspice" title="Allspice">allspice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>. This development kept the spice trade, with America as a latecomer with its new seasonings, profitable well into the 19th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Common_Indian_spices.jpg"><img alt="Chili powder, mustard seeds, turmeric powder, cumin seeds" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Common_Indian_spices.jpg/220px-Common_Indian_spices.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Common_Indian_spices.jpg/330px-Common_Indian_spices.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Common_Indian_spices.jpg/440px-Common_Indian_spices.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Common_Indian_spices.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Turmeric powder, mustard seeds, chili powder, cumin seeds</div></div></div>
<p>Spices are primarily used as food <a href="/wiki/Flavoring" title="Flavoring">flavoring</a> or to create variety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[17]</a></sup> They are also used to perfume <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Incense" title="Incense">incense</a>. At various periods, many spices have been believed to have medicinal value. Finally, since they are expensive, rare, and exotic commodities, their <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">conspicuous consumption</a> has often been a symbol of wealth and social class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservative_claim">Preservative claim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Preservative claim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1062260506">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:35%; ;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>The most popular explanation for the love of spices in the Middle Ages is that they were used to preserve meat from spoiling, or to cover up the taste of meat that had already gone off. This compelling but false idea constitutes something of an urban legend, a story so instinctively attractive that mere fact seems unable to wipe it out... Anyone who could afford spices could easily find meat fresher than what city dwellers today buy in their local supermarket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
</div>
<p>It is often claimed that spices were used either as <a href="/wiki/Food_preservation" title="Food preservation">food preservatives</a> or to mask the taste of <a href="/wiki/Meat_spoilage" title="Meat spoilage">spoiled meat</a>, especially in the European <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ThomasDaoust2012_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ThomasDaoust2012-19">[18]</a></sup> This is false.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup> In fact, spices are rather ineffective as preservatives as compared to <a href="/wiki/Salting_(food)" title="Salting (food)">salting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smoking_(cooking)" title="Smoking (cooking)">smoking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickling</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Food_drying" title="Food drying">drying</a>, and are ineffective in covering the taste of spoiled meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup> Moreover, spices have always been comparatively expensive: in 15th century Oxford, a whole pig cost about the same as a pound of the cheapest spice, pepper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-freedman-16">[15]</a></sup> There is also no evidence of such use from contemporary cookbooks: "Old cookbooks make it clear that spices weren't used as a preservative. They typically suggest adding spices toward the end of the cooking process, where they could have no preservative effect whatsoever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krondl_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-krondl-23">[22]</a></sup> Indeed, <a href="/wiki/Cristoforo_di_Messisbugo" title="Cristoforo di Messisbugo">Cristoforo di Messisbugo</a> suggested in the 16th century that pepper may speed up spoilage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krondl_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-krondl-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though some spices have <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial" title="Antimicrobial">antimicrobial</a> properties in vitro,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> pepper—by far the most common spice—is relatively ineffective, and in any case, salt, which is far cheaper, is also far more effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krondl_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-krondl-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Handling">Handling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Handling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spice-shelf.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="738" data-file-width="982" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Spice-shelf.jpg/220px-Spice-shelf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Spice-shelf.jpg/330px-Spice-shelf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Spice-shelf.jpg/440px-Spice-shelf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spice-shelf.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A shelf of common spices for a home kitchen in Canada or the United States</div></div></div>
<p><span class="anchor" id="Ground_spices"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3878" data-file-width="2589" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg/110px-Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg/165px-Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg/220px-Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peugeot_pepper_mill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pepper mill</div></div></div>
<p>For ground spices, to grind a whole spice, the classic tool is <a href="/wiki/Mortar_and_pestle" title="Mortar and pestle">mortar and pestle</a>. Less labor-intensive tools are more common now: a <a href="/wiki/Microplane" title="Microplane">microplane</a> or fine <a href="/wiki/Grater" title="Grater">grater</a> can be used to grind small amounts; a <a href="/wiki/Blade_grinder" title="Blade grinder">coffee grinder</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[note 2]</a></sup> is useful for larger amounts. A frequently used spice such as black pepper may merit storage in its own hand grinder or <a href="/wiki/Burr_mill#Manual_burr_grinders" title="Burr mill">mill</a>.
</p><p>The flavor of a spice is derived in part from compounds (volatile oils) that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidize" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a> or evaporate when exposed to air. Grinding a spice greatly increases its surface area and so increases the rates of oxidation and evaporation. Thus, the flavor is maximized by storing a spice whole and grinding when needed. The shelf life of a whole dry spice is roughly two years; of a ground spice roughly six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GE714_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-GE714-26">[24]</a></sup> The "flavor life" of a ground spice can be much shorter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[note 3]</a></sup> Ground spices are better stored away from light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[note 4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some flavor elements in spices are soluble in water; many are soluble in oil or fat. As a general rule, the flavors from a spice take time to infuse into the food so spices are added early in preparation. This contrasts to <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a> which are usually added late in preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GE714_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-GE714-26">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salmonella_contamination">Salmonella contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Salmonella contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> of shipments of spices to the United States during fiscal years 2007–2009 showed about 7% of the shipments were contaminated by <a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a> bacteria, some of it antibiotic-resistant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[25]</a></sup> As most spices are cooked before being served salmonella contamination often has no effect, but some spices, particularly pepper, are often eaten raw and present at table for convenient use. Shipments from Mexico and India, a major producer, were the most frequently contaminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT82713_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT82713-30">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Food_irradiation" title="Food irradiation">Food irradiation</a>, is said to minimise this risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_research">Health research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Health research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spices have been claimed to have health effects during both ancient and current times;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[17]</a></sup> for example, in 2017, the <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">Washington Post</a> said spices put "a natural pharmacy in your kitchen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[17]</a></sup> The supposed health benefits include antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects, and benefits towards certain diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-2">[2]</a></sup> A 2019 systematic review looked at studies of 25 spices and concluded that there is not enough evidence yet to support these supposed benefits, and that "further work is needed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-2">[2]</a></sup> The review noted that many studies were conducted by administering capsule or "artificial" forms of the spices, but spices are not normally consumed in this manner, and are normally consumed along other foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-2">[2]</a></sup> Spices may reduce the need for salt as a flavoring agent in dishes, which has cardiovascular benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[29]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization">Standardization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Standardization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> addresses spices and <a href="/wiki/Condiment" title="Condiment">condiments</a>, along with related food additives, as part of the <a href="/wiki/International_Classification_for_Standards" title="International Classification for Standards">International Classification for Standards</a> 67.220 series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Spices_Research" title="Indian Institute of Spices Research">Indian Institute of Spices Research</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Kozhikode</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, is devoted exclusively to conducting research for ten spice crops: black pepper, cardamom, cinnamon, clove, <a href="/wiki/Garcinia" title="Garcinia">garcinia</a>, ginger, nutmeg, paprika, turmeric, and vanilla.
</p>
