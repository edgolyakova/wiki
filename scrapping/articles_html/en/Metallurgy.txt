<p><b>Metallurgy</b> is a domain of <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science and engineering</a> that studies the physical and chemical behavior of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metallic</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a>, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inter-metallic_alloy" title="Inter-metallic alloy">inter-metallic compounds</a>, and their mixtures, which are known as <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>.
Metallurgy encompasses both the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> of metals; that is, the way in which science is applied to the production of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, and the engineering of metal components used in products for both consumers and manufacturers. Metallurgy is distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a> of <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a>. Metalworking relies on metallurgy in a similar manner to how <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> relies on medical science for technical advancement. A specialist <a href="/wiki/Practitioner%E2%80%93scholar_model" title="Practitioner–scholar model">practitioner</a> of metallurgy is known as a <a href="/wiki/Category:Metallurgists" title="Category:Metallurgists">metallurgist</a>. 
</p><p>The science of metallurgy is further subdivided into two broad categories: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_metallurgy" title="Chemical metallurgy">chemical metallurgy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_metallurgy" title="Physical metallurgy">physical metallurgy</a>. Chemical metallurgy is chiefly concerned with the reduction and oxidation of metals, and the chemical performance of metals.  Subjects of study in chemical metallurgy include <a href="/wiki/Mineral_processing" title="Mineral processing">mineral processing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">extraction of metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, and chemical degradation (<a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Physical_metallurgy" title="Physical metallurgy">physical metallurgy</a> focuses on the mechanical properties of metals, the physical properties of metals, and the physical performance of metals. Topics studied in physical metallurgy include <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Characterization_(materials_science)" title="Characterization (materials science)">material characterization</a>, mechanical metallurgy, <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transformations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metallurgical_failure_analysis" title="Metallurgical failure analysis">failure mechanisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, metallurgy has predominately focused on the production of metals. Metal production begins with the processing of <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> to extract the metal, and includes the mixture of metals to make <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>. Metal alloys are often a blend of at least two different metallic elements. However, non-metallic elements are often added to alloys in order to achieve properties suitable for an application. The study of metal production is subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy" title="Ferrous metallurgy">ferrous metallurgy</a> (also known as <i>black metallurgy</i>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-ferrous_metallurgy" title="Non-ferrous metallurgy">non-ferrous metallurgy</a> (also known as <i>colored metallurgy</i>).
Ferrous metallurgy involves processes and alloys based on <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, while non-ferrous metallurgy involves processes and alloys based on other metals. The production of ferrous metals accounts for 95% of world metal production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern metallurgists work in both emerging and traditional areas as part of an interdisciplinary team alongside material scientists and other engineers. Some traditional areas include mineral processing, metal production, heat treatment, <a href="/wiki/Metallurgical_failure_analysis" title="Metallurgical failure analysis">failure analysis</a>, and the joining of metals (including <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazing" title="Brazing">brazing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soldering" title="Soldering">soldering</a>).  Emerging areas for metallurgists include <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomaterial" title="Biomaterial">biomedical materials</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_Materials" title="Electronic Materials">electronic materials</a> (semiconductors) and <a href="/wiki/Surface_engineering" title="Surface engineering">surface engineering</a>. Many applications, practices, and devices associated or involved in metallurgy were established in ancient China, such as the innovation of the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammers</a>, and double acting piston <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temple-r_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-temple-r-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_pronunciation">Etymology and pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Metallurgy</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">μεταλλουργός</span></span>, <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization"><i lang="grc-Latn">metallourgós</i></span>, "worker in metal", from <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">μέταλλον</span></span>, <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization"><i lang="grc-Latn">métallon</i></span>, "mine, metal" + <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">ἔργον</span></span>, <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization"><i lang="grc-Latn">érgon</i></span>, "work" The word was originally an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alchemist" title="Alchemist">alchemist</a>'s term for the extraction of metals from minerals, the ending <i>-urgy</i> signifying a process, especially manufacturing: it was discussed in this sense in the 1797 <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the late 19th century, it was extended to the more general scientific study of metals, alloys, and related processes. In English, the <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> pronunciation is the more common one in the UK and Commonwealth. The <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/əl/: 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> pronunciation is the more common one in the US and is the first-listed variant in various American dictionaries (e.g., <i>Merriam-Webster Collegiate</i>, <i>American Heritage</i>).
</p>

Small amounts of natural gold have been found in Spanish caves dating to the late <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> period, 40,000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">Silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and meteoric <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> can also be found in native form, allowing a limited amount of <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a> in early cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ephotos_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ephotos-8">[8]</a></sup> Certain metals, notably tin, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, and at a higher temperature, copper, can be recovered from their ores by simply heating the rocks in a fire or blast furnace, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a>. The first evidence of this extractive metallurgy, dating from the 5th and 6th millennia BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> has been found at archaeological sites in <a href="/wiki/Majdanpek" title="Majdanpek">Majdanpek</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jarmovac" title="Jarmovac">Jarmovac</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plo%C4%8Dnik_(archeological_site)" title="Pločnik (archeological site)">Pločnik</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> To date, the earliest evidence of copper smelting is found at the Belovode site near Plocnik.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This site produced a copper axe from 5,500 BC, belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_culture" title="Vinča culture">Vinča culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest use of lead is documented from the late <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> settlements of <a href="/wiki/Yarim_Tepe" title="Yarim Tepe">Yarim Tepe</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arpachiyah" title="Arpachiyah">Arpachiyah</a> in Iraq. The artifacts suggest that lead smelting predated copper smelting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potts_2012_p._302_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Potts_2012_p._302-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Copper smelting is also documented at this site at about the same time period (soon after 6,000 BC), although the use of lead seems to precede copper smelting. Early metallurgy is also documented at the nearby site of <a href="/wiki/Tell_Maghzaliyah" title="Tell Maghzaliyah">Tell Maghzaliyah</a>, which seems to be dated even earlier, and completely lacks that pottery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Balkans were the site of major Neolithic cultures, including <a href="/wiki/Butmir_culture" title="Butmir culture">Butmir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da" title="Vinča">Vinča</a>, <a href="/wiki/Varna_culture" title="Varna culture">Varna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karanovo_culture" title="Karanovo culture">Karanovo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hamangia_culture" title="Hamangia culture">Hamangia</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Varna_Necropolis" title="Varna Necropolis">Varna Necropolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, is a burial site in the western industrial zone of <a href="/wiki/Varna,_Bulgaria" title="Varna, Bulgaria">Varna</a> (approximately 4 km from the city centre), internationally considered one of the key archaeological sites in world prehistory. The oldest <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> treasure in the world, dating from 4,600 BC to 4,200 BC, was discovered at the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The gold piece dating from 4,500 BC, recently founded in <a href="/wiki/Durankulak" title="Durankulak">Durankulak</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Varna,_Bulgaria" title="Varna, Bulgaria">Varna</a> is another important example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Other signs of early metals are found from the third millennium BC in places like <a href="/wiki/Palmela" title="Palmela">Palmela</a> (Portugal), <a href="/wiki/Los_Millares" title="Los Millares">Los Millares</a> (Spain), and <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> (United Kingdom). However, the ultimate beginnings cannot be clearly ascertained and new discoveries are both continuous and ongoing.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="693" data-file-width="1249" decoding="async" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg/600px-Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg/900px-Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg/1200px-Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg.png 2x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metal_production_in_Ancient_Middle_East.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mining areas of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Boxes colors: <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> is in brown, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> in red, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> in grey, iron in reddish brown, gold in yellow, silver in white and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> in black. Yellow area stands for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenic_bronze" title="Arsenic bronze">arsenic bronze</a>, while grey area stands for tin <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>.</div></div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Near East</a>, about 3,500 BC, it was discovered that by combining copper and tin, a superior metal could be made, an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> called <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>. This represented a major technological shift known as the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.
</p><p>The extraction of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> from its ore into a workable metal is much more difficult than for copper or tin. The process appears to have been invented by the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> in about 1200 BC, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. The secret of extracting and working iron was a key factor in the success of the <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keller_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-keller-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historical developments in ferrous metallurgy can be found in a wide variety of past cultures and civilizations. This includes the ancient and medieval kingdoms and empires of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>, ancient <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, ancient <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, ancient <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nok_culture" title="Nok culture">Ancient Nok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> of ancient <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, medieval Europe, ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, amongst others. Many applications, practices, and devices associated or involved in metallurgy were established in ancient China, such as the innovation of the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammers</a>, and double acting piston <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temple-r_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-temple-r-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 16th century book by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Agricola" title="Georg Agricola">Georg Agricola</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/De_re_metallica" title="De re metallica">De re metallica</a></i> describes the highly developed and complex processes of mining metal ores, metal extraction and metallurgy of the time. Agricola has been described as the "father of metallurgy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraction">Extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">Extractive metallurgy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="592" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png/220px-Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png/330px-Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png/440px-Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Furnace bellows operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterwheel" title="Waterwheel">waterwheels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a>, China.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">Extractive metallurgy</a> is the practice of removing valuable metals from an <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> and refining the extracted raw metals into a purer form. In order to convert a metal <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulphide</a> to a purer metal, the ore must be <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> physically, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemically</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytically</a>. Extractive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallurgist" title="Metallurgist">metallurgists</a> are interested in three primary streams: feed, concentrate (metal oxide/sulphide) and <a href="/wiki/Tailings" title="Tailings">tailings</a> (waste).
</p><p>After mining, large pieces of the ore feed are broken through crushing or grinding in order to obtain particles small enough, where each particle is either mostly valuable or mostly waste. Concentrating the particles of value in a form supporting separation enables the desired metal to be removed from waste products.
</p><p>Mining may not be necessary, if the ore body and physical environment are conducive to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_leaching" title="In-situ leaching">leaching</a>. Leaching dissolves minerals in an ore body and results in an enriched solution. The solution is collected and processed to extract valuable metals. Ore bodies often contain more than one valuable metal.
</p><p>Tailings of a previous process may be used as a feed in another process to extract a secondary product from the original ore. Additionally, a concentrate may contain more than one valuable metal. That concentrate would then be processed to separate the valuable metals into individual constituents.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_and_its_alloys">Metal and its alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metal and its alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">Metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">Alloy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3197" data-file-width="5135" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/220px-Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/330px-Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/440px-Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iron_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Iron, the most common metal used in metallurgy, is shown in different forms (cube, chips and nuggets)</div></div></div>
<p>Much effort has been placed on understanding <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>–carbon alloy system, which includes <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast irons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">Plain carbon steels</a> (those that contain essentially only carbon as an alloying element) are used in low-cost, high-strength applications, where neither weight nor <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> are a major concern. Cast irons, including <a href="/wiki/Ductile_iron" title="Ductile iron">ductile iron</a>, are also part of the iron-carbon system.  Iron-Manganese-Chromium alloys (Hadfield-type steels) are also used in non-magnetic applications such as directional drilling.
</p><p>Other common engineering <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> include <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. These metals are most often used as alloys with the noted exception of silicon, as it is not a metal.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">Stainless steel</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Austenitic_stainless_steel" title="Austenitic stainless steel">Austenitic stainless steels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galvanized_steel" title="Galvanized steel">galvanized steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Nickel_alloys" title="Category:Nickel alloys">nickel alloys</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanium_alloys" title="Titanium alloys">titanium alloys</a>, or occasionally <a href="/wiki/List_of_copper_alloys" title="List of copper alloys">copper alloys</a> are used, where resistance to corrosion is important.</li>
<li>Aluminium alloys and magnesium alloys are commonly used, when a lightweight strong part is required such as in automotive and aerospace applications.</li>
<li>Copper-nickel alloys (such as <a href="/wiki/Monel" title="Monel">Monel</a>) are used in highly corrosive environments and for non-magnetic applications.</li>
<li>Nickel-based <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> like <a href="/wiki/Inconel" title="Inconel">Inconel</a> are used in high-temperature applications such as <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbochargers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a>.</li>
<li>For extremely high temperatures, <a href="/wiki/Single_crystal" title="Single crystal">single crystal</a> alloys are used to minimize <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a>. In modern electronics, high purity single crystal silicon is essential for <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">metal-oxide-silicon</a> transistors (MOS) and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">production engineering</a>, metallurgy is concerned with the production of metallic components for use in consumer or <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> products. This involves production of alloys, shaping, heat treatment and surface treatment of product. The task of the metallurgist is to achieve balance between material properties, such as cost, <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">strength</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">toughness</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardness_(materials_science)" title="Hardness (materials science)">hardness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a> resistance and performance in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> extremes. To achieve this goal, the operating environment must be carefully considered.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Determining the hardness of the metal using the Rockwell, Vickers, and Brinell hardness scales is a commonly used practice that helps better understand the metal's elasticity and plasticity for different applications and production processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In a saltwater environment, most ferrous metals and some non-ferrous alloys corrode quickly. Metals exposed to cold or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> conditions may undergo a ductile to brittle transition and lose their toughness, becoming more brittle and prone to cracking. Metals under continual cyclic loading can suffer from <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">metal fatigue</a>. Metals under constant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> at elevated temperatures can <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metalworking_processes">Metalworking processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metalworking processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">Metalworking</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg/220px-Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg/330px-Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg/440px-Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bochumer_Verein-08-50124.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Open-die drop forging (with two dies) of an ingot to be further processed into a wheel</div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Casting_(metalworking)" title="Casting (metalworking)">Casting</a> – molten metal is poured into a shaped <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">mold</a>. Variants of casting include <a href="/wiki/Sand_casting" title="Sand casting">sand casting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Investment_casting" title="Investment casting">investment casting</a> (also called the lost wax process), <a href="/wiki/Die_casting" title="Die casting">die casting</a>, and continuous castings. Each of these forms has advantages for certain metals and applications considering factors like magnetism and corrosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">Forging</a> – a red-hot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billet_(manufacturing)" title="Billet (manufacturing)">billet</a> is hammered into shape.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rolling_(metalworking)" title="Rolling (metalworking)">Rolling</a> – a billet is passed through successively narrower rollers to create a sheet.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extrusion" title="Extrusion">Extrusion</a> – a hot and malleable metal is forced under pressure through a <a href="/wiki/Die_(manufacturing)" title="Die (manufacturing)">die</a>, which shapes it before it cools.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">Machining</a> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lathe_(tool)" title="Lathe (tool)">lathes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milling_machine" title="Milling machine">milling machines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drill" title="Drill">drills</a> cut the cold metal to shape.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">Sintering</a> – a <a href="/wiki/Powder_metallurgy" title="Powder metallurgy">powdered metal</a> is heated in a non-oxidizing environment after being compressed into a die.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrication_(metal)" title="Fabrication (metal)">Fabrication</a> – sheets of metal are cut with <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotines</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_welding" title="Gas welding">gas cutters</a> and bent and welded into structural shape.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cladding_(metalworking)" title="Cladding (metalworking)">Laser cladding</a> – metallic powder is blown through a movable laser beam (e.g. mounted on a NC 5-axis machine). The resulting melted metal reaches a substrate to form a melt pool. By moving the laser head, it is possible to stack the tracks and build up a three-dimensional piece.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a> – Sintering or melting amorphous powder metal in a 3D space to make any object to shape.</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cold_working" title="Cold working">Cold-working</a> processes, in which the product's shape is altered by rolling, fabrication or other processes, while the product is cold, can increase the strength of the product by a process called <a href="/wiki/Work_hardening" title="Work hardening">work hardening</a>. Work hardening creates <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">microscopic defects</a> in the metal, which resist further changes of shape.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_treatment">Heat treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heat treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="731" data-file-width="991" decoding="async" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg/220px-Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg/330px-Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg/440px-Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heat-Treating-Furnace.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Heat treating furnace at 1,800 °F (980 °C)</div></div></div>
<p>Metals can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_treatment" title="Heat treatment">heat-treated</a> to alter the properties of strength, ductility, toughness, hardness and resistance to corrosion. Common heat treatment processes include annealing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_strengthening" title="Precipitation strengthening">precipitation strengthening</a>, quenching, and tempering:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">Annealing</a> process softens the metal by heating it and then allowing it to cool very slowly, which gets rid of stresses in the metal and makes the grain structure large and soft-edged so that, when the metal is hit or stressed it dents or perhaps bends, rather than breaking; it is also easier to sand, grind, or cut annealed metal.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quenching" title="Quenching">Quenching</a> is the process of cooling metal very quickly after heating, thus "freezing" the metal's molecules in the very hard martensite form, which makes the metal harder.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tempering_(metallurgy)" title="Tempering (metallurgy)">Tempering</a> relieves stresses in the metal that were caused by the hardening process; tempering makes the metal less hard while making it better able to sustain impacts without breaking.</li></ul>
<p>Often, mechanical and thermal treatments are combined in what are known as thermo-mechanical treatments for better properties and more efficient processing of materials. These processes are common to high-alloy special steels, <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> and titanium alloys.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plating">Plating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Plating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plating" title="Plating">Plating</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Copper_electroplating_principle_(multilingual).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Copper_electroplating_principle_%28multilingual%29.svg/220px-Copper_electroplating_principle_%28multilingual%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Copper_electroplating_principle_%28multilingual%29.svg/330px-Copper_electroplating_principle_%28multilingual%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Copper_electroplating_principle_%28multilingual%29.svg/440px-Copper_electroplating_principle_%28multilingual%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Copper_electroplating_principle_(multilingual).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A simplified diagram of electroplating copper on a metal</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">Electroplating</a> is a chemical surface-treatment technique. It involves bonding a thin layer of another metal such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> to the surface of the product. This is done by selecting the coating material electrolyte solution, which is the material that is going to coat the workpiece (gold, silver, zinc). There needs to be two electrodes of different materials: one the same material as the coating material and one that is receiving the coating material. Two electrodes are electrically charged and the coating material is stuck to the work piece. It is used to reduce corrosion as well as to improve the product's aesthetic appearance. It is also used to make inexpensive metals look like the more expensive ones (gold, silver).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shot_peening">Shot peening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Shot peening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shot_peening" title="Shot peening">Shot peening</a></div>
<p>Shot peening is a cold working process used to finish metal parts. In the process of shot peening, small round shot is blasted against the surface of the part to be finished. This process is used to prolong the product life of the part, prevent stress corrosion failures, and also prevent fatigue. The shot leaves small dimples on the surface like a peen hammer does, which cause compression stress under the dimple. As the shot media strikes the material over and over, it forms many overlapping dimples throughout the piece being treated. The compression stress in the surface of the material strengthens the part and makes it more resistant to fatigue failure, stress failures, corrosion failure, and cracking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_spraying">Thermal spraying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Thermal spraying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_spraying" title="Thermal spraying">Thermal spraying</a></div>
<p>Thermal spraying techniques are another popular finishing option, and often have better high temperature properties than electroplated coatings. Thermal spraying, also known as a spray welding process,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> is an industrial coating process that consists of a heat source (flame or other) and a coating material that can be in a powder or wire form, which is melted then sprayed on the surface of the material being treated at a high velocity. The spray treating process is known by many different names such as HVOF (High Velocity Oxygen Fuel), plasma spray, flame spray, arc spray and metalizing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization">Characterization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metallurgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Characterization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Characterization_(materials_science)" title="Characterization (materials science)">Characterization</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="960" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png/220px-AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png/330px-AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png/440px-AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AlubronzeCuAl20v500.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Metallography allows the metallurgist to study the microstructure of metals.</div></div></div> <p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallurgist" title="Metallurgist">Metallurgists</a> study the microscopic and macroscopic structure of metals using <a href="/wiki/Metallography" title="Metallography">metallography</a>, a technique invented by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clifton_Sorby" title="Henry Clifton Sorby">Henry Clifton Sorby</a>.
</p><p>In metallography, an alloy of interest is ground flat and polished to a mirror finish. The sample can then be etched to reveal the microstructure and macrostructure of the metal. The sample is then examined in an optical or <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscope</a>, and the image contrast provides details on the composition, mechanical properties, and processing history.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">Crystallography</a>, often using <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> of <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, is another valuable tool available to the modern metallurgist. Crystallography allows identification of unknown materials and reveals the crystal structure of the sample. Quantitative crystallography can be used to calculate the amount of phases present as well as the degree of strain to which a sample has been subjected.
</p>
