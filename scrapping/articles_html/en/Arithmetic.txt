<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Elementary branch of mathematics</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4038" data-file-width="2691" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg/220px-Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg/330px-Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg/440px-Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tables_generales_aritmetique_MG_2108.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arithmetic tables for children, <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, 1835</div></div></div>
<p><b>Arithmetic</b> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> <i> </i><span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CF%81%CE%B9%CE%B8%CE%BC%CF%8C%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:ἀριθμός">ἀριθμός</a></span><i> (<span lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>arithmós</i></span>)</i> 'number', and <i> </i><span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%CE%B9%CE%BA%CE%AE#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:τική">τική</a></span> [<span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%CE%AD%CF%87%CE%BD%CE%B7#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:τέχνη">τέχνη</a></span>]<i> (<span lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>tikḗ [tékhnē]</i></span>)</i> 'art, craft') is an elementary part of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> that consists of the study of the properties of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Operation_(mathematics)" title="Operation (mathematics)">operations</a> on numbers—<a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">addition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subtraction" title="Subtraction">subtraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">multiplication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Division_(mathematics)" title="Division (mathematics)">division</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponentiation</a>, and extraction of <a href="/wiki/Nth_root" title="Nth root">roots</a>. In the 19th century, Italian mathematician <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Peano" title="Giuseppe Peano">Giuseppe Peano</a> formalized arithmetic with his <a href="/wiki/Peano_axioms" title="Peano axioms">Peano axioms</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (August 2022)">disputed</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Arithmetic#Peano_formalizing_Arithmetic" title="Talk:Arithmetic">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> which are highly important to the field of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_logic" title="Mathematical logic">mathematical logic</a> today. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_arithmetic" title="History of arithmetic">History of arithmetic</a></div>
<p>The prehistory of arithmetic is limited to a small number of artifacts, which may indicate the conception of addition and subtraction, the best-known being the <a href="/wiki/Ishango_bone" title="Ishango bone">Ishango bone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">central Africa</a>, dating from somewhere between 20,000 and 18,000 BC, although its interpretation is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest written records indicate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_mathematics" title="Egyptian mathematics">Egyptians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics" title="Babylonian mathematics">Babylonians</a> used all the <a href="/wiki/Elementary_arithmetic" title="Elementary arithmetic">elementary arithmetic</a> operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division, as early as 2000 BC. These artifacts do not always reveal the specific process used for solving problems, but the characteristics of the particular <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numeral system</a> strongly influence the complexity of the methods. The hieroglyphic system for <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_numerals" title="Egyptian numerals">Egyptian numerals</a>, like the later <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">Roman numerals</a>, descended from <a href="/wiki/Tally_marks" title="Tally marks">tally marks</a> used for counting. In both cases, this origin resulted in values that used a <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimal</a> base, but did not include <a href="/wiki/Positional_notation" title="Positional notation">positional notation</a>. Complex calculations with Roman numerals required the assistance of a <a href="/wiki/Counting_board" title="Counting board">counting board</a> (or the <a href="/wiki/Roman_abacus" title="Roman abacus">Roman abacus</a>) to obtain the results.
</p><p>Early number systems that included positional notation were not decimal; these include the <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">sexagesimal</a> (base 60) system for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_numerals" title="Babylonian numerals">Babylonian numerals</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Vigesimal" title="Vigesimal">vigesimal</a> (base 20) system that defined <a href="/wiki/Maya_numerals" title="Maya numerals">Maya numerals</a>. Because of the place-value concept, the ability to reuse the same digits for different values contributed to simpler and more efficient methods of calculation.
</p><p>The continuous historical development of modern arithmetic starts with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic period</a> of ancient Greece; it originated much later than the Babylonian and Egyptian examples. Prior to the works of <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> around 300 BC, <a href="/wiki/Greek_mathematics" title="Greek mathematics">Greek studies in mathematics</a> overlapped with philosophical and mystical beliefs. <a href="/wiki/Nicomachus" title="Nicomachus">Nicomachus</a> is an example of this viewpoint, using the earlier <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism" title="Pythagoreanism">Pythagorean</a> approach to numbers and their relationships to each other in his work <i><a href="/wiki/Introduction_to_Arithmetic" title="Introduction to Arithmetic">Introduction to Arithmetic</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greek_numerals" title="Greek numerals">Greek numerals</a> were used by <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diophantus" title="Diophantus">Diophantus</a> and others in a <a href="/wiki/Positional_notation" title="Positional notation">positional notation</a> not very different from the modern notation. The ancient Greeks lacked a symbol for zero until the Hellenistic period, and they used three separate sets of symbols as <a href="/wiki/Numerical_digit" title="Numerical digit">digits</a>: one set for the units place, one for the tens place, and one for the hundreds. For the thousands place, they would reuse the symbols for the units place, and so on. Their addition algorithm was identical to the modern method, and their multiplication algorithm was only slightly different. Their long division algorithm was the same, and the <a href="/wiki/Methods_of_computing_square_roots#Digit-by-digit_calculation" title="Methods of computing square roots">digit-by-digit square root algorithm</a>, popularly used as recently as the 20th century, was known to Archimedes (who may have invented it). He preferred it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heron%27s_method" title="Heron's method">Hero's method</a> of successive approximation because, once computed, a digit does not change, and the square roots of perfect squares, such as 7485696, terminate immediately as 2736. For numbers with a fractional part, such as 546.934, they used negative powers of 60—instead of negative powers of 10 for the fractional part 0.934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ancient Chinese had advanced arithmetic studies dating from the Shang Dynasty and continuing through the Tang Dynasty, from basic numbers to advanced algebra. The ancient Chinese used a positional notation similar to that of the Greeks. Since they also lacked a symbol for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero" title="Zero">zero</a>, they had one set of symbols for the units place, and a second set for the tens place. For the hundreds place, they then reused the symbols for the units place, and so on. Their symbols were based on the ancient <a href="/wiki/Counting_rods" title="Counting rods">counting rods</a>. The exact time where the Chinese started calculating with positional representation is unknown, though it is known that the adoption started before 400 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The ancient Chinese were the first to meaningfully discover, understand, and apply negative numbers. This is explained in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine_Chapters_on_the_Mathematical_Art" title="Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art">Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art</a></i> (<i>Jiuzhang Suanshu</i>), which was written by <a href="/wiki/Liu_Hui" title="Liu Hui">Liu Hui</a> dated back to 2nd century BC.
</p><p>The gradual development of the <a href="/wiki/Hindu%E2%80%93Arabic_numeral_system" title="Hindu–Arabic numeral system">Hindu–Arabic numeral system</a> independently devised the place-value concept and positional notation, which combined the simpler methods for computations with a decimal base, and the use of a digit representing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/0_(number)" title="0 (number)">0</a>. This allowed the system to consistently represent both large and small integers—an approach which eventually replaced all other systems. In the early <span class="nowrap">6th century AD,</span> the Indian mathematician <a href="/wiki/Aryabhata" title="Aryabhata">Aryabhata</a> incorporated an existing version of this system in his work, and experimented with different notations. In the 7th century, <a href="/wiki/Brahmagupta" title="Brahmagupta">Brahmagupta</a> established the use of 0 as a separate number, and determined the results for multiplication, division, addition and subtraction of zero and all other numbers—except for the result of <a href="/wiki/Division_by_zero" title="Division by zero">division by zero</a>. His contemporary, the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Christianity" title="Syriac Christianity">Syriac</a> bishop <a href="/wiki/Severus_Sebokht" title="Severus Sebokht">Severus Sebokht</a> (650 AD) said, "Indians possess a method of calculation that no word can praise enough. Their rational system of mathematics, or of their method of calculation. I mean the system using nine symbols."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Arabs also learned this new method and called it <i>hesab</i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="803" data-file-width="1235" decoding="async" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png/200px-Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png/300px-Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png/400px-Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leibniz_Stepped_Reckoner.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leibniz's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stepped_Reckoner" title="Stepped Reckoner">Stepped Reckoner</a> was the first calculator that could perform all four arithmetic operations.</div></div></div>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Codex_Vigilanus" title="Codex Vigilanus">Codex Vigilanus</a> described an early form of Arabic numerals (omitting 0) by 976 AD, Leonardo of Pisa (<a href="/wiki/Fibonacci" title="Fibonacci">Fibonacci</a>) was primarily responsible for spreading their use throughout Europe after the publication of his book <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Abaci" title="Liber Abaci">Liber Abaci</a></i> in 1202. He wrote, "The method of the Indians (Latin <i>Modus Indorum</i>) surpasses any known method to compute. It's a marvelous method. They do their computations using nine figures and symbol <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero" title="Zero">zero</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Middle Ages, arithmetic was one of the seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a> taught in universities.
</p><p>The flourishing of <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> world, and also in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, was an outgrowth of the enormous simplification of <a href="/wiki/Computation" title="Computation">computation</a> through <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimal</a> notation.
</p><p>Various types of tools have been invented and widely used to assist in numeric calculations. Before Renaissance, they were various types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abaci" title="Abaci">abaci</a>. More recent examples include <a href="/wiki/Slide_rule" title="Slide rule">slide rules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nomogram" title="Nomogram">nomograms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_calculator" title="Mechanical calculator">mechanical calculators</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_calculator" title="Pascal's calculator">Pascal's calculator</a>. At present, they have been supplanted by electronic <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arithmetic_operations">Arithmetic operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arithmetic operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_operation" title="Algebraic operation">Algebraic operation</a></div>
<p>The basic arithmetic operations are addition, subtraction, multiplication and division, although arithmetic also includes more advanced operations, such as manipulations of <a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">percentages</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Square_root" title="Square root">square roots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponentiation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logarithmic_function" title="Logarithmic function">logarithmic functions</a>, and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonometric_function" title="Trigonometric function">trigonometric functions</a>, in the same vein as logarithms (<a href="/wiki/Prosthaphaeresis" title="Prosthaphaeresis">prosthaphaeresis</a>).  Arithmetic expressions must be evaluated according to the intended sequence of operations. There are several methods to specify this, either—most common, together with <a href="/wiki/Infix_notation" title="Infix notation">infix notation</a>—explicitly using parentheses and relying on <a href="/wiki/Order_of_operations" title="Order of operations">precedence rules</a>, or using a <a href="/wiki/Polish_notation" title="Polish notation">prefix</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reverse_Polish_notation" title="Reverse Polish notation">postfix</a> notation, which uniquely fix the order of execution by themselves. Any set of objects upon which all four arithmetic operations (except <a href="/wiki/Division_by_zero" title="Division by zero">division by zero</a>) can be performed, and where these four operations obey the usual laws (including distributivity), is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_mathematics" title="Field mathematics">field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition">Addition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Addition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">Addition</a></div>
<p>Addition, denoted by the symbol <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle +}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mo>+</mo>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle +}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="+" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6ef363cd19902d1a7a71fb1c8b21e8ede52406" style="vertical-align: -0.505ex; width:1.808ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>, is the most basic operation of arithmetic. In its simple form, addition combines two numbers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Addend" title="Addend"><i>addends</i> or <i>terms</i></a>, into a single number, the <a href="/wiki/Summation" title="Summation"><i>sum</i></a> of the numbers (such as <span class="texhtml">2 + 2 = 4</span> or <span class="texhtml">3 + 5 = 8</span>).
</p><p>Adding finitely many numbers can be viewed as repeated simple addition; this procedure is known as <a href="/wiki/Summation" title="Summation">summation</a>, a term also used to denote the definition for "adding infinitely many numbers" in an <a href="/wiki/Series_(mathematics)" title="Series (mathematics)">infinite series</a>. Repeated addition of the number <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One" title="One">1</a> is the most basic form of <a href="/wiki/Counting" title="Counting">counting</a>; the result of adding <span class="texhtml">1</span> is usually called the <a href="/wiki/Successor_function" title="Successor function">successor</a> of the original number.
</p><p>Addition is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutative" title="Commutative">commutative</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associative" title="Associative">associative</a>, so the order in which finitely many terms are added does not matter.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/0_(number)" title="0 (number)">number <span class="texhtml">0</span></a> has the property that, when added to any number, it yields that same number; so, it is the <a href="/wiki/Identity_element" title="Identity element">identity element</a> of addition, or the <a href="/wiki/Additive_identity" title="Additive identity">additive identity</a>.
</p><p>For every number <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span>, there is a number denoted <span class="texhtml">–<i>x</i></span>, called the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposite_(mathematics)" title="Opposite (mathematics)">opposite</a></i> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span>, such that <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i> + (–<i>x</i>) = 0</span> and  <span class="texhtml">(–<i>x</i>) + <i>x</i> = 0</span>. So, the opposite of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span> is the <a href="/wiki/Inverse_element" title="Inverse element">inverse</a> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span> with respect to addition, or the <a href="/wiki/Additive_inverse" title="Additive inverse">additive inverse</a> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span>. For example, the opposite of <span class="texhtml">7</span> is <span class="texhtml">−7</span>, since <span class="texhtml">7 + (−7) = 0</span>.
</p><p>Addition can also be interpreted geometrically, as in the following example.
If we have two sticks of lengths <i>2</i> and <i>5</i>, then, if the sticks are aligned one after the other, the length of the combined stick becomes <i>7</i>, since <span class="texhtml">2 + 5 = 7</span>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subtraction">Subtraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subtraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subtraction" title="Subtraction">Subtraction</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Method_of_complements" title="Method of complements">Method of complements</a></div>
<p>Subtraction, denoted by the symbol <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle -}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mo>−<!-- − --></mo>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle -}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="-" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04bd52ce670743d3b61bec928a7ec9f47309eb36" style="vertical-align: -0.505ex; width:1.808ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>, is the inverse operation to addition. Subtraction finds the <i>difference</i> between two numbers, the <i>minuend</i> minus the <i>subtrahend</i>: <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i> = <i>M</i> − <i>S</i>.</span> Resorting to the previously established addition, this is to say that the difference is the number that, when added to the subtrahend, results in the minuend: <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i> + <i>S</i> = <i>M</i>.</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>For positive arguments <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">M</span> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">S</span> holds:
</p>
<dl><dd>If the minuend is larger than the subtrahend, the difference <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">D</span> is positive.</dd>
<dd>If the minuend is smaller than the subtrahend, the difference <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">D</span> is negative.</dd></dl>
<p>In any case, if minuend and subtrahend are equal, the difference <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i> = 0.</span>
</p><p>Subtraction is neither <a href="/wiki/Commutative_property" title="Commutative property">commutative</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Associative_property" title="Associative property">associative</a>. For that reason, the construction of this inverse operation in modern algebra is often discarded in favor of introducing the concept of inverse elements (as sketched under <a href="#Addition">§ Addition</a>), where subtraction is regarded as adding the additive inverse of the subtrahend to the minuend, that is, <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> − <i>b</i> = <i>a</i> + (−<i>b</i>)</span>. The immediate price of discarding the binary operation of subtraction is the introduction of the (trivial) <a href="/wiki/Unary_operation" title="Unary operation">unary operation</a>, delivering the additive inverse for any given number, and losing the immediate access to the notion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Difference_(mathematics)" title="Difference (mathematics)">difference</a>, which is potentially misleading when negative arguments are involved.
</p><p>For any representation of numbers, there are methods for calculating results, some of which are particularly advantageous in exploiting procedures, existing for one operation, by small alterations also for others. For example, digital computers can reuse existing adding-circuitry and save additional circuits for implementing a subtraction, by employing the method of <a href="/wiki/Two%27s_complement" title="Two's complement">two's complement</a> for representing the additive inverses, which is extremely easy to implement in hardware (<a href="/wiki/Inverter_(logic_gate)" title="Inverter (logic gate)">negation</a>). The trade-off is the halving of the number range for a fixed word length.
</p><p>A formerly widespread method to achieve a correct change amount, knowing the due and given amounts, is the <i>counting up method</i>, which does not explicitly generate the value of the difference. Suppose an amount <i>P</i> is given in order to pay the required amount <i>Q</i>, with <i>P</i> greater than <i>Q</i>. Rather than explicitly performing the subtraction <i>P</i> − <i>Q</i> = <i>C</i> and counting out that amount <i>C</i> in change, money is counted out starting with the successor of <i>Q</i>, and continuing in the steps of the currency, until <i>P</i> is reached. Although the amount counted out must equal the result of the subtraction <i>P</i> − <i>Q</i>, the subtraction was never really done and the value of <i>P</i> − <i>Q</i> is not supplied by this method.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiplication">Multiplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Multiplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">Multiplication</a></div>

</math></span><img alt="\cdot " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba" style="vertical-align: 0.439ex; margin-bottom: -0.61ex; width:0.647ex; height:1.176ex;"/></span>, is the second basic operation of arithmetic. Multiplication also combines two numbers into a single number, the <i>product</i>. The two original numbers are called the <i>multiplier</i> and the <i>multiplicand</i>, mostly both are simply called <i>factors</i>.
</p><p>Multiplication may be viewed as a scaling operation. If the numbers are imagined as lying in a line, multiplication by a number greater than 1, say <i>x</i>, is the same as stretching everything away from 0 uniformly, in such a way that the number 1 itself is stretched to where <i>x</i> was. Similarly, multiplying by a number less than 1 can be imagined as squeezing towards 0, in such a way that 1 goes to the multiplicand.
</p><p>Another view on multiplication of integer numbers (extendable to rationals but not very accessible for real numbers) is by considering it as repeated addition. For example. <span class="texhtml">3 × 4</span> corresponds to either adding <span class="texhtml">3</span> times a <span class="texhtml">4</span>, or <span class="texhtml">4</span> times a <span class="texhtml">3</span>, giving the same result. There are different opinions on the advantageousness of these <a href="/wiki/Multiplication_and_repeated_addition" title="Multiplication and repeated addition">paradigmata</a> in math education.
</p><p>Multiplication is commutative and associative; further, it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distributivity" title="Distributivity">distributive</a> over addition and subtraction. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplicative_identity" title="Multiplicative identity">multiplicative identity</a> is 1, since multiplying any number by 1 yields that same number. The <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">multiplicative inverse</a> for any number except <span class="texhtml">0</span> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reciprocal_(mathematics)" title="Reciprocal (mathematics)">reciprocal</a> of this number, because  multiplying the reciprocal of any number by the number itself yields the multiplicative identity <span class="texhtml">1</span>.  <span class="texhtml">0</span> is the only number without a multiplicative inverse, and the result of multiplying any number and <span class="texhtml">0</span> is again <span class="texhtml">0.</span> One says that <span class="texhtml">0</span> is not contained in the multiplicative <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a> of the numbers.
</p><p>The product of <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> is written as <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> × <i>b</i></span> or <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i>·<i>b</i></span>. When <i>a</i> or <i>b</i> are expressions not written simply with digits, it is also written by simple juxtaposition: <i>ab</i>. In computer programming languages and software packages (in which one can only use characters normally found on a keyboard), it is often written with an asterisk: <code>a * b</code>.
</p><p>Algorithms implementing the operation of multiplication for various representations of numbers are by far more costly and laborious than those for addition. Those accessible for manual computation either rely on breaking down the factors to single place values and applying repeated addition, or on employing <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_table" title="Mathematical table">tables</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slide_rules" title="Slide rules">slide rules</a>, thereby mapping multiplication to addition and vice versa. These methods are outdated and are gradually replaced by mobile devices. Computers utilize diverse sophisticated and highly optimized algorithms, to implement multiplication and division for the various number formats supported in their system.
</p>

Different implementations of division (floored, truncated, Euclidean, etc.) correspond with different implementations of modulus.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic">Fundamental theorem of arithmetic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fundamental theorem of arithmetic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic" title="Fundamental theorem of arithmetic">Fundamental theorem of arithmetic</a></div>
<p>The fundamental theorem of arithmetic states that any integer greater than 1 has a unique prime factorization (a representation of a number as the product of prime factors), excluding the order of the factors. For example, 252 only has one prime factorization:
</p>
<dl><dd>252 = 2<sup>2</sup> × 3<sup>2</sup> × 7<sup>1</sup></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid's Elements">Euclid's <i>Elements</i></a> first introduced this theorem, and gave a partial proof (which is called <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_lemma" title="Euclid's lemma">Euclid's lemma</a>). The fundamental theorem of arithmetic was first proven by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a>.
</p><p>The fundamental theorem of arithmetic is one of the reasons <a href="/wiki/Prime_number#Primality_of_one" title="Prime number">why 1 is not considered a prime number</a>. Other reasons include the <a href="/wiki/Sieve_of_Eratosthenes" title="Sieve of Eratosthenes">sieve of Eratosthenes</a>, and the definition of a prime number itself (a natural number greater than 1 that cannot be formed by multiplying two smaller natural numbers.).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decimal_arithmetic">Decimal arithmetic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Decimal arithmetic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><em><a href="/wiki/Decimal_representation" title="Decimal representation">Decimal representation</a></em> refers exclusively, in common use, to the written <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numeral system</a> employing <a href="/wiki/Arabic_numerals" title="Arabic numerals">arabic numerals</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Numerical_digit" title="Numerical digit">digits</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Radix" title="Radix">radix</a> <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">10 ("decimal")</a> <a href="/wiki/Positional_notation" title="Positional notation">positional notation</a>; however, any <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numeral system</a> based on powers of 10, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Numerals" title="Greek Numerals">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_numerals" title="Cyrillic numerals">Cyrillic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">Roman</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Chinese_numerals" title="Chinese numerals">Chinese numerals</a> may conceptually be described as "decimal notation" or "decimal representation".
</p><p>Modern methods for four fundamental operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication and division) were first devised by <a href="/wiki/Brahmagupta" title="Brahmagupta">Brahmagupta</a> of India. This was known during medieval Europe as "Modus Indorum" or Method of the Indians. Positional notation (also known as "place-value notation") refers to the representation or encoding of <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a> using the same symbol for the different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude" title="Orders of magnitude">orders of magnitude</a> (e.g., the "ones place", "tens place", "hundreds place") and, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radix_point" title="Radix point">radix point</a>, using those same symbols to represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_fraction" title="Arithmetic fraction">fractions</a> (e.g., the "tenths place", "hundredths place"). For example, 507.36 denotes 5 hundreds (10<sup>2</sup>), plus 0 tens (10<sup>1</sup>), plus 7 units (10<sup>0</sup>), plus 3 tenths (10<sup>−1</sup>) plus 6 hundredths (10<sup>−2</sup>).
</p><p>The concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero" title="Zero">0</a> as a number comparable to the other basic digits is essential to this notation, as is the concept of 0's use as a placeholder, and as is the definition of multiplication and addition with 0. The use of 0 as a placeholder and, therefore, the use of a positional notation is first attested to in the <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jain</a> text from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> entitled the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lokavibh%C3%A2ga" title="Lokavibhâga">Lokavibhâga</a></i>, dated 458 AD and it was only in the early 13th century that these concepts, transmitted via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematics_in_medieval_Islam" title="Mathematics in medieval Islam">scholarship of the Arabic world</a>, were introduced into <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> by <a href="/wiki/Fibonacci" title="Fibonacci">Fibonacci</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> using the Hindu–Arabic numeral system.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Algorism" title="Algorism">Algorism</a> comprises all of the rules for performing arithmetic computations using this type of written numeral. For example, addition produces the sum of two arbitrary numbers. The result is calculated by the repeated addition of single digits from each number that occupies the same position, proceeding from right to left. An addition table with ten rows and ten columns displays all possible values for each sum. If an individual sum exceeds the value 9, the result is represented with two digits. The rightmost digit is the value for the current position, and the result for the subsequent addition of the digits to the left increases by the value of the second (leftmost) digit, which is always one (if not zero). This adjustment is termed a <i>carry</i> of the value 1.
</p><p>The process for multiplying two arbitrary numbers is similar to the process for addition. A multiplication table with ten rows and ten columns lists the results for each pair of digits. If an individual product of a pair of digits exceeds 9, the <i>carry</i> adjustment increases the result of any subsequent multiplication from digits to the left by a value equal to the second (leftmost) digit, which is any value from <span class="nowrap">1 to 8</span> (<span class="texhtml">9 × 9 = 81</span>). Additional steps define the final result.
</p><p>Similar techniques exist for subtraction and division.
</p><p>The creation of a correct process for multiplication relies on the relationship between values of adjacent digits. The value for any single digit in a numeral depends on its position. Also, each position to the left represents a value ten times larger than the position to the right. In mathematical terms, the <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponent</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Radix" title="Radix">radix</a> (base) of 10 increases by 1 (to the left) or decreases by 1 (to the right). Therefore, the value for any arbitrary digit is multiplied by a value of the form 10<sup><i>n</i></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> <i>n</i>. The list of values corresponding to all possible positions for a single digit is written <span class="nowrap">as {..., 10<sup>2</sup>, 10, 1, 10<sup>−1</sup>, 10<sup>−2</sup>, ...}.</span>
</p><p>Repeated multiplication of any value in this list by 10 produces another value in the list. In mathematical terminology, this characteristic is defined as <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">closure</a>, and the previous list is described as <em>closed under multiplication</em>. It is the basis for correctly finding the results of multiplication using the previous technique. This outcome is one example of the uses of <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_unit_arithmetic">Compound unit arithmetic<span class="anchor" id="Compound_Unit_Arithmetic"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Compound unit arithmetic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compound<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> unit arithmetic is the application of arithmetic operations to <a href="/wiki/Mixed_radix" title="Mixed radix">mixed radix</a> quantities such as feet and inches; gallons and pints; pounds, shillings and pence; and so on. Before decimal-based systems of money and units of measure, compound unit arithmetic was widely used in commerce and industry.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_arithmetic_operations">Basic arithmetic operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Basic arithmetic operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The techniques used in compound unit arithmetic were developed over many centuries and are well documented in many textbooks in many different languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FR_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FR-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NL2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NL2-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DE1842_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DE1842-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1772_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-eb1772-15">[15]</a></sup> In addition to the basic arithmetic functions encountered in decimal arithmetic, compound unit arithmetic employs three more functions:
</p>
<ul><li><em><a href="/wiki/Reduction_(mathematics)" title="Reduction (mathematics)">Reduction</a></em>, in which a compound quantity is reduced to a single quantity—for example, conversion of a distance expressed in yards, feet and inches to one expressed in inches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><em>Expansion</em>, the <a href="/wiki/Inverse_function" title="Inverse function">inverse function</a> to reduction, is the conversion of a quantity that is expressed as a single unit of measure to a compound unit, such as expanding 24 oz to <span class="nowrap">1 lb 8 oz</span>.</li>
<li><em>Normalization</em> is the conversion of a set of compound units to a standard form—for example, rewriting "<span class="nowrap">1 ft 13 in</span>" as "<span class="nowrap">2 ft 1 in</span>".</li></ul>
<p>Knowledge of the relationship between the various units of measure, their multiples and their submultiples forms an essential part of compound unit arithmetic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_of_compound_unit_arithmetic">Principles of compound unit arithmetic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Principles of compound unit arithmetic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two basic approaches to compound unit arithmetic:
</p>
<ul><li><em>Reduction–expansion method</em> where all the compound unit variables are reduced to single unit variables, the calculation performed and the result expanded back to compound units. This approach is suited for automated calculations. A typical example is the handling of time by <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Excel" title="Microsoft Excel">Microsoft Excel</a> where all time intervals are processed internally as days and decimal fractions of a day.</li>
<li><em>On-going normalization method</em> in which each unit is treated separately and the problem is continuously normalized as the solution develops. This approach, which is widely described in classical texts, is best suited for manual calculations. An example of the ongoing normalization method as applied to addition is shown below.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MixedUnitAddition.svg"><img alt="MixedUnitAddition.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="220" decoding="async" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/MixedUnitAddition.svg/220px-MixedUnitAddition.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/MixedUnitAddition.svg/330px-MixedUnitAddition.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/MixedUnitAddition.svg/440px-MixedUnitAddition.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MixedUnitAddition.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>The addition operation is carried out from right to left; in this case, pence are processed first, then shillings followed by pounds. The numbers below the "answer line" are intermediate results.
</p><p>The total in the pence column is 25. Since there are 12 pennies in a shilling, 25 is divided by 12 to give 2 with a remainder of 1. The value "1" is then written to the answer row and the value "2" carried forward to the shillings column. This operation is repeated using the values in the shillings column, with the additional step of adding the value that was carried forward from the pennies column. The intermediate total is divided by 20 as there are 20 shillings in a pound. The pound column is then processed, but as pounds are the largest unit that is being considered, no values are carried forward from the pounds column.
</p><p>For the sake of simplicity, the example chosen did not have farthings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_in_practice">Operations in practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Operations in practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4672" data-file-width="3104" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg/220px-Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg/330px-Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg/440px-Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yarloop_wkshop_gnangarra_14.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A scale calibrated in imperial units with an associated cost display.</div></div></div>
<p>During the 19th and 20th centuries various aids were developed to aid the manipulation of compound units, particularly in commercial applications. The most common aids were <a href="/wiki/Cash_register" title="Cash register">mechanical tills</a> which were adapted in countries such as the United Kingdom to accommodate pounds, shillings, pennies and farthings, and <a href="/wiki/Ready_reckoner" title="Ready reckoner">ready reckoners</a>, which are books aimed at traders that catalogued the results of various routine calculations such as the percentages or multiples of various sums of money. One typical booklet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> that ran to 150 pages tabulated multiples "from one to ten thousand at the various prices from one farthing to one pound".
</p><p>The cumbersome nature of compound unit arithmetic has been recognized for many years—in 1586, the Flemish mathematician <a href="/wiki/Simon_Stevin" title="Simon Stevin">Simon Stevin</a> published a small pamphlet called <i><a href="/wiki/De_Thiende" title="De Thiende">De Thiende</a></i> ("the tenth")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in which he declared the universal introduction of decimal coinage, measures, and weights to be merely a question of time. In the modern era, many conversion programs, such as that included in the Microsoft Windows 7 operating system calculator, display compound units in a reduced decimal format rather than using an expanded format (e.g. "2.5 ft" is displayed rather than <span class="nowrap">"2 ft 6 in"</span>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_theory">Number theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Number theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">Number theory</a></div>
<p>Until the 19th century, <i>number theory</i> was a synonym of "arithmetic". The addressed problems were directly related to the basic operations and concerned <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">primality</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divisibility" title="Divisibility">divisibility</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Diophantine_equation" title="Diophantine equation">solution of equations in integers</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Fermat's Last Theorem">Fermat's Last Theorem</a>. It appeared that most of these problems, although very elementary to state, are very difficult and may not be solved without very deep mathematics involving concepts and methods from many other branches of mathematics. This led to new branches of number theory such as <a href="/wiki/Analytic_number_theory" title="Analytic number theory">analytic number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory" title="Algebraic number theory">algebraic number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diophantine_geometry" title="Diophantine geometry">Diophantine geometry</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_algebraic_geometry" title="Arithmetic algebraic geometry">arithmetic algebraic geometry</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiles%27_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem">Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem</a> is a typical example of the necessity of sophisticated methods, which go far beyond the classical methods of arithmetic, for solving problems that can be stated in elementary arithmetic.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arithmetic_in_education">Arithmetic in education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Arithmetic in education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">Primary education</a> in mathematics often places a strong focus on algorithms for the arithmetic of <a href="/wiki/Natural_number" title="Natural number">natural numbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_fraction" title="Arithmetic fraction">fractions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimals</a> (using the decimal place-value system). This study is sometimes known as algorism.
</p><p>The difficulty and unmotivated appearance of these algorithms has long led educators to question this curriculum, advocating the early teaching of more central and intuitive mathematical ideas. One notable movement in this direction was the <a href="/wiki/New_Math" title="New Math">New Math</a> of the 1960s and 1970s, which attempted to teach arithmetic in the spirit of axiomatic development from set theory, an echo of the prevailing trend in higher mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, arithmetic was used by <a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">Islamic Scholars</a> in order to teach application of the rulings related to <a href="/wiki/Zakat" title="Zakat">Zakat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_inheritance_jurisprudence" title="Islamic inheritance jurisprudence">Irth</a>. This was done in a book entitled <i>The Best of Arithmetic</i> by Abd-al-Fattah-al-Dumyati.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The book begins with the foundations of mathematics and proceeds to its application in the later chapters.
</p>
