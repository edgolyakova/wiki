<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="542" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg/330px-Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg/495px-Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg/660px-Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coastal_Andean_Men%E2%80%99s_tunic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coastal Andean Men's tunic, 13th -15th century</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Andean civilizations</b> were <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">complex societies</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_South_America" title="Indigenous peoples of South America">cultures and peoples</a> mainly developed in the river valleys of the coastal deserts of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. They stretched from the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> of southern <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> southward down the Andes to <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. Archaeologists believe that Andean <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a> first developed on the narrow coastal plain of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_civilization" title="Norte Chico civilization">Caral or Norte Chico civilization</a> of Peru is the oldest known civilization in the Americas, dating back to 3200 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caral_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Caral-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite severe environmental challenges, the Andean civilizations domesticated a wide variety of crops, some of which became of worldwide importance.  The Andean civilizations were also noteworthy for monumental architecture, textile weaving, and many unique characteristics of the societies they created.
</p><p>Less than a century prior to the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> conquerors, the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Incas</a>, from their homeland centered on the city of <a href="/wiki/Cusco" title="Cusco">Cusco</a>, united most of the Andean cultures into one single empire that encompassed all of what is usually called Andean civilization. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timoto-Cuica_people" title="Timoto-Cuica people">Timoto Cuica</a> of Venezuela remained outside the Inca orbit. The Inca Empire was a patchwork of languages, cultures and peoples.
</p><p>Spanish rule ended or transformed many elements of the Andean civilizations, notably influencing religion and architecture.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniqueness">Uniqueness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uniqueness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inca_Quipu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="625" decoding="async" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Inca_Quipu.jpg/250px-Inca_Quipu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Inca_Quipu.jpg/375px-Inca_Quipu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Inca_Quipu.jpg/500px-Inca_Quipu.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inca_Quipu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A quipu</div></div></div>
<p>The civilization of the Andes was one of five in the world deemed by scholars to be "pristine", that is indigenous and not derived from other civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to its isolation from other civilizations, the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_South_America" title="Indigenous peoples of South America">indigenous people of the Andes</a> had to come up with their own, often unique solutions to environmental and societal challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcEwan20065_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcEwan20065-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Andean civilization lacked several characteristics distinguishing it from the pristine civilizations in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and from the Mesoamerican cultures. First, and perhaps most important, Andean civilizations did not have a written language.  Instead, their societies used the <a href="/wiki/Quipu" title="Quipu">quipu</a>, a system of knotted and colored strings, to convey information.  Few quipus survive and they have never been fully deciphered.  Scholars differ on whether the knotted cords of the quipu were able only to record numerical data or could also be used for narrative communication, a true system of writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McEwan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-McEwan-4">[4]</a></sup> The use of the quipu dates back at least to the <a href="/wiki/Wari_Empire" title="Wari Empire">Wari Empire</a> (600–1000 CE) and possibly to the much earlier civilization of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_civilization" title="Norte Chico civilization">Caral/Norte Chico</a> of the third millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirst_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirst-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Andean civilizations also lacked wheeled vehicles and draft animals.  People on land traveled only by foot and the transport of goods was only by humans or <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llama</a>, pack animals which could carry loads of up to one-fourth of their weight, a maximum of 45 kilograms (99 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Llamas were not big or strong enough to be used for <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">plowing</a> or as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riding_animal" title="Riding animal">riding</a> animals for adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcEwan20065_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcEwan20065-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Moreover, Andean civilizations faced severe environmental challenges.  The earliest civilizations were on the hyper-arid desert coast of Peru.  Agriculture was possible only with irrigation in valleys crossed by rivers coming from the high Andes, plus in a few fog oases called <a href="/wiki/Lomas" title="Lomas">lomas</a>. In the Andes, agriculture was limited by thin soils, cold climate, low or seasonal precipitation, and a scarcity of flat land. Freezing temperatures may occur in every month of the year at altitudes of more than 3,000 metres (9,800 ft), the homeland of many of the highland Andean civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, the Andean civilizations lacked money. Copper <a href="/wiki/Axe-monies" title="Axe-monies">axe-monies</a> (also called "naipes")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spondylus" title="Spondylus">Spondylus</a></i> shells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> functioned as mediums of exchange in some areas, especially coastal <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, but most of the Andes area had economies organized on reciprocity and redistribution rather than money and markets. These characteristics were especially notable during the Inca Empire but originated in much earlier times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pisac006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Pisac006.jpg/250px-Pisac006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Pisac006.jpg/375px-Pisac006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Pisac006.jpg/500px-Pisac006.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pisac006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Agricultural terraces (<a href="/wiki/And%C3%A9n" title="Andén">andenes</a>) were widely built and used for agriculture in the Andes.</div></div></div>
<p>Agriculture in South America may have begun in coastal Ecuador with the domestication of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squash_(plant)" title="Squash (plant)">squash</a> about 8000 BCE by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_culture" title="Las Vegas culture">Las Vegas culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scholars believe that the earliest civilizations on the Peruvian coast initially relied more upon maritime resources than agriculture during the formative period of their societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, as in all civilizations until the late 19th century, agriculture was the principal occupation of the great majority of the people.  The greatest contribution of Andean civilization to the modern world has been the plants its people domesticated.  Crops grown by the Andeans were often unique to the region. <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">Maize</a>, which found its way to the Andes from <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, was often the most important crop at lower and intermediate elevations.  The Andeans cultivated an estimated 70 different plants, almost as many as were cultivated in all of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Many of these plants are no longer cultivated, or are minor crops, but important plants which were domesticated in or near the Andes include  <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chile_peppers" title="Chile peppers">chile peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coca" title="Coca">coca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, and several varieties of <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Animals domesticated in the Andes were llamas and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinea_pigs" title="Guinea pigs">guinea pigs</a>.
</p><p>The challenges of the environment required sophisticated agricultural technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D'Altroy_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-D'Altroy-16">[16]</a></sup> Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, the Andes lacked easily domesticated and large-seeded plants such as <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> and large and easily domesticated animals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horses" title="Horses">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Agriculture on the desert coast required the development of irrigation. In the mountains, the elevation, cold climate and steep terrain required a range of technological solutions such as terraces (<a href="/wiki/And%C3%A9n" title="Andén">andén</a>), exploitation of microclimates, and <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selective breeding</a>. Due to the climatic uncertainties, farmers traditionally farmed several crops at several elevations and exposures.  At a macro level, societies and states did the same with the <a href="/wiki/Vertical_archipelago" title="Vertical archipelago">vertical archipelago</a>, establishing colonies at different elevations and locations to increase the possibilities of agricultural success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTED'Altroy200327–35_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTED'Altroy200327–35-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcEwan200623–27,_83–85_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcEwan200623–27,_83–85-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caral">Caral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Caral-Supe_civilization" title="Caral-Supe civilization">Caral-Supe civilization</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PeruCaral01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/PeruCaral01.jpg/220px-PeruCaral01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/PeruCaral01.jpg/330px-PeruCaral01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/PeruCaral01.jpg/440px-PeruCaral01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PeruCaral01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Caral pyramids in the arid Supe Valley, some 20 km from the Pacific coast</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_civilization" title="Norte Chico civilization">Norte Chico civilization</a>, also called Caral,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> was a complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> society that included as many as 30 major population centers in what is now the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_(Peruvian_region)" title="Norte Chico (Peruvian region)">Norte Chico region</a> of north-central coastal <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. It is the oldest known civilization in the Americas and one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cradles_of_civilization" title="Cradles of civilization">Cradles of civilization</a> where civilization separately originated in the ancient world. It flourished between the 30th century BCE and the 18th century BCE. The alternative name, Caral-Supe, is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_City_of_Caral" title="Sacred City of Caral">Sacred City of Caral</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in the Supe Valley, a large and well-studied Norte Chico site. Complex society in Norte Chico arose a millennium after <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, was contemporaneous with the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids" title="Egyptian pyramids">Egyptian pyramids</a>, and predated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoamerican" title="Mesoamerican">Mesoamerican</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olmec" title="Olmec">Olmec</a> by nearly two millennia.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Valdivia">Valdivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Valdivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Valdivia_culture" title="Valdivia culture">Valdivia culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="414" data-file-width="764" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG/220px-MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG/330px-MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG/440px-MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MORTERO_JAGUAR_VALDIVIA_2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mortar, Jaguar Valdivia, South Coast (4000 BCE to 1500 BCE)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdivia_Culture" title="Valdivia Culture">Valdivia Culture</a> is one of the oldest settled cultures recorded in the Americas. It emerged from the earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_culture" title="Las Vegas culture">Las Vegas culture</a> and thrived on the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Elena,_Ecuador" title="Santa Elena, Ecuador">Santa Elena</a> peninsula near the modern-day town of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Valdivia,_Ecuador&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Valdivia, Ecuador (page does not exist)">Valdivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> between 3500 BCE and 1800 BCE.
</p>
<h3><span id="Chav.C3.ADn"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Chavín">Chavín</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chavín">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chav%C3%ADn_culture" title="Chavín culture">Chavín culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chavinmuseolarco.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Chavinmuseolarco.jpg/220px-Chavinmuseolarco.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Chavinmuseolarco.jpg/330px-Chavinmuseolarco.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Chavinmuseolarco.jpg/440px-Chavinmuseolarco.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chavinmuseolarco.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chavín Gold Crown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_periods_of_Peru" title="Cultural periods of Peru">Formative Epoch</a> 1200–300 BCE (<a href="/wiki/Larco_Museum" title="Larco Museum">Larco Museum Collection</a>, Lima)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chav%C3%ADn_culture" title="Chavín culture">Chavín culture</a> is thought to have been primarily a religious movement. The culture apparently began in the Peruvian highlands and then spread outward throughout the country. The Chavín culture has very distinctive art styles, particularly in effigy pots, a number of which were in feline shapes. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chavin_de_Huantar" title="Chavin de Huantar">Chavin de Huantar</a> was an important ritual centre for Chavin Culture, dating to around 1,500 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nazca_culture" title="Nazca culture">Nazca culture</a> (also Nasca) was the <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_culture" title="Archaeological culture">archaeological culture</a> that flourished from 100 to 800 CE beside the dry southern coast of Peru in the river valleys of the <a href="/wiki/Nazca" title="Nazca">Rio Grande de Nazca</a> drainage and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ica_Region" title="Ica Region">Ica Valley</a> (Silverman and Proulx, 2002). Having been heavily influenced by the preceding <a href="/wiki/Paracas_culture" title="Paracas culture">Paracas culture</a>, which was known for extremely complex textiles, the Nazca produced an array of beautiful crafts and technologies such as ceramics, textiles, and <a href="/wiki/Geoglyph" title="Geoglyph">geoglyphs</a> (most commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Lines" title="Nazca Lines">Nazca lines</a>). They also built an impressive system of underground <a href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(water_supply)" title="Aqueduct (water supply)">aqueducts</a>, known as <i><a href="/wiki/Puquios" title="Puquios">puquios</a></i>, that still function today. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazca_Province" title="Nazca Province">Nazca Province</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ica_Region" title="Ica Region">Ica Region</a> was named for this people.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moche">Moche</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Moche">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moche_culture" title="Moche culture">Moche culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Condormuseolarco.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="250" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Condormuseolarco.jpg/200px-Condormuseolarco.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Condormuseolarco.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Condormuseolarco.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Moche culture is world-renowned for its pottery, in picture a <a href="/wiki/Andean_condor" title="Andean condor">Condor</a> from about 300 CE.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moche_(culture)" title="Moche (culture)">Moche</a> civilization (alternately, the Mochica culture, Early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chimu" title="Chimu">Chimu</a>, Pre-Chimu, Proto-Chimu, etc.) flourished in northern Peru from about 100 CE to 800 CE, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_periods_of_Peru" title="Cultural periods of Peru">Regional Development Epoch</a>. While this issue is the subject of some debate, many scholars contend that the Moche were not politically organized as a monolithic empire or state.   Rather, they were likely a group of autonomous polities that shared a common elite culture, as seen in the rich <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monumental</a> architecture that survive today. They are particularly noted for their elaborately painted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramics_(art)" title="Ceramics (art)">ceramics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> work, monumental constructions (<i><a href="/wiki/Huaca" title="Huaca">huacas</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Moche history may be broadly divided into three periods – the emergence of the Moche culture in Early Moche (CE 100–300), its expansion and florescence during Middle Moche (CE 300–600), and the urban nucleation and subsequent collapse in Late Moche (CE 500–750).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chachapoyas">Chachapoyas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chachapoyas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chachapoya_culture" title="Chachapoya culture">Chachapoya culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg/220px-Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg/330px-Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg/440px-Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ruinas-soloco_chachapoyas_amazonas_peru.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Walls of Soloco fortress, Chachapoyas, Peru.</div></div></div>
<p>The Chachapoyas, or the 'Cloud People', were an Andean civilization living in cloud forests of the Amazonas region of present-day northern Peru. The Incas conquered the Chachapoyas shortly before the arrival of the Spanish in Peru. The first firm evidence of their existence dates back to around 700 CE, although it is possible that they built a settlement called Gran Pajáten where some ceramics have been dated to 200 BCE. The largest Chacapoyan site discovered so far is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuelap" title="Kuelap">Kuelap</a>. A number of mummified burial sites have also been discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wari">Wari</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Wari">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wari_culture" title="Wari culture">Wari culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg/200px-Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg/300px-Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg/400px-Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Piquillacta_Archaeological_site_-_street.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pikillaqta administrative center, built by the Wari civilization in <a href="/wiki/Cusco" title="Cusco">Cusco</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wari_culture" title="Wari culture">Wari</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Huari</i>) were a <a href="/wiki/Periodization_of_pre-Columbian_Peru" title="Periodization of pre-Columbian Peru">Middle Horizon</a> civilization that flourished in the south-central <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> and coastal area of Peru, from about CE 500 to 1000. (The Wari culture is not to be confused with the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic" title="Ethnic">ethnic</a> group and language known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wari%27" title="Wari'">Wari'</a></i>, with which it has no known link.) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wari_ruins" title="Wari ruins">Wari</a>, as the former capital city was called, is located 11 km (6.8 mi) north-east of the city of <a href="/wiki/Ayacucho" title="Ayacucho">Ayacucho</a>. This city was the center of a civilization that covered much of the highlands and coast of Peru. The best-preserved remnants, beside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wari_ruins" title="Wari ruins">Wari Ruins</a>, are the recently discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Wari_ruins" title="Northern Wari ruins">Northern Wari ruins</a> near the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiclayo,_Peru" title="Chiclayo, Peru">Chiclayo</a>, and Cerro Baul in <a href="/wiki/Moquegua" title="Moquegua">Moquegua</a>. Also well-known are the Wari ruins of <i>Pikillaqta</i> ("Flea Town"), a short distance south-east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuzco" title="Cuzco">Cuzco</a> <i>en route</i> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tiwanaku">Tiwanaku</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tiwanaku">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tiwanaku_Empire" title="Tiwanaku Empire">Tiwanaku Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg/220px-Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg/330px-Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg/440px-Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zonnepoort_tiwanaku.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "<a href="/wiki/Gate_of_the_Sun" title="Gate of the Sun">Gate of the Sun</a>" built by the Tiwanaku culture</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tiwanaku" title="Tiwanaku">Tiwanaku</a> (Spanish: Tiahuanaco and Tiahuanacu) is an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">Pre-Columbian</a> archaeological site in western <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, South America. Tiwanaku is recognized by Andean scholars as one of the most important precursors to the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a>, flourishing as the ritual and administrative capital of a major state power for approximately five hundred years. The ruins of the ancient city state are near the south-eastern shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Paz_Department,_Bolivia" title="La Paz Department, Bolivia">La Paz Department</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingavi_Province" title="Ingavi Province">Ingavi Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiwanaku_Municipality" title="Tiwanaku Municipality">Tiwanaku Municipality</a>, about 72 km (45 mi) west of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Paz,_Bolivia" title="La Paz, Bolivia">La Paz</a>. The site was first recorded in written history by Spanish conquistador and self-acclaimed "first chronicler of the Indies" <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Cieza_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Pedro Cieza de León">Pedro Cieza de León</a>. Leon stumbled upon the remains of Tiwanaku in 1549 while searching for the Inca capital <a href="/wiki/Qullasuyu" title="Qullasuyu">Qullasuyu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kolata_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kolata-27">[27]</a></sup> Some have hypothesized that Tiwanaku's modern name is related to the <a href="/wiki/Aymara_language" title="Aymara language">Aymara</a> term <i>taypiqala</i>, meaning "stone in the center", alluding to the belief that it lay at the center of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancient_Skies_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ancient_Skies-28">[28]</a></sup> However, the name by which Tiwanaku was known to its inhabitants may have been lost, as the people of Tiwanaku had no written language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frommers_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frommers-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Genographic_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Genographic-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_cultures">Historical cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Historical cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Chim.C3.BA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Chimú">Chimú</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chimú">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chimor" title="Chimor">Chimor</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_(M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788)_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1814" data-file-width="1931" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_%28M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788%29_01.jpg/200px-Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_%28M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788%29_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_%28M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788%29_01.jpg/300px-Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_%28M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788%29_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_%28M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788%29_01.jpg/400px-Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_%28M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788%29_01.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vasija_chim%C3%BA_caballito_de_totora_(M._Am%C3%A9rica_10788)_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chimú vessel representing a fisherman on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caballitos_de_totora" title="Caballitos de totora">caballitos de totora</a> (1100–1400 CE)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chim%C3%BA_culture" title="Chimú culture">Chimú</a> were the residents of <a href="/wiki/Chimor" title="Chimor">Chimor</a>, with its capital at the city of <a href="/wiki/Chan_Chan" title="Chan Chan">Chan Chan</a>, a large adobe city in the <a href="/wiki/Moche_Valley" title="Moche Valley">Moche Valley</a> of present-day <a href="/wiki/Trujillo,_Peru" title="Trujillo, Peru">Trujillo, Peru</a>. The culture arose about 900 CE. The Inca ruler <a href="/wiki/Topa_Inca_Yupanqui" title="Topa Inca Yupanqui">Topa Inca Yupanqui</a> led a campaign which conquered the Chimú around 1470 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>This was just fifty years before the arrival of the Spanish in the region. Consequently, Spanish chroniclers were able to record accounts of Chimú culture from individuals who had lived before the Inca conquest. Similarly,  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> evidence suggest Chimor grew out of the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Moche_culture" title="Moche culture">Moche culture</a>; early Chimú pottery had some resemblance to that of the Moche. Their ceramics are all black, and their work in precious metals is very detailed and intricate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inca_Empire">Inca Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Inca Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2987" data-file-width="2448" decoding="async" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg/220px-Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg/330px-Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg/440px-Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Over_Machu_Picchu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of <a href="/wiki/Machu_Picchu" title="Machu Picchu">Machu Picchu</a> built by the Incas</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a>, or Incan Empire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Quechuan_languages" title="Quechuan languages">Quechua</a>: <i>Tawantinsuyu</i>), was the largest empire in <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">pre-Columbian America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The administrative, political and military center of the empire was located in <a href="/wiki/Cusco" title="Cusco">Cusco</a>. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Incas" title="History of the Incas">Inca civilization</a> arose from the Peruvian highlands sometime in the early 13th century, and the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Inca_State" title="Neo-Inca State">last Inca stronghold</a> was conquered by the Spanish in 1572. From 1438 to 1533 CE, the Incas used a variety of methods, from conquest to peaceful assimilation, to incorporate a large portion of western South America, centered on the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andean</a> mountain ranges, including Peru, southwest <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, western and south central <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, northwest <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, and a small part of southwest <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> into a state comparable to the historical <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empires</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muisca">Muisca</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Muisca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Muisca_Confederation" title="Muisca Confederation">Muisca Confederation</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Muisca_and_pre-Muisca_sites" title="List of Muisca and pre-Muisca sites">List of Muisca and pre-Muisca sites</a></div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muisca_people" title="Muisca people">Muisca</a> was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chibcha" title="Chibcha">Chibcha</a>-speaking people that formed the <a href="/wiki/Muisca_Confederation" title="Muisca Confederation">Muisca Confederation</a> in the central highlands of present-day <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>. They were encountered by the troops of <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Jim%C3%A9nez_de_Quesada" title="Gonzalo Jiménez de Quesada">Gonzalo Jiménez de Quesada</a>, in name of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Muisca" title="Spanish conquest of the Muisca">conquest</a> in the spring of 1537. The Muisca comprised two confederations: <a href="/wiki/Tunja" title="Tunja">Hunza</a> (present-day Tunja) was located in the northern area, whose sovereign was the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaque" title="Zaque">zaque</a></i>; and <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">Bacatá</a> the southern area, whose sovereign was the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zipa" title="Zipa">zipa</a></i>. Both confederations were located in the highlands of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Cundinamarca_Department" title="Cundinamarca Department">Cundinamarca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boyac%C3%A1_Department" title="Boyacá Department">Boyacá</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Altiplano_Cundiboyacense" title="Altiplano Cundiboyacense">Altiplano Cundiboyacense</a></i>) in the central area of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Oriental_(Colombia)" title="Cordillera Oriental (Colombia)">Eastern Ranges</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timoto-Cuica">Timoto-Cuica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andean_civilizations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Timoto-Cuica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Timoto%E2%80%93Cuica_people" title="Timoto–Cuica people">Timoto–Cuica people</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2056" data-file-width="3648" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG/220px-Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG/330px-Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG/440px-Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Campo_de_M%C3%A9rida.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Timoto-Cuica territory in present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida_State" title="Mérida State">Mérida, Venezuela</a></div></div></div>
<p>Timoto–Cuica people was composed primarily of two tribes, the <b>Timotes</b> and the <b>Cuicas</b>, that inhabited in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andean</a> region of western <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahoney_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-mahoney-34">[34]</a></sup> They were closely related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muisca_people" title="Muisca people">Muisca people</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, who spoke a <a href="/wiki/Chibcha_language" title="Chibcha language">Chibcha language</a>. The Timoto-Cuicas were not only composed of the Timoto and the Cuica tribes, but also the Mucuchíes, the Migures, the Tabares, and the Mucuñuques. Timoto-Cuica society was complex with pre-planned permanent villages, surrounded by irrigated, terraced fields. They also stored water in tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahoney_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-mahoney-34">[34]</a></sup> Their houses were made primarily of stone and wood with thatched roofs. They were peaceful, for the most part, and depended on growing crops. Regional crops included potatoes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulluco" title="Ulluco">ullucos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-art-35">[35]</a></sup> They left behind works of art, particularly anthropomorphic ceramics, but no major monuments. They spun vegetable fibers to weave into textiles and mats for housing. They are credited with having invented the <a href="/wiki/Arepa" title="Arepa">arepa</a>, a staple in <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_cuisine" title="Venezuelan cuisine">Venezuelan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colombian_cuisine" title="Colombian cuisine">Colombian cuisine</a>.
</p>
