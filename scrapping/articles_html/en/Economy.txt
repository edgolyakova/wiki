is an area of the <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goods_(economics)" title="Goods (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>. In general, it is defined as a social domain that emphasize the practices, discourses, and material expressions associated with the production, use, and management of <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">scarce resources</a>'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A given economy is a set of processes that involves its <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Values" title="Values">values</a>, education, technological evolution, history, <a href="/wiki/Social_organization" title="Social organization">social organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_structure" title="Political structure">political structure</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_systems" title="Legal systems">legal systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> as main factors. These factors give context, content, and set the conditions and parameters in which an economy functions. In other words, the economic domain is a social domain of interrelated human practices and transactions that does not stand alone.
</p><p>Economic agents can be individuals, <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organizations</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a>. Economic transactions occur when two groups or parties agree to the value or price of the transacted good or service, commonly expressed in a certain <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a>. However, monetary transactions only account for a small part of the economic domain.
</p><p>Economic activity is spurred by production which uses natural resources, labor and capital. It has changed over time due to <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a> (new products, services, processes, expanding markets, diversification of markets, niche markets, increases revenue functions) such as, that which produces intellectual property and changes in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_relations" title="Industrial relations">industrial relations</a> (most notably <a href="/wiki/Child_labour" title="Child labour">child labor</a> being replaced in some parts of the world with <a href="/wiki/Universal_access_to_education" title="Universal access to education">universal access to education</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>economy</i> in English is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a>'s <span title="Middle French (ca. 1400-1600)-language text"><i lang="frm">yconomie</i></span>, which itself derived from the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a>'s <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oeconomia" title="wikt:oeconomia">oeconomia</a></i></span>. The Latin word has its origin at the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>'s <i>oikonomia</i> or <i>oikonomos</i>. The word's first part <i>oikos</i> means "house", and the second part <i>nemein</i> means "to manage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most frequently used current sense, denoting "the economic system of a country or an area", seems not to have developed until the 1650s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">Economic history</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_roots">Earliest roots</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BosraMosaicCamels.png"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="1140" decoding="async" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/BosraMosaicCamels.png/220px-BosraMosaicCamels.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/BosraMosaicCamels.png/330px-BosraMosaicCamels.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/BosraMosaicCamels.png/440px-BosraMosaicCamels.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BosraMosaicCamels.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ancient Roman mosaic from <a href="/wiki/Bosra" title="Bosra">Bosra</a>, depicting a merchant leading camels through the desert</div></div></div>
<p>As long as someone has been making, supplying and distributing goods or services, there has been some sort of economy; economies grew larger as societies grew and became more complex. <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> developed a large-scale economy based on <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a>, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">Babylonians</a> and their neighboring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_states" title="City states">city states</a> later developed the earliest system of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> as we think of, in terms of rules/laws on <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>, legal contracts and law codes relating to business practices, and private property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheila_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheila-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Babylonians and their city state neighbors developed forms of economics comparable to currently used civil society (law) concepts. They developed the first known codified legal and administrative systems, complete with courts, jails, and government records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yale2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-yale2-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ancient economy was mainly based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence_farming" title="Subsistence farming">subsistence farming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Shekel" title="Shekel">Shekel</a> are the first to refer to a unit of weight and currency, used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semitic_peoples" title="Semitic peoples">Semitic peoples</a>. The first usage of the term came from <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> circa 3000 BC. and referred to a specific mass of <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> which related other values in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metric</a> such as silver, bronze, copper, etc. A barley/shekel was originally both a unit of <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> and a unit of weight, just as the British Pound was originally a unit denominating a one-pound mass of silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brony_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-brony-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>For most people, the exchange of goods occurred through social relationships. There were also traders who bartered in the marketplaces. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>, where the present English word 'economy' originated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> many people were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_slaves" title="Bond slaves">bond slaves</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeholder_(government)" title="Freeholder (government)">freeholders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The economic discussion was driven by <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">scarcity</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span></h3>
<p>In the Middle Ages, what is now known as an economy was not far from the subsistence level. Most exchange occurred within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_groups" title="Social groups">social groups</a>. On top of this, the great conquerors raised what we now call <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> (from <i>ventura</i>, ital.; <i>risk</i>) to finance their captures. The <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> should be refunded by the goods they would bring up in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. The discoveries of <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> (1254–1324), <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> (1451–1506) and <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> (1469–1524) led to a first <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">global</a> economy. The first <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">enterprises</a> were trading establishments. In 1513, the first <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">stock exchange</a> was founded in <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>. Economy at the time meant primarily <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>.
</p><p>The European captures became branches of the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> states, the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonies" title="Colonies">colonies</a>. The rising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nation-state" title="Nation-state">nation-states</a> <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> tried to control the trade through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Custom_duties" title="Custom duties">custom duties</a> and (from <i>mercator</i>, lat.: <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a>) was a first approach to intermediate between private wealth and <a href="/wiki/Public_interest" title="Public interest">public interest</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a> in Europe allowed states to use the immense property of the church for the development of towns. The influence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobles" title="Nobles">nobles</a> decreased. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretaries_of_State" title="Secretaries of State">Secretaries of State</a> for economy started their work. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bankers" title="Bankers">Bankers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Amschel_Mayer_Rothschild" title="Amschel Mayer Rothschild">Amschel Mayer Rothschild</a> (1773–1855) started to finance national projects such as wars and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. Economy from then on meant national economy as a topic for the economic activities of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens" title="Citizens">citizens</a> of a state.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span></h3>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economist</a> in the true modern meaning of the word was the Scotsman <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> (1723–1790) who was inspired partly by the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Physiocracy" title="Physiocracy">physiocracy</a>, a reaction to mercantilism and also later Economics student, Adam Mari.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He defined the elements of a national economy: <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> are offered at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_price" title="Natural price">natural price</a> generated by the use of <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competition</a> - <a href="/wiki/Supply_and_demand" title="Supply and demand">supply and demand</a> - and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_of_labor" title="Division of labor">division of labor</a>. He maintained that the basic motive for <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> is human self-interest. The so-called self-interest hypothesis became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropological" title="Anthropological">anthropological</a> basis for economics. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> (1766–1834) transferred the idea of supply and demand to the problem of <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> was a period from the 18th to the 19th century where major changes in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> had a profound effect on the socioeconomic and cultural conditions starting in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, then subsequently spreading throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, and eventually the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The onset of the Industrial Revolution marked a major turning point in human history; almost every aspect of daily life was eventually influenced in some way.
In Europe wild <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> started to replace the system of <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a> (today: <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a>) and led to <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>. The period today is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> because the system of <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">Production</a>, production and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_of_labor" title="Division of labor">division of labor</a> enabled the <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span></h3>
<p>The contemporary concept of "the economy" wasn't popularly known until the American <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> in the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-invention_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-invention-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the chaos of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_War" title="World War">World Wars</a> and the devastating Great Depression, policymakers searched for new ways of controlling the course of the economy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This was explored and discussed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_August_von_Hayek" title="Friedrich August von Hayek">Friedrich August von Hayek</a> (1899–1992) and <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a> (1912–2006) who pleaded for a global <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> and are supposed to be the fathers of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boas20092_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boas20092-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the prevailing view was that held by <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> (1883–1946), who argued for a stronger control of the <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">markets</a> by the state. The theory that the state can alleviate economic problems and instigate economic growth through state manipulation of aggregate demand is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keynesianism" title="Keynesianism">Keynesianism</a> in his honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the late 1950s, the economic growth in America and Europe—often called <a href="/wiki/Wirtschaftswunder" title="Wirtschaftswunder">Wirtschaftswunder</a> (ger: <i>economic miracle</i>) —brought up a new form of economy: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_consumption_economy" title="Mass consumption economy">mass consumption economy</a>. In 1958, <a href="/wiki/John_Kenneth_Galbraith" title="John Kenneth Galbraith">John Kenneth Galbraith</a> (1908–2006) was the first to speak of an <a href="/wiki/Affluent_society" title="Affluent society">affluent society</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Affluent_Society" title="The Affluent Society">The Affluent Society</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In most of the countries the economic system is called a <a href="/wiki/Social_market_economy" title="Social market economy">social market economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_(Ank_Kumar)_02.jpg"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_%28Ank_Kumar%29_02.jpg/220px-Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_%28Ank_Kumar%29_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_%28Ank_Kumar%29_02.jpg/330px-Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_%28Ank_Kumar%29_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_%28Ank_Kumar%29_02.jpg/440px-Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_%28Ank_Kumar%29_02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange_(Ank_Kumar)_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange" title="Frankfurt Stock Exchange">Frankfurt Stock Exchange</a> in 2015</div></div></div>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the fall of the Iron Curtain</a> and the transition of the countries of the Eastern Bloc towards democratic government and market economies, the idea of the <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial society</a> is brought into importance as its role is to mark together the significance that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_sector" title="Service sector">service sector</a> receives instead of industrialization. Some attribute the first use of this term to Daniel Bell's 1973 book, <i>The Coming of Post-Industrial Society</i>, while others attribute it to social philosopher Ivan Illich's book, <i><a href="/wiki/Tools_for_Conviviality" title="Tools for Conviviality">Tools for Conviviality</a></i>. The term is also applied in philosophy to designate the fading of <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a> in the late 90s and especially in the beginning of the 21st century.
</p><p>With the spread of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> as a mass media and communication medium especially after 2000–2001, the idea for the Internet and <a href="/wiki/Information_economy" title="Information economy">information economy</a> is given place because of the growing importance of <a href="/wiki/E-commerce" title="E-commerce">e-commerce</a> and electronic businesses, also the term for a global information society as understanding of a new type of "all-connected" society is created. In the late 2000s, the new type of economies and economic expansions of countries like <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Century" title="Chinese Century">China</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazil_as_an_emerging_superpower" title="Brazil as an emerging superpower">Brazil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India_as_a_potential_superpower" title="India as a potential superpower">India</a> bring attention and interest to different from the usually dominating Western type economies and economic models.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">Elements</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a> is one where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> are produced and exchanged according to <a href="/wiki/Demand" title="Demand">demand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supply_(economics)" title="Supply (economics)">supply</a> between participants (economic agents) by <a href="/wiki/Barter" title="Barter">barter</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debit" title="Debit">debit</a> <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> accepted within the network, such as a unit of currency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">planned economy</a> is one where political agents directly control what is produced and how it is sold and distributed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economy</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-carbon" title="Low-carbon">low-carbon</a> and resource efficient. In a green economy, growth in income and employment is driven by public and private investments that reduce carbon emissions and pollution, enhance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_(physics)" title="Energy efficiency (physics)">energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Resource_efficiency" title="Resource efficiency">resource efficiency</a>, and prevent the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gig_economy" title="Gig economy">gig economy</a> is one in which short-term jobs are assigned or chosen on-demand. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_economy" title="Global economy">global economy</a> refers to humanity's <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic system or systems</a> overall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An <a href="/wiki/Informal_economy" title="Informal economy">informal economy</a> is neither taxed nor monitored by any form of government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sectors">Sectors</span></h3>
<p>The economy may be considered as having developed through the following phases or degrees of precedence:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2021)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient economy</a> was mainly based on <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_economy" title="Subsistence economy">subsistence</a> farming.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> phase lessened the role of subsistence farming, converting it to more <a href="/wiki/Extensive_farming" title="Extensive farming">extensive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">mono-cultural</a> forms of agriculture in the last three centuries. The economic growth took place mostly in mining, construction and manufacturing industries. <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">Commerce</a> became more significant due to the need for improved exchange and distribution of produce throughout the community.</li>
<li>In the economies of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_society" title="Consumer society">consumer societies</a> phase there is a growing part played by <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_economy" title="Knowledge economy">knowledge economy</a>.</li></ul>
<p>In modern economies, these phase precedences are somewhat differently expressed by the <a href="/wiki/Three-sector_model" title="Three-sector model">three-sector model</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">Primary</a>: Involves the extraction and production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raw_materials" title="Raw materials">raw materials</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">Secondary</a>: Involves the transformation of raw or intermediate materials into goods e.g. manufacturing steel into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cars" title="Cars">cars</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a> into clothing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">Tertiary</a>: Involves the provision of services to consumers and businesses, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby-sitting" title="Baby-sitting">baby-sitting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">cinema</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banking" title="Banking">banking</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Other sectors of the developed community include:
</p>
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a> or state sector (which usually includes: parliament, law-courts and government centers, various emergency services, public health, shelters for impoverished and threatened people, transport facilities, air/sea ports, post-natal care, hospitals, schools, libraries, museums, preserved historical buildings, parks/gardens, nature-reserves, some universities, national sports grounds/stadiums, national arts/concert-halls or theaters and centers for various religions).</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a> or privately run businesses.</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_sector" title="Voluntary sector">voluntary sector</a> or social sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indicators">Indicators</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_indicator" title="Economic indicator">Economic indicator</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) of a country is a measure of the size of its economy, or more specifically, monetary measure of the market value of all the <a href="/wiki/Final_good" title="Final good">final goods</a> and services produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The most conventional economic analysis of a country relies heavily on economic indicators like the GDP and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a>. While often useful, GDP only includes economic activity for which money is exchanged.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Due to the growing importance of the financial sector in modern times,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the term <i>real economy</i> is used by analysts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> as well as politicians<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> to denote the part of the economy that is concerned with the actual production of goods and services,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> as ostensibly contrasted with the <i>paper economy</i>, or the financial side of the economy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> which is concerned with buying and selling on the financial markets. Alternate and long-standing terminology distinguishes measures of an economy expressed in <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">real</a> values (adjusted for <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>), such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_GDP" title="Real GDP">real GDP</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">nominal</a> values (unadjusted for inflation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies">Studies</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">Economics</a></div>
<p>The study of economics are roughly divided into <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microeconomics" title="Microeconomics">microeconomics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Today, the range of fields of study examining the economy revolves around the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> of economics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> but may also include <a href="/wiki/Economic_sociology" title="Economic sociology">sociology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press.princeton.edu_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-press.princeton.edu-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">history</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Economic_anthropology" title="Economic anthropology">anthropology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_geography" title="Economic geography">geography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Practical fields directly related to the human activities involving <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">exchange</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> as a whole are <a href="/wiki/Business_economics" title="Business economics">business</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Engineering_economics" title="Engineering economics">engineering</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dharmaraj2010_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dharmaraj2010-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Economic_policy" title="Economic policy">government</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Health_economics" title="Health economics">health care</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
