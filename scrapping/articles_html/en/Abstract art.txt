<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Abstract art</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Art with a degree of independence from visual references in the world</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Delaunay,_1913,_Premier_Disque,_134_cm,_52.7_inches,_Private_collection.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="893" decoding="async" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Robert_Delaunay%2C_1913%2C_Premier_Disque%2C_134_cm%2C_52.7_inches%2C_Private_collection.jpg/300px-Robert_Delaunay%2C_1913%2C_Premier_Disque%2C_134_cm%2C_52.7_inches%2C_Private_collection.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Robert_Delaunay%2C_1913%2C_Premier_Disque%2C_134_cm%2C_52.7_inches%2C_Private_collection.jpg/450px-Robert_Delaunay%2C_1913%2C_Premier_Disque%2C_134_cm%2C_52.7_inches%2C_Private_collection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Robert_Delaunay%2C_1913%2C_Premier_Disque%2C_134_cm%2C_52.7_inches%2C_Private_collection.jpg/600px-Robert_Delaunay%2C_1913%2C_Premier_Disque%2C_134_cm%2C_52.7_inches%2C_Private_collection.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Delaunay,_1913,_Premier_Disque,_134_cm,_52.7_inches,_Private_collection.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Robert_Delaunay" title="Robert Delaunay">Robert Delaunay</a>, 1912–13, <i>Le Premier Disque</i>, 134 cm (52.7 in.), private collection</div></div></div>
<p><b>Abstract art</b> uses  <a href="/wiki/Visual_language" title="Visual language">visual language</a> of shape, form, color and line to create a composition which may exist with a degree of independence from visual references in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_art" title="Western art">Western art</a> had been, from the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> up to the middle of the 19th century, underpinned by the logic of <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">perspective</a> and an attempt to reproduce an illusion of visible reality. By the end of the 19th century many artists felt a need to create a new kind of art which would encompass the fundamental changes taking place in <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. The sources from which individual artists drew their theoretical arguments were diverse, and reflected the social and intellectual preoccupations in all areas of Western culture at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Abstract art, non-figurative art, non-objective art, and non-representational art are all closely related terms. They have similar, but perhaps not identical, meanings.
</p><p>Abstraction indicates a departure from reality in depiction of <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">imagery</a> in art. This departure from accurate representation can be slight, partial, or complete. Abstraction exists along a continuum. Even art that aims for verisimilitude of the highest degree can be said to be abstract, at least theoretically, since perfect representation is impossible. Artwork which takes liberties, e.g. altering color or form in ways that are conspicuous, can be said to be partially abstract. Total abstraction bears no trace of any reference to anything recognizable. In <a href="/wiki/Geometric_abstraction" title="Geometric abstraction">geometric abstraction</a>, for instance, one is unlikely to find references to naturalistic entities. <a href="/wiki/Figurative_art" title="Figurative art">Figurative art</a> and total abstraction are almost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_exclusive_events" title="Mutually exclusive events">mutually exclusive</a>. But figurative and <a href="/wiki/Representation_(arts)" title="Representation (arts)">representational</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Realism_(visual_arts)" title="Realism (visual arts)">realistic</a>) art often contain partial abstraction.
</p><p>Both geometric abstraction and <a href="/wiki/Lyrical_abstraction" title="Lyrical abstraction">lyrical abstraction</a> are often totally abstract. Among the very numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_movements" title="Art movements">art movements</a> that embody partial abstraction would be for instance <a href="/wiki/Fauvism" title="Fauvism">fauvism</a> in which color is conspicuously and deliberately altered vis-a-vis reality, and <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">cubism</a>, which alters the forms of the real-life entities depicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric">Prehistoric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistoric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_art" title="Prehistoric art">Prehistoric art</a></div>
<p>Much of the art of earlier cultures – signs and marks on pottery, textiles, and inscriptions and paintings on rock – used simple, geometric and linear forms which might have had a symbolic or decorative purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is at this level of visual meaning that abstract art communicates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One can enjoy the beauty of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">Chinese calligraphy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calligraphy" title="Islamic calligraphy">Islamic calligraphy</a> without being able to read it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_antiquity">Classical antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classical antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_world">Islamic world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Islamic world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_(written_portrait_of_the_Prophet)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3976" data-file-width="2342" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_%28written_portrait_of_the_Prophet%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/170px-Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_%28written_portrait_of_the_Prophet%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_%28written_portrait_of_the_Prophet%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/255px-Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_%28written_portrait_of_the_Prophet%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_%28written_portrait_of_the_Prophet%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/340px-Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_%28written_portrait_of_the_Prophet%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Signed_Abd%C3%BClkadir_%C5%9E%C3%BCkri_Efendi_-_Hilye-i_%C5%9Eerif_(written_portrait_of_the_Prophet)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <i><a href="/wiki/Hilya" title="Hilya">hilya</a></i>, a decorated description of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>’s physical appearance, dating to the 19th century.</div></div></div>
<p>Islam's <a href="/wiki/Aniconism_in_Islam" title="Aniconism in Islam">sometimes negative view of figurative art</a> has led to the proliferation of complex non-figurative artistic expression across the Islamic world. Non-figurative Islamic art is nearly as old as Islam itself, and some of the oldest examples of it can be found on such early buildings as the <a href="/wiki/Dome_of_the_Rock" title="Dome of the Rock">Dome of the Rock</a>. While Islamic art may have reservations about painting humans and animals, it does not, however, feel the same about plants and inanimate objects.
</p><p>Islamic art traditionally revolves around <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calligraphy" title="Islamic calligraphy">calligraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islamic_geometric_patterns" title="Islamic geometric patterns">tessellating geometric patterns</a>, and vegetal motifs called <a href="/wiki/Arabesque" title="Arabesque">arabesques</a>. These elements can be found in all Islamic applied arts, including <a href="/wiki/Islamic_architecture" title="Islamic architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_rug" title="Oriental rug">rugmaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islamic_pottery" title="Islamic pottery">pottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islamic_glass" title="Islamic glass">glassmaking</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_art#Metalwork" title="Islamic art">metalwork</a>, as well as decorating the borders of otherwise figurative paintings.
</p><p>As a result of the extensive dialogue between the Christian and Islamic world brought about by the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>, Islamic patterns and techniques would also come to be applied to European decorative arts, especially in Italy and Spain. Prominent examples include Spanish <a href="/wiki/Mud%C3%A9jar_art" title="Mudéjar art">Mudéjar art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venetian_Gothic_architecture" title="Venetian Gothic architecture">Venetian Gothic architecture</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="524" data-file-width="1044" decoding="async" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg/220px-Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg/330px-Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg/440px-Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yujian_ink_Mercadodemonta%C3%B1a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Mountain market, clearing Mist</i>, Yu Jian, China</div></div></div>
<p>In Chinese painting, abstraction can be traced to the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> painter Wang Mo (王墨), who is credited to have invented the splashed-ink painting style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> While none of his paintings remain, this style is clearly seen in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> Paintings. The <a href="/wiki/Chan_Buddhism" title="Chan Buddhism">Chan buddhist</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Liang_Kai" title="Liang Kai">Liang Kai</a> (梁楷, c. 1140–1210) applied the style to figure painting in his "Immortal in splashed ink" in which accurate representation is sacrificed to enhance spontaneity linked to the non-rational mind of the enlightened. A late Song painter named Yu Jian, adept to <a href="/wiki/Tiantai" title="Tiantai">Tiantai buddhism</a>, created a series of splashed ink landscapes that eventually inspired many Japanese Zen painters. His paintings show heavily misty mountains in which the shapes of the objects are barely visible and extremely simplified. This type of painting was continued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sesshu_Toyo" title="Sesshu Toyo">Sesshu Toyo</a> in his later years.
</p><p>Another instance of abstraction in Chinese painting is seen in Zhu Derun's <i>Cosmic Circle</i>. On the left side of this painting is a pine tree in rocky soil, its branches laced with vines that extend in a disorderly manner to the right side of the painting in which a perfect circle (probably made with help of a compass<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>) floats in the void. The painting is a reflection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daoism" title="Daoism">Daoist</a> metaphysics in which chaos and reality are complementary stages of the regular course of nature.
</p><p>In Tokugawa Japan, some Zen monk-painters created <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enso" title="Enso">Enso</a>, a circle who represents the absolute enlightenment. Usually made in one spontaneous brush stroke, it became the paradigm of the minimalist aesthetic that guided part of the Zen painting.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">Post-Impressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spiritualist_art" title="Spiritualist art">Spiritualist art</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2718" data-file-width="2024" decoding="async" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg/220px-James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg/330px-James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg/440px-James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James_Abbot_McNeill_Whistler_012.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_McNeill_Whistler" title="James McNeill Whistler">James McNeill Whistler</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturne_in_Black_and_Gold:_The_Falling_Rocket" title="Nocturne in Black and Gold: The Falling Rocket">Nocturne in Black and Gold: The Falling Rocket</a></i> (1874)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div></div></div><p>Patronage from the church diminished and private patronage from the public became more capable of providing a livelihood for artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Three <a href="/wiki/Art_movement" title="Art movement">art movements</a> which contributed to the development of abstract art were <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a>. Artistic independence for artists was advanced during the 19th century. An <i>objective interest in what is seen</i>, can be discerned from the paintings of <a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">John Constable</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J_M_W_Turner" title="J M W Turner">J M W Turner</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camille_Corot" title="Camille Corot">Camille Corot</a> and from them to the Impressionists who continued the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plein_air" title="Plein air">plein air</a></i> painting of the <a href="/wiki/Barbizon_school" title="Barbizon school">Barbizon school</a>.
</p><p>Early intimations of a new art had been made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_McNeill_Whistler" title="James McNeill Whistler">James McNeill Whistler</a> who, in his painting <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturne_in_Black_and_Gold:_The_falling_Rocket" title="Nocturne in Black and Gold: The falling Rocket">Nocturne in Black and Gold: The falling Rocket</a></i>, (1872), placed greater emphasis on visual sensation than the depiction of objects. Even earlier than that, with her 'spirit' drawings, <a href="/wiki/Georgiana_Houghton" title="Georgiana Houghton">Georgiana Houghton</a>'s choice to work with abstract shapes correlate with the unnatural nature of her subject, in a time when abstraction” isn't yet a concept (she organized an exhibit in 1871).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionist</a> painters explored the bold use of paint surface, drawing distortions and exaggerations, and intense color. Expressionists produced emotionally charged paintings that were reactions to and perceptions of contemporary experience; and reactions to <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a> and other more conservative directions of late 19th-century painting. The Expressionists drastically changed the emphasis on subject matter in favor of the portrayal of psychological states of being. Although artists like <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">Edvard Munch</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Ensor" title="James Ensor">James Ensor</a> drew influences principally from the work of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Impressionists" title="Post-Impressionists">Post-Impressionists</a> they were instrumental to the advent of abstraction in the 20th century.
<a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a> had begun as an Impressionist but his aim – to make a logical construction of reality based on a view from a single point,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> with modulated color in flat areas – became the basis of a new visual art, later to be developed into <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a> by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">Georges Braque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>.
</p><p>Additionally in the late 19th century in Eastern Europe <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mysticism</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernist</a> religious philosophy as expressed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theosophy_(Blavatskian)" title="Theosophy (Blavatskian)">theosophist</a> <a href="/wiki/Helena_Blavatsky" title="Helena Blavatsky">Mme. Blavatsky</a> had a profound impact on pioneer <a href="/wiki/Geometric_abstraction" title="Geometric abstraction">geometric</a> artists like <a href="/wiki/Hilma_af_Klint" title="Hilma af Klint">Hilma af Klint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>. The mystical teaching of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georges_Gurdjieff" title="Georges Gurdjieff">Georges Gurdjieff</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P.D._Ouspensky" title="P.D. Ouspensky">P.D. Ouspensky</a> also had an important influence on the early formations of the geometric abstract styles of <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a> and his colleagues in the early 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spiritualism" title="Spiritualism">spiritualism</a> also inspired the abstract art of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasimir_Malevich" title="Kasimir Malevich">Kasimir Malevich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka" title="František Kupka">František Kupka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span id="Post-Impressionism.2C_Fauvism.2C_and_Cubism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Impressionism,_Fauvism,_and_Cubism">Post-Impressionism, Fauvism, and Cubism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post-Impressionism, Fauvism, and Cubism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1351" data-file-width="1881" decoding="async" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg/300px-Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg/450px-Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg/600px-Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Francis_Picabia_-_Caoutchouc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">Francis Picabia</a>, c. 1909, <i><a href="/wiki/Caoutchouc_(Picabia)" title="Caoutchouc (Picabia)">Caoutchouc</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Centre_Pompidou" title="Centre Pompidou">Centre Pompidou</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_national_d%27art_moderne" title="Musée national d'art moderne">Musée national d'art moderne</a>, Paris</div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">Post-Impressionism</a> as practiced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Seurat" title="Georges Seurat">Georges Seurat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a> had an enormous impact on <a href="/wiki/20th-century_art" title="20th-century art">20th-century art</a> and led to the advent of 20th-century abstraction. The heritage of painters like Van Gogh, Cézanne, Gauguin, and Seurat was essential for the development of <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a>. At the beginning of the 20th century <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Henri Matisse</a> and several other young artists including the pre-cubist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">Georges Braque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Derain" title="André Derain">André Derain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Dufy" title="Raoul Dufy">Raoul Dufy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_de_Vlaminck" title="Maurice de Vlaminck">Maurice de Vlaminck</a> revolutionized the Paris art world with "wild", multi-colored, expressive landscapes and figure paintings that the critics called <a href="/wiki/Fauvism" title="Fauvism">Fauvism</a>. With his expressive use of color and his free and imaginative drawing Henri Matisse comes very close to pure abstraction in <i>French Window at Collioure</i> (1914), <i><a href="/wiki/View_of_Notre-Dame" title="View of Notre-Dame">View of Notre-Dame</a></i> (1914), and <i><a href="/wiki/Le_rideau_jaune" title="Le rideau jaune">The Yellow Curtain</a></i> from 1915. The raw language of color as developed by the Fauves directly influenced another pioneer of abstraction, <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>.
</p><p>Although the exact meaning of <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a> ultimately depends upon subject matter, it became, along with <a href="/wiki/Fauvism" title="Fauvism">Fauvism</a>, the art movement that directly opened the door to abstraction in the 20th century. <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a> made his first <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">cubist</a> paintings based on Cézanne's idea that all depiction of nature can be reduced to three solids: <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cone_(geometry)" title="Cone (geometry)">cone</a>. With the painting <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Demoiselles_d%27Avignon" title="Les Demoiselles d'Avignon">Les Demoiselles d'Avignon</a></i> (1907), Picasso dramatically created a new and radical picture depicting a raw and primitive brothel scene with five prostitutes, violently painted women, reminiscent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_tribal_masks" title="African tribal masks">African tribal masks</a> and his own new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubist" title="Cubist">Cubist</a> inventions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic_cubism" title="Analytic cubism">Analytic cubism</a> was jointly developed by Pablo Picasso and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">Georges Braque</a>, from about 1908 through 1912. Analytic cubism, the first clear manifestation of cubism, was followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_cubism" title="Synthetic cubism">Synthetic cubism</a>, practiced by Braque, Picasso, <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Léger">Fernand Léger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gris" title="Juan Gris">Juan Gris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gleizes" title="Albert Gleizes">Albert Gleizes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a> and others into the 1920s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_cubism" title="Synthetic cubism">Synthetic cubism</a> is characterized by the introduction of different textures, surfaces, <a href="/wiki/Collage" title="Collage">collage</a> elements, <a href="/wiki/Papier_coll%C3%A9" title="Papier collé">papier collé</a> and a large variety of merged subject matter. The collage artists like <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">Kurt Schwitters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">Man Ray</a> and others taking the clue from <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a> were instrumental to the development of the movement called <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dada</a>.
</p><p>The Italian poet <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Tommaso_Marinetti" title="Filippo Tommaso Marinetti">Filippo Tommaso Marinetti</a> published the <a href="/wiki/Manifesto_of_Futurism" title="Manifesto of Futurism">Manifesto of Futurism</a> in 1909, which later inspired artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlo_Carra" title="Carlo Carra">Carlo Carra</a> in <i>Painting of Sounds, Noises and Smells</i> and <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Boccioni" title="Umberto Boccioni">Umberto Boccioni</a> <i>Train in Motion</i>, 1911, to a further stage of abstraction that would, along with <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>, profoundly influenced art movements throughout Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Abstract_Art">Early Abstract Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early Abstract Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka,_1912,_Amorpha,_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_(Fugue_in_Two_Colors),_210_x_200_cm,_Narodni_Galerie,_Prague.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2813" data-file-width="2933" decoding="async" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka%2C_1912%2C_Amorpha%2C_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_%28Fugue_in_Two_Colors%29%2C_210_x_200_cm%2C_Narodni_Galerie%2C_Prague.jpg/220px-Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka%2C_1912%2C_Amorpha%2C_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_%28Fugue_in_Two_Colors%29%2C_210_x_200_cm%2C_Narodni_Galerie%2C_Prague.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka%2C_1912%2C_Amorpha%2C_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_%28Fugue_in_Two_Colors%29%2C_210_x_200_cm%2C_Narodni_Galerie%2C_Prague.jpg/330px-Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka%2C_1912%2C_Amorpha%2C_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_%28Fugue_in_Two_Colors%29%2C_210_x_200_cm%2C_Narodni_Galerie%2C_Prague.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka%2C_1912%2C_Amorpha%2C_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_%28Fugue_in_Two_Colors%29%2C_210_x_200_cm%2C_Narodni_Galerie%2C_Prague.jpg/440px-Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka%2C_1912%2C_Amorpha%2C_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_%28Fugue_in_Two_Colors%29%2C_210_x_200_cm%2C_Narodni_Galerie%2C_Prague.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka,_1912,_Amorpha,_fugue_en_deux_couleurs_(Fugue_in_Two_Colors),_210_x_200_cm,_Narodni_Galerie,_Prague.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka" title="František Kupka">František Kupka</a>, <i>Amorpha, Fugue en deux couleurs</i> (<i>Fugue in Two Colors</i>), 1912, oil on canvas, 210 x 200 cm, Narodni Galerie, Prague. Published in <i>Au Salon d'Automne "Les Indépendants"</i> 1912, Exhibited at the 1912 Salon d'Automne, Paris.</div></div></div>
<p>During the 1912 Salon de la <a href="/wiki/Section_d%27Or" title="Section d'Or">Section d'Or</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka" title="František Kupka">František Kupka</a> exhibited the abstract painting <i>Amorpha, Fugue en deux couleurs</i> (<i>Fugue in Two Colors</i>) (1912), the poet <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Apollinaire" title="Guillaume Apollinaire">Guillaume Apollinaire</a> named the work of several artists including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Delaunay" title="Robert Delaunay">Robert Delaunay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orphism_(art)" title="Orphism (art)">Orphism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He defined it as, "the art of painting new structures out of elements that have not been borrowed from the visual sphere, but had been created entirely by the artist...it is a pure art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the turn of the century, cultural connections between artists of the major European cities had become extremely active as they strove to create an art form equal to the high aspirations of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a>. Ideas were able to cross-fertilize by means of artist's books, exhibitions and <a href="/wiki/Art_manifesto" title="Art manifesto">manifestos</a> so that many sources were open to experimentation and discussion, and formed a basis for a diversity of modes of abstraction. The following extract from <i>The World Backwards</i> gives some impression of the inter-connectedness of culture at the time: "<a href="/wiki/David_Burliuk" title="David Burliuk">David Burliuk</a>'s knowledge of modern art movements must have been extremely up-to-date, for the second <a href="/wiki/Russian_avant-garde" title="Russian avant-garde">Knave of Diamonds exhibition</a>, held in January 1912 (in Moscow) included not only paintings sent from Munich, but some members of the German <a href="/wiki/Die_Br%C3%BCcke" title="Die Brücke">Die Brücke</a> group, while from Paris came work by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Delaunay" title="Robert Delaunay">Robert Delaunay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Henri Matisse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Léger">Fernand Léger</a>, as well as Picasso. During the Spring David Burliuk gave two lectures on cubism and planned a polemical publication, which the Knave of Diamonds was to finance. He went abroad in May and came back determined to rival the almanac <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Blaue_Reiter" title="Der Blaue Reiter">Der Blaue Reiter</a></i> which had emerged from the printers while he was in Germany".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1909 to 1913 many experimental works in the search for this 'pure art' had been created by a number of artists: <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">Francis Picabia</a> painted <i><a href="/wiki/Caoutchouc_(Picabia)" title="Caoutchouc (Picabia)">Caoutchouc</a></i>, c. 1909,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spring" title="The Spring">The Spring</a></i>, 1912,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>Dances at the Spring</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and <i>The Procession, Seville</i>, 1912;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a> painted <i>Untitled (First Abstract Watercolor)</i>, 1913,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Improvisation 21A</i>, the <i>Impression</i> series, and <i>Picture with a Circle</i> (1911);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kupka" title="František Kupka">František Kupka</a> had painted the Orphist works, <i>Discs of Newton</i> (Study for <i>Fugue in Two Colors</i>), 1912<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and <i>Amorpha, Fugue en deux couleurs</i> (<i>Fugue in Two Colors</i>), 1912; <a href="/wiki/Robert_Delaunay" title="Robert Delaunay">Robert Delaunay</a> painted a series entitled <i>Simultaneous Windows</i> and <i>Formes Circulaires, Soleil n°2</i> (1912–13);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opold_Survage" title="Léopold Survage">Léopold Survage</a> created <i>Colored Rhythm</i> (Study for the film), 1913;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>, painted <i>Tableau No. 1</i> and <i>Composition No. 11</i>, 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>And the search continued: The <a href="/wiki/Rayonism" title="Rayonism">Rayist</a> (Luchizm) drawings of <a href="/wiki/Natalia_Goncharova" title="Natalia Goncharova">Natalia Goncharova</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Larionov" title="Mikhail Larionov">Mikhail Larionov</a>, used lines like rays of light to make a construction. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasimir_Malevich" title="Kasimir Malevich">Kasimir Malevich</a> completed his first entirely abstract work, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suprematist" title="Suprematist">Suprematist</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Square_(painting)" title="Black Square (painting)">Black Square</a></i>, in 1915. Another of the Suprematist group' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liubov_Popova" title="Liubov Popova">Liubov Popova</a>, created the Architectonic Constructions and Spatial Force Constructions between 1916 and 1921. <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a> was evolving his abstract language, of horizontal and vertical lines with rectangles of color, between 1915 and 1919, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Plasticism" title="Neo-Plasticism">Neo-Plasticism</a> was the aesthetic which Mondrian, Theo van Doesburg and other in the group <a href="/wiki/De_Stijl" title="De Stijl">De Stijl</a> intended to reshape the environment of the future.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_avant-garde">Russian avant-garde</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Russian avant-garde">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Russian_avant-garde" title="Russian avant-garde">Russian avant-garde</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Futurism_(art)" title="Futurism (art)">Futurism (art)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_Square.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="709" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Black_Square.jpg/220px-Black_Square.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Black_Square.jpg/330px-Black_Square.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Black_Square.jpg/440px-Black_Square.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_Square.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kazimir_Malevich" title="Kazimir Malevich">Kazimir Malevich</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Square_(painting)" title="Black Square (painting)">Black Square</a></i>, 1923, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Russian_Museum" title="The Russian Museum">The Russian Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>Many of the abstract artists in Russia became <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(art)" title="Constructivism (art)">Constructivists</a> believing that art was no longer something remote, but life itself. The artist must become a technician, learning to use the tools and materials of modern production. <i>Art into life!</i> was <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Tatlin" title="Vladimir Tatlin">Vladimir Tatlin</a>'s slogan, and that of all the future Constructivists. <a href="/wiki/Varvara_Stepanova" title="Varvara Stepanova">Varvara Stepanova</a> and Alexandre Exter and others abandoned easel painting and diverted their energies to theatre design and graphic works.
On the other side stood <a href="/wiki/Kazimir_Malevich" title="Kazimir Malevich">Kazimir Malevich</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anton_Pevsner" title="Anton Pevsner">Anton Pevsner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naum_Gabo" title="Naum Gabo">Naum Gabo</a>. They argued that art was essentially a spiritual activity; to create the individual's place in the world, not to organize life in a practical, materialistic sense. Many of those who were hostile to the materialist production idea of art left Russia. Anton Pevsner went to France, Gabo went first to Berlin, then to England and finally to America. Kandinsky studied in Moscow then left for the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a>. By the mid-1920s the revolutionary period (1917 to 1921) when artists had been free to experiment was over; and by the 1930s only <a href="/wiki/Socialist_realism" title="Socialist realism">socialist realism</a> was allowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As visual art becomes more abstract, it develops some characteristics of music<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>: an art form which uses the abstract elements of sound and divisions of time. <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>, himself an amateur musician,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> was inspired by the possibility of marks and associative color <i>resounding in the soul.</i> The idea had been put forward by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Baudelaire" title="Charles Baudelaire">Charles Baudelaire</a>, that all our senses respond to various stimuli but the senses are connected at a deeper aesthetic level.
</p><p>Closely related to this, is the idea that art has <i>The spiritual dimension</i> and can transcend 'every-day' experience, reaching a spiritual plane. The <a href="/wiki/Theosophical_Society" title="Theosophical Society">Theosophical Society</a> popularized the ancient wisdom of the sacred books of India and China in the early years of the century. It was in this context that <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>, Wassily Kandinsky, <a href="/wiki/Hilma_af_Klint" title="Hilma af Klint">Hilma af Klint</a> and other artists working towards an 'objectless state' became interested in the occult as a way of creating an 'inner' object.
The universal and timeless shapes found in <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>: the circle, square and triangle become the spatial elements in abstract art; they are, like color, fundamental systems underlying visible reality.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bauhaus">The Bauhaus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Bauhaus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> at Weimar, Germany was founded in 1919 by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gropius" title="Walter Gropius">Walter Gropius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The philosophy underlying the teaching program was unity of all the visual and plastic arts from architecture and painting to weaving and stained glass. This philosophy had grown from the ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a> in England and the <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Werkbund" title="Deutscher Werkbund">Deutscher Werkbund</a>. Among the teachers were <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">Paul Klee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Itten" title="Johannes Itten">Johannes Itten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Josef_Albers" title="Josef Albers">Josef Albers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anni_Albers" title="Anni Albers">Anni Albers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Moholy-Nagy" title="László Moholy-Nagy">László Moholy-Nagy</a>. In 1925 the school was moved to Dessau and, as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_party" title="Nazi party">Nazi party</a> gained control in 1932, The Bauhaus was closed. In 1937 an exhibition of <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_art" title="Degenerate art">degenerate art</a>, 'Entartete Kunst' contained all types of <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> art disapproved of by the Nazi party. Then the exodus began: not just from the Bauhaus but from Europe in general; to Paris, London and America. Paul Klee went to Switzerland but many of the artists at the Bauhaus went to America.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abstraction_in_Paris_and_London">Abstraction in Paris and London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Abstraction in Paris and London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DasUndbild.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="843" data-file-width="665" decoding="async" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/DasUndbild.jpg/220px-DasUndbild.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/DasUndbild.jpg/330px-DasUndbild.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/DasUndbild.jpg/440px-DasUndbild.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DasUndbild.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">Kurt Schwitters</a>, <i>Das Undbild</i>, 1919, <a href="/wiki/Staatsgalerie_Stuttgart" title="Staatsgalerie Stuttgart">Staatsgalerie Stuttgart</a></div></div></div>
<p>During the 1930s Paris became the host to artists from Russia, Germany, the Netherlands and other European countries affected by the rise of <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarianism</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophie_Tauber" title="Sophie Tauber">Sophie Tauber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Arp" title="Jean Arp">Jean Arp</a> collaborated on paintings and sculpture using organic/geometric forms. The Polish <a href="/wiki/Katarzyna_Kobro" title="Katarzyna Kobro">Katarzyna Kobro</a> applied mathematically based ideas to sculpture. The many types of abstraction now in close proximity led to attempts by artists to analyse the various conceptual and aesthetic groupings. An exhibition by forty-six members of the <a href="/wiki/Cercle_et_Carr%C3%A9" title="Cercle et Carré">Cercle et Carré</a> group organized by <a href="/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Torres-Garc%C3%ADa" title="Joaquín Torres-García">Joaquín Torres-García</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> assisted by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Seuphor" title="Michel Seuphor">Michel Seuphor</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> contained work by the Neo-Plasticists as well as abstractionists as varied as Kandinsky, Anton Pevsner and <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">Kurt Schwitters</a>. Criticized by <a href="/wiki/Theo_van_Doesburg" title="Theo van Doesburg">Theo van Doesburg</a> to be too indefinite a collection he published the journal <i>Art Concret</i> setting out a manifesto defining an abstract art in which the line, color and surface only, are the concrete reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Abstraction-Création founded in 1931 as a more open group, provided a point of reference for abstract artists, as the political situation worsened in 1935, and artists again regrouped, many in London. The first exhibition of British abstract art was held in England in 1935. The following year the more international <i>Abstract and Concrete</i> exhibition was organized by <a href="/wiki/Nicolete_Gray" title="Nicolete Gray">Nicolete Gray</a> including work by <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Joan Miró</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nicholson" title="Ben Nicholson">Ben Nicholson</a>. Hepworth, Nicholson and Gabo moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Ives_group" title="St. Ives group">St. Ives group</a> in Cornwall to continue their 'constructivist' work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th_century">Late 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Late 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Late_modernism" title="Late modernism">Late modernism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Modernism" title="American Modernism">American Modernism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Piet_Mondriaan,_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="841" decoding="async" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Piet_Mondriaan%2C_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg/220px-Piet_Mondriaan%2C_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Piet_Mondriaan%2C_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg/330px-Piet_Mondriaan%2C_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Piet_Mondriaan%2C_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg/440px-Piet_Mondriaan%2C_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Piet_Mondriaan,_1939-1942_-_Composition_10.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1939–1942 oil on canvas painting by <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a> titled <i>Composition No. 10</i>. Responding to it, fellow <a href="/wiki/De_Stijl" title="De Stijl">De Stijl</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Theo_van_Doesburg" title="Theo van Doesburg">Theo van Doesburg</a> suggested a link between non-representational works of art and ideals of peace and spirituality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>During the Nazi rise to power in the 1930s many artists fled Europe to the United States. By the early 1940s the main movements in modern art, expressionism, cubism, abstraction, <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">dada</a> were represented in New York: <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Léger">Fernand Léger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lipchitz" title="Jacques Lipchitz">Jacques Lipchitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Masson" title="André Masson">André Masson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">Max Ernst</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="André Breton">André Breton</a>, were just a few of the exiled Europeans who arrived in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The rich cultural influences brought by the European artists were distilled and built upon by local New York painters. The climate of freedom in New York allowed all of these influences to flourish. The art galleries that primarily had focused on European art began to notice the local art community and the work of younger American artists who had begun to mature. Certain artists at this time became distinctly abstract in their mature work. During this period Piet Mondrian's painting <i>Composition No. 10</i>, 1939–1942, characterized by primary colors, white ground and black grid lines clearly defined his radical but classical approach to the rectangle and abstract art in general.
Some artists of the period defied categorization, such as <a href="/wiki/Georgia_O%27Keeffe" title="Georgia O'Keeffe">Georgia O'Keeffe</a> who, while a modernist abstractionist, was a pure maverick in that she painted highly abstract forms while not joining any specific group of the period.
</p><p>Eventually American artists who were working in a great diversity of styles began to coalesce into cohesive stylistic groups. The best-known group of American artists became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstract_expressionists" title="Abstract expressionists">Abstract expressionists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_School_(art)" title="New York School (art)">New York School</a>. In New York City there was an atmosphere which encouraged discussion and there was a new opportunity for learning and growing. Artists and teachers <a href="/wiki/John_D._Graham" title="John D. Graham">John D. Graham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Hofmann" title="Hans Hofmann">Hans Hofmann</a> became important bridge figures between the newly arrived European Modernists and the younger American artists coming of age. <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a>, born in Russia, began with strongly surrealist imagery which later dissolved into his powerful color compositions of the early 1950s. The <a href="/wiki/Action_painting" title="Action painting">expressionistic gesture</a> and the act of painting itself, became of primary importance to <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Pollock" title="Jackson Pollock">Jackson Pollock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Motherwell" title="Robert Motherwell">Robert Motherwell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Kline" title="Franz Kline">Franz Kline</a>. While during the 1940s <a href="/wiki/Arshile_Gorky" title="Arshile Gorky">Arshile Gorky</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Kooning" title="Willem de Kooning">Willem de Kooning</a>'s figurative work evolved into abstraction by the end of the decade. New York City became the center, and artists worldwide gravitated towards it; from other places in America as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Abstract_expressionism" title="Abstract expressionism">Abstract expressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Color_field" title="Color field">Color field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyrical_abstraction" title="Lyrical abstraction">Lyrical abstraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-painterly_abstraction" title="Post-painterly abstraction">Post-painterly abstraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">Sculpture</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minimal_art" title="Minimal art">Minimal art</a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Digital_art" title="Digital art">Digital art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hard-edge_painting" title="Hard-edge painting">hard-edge painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geometric_abstraction" title="Geometric abstraction">geometric abstraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minimalism" title="Minimalism">minimalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyrical_abstraction" title="Lyrical abstraction">lyrical abstraction</a>, op art, abstract expressionism, color field painting, <a href="/wiki/Monochrome_painting" title="Monochrome painting">monochrome painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assemblage_(art)" title="Assemblage (art)">assemblage</a>, neo-Dada, <a href="/wiki/Shaped_canvas" title="Shaped canvas">shaped canvas</a> painting, are a few directions relating to abstraction in the second half of the 20th century.
</p><p>In the United States, <i>Art as Object</i> as seen in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minimalist" title="Minimalist">Minimalist</a> sculpture of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Judd" title="Donald Judd">Donald Judd</a> and the paintings of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Stella" title="Frank Stella">Frank Stella</a> are seen today as newer permutations. Other examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyrical_Abstraction" title="Lyrical Abstraction">Lyrical Abstraction</a> and the sensuous use of color seen in the work of painters as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Motherwell" title="Robert Motherwell">Robert Motherwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Heron" title="Patrick Heron">Patrick Heron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Noland" title="Kenneth Noland">Kenneth Noland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Francis" title="Sam Francis">Sam Francis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cy_Twombly" title="Cy Twombly">Cy Twombly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Diebenkorn" title="Richard Diebenkorn">Richard Diebenkorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helen_Frankenthaler" title="Helen Frankenthaler">Helen Frankenthaler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mitchell" title="Joan Mitchell">Joan Mitchell</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">Elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstract_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One socio-historical explanation that has been offered for the growing prevalence of the abstract in modern art – an explanation linked to the name of <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno" title="Theodor W. Adorno">Theodor W. Adorno</a> – is that such abstraction is a response to, and a reflection of, the growing abstraction of social relations in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">industrial society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederic_Jameson" title="Frederic Jameson">Frederic Jameson</a> similarly sees modernist abstraction as a function of the abstract power of money, equating all things equally as exchange-values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The social <i>content</i> of abstract art is then precisely the abstract nature of social existence – legal formalities, bureaucratic impersonalization, information/power – in the world of <a href="/wiki/Late_modernity" title="Late modernity">late modernity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Analytical_psychology#Post-Jungian_approaches" title="Analytical psychology">Post-Jungians</a> by contrast would see the quantum theories with their disintegration of conventional ideas of form and matter as underlying the divorce of the concrete and the abstract in modern art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
