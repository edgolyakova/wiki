<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, the <b>grammar</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a> is its set of <a href="/wiki/Structure" title="Structure">structural</a> constraints on speakers' or writers' composition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clause_(linguistics)" title="Clause (linguistics)">clauses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrases</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Words" title="Words">words</a>. The term can also refer to the study of such constraints, a field that includes domains such as <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, often complemented by <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">phonetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a>. There are currently two different approaches to the study of grammar: <a href="/wiki/Traditional_grammar" title="Traditional grammar">traditional grammar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammar#Theoretical_frameworks" title="Grammar">theoretical grammar</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fluency" title="Fluency">Fluent</a> speakers of a <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">language variety</a> or <i>lect</i> have effectively internalized these constraints,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the vast majority of which – at least in the case of one's <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">native language(s)</a> – are <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">acquired</a> not by conscious study or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_teaching" title="Language teaching">instruction</a> but by hearing other speakers. Much of this internalization occurs during early childhood; learning a language later in life usually involves more explicit instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In this view, grammar is understood as the cognitive information underlying a specific instance of language production.
</p><p>The term "grammar" can also describe the linguistic behavior of groups of speakers and writers rather than individuals. Differences in scales are important to this sense of the word: for example, the term "English grammar" could refer to the whole of English grammar (that is, to the grammar of all the speakers of the language), in which case the term encompasses a great deal of <a href="/wiki/Variation_(linguistics)" title="Variation (linguistics)">variation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At a smaller scale, it may refer only to what is shared among the grammars of all or most English speakers (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" title="Subject–verb–object">subject–verb–object</a> word order in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_sentence" title="Simple sentence">simple declarative sentences</a>). At the smallest scale, this sense of "grammar" can describe the conventions of just one relatively well-defined form of English (such as <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">standard English</a> for a region).
</p><p>A description, study, or analysis of such rules may also be referred to as grammar. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reference_book" title="Reference book">reference book</a> describing the grammar of a language is called a "reference grammar" or simply "a grammar" (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_English_grammars" title="History of English grammars">History of English grammars</a>). A fully explicit grammar, which exhaustively describes the <a href="/wiki/Grammaticality" title="Grammaticality">grammatical</a> constructions of a particular speech variety, is called descriptive grammar. This kind of <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_description" title="Linguistic description">linguistic description</a> contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">linguistic prescription</a>, an attempt to actively discourage or suppress some grammatical constructions while <a href="/wiki/Codification_(linguistics)" title="Codification (linguistics)">codifying</a> and promoting others, either in an absolute sense or about a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_variety" title="Standard variety">standard variety</a>. For example, some prescriptivists maintain that sentences in English should not end with prepositions, a prohibition that has been traced to <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a> (13 April 1668 – January 1688) whose unexplained objection to the practice perhaps led other English speakers to avoid the construction and discourage its use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CB1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CB1-5">[5]</a></sup> Yet <a href="/wiki/Preposition_stranding" title="Preposition stranding">preposition stranding</a> has a long history in <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> like English, where it is so widespread as to be a standard usage.
</p><p>Outside linguistics, the term <i>grammar</i> is often used in a rather different sense. It may be used more broadly to include conventions of <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">spelling and punctuation</a>, which linguists would not typically consider as part of grammar but rather as part of <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Convention_(norm)" title="Convention (norm)">conventions</a> used for writing a language. It may also be used more narrowly to refer to a set of <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">prescriptive norms</a> only, excluding those aspects of a language's grammar which are not subject to <a href="/wiki/Variation_(linguistics)" title="Variation (linguistics)">variation</a> or debate on their normative acceptability. Jeremy Butterfield claimed that, for non-linguists, "Grammar is often a generic way of referring to any aspect of English that people object to."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>grammar</i> is derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Greek</a> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">γραμματικὴ τέχνη</span></span> (<i>grammatikḕ téchnē</i>), which means "art of letters", from <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">γράμμα</span></span> (<i>grámma</i>), "letter", itself from <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">γράφειν</span></span> (<i>gráphein</i>), "to draw, to write".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The same Greek root also appears in <a href="/wiki/Graphics" title="Graphics">graphics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">grapheme</a>, and photographs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_linguistics" title="History of linguistics">History of linguistics</a></div>
<p>The first systematic grammar of <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, originated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_Age_India" title="Iron Age India">Iron Age India</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaska" title="Yaska">Yaska</a> (6th century BC), <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a> (6th–5th century BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britpanini_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-britpanini-8">[8]</a></sup>) and his commentators <a href="/wiki/Pingala" title="Pingala">Pingala</a> (c. 200 BC), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katyayana" title="Katyayana">Katyayana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Patanjali" title="Patanjali">Patanjali</a> (2nd century BC). <a href="/wiki/Tolk%C4%81ppiyam" title="Tolkāppiyam">Tolkāppiyam</a>, the earliest <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a> grammar, is mostly dated to before the 5th century AD. The <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonians</a> also made some early attempts at language description.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grammar appeared as a discipline in <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenism</a> from the 3rd century BC forward with authors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhyanus" title="Rhyanus">Rhyanus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samothrace" title="Aristarchus of Samothrace">Aristarchus of Samothrace</a>. The oldest known grammar handbook is the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Grammar" title="Art of Grammar">Art of Grammar</a></i> (<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Τέχνη Γραμματική</span></span>), a succinct guide to speaking and writing clearly and effectively, written by the ancient Greek scholar <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Thrax" title="Dionysius Thrax">Dionysius Thrax</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 170–<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 90 BC), a student of Aristarchus of Samothrace who founded a school on the Greek island of Rhodes. Dionysius Thrax's grammar book remained the primary grammar textbook for Greek schoolboys until as late as the twelfth century AD. The Romans based their grammatical writings on it and its basic format remains the basis for grammar guides in many languages even today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casson2001_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Casson2001-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latin_grammar" title="Latin grammar">Latin grammar</a> developed by following Greek models from the 1st century BC, due to the work of authors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbilius_Pupillus" title="Orbilius Pupillus">Orbilius Pupillus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remmius_Palaemon" title="Remmius Palaemon">Remmius Palaemon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Valerius_Probus" title="Marcus Valerius Probus">Marcus Valerius Probus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verrius_Flaccus" title="Verrius Flaccus">Verrius Flaccus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aemilius_Asper" title="Aemilius Asper">Aemilius Asper</a>.
</p><p>The grammar of <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Irish</a> originated in the 7th century with the <a href="/wiki/Auraicept_na_n-%C3%89ces" title="Auraicept na n-Éces">Auraicept na n-Éces</a>. <a href="/wiki/Arabic_grammar" title="Arabic grammar">Arabic grammar</a> emerged with <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Aswad_al-Du%27ali" title="Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali">Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali</a> in the 7th century. The first treatises on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_grammar" title="Hebrew grammar">Hebrew grammar</a> appeared in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>, in the context of <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a> (exegesis of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Karaite_Judaism" title="Karaite Judaism">Karaite</a> tradition originated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diqduq" title="Diqduq">Diqduq</a></i> (10th century) is one of the earliest grammatical commentaries on the Hebrew Bible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Barun" title="Ibn Barun">Ibn Barun</a> in the 12th century, compares the Hebrew language with Arabic in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_grammatical_tradition" title="Islamic grammatical tradition">Islamic grammatical tradition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Belonging to the <i><a href="/wiki/Trivium" title="Trivium">trivium</a></i> of the seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a>, grammar was taught as a core discipline throughout the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, following the influence of authors from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Priscian" title="Priscian">Priscian</a>. Treatment of vernaculars began gradually during the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>, with isolated works such as the <a href="/wiki/First_Grammatical_Treatise" title="First Grammatical Treatise">First Grammatical Treatise</a>, but became influential only in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> periods. In 1486, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Nebrija" title="Antonio de Nebrija">Antonio de Nebrija</a> published <i>Las introduciones Latinas contrapuesto el romance al Latin</i>, and the first <a href="/wiki/Spanish_grammar" title="Spanish grammar">Spanish grammar</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gram%C3%A1tica_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Gramática de la lengua castellana">Gramática de la lengua castellana</a></i>, in 1492. During the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>, the <i>Questione della lingua</i> was the discussion on the status and ideal form of the Italian language, initiated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/De_vulgari_eloquentia" title="De vulgari eloquentia">de vulgari eloquentia</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bembo" title="Pietro Bembo">Pietro Bembo</a>, <i>Prose della volgar lingua</i> Venice 1525). The first grammar of <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a> was written in 1583 by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Bohori%C4%8D" title="Adam Bohorič">Adam Bohorič</a>.
</p><p>Grammars of some languages began to be compiled for the purposes of evangelism and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_translation" title="Bible translation">Bible translation</a> from the 16th century onward, such as <i>Grammatica o Arte de la Lengua General de Los Indios de Los Reynos del Perú</i> (1560), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">Quechua</a> grammar by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fray_Domingo_de_Santo_Tom%C3%A1s" title="Fray Domingo de Santo Tomás">Fray Domingo de Santo Tomás</a>.
</p><p>From the latter part of the 18th century, grammar came to be understood as a subfield of the emerging discipline of modern linguistics. The <i>Deutsche Grammatik</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">Jacob Grimm</a> was first published in the 1810s. The <i>Comparative Grammar</i> of <a href="/wiki/Franz_Bopp" title="Franz Bopp">Franz Bopp</a>, the starting point of modern <a href="/wiki/Comparative_linguistics" title="Comparative linguistics">comparative linguistics</a>, came out in 1833.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_frameworks"><span class="anchor" id="Frameworks"></span> Theoretical frameworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical frameworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Syntax#Theories" title="Syntax">Syntax § Theories</a></div>

<p>Frameworks of grammar which seek to give a precise scientific theory of the syntactic rules of grammar and their function have been developed in <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_linguistics" title="Theoretical linguistics">theoretical linguistics</a>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_grammar">Development of grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development of grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">Historical linguistics</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_English_grammars" title="History of English grammars">History of English grammars</a></div>
<p>Grammars evolve through <a href="/wiki/Usage_(language)" title="Usage (language)">usage</a>. Historically, with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">written representations</a>, formal rules about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_usage" title="Language usage">language usage</a> tend to appear also, although such rules tend to describe writing conventions more accurately than conventions of speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Formal_grammar" title="Formal grammar">Formal grammars</a> are <a href="/wiki/Codification_(linguistics)" title="Codification (linguistics)">codifications</a> of usage which are developed by repeated documentation and <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a> over time. As rules are established and developed, the prescriptive concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_correctness" title="Grammatical correctness">grammatical correctness</a> can arise. This often produces a discrepancy between contemporary usage and that which has been accepted, over time, as being standard or "correct". Linguists tend to view prescriptive grammar as having little justification beyond their authors' aesthetic tastes, although style guides may give useful advice about <i>standard language employment</i> based on descriptions of usage in contemporary writings of the same language. Linguistic prescriptions also form part of the explanation for variation in speech, particularly variation in the speech of an individual speaker (for example, why some speakers say "I didn't do nothing", some say "I didn't do anything", and some say one or the other depending on social context).
</p><p>The formal study of grammar is an important part of children's schooling from a young age through advanced <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>, though the rules taught in schools are not a "grammar" in the sense that most <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguists</a> use, particularly as they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescriptive" title="Prescriptive">prescriptive</a> in intent rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive" title="Descriptive">descriptive</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">Constructed languages</a> (also called <i>planned languages</i> or <i>conlangs</i>) are more common in the modern-day, although still extremely uncommon compared to natural languages. Many have been designed to aid human communication (for example, naturalistic <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">Interlingua</a>, schematic <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a>, and the highly logic-compatible <a href="/wiki/Artificial_language" title="Artificial language">artificial language</a> <a href="/wiki/Lojban" title="Lojban">Lojban</a>). Each of these languages has its own grammar.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">Syntax</a> refers to the linguistic structure above the word level (for example, how sentences are formed) – though without taking into account <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">intonation</a>, which is the domain of phonology. Morphology, by contrast, refers to the structure at and below the word level (for example, how <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compound words</a> are formed), but above the level of individual sounds, which, like intonation, are in the domain of phonology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, no clear line can be drawn between syntax and morphology. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic_languages" title="Analytic languages">Analytic languages</a> use syntax to convey information that is encoded by <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflection</a> in <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic languages</a>. In other words, word order is not significant, and morphology is highly significant in a purely synthetic language, whereas morphology is not significant and syntax is highly significant in an analytic language. For example, Chinese and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afrikaans_language" title="Afrikaans language">Afrikaans</a> are highly analytic, thus meaning is very context-dependent. (Both have some inflections, and both have had more in the past; thus, they are becoming even less synthetic and more "purely" analytic over time.) <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, which is highly <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic</a>, uses <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflections</a> to convey the same information that Chinese does with syntax. Because Latin words are quite (though not totally) self-contained, an intelligible Latin <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentence</a> can be made from elements that are arranged almost arbitrarily. Latin has a complex affixation and simple syntax, whereas Chinese has the opposite.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2021</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescriptive" title="Prescriptive">Prescriptive</a> grammar is taught in primary and secondary school. The term "grammar school" historically referred to a school (attached to a cathedral or monastery) that teaches Latin grammar to future priests and monks. It originally referred to a school that taught students how to read, scan, interpret, and declaim Greek and Latin poets (including Homer, Virgil, Euripides, and others). These should not be mistaken for the related, albeit distinct, modern British grammar schools.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard language</a> is a dialect that is promoted above other dialects in writing, education, and, broadly speaking, in the public sphere; it contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">vernacular dialects</a>, which may be the objects of study in academic, <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_description" title="Linguistic description">descriptive linguistics</a> but which are rarely taught prescriptively. The standardized "<a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a>" taught in primary education may be subject to <a href="/wiki/Language_politics" title="Language politics">political</a> controversy because it may sometimes establish a standard defining nationality or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">ethnicity</a>.
</p><p>Recently, efforts have begun to update <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistics_in_Education" title="Linguistics in Education">grammar instruction</a> in primary and secondary education. The main focus has been to prevent the use of outdated prescriptive rules in favor of setting norms based on earlier descriptive research and to change perceptions about the relative "correctness" of prescribed standard forms in comparison to non-standard dialects. A series of metastudies have found that the explicit teaching of grammatical parts of speech and syntax has little or no effect on the improvement of student writing quality in elementary school, middle school of high school; other methods of writing instruction had far greater positive effect, including strategy instruction, collaborative writing, summary writing, process instruction, sentence combining and inquiry projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The preeminence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parisian_French" title="Parisian French">Parisian French</a> has reigned largely unchallenged throughout the history of modern French literature. Standard Italian is based on the speech of Florence rather than the capital because of its influence on early literature. Likewise, standard Spanish is not based on the speech of Madrid but on that of educated speakers from more northern areas such as Castile and León (see <i><a href="/wiki/Gram%C3%A1tica_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Gramática de la lengua castellana">Gramática de la lengua castellana</a></i>). In <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> the Spanish standard is based on the local dialects of Buenos Aires and Montevideo (<a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">Rioplatense Spanish</a>). <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> has, for now, two official <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standards</a>, respectively <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Brazilian Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">European Portuguese</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a> variant of <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a> is likewise divided; <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republika_Srpska" title="Republika Srpska">Republika Srpska</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> use their own distinct normative subvarieties, with differences in <a href="/wiki/Yat" title="Yat">yat</a> reflexes. The existence and codification of a distinct Montenegrin standard is a matter of controversy, some treat <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">Montenegrin</a> as a separate standard lect, and some think that it should be considered another form of Serbian.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> has two standards, <i><a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">Bokmål</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">Nynorsk</a></i>, the choice between which is subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_language_struggle" title="Norwegian language struggle">controversy</a>: Each Norwegian municipality can either declare one as its official language or it can remain "language neutral". Nynorsk is backed by 27 percent of municipalities. The main language used in primary schools, chosen by referendum within the local school district, normally follows the official language of its municipality. <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">Standard German</a> emerged from the standardized chancellery use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_New_High_German_language" title="Early New High German language">High German</a> in the 16th and 17th centuries. Until about 1800, it was almost exclusively a written language, but now it is so widely spoken that most of the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_dialect" title="German dialect">German dialects</a> are nearly extinct.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Standard Chinese</a> has official status as the standard spoken form of the Chinese language in the People's Republic of China (PRC), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> (ROC), and the <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Republic of Singapore</a>. Pronunciation of Standard Chinese is based on the local accent of <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a> from Luanping, Chengde in Hebei Province near Beijing, while grammar and syntax are based on modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernacular_written_Chinese" title="Vernacular written Chinese">vernacular written Chinese</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">Modern Standard Arabic</a> is directly based on <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">Classical Arabic</a>, the language of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani language</a> has two standards, <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>.
</p><p>In the United States, the Society for the Promotion of Good Grammar designated 4 March as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Grammar_Day" title="National Grammar Day">National Grammar Day</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
