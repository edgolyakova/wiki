<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Hormone</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Biological signalling molecule</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Hormone_(disambiguation)" title="Hormone (disambiguation)">Hormone (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:285px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hormone_Transport.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3933" data-file-width="4608" decoding="async" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Hormone_Transport.png/283px-Hormone_Transport.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Hormone_Transport.png/425px-Hormone_Transport.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Hormone_Transport.png/566px-Hormone_Transport.png 2x" width="283"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hormone_Transport.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Left: A hormone feedback loop in a female adult. (1) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Follicle-Stimulating_Hormone" title="Follicle-Stimulating Hormone">Follicle-Stimulating Hormone</a>, (2) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luteinizing_Hormone" title="Luteinizing Hormone">Luteinizing Hormone</a>, (3) <a href="/wiki/Progesterone" title="Progesterone">Progesterone</a>, (4) <a href="/wiki/Estradiol" title="Estradiol">Estradiol</a>. Right: <a href="/wiki/Auxin" title="Auxin">Auxin</a> transport from leaves to roots in Arabidopsis thaliana</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>hormone</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> participle <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">ὁρμῶν</span></span>, "setting in motion") is a class of <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">signaling molecules</a> in <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular organisms</a> that are sent to distant organs by complex biological processes to regulate <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Hormones are required for the correct development of <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>. Due to the broad definition of a hormone (as a signaling molecule that exerts its effects far from its site of production), numerous kinds of molecules can be classified as hormones. Among the substances that can be considered hormones, are <a href="/wiki/Eicosanoid" title="Eicosanoid">eicosanoids</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Prostaglandin" title="Prostaglandin">prostaglandins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thromboxane" title="Thromboxane">thromboxanes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">oestrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brassinosteroid" title="Brassinosteroid">brassinosteroid</a>), <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> derivatives (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">epinephrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auxin" title="Auxin">auxin</a>), <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> or <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> and <a href="/wiki/CLE_peptide" title="CLE peptide">CLE peptides</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>).
</p><p>Hormones are used to communicate between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, hormones are responsible for regulating a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiological" title="Physiological">physiological</a> processes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral" title="Behavioral">behavioral</a> activities such as <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_perception" title="Sensory perception">sensory perception</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sleep" title="Sleep">sleep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Excretion" title="Excretion">excretion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">lactation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physiology)" title="Stress (physiology)">stress</a> induction, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_development_(biology)" title="Human development (biology)">growth and development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_coordination" title="Motor coordination">movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mood_(psychology)" title="Mood (psychology)">mood</a> manipulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In plants, hormones modulate almost all aspects of development, from <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a> to <a href="/wiki/Senescence" title="Senescence">senescence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hormones affect distant cells by binding to specific <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptor</a> proteins in the target cell, resulting in a change in cell function. When a hormone binds to the receptor, it results in the activation of a <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">signal transduction</a> pathway that typically activates gene <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a>, resulting in increased <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">expression</a> of target <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. Hormones can also act in non-genomic pathways that synergize with genomic effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Water-soluble hormones (such as peptides and amines) generally act on the surface of target cells via <a href="/wiki/Second_messenger_system" title="Second messenger system">second messengers</a>. Lipid soluble hormones, (such as <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a>) generally pass through the plasma membranes of target cells (both <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cytoplasmic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_membrane" title="Nuclear membrane">nuclear</a>) to act within their <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nuclei</a>. Brassinosteroids, a type of polyhydroxysteroids, are a sixth class of plant hormones and may be useful as an anticancer drug for endocrine-responsive tumors to cause apoptosis and limit plant growth. Despite being lipid soluble, they nevertheless attach to their receptor at the cell surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In vertebrates, <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_gland" title="Endocrine gland">endocrine glands</a> are specialized organs that <a href="/wiki/Secretion" title="Secretion">secrete</a> hormones into the <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine signaling system</a>. Hormone secretion occurs in response to specific biochemical signals and is often subject to <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback regulation</a>. For instance, high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_sugar" title="Blood sugar">blood sugar</a> (serum glucose concentration) promotes <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> synthesis. Insulin then acts to reduce glucose levels and maintain <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>, leading to reduced insulin levels. Upon secretion, water-soluble hormones are readily transported through the circulatory system. Lipid-soluble hormones must bond to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrier_plasma_glycoprotein" title="Carrier plasma glycoprotein">carrier plasma glycoproteins</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Thyroxine-binding_globulin" title="Thyroxine-binding globulin">thyroxine-binding globulin</a> (TBG)) to form <a href="/wiki/Ligand_(biochemistry)" title="Ligand (biochemistry)">ligand</a>-protein complexes. Some hormones, such as insulin and growth hormones, can be released into the bloodstream already fully active. Other hormones, called prohormones, must be activated in certain cells through a series of steps that are usually tightly controlled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine system</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secrete" title="Secrete">secretes</a> hormones directly into the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">bloodstream</a>, typically via <a href="/wiki/Capillary#Types" title="Capillary">fenestrated capillaries</a>, whereas the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exocrine_system" title="Exocrine system">exocrine system</a> secretes its hormones indirectly using <a href="/wiki/Duct_(anatomy)" title="Duct (anatomy)">ducts</a>. Hormones with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paracrine" title="Paracrine">paracrine</a> function diffuse through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial spaces</a> to nearby target tissue.
</p><p>Plants lack specialized organs for the secretion of hormones, although there is spatial distribution of hormone production. For example, the hormone auxin is produced mainly at the tips of young <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Meristem" title="Meristem">shoot apical meristem</a>. The lack of specialised glands means that the main site of hormone production can change throughout the life of a plant, and the site of production is dependent on the plant's age and environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_and_overview">Introduction and overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction and overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">Signal transduction</a></div>
<p>Hormonal signaling involves the following steps:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nussey_Whitehead_2001_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nussey_Whitehead_2001-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><b><a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</a></b> of a particular hormone in a particular tissue.</li>
<li><b>Storage and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellular_secretion" title="Cellular secretion">secretion</a></b> of the hormone.</li>
<li><b>Transport</b> of the hormone to the target cell(s).</li>
<li><b>Recognition</b> of the hormone by an <a href="/wiki/Membrane_protein" title="Membrane protein">associated cell membrane</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intracellular" title="Intracellular">intracellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptor</a> protein.</li>
<li><b>Relay and amplification</b> of the received hormonal signal via a <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">signal transduction</a> process: This then leads to a cellular response. The reaction of the target cells may then be recognized by the original hormone-producing cells, leading to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downregulation" title="Downregulation">downregulation</a> in hormone production. This is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostatic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_feedback_loop" title="Negative feedback loop">negative feedback loop</a>.</li>
<li><b>Breakdown</b> of the hormone.</li></ol>
<p>Hormone producing cells are found in the <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine glands</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_gland" title="Thyroid gland">thyroid gland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovaries</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testes" title="Testes">testes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Exocytosis" title="Exocytosis">Exocytosis</a> and other methods of <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">membrane transport</a> are used to secrete hormones when the endocrine glands are signaled. The hierarchical model is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oversimplification" title="Oversimplification">oversimplification</a> of the hormonal signaling process. Cellular recipients of a particular hormonal signal may be one of several cell types that reside within a number of different tissues, as is the case for <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>, which triggers a diverse range of systemic physiological effects. Different tissue types may also respond differently to the same hormonal signal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Arnold_Adolph_Berthold_.281849.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Arnold_Adolph_Berthold_(1849)">Arnold Adolph Berthold (1849)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arnold Adolph Berthold (1849)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arnold_Adolph_Berthold" title="Arnold Adolph Berthold">Arnold Adolph Berthold</a> was a German <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a>, who, in 1849, had a question about the function of the <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testes</a>. He noticed in castrated roosters that they did not have the same sexual behaviors as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rooster" title="Rooster">roosters</a> with their testes intact. He decided to run an experiment on male roosters to examine this phenomenon. He kept a group of roosters with their testes intact, and saw that they had normal sized wattles and combs (secondary <a href="/wiki/Sex_organ" title="Sex organ">sexual organs</a>), a normal crow, and normal sexual and aggressive behaviors. He also had a group with their testes surgically removed, and noticed that their secondary sexual organs were decreased in size, had a weak crow, did not have sexual attraction towards females, and were not aggressive. He realized that this organ was essential for these behaviors, but he did not know how. To test this further, he removed one testis and placed it in the abdominal cavity. The roosters acted and had normal physical <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>. He was able to see that location of the testes does not matter. He then wanted to see if it was a <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> factor that was involved in the testes that provided these functions. He transplanted a testis from another rooster to a rooster with one testis removed, and saw that they had normal behavior and physical anatomy as well. Berthold determined that the location or genetic factors of the testes do not matter in relation to sexual organs and behaviors, but that some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> in the testes being secreted is causing this phenomenon. It was later identified that this factor was the hormone <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Charles_and_Francis_Darwin_.281880.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_and_Francis_Darwin_(1880)">Charles and Francis Darwin (1880)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Charles and Francis Darwin (1880)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although known primarily for his work on the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">Theory of Evolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> was also keenly interested in plants. Through the 1870s, he and his son <a href="/wiki/Francis_Darwin" title="Francis Darwin">Francis</a> studied the movement of plants towards light. They were able to show that light is perceived at the tip of a young stem (the <a href="/wiki/Coleoptile" title="Coleoptile">coleoptile</a>), whereas the bending occurs lower down the stem. They proposed that a 'transmissible substance' communicated the direction of light from the tip down to the stem. The idea of a 'transmissible substance' was initially dismissed by other plant biologists, but their work later led to the discovery of the first plant hormone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-13">[13]</a></sup> In the 1920s Dutch scientist <a href="/wiki/Frits_Warmolt_Went" title="Frits Warmolt Went">Frits Warmolt Went</a> and Russian scientist <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Cholodny" title="Nikolai Cholodny">Nikolai Cholodny</a> (working independently of each other) conclusively showed that asymmetric accumulation of a growth hormone was responsible for this bending. In 1933 this hormone was finally isolated by Kögl, Haagen-Smit and Erxleben and given the name '<a href="/wiki/Auxin" title="Auxin">auxin</a>'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Bayliss_and_Starling_.281902.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bayliss_and_Starling_(1902)">Bayliss and Starling (1902)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bayliss and Starling (1902)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Bayliss" title="William Bayliss">William Bayliss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Starling" title="Ernest Starling">Ernest Starling</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologist</a>, respectively, wanted to see if the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a> had an impact on the <a href="/wiki/Human_digestive_system" title="Human digestive system">digestive system</a>. They knew that the <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a> was involved in the secretion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_fluid" title="Digestive fluid">digestive fluids</a> after the passage of food from the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">intestines</a>, which they believed to be due to the nervous system. They cut the nerves to the pancreas in an animal model and discovered that it was not nerve impulses that controlled secretion from the pancreas. It was determined that a factor secreted from the intestines into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloodstream" title="Bloodstream">bloodstream</a> was stimulating the pancreas to secrete digestive fluids. This factor was named <a href="/wiki/Secretin" title="Secretin">secretin</a>: a hormone, although the term hormone was not coined until 1905 by Starling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_classes">Chemical classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chemical classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As hormones are defined functionally, not structurally, they may have diverse chemical structures. Hormones occur in <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular organisms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown_algae" title="Brown algae">brown algae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Red_algae" title="Red algae">red algae</a>). These compounds occur also in <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">unicellular organisms</a>, and may act as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signaling_molecule" title="Signaling molecule">signaling molecules</a> however there is no agreement that these molecules can be called hormones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1585458_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1585458-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Receptors">Receptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Receptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg/400px-Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg/600px-Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg/800px-Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steroid_and_Lipid_Hormones.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The left diagram shows a steroid (lipid) hormone (1) entering a cell and (2) binding to a receptor protein in the nucleus, causing (3) mRNA synthesis which is the first step of protein synthesis. The right side shows protein hormones (1) binding with receptors which (2) begins a transduction pathway. The transduction pathway ends (3) with transcription factors being activated in the nucleus, and protein synthesis beginning. In both diagrams, a is the hormone, b is the cell membrane, c is the cytoplasm, and d is the nucleus.</div></div></div><p> Most hormones initiate a cellular response by initially binding to either <a href="/wiki/Membrane_protein" title="Membrane protein">cell membrane associated</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intracellular" title="Intracellular">intracellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptors</a>. A cell may have several different receptor types that recognize the same hormone but activate different <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">signal transduction</a> pathways, or a cell may have several different receptors that recognize different hormones and activate the same biochemical pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Receptors for most <a href="/wiki/Peptide_hormone" title="Peptide hormone">peptide</a> as well as many <a href="/wiki/Eicosanoid" title="Eicosanoid">eicosanoid</a> hormones are embedded in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_membrane" title="Plasma membrane">plasma membrane</a> at the surface of the cell and the majority of these receptors belong to the <a href="/wiki/G_protein-coupled_receptor" title="G protein-coupled receptor">G protein-coupled receptor</a> (GPCR) class of seven <a href="/wiki/Alpha_helix" title="Alpha helix">alpha helix</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmembrane" title="Transmembrane">transmembrane</a> proteins. The interaction of hormone and receptor typically triggers a cascade of secondary effects within the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> of the cell, described as <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">signal transduction</a>, often involving <a href="/wiki/Phosphorylation" title="Phosphorylation">phosphorylation</a> or dephosphorylation of various other cytoplasmic proteins, changes in <a href="/wiki/Ion_channel" title="Ion channel">ion channel</a> permeability, or increased concentrations of intracellular molecules that may act as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_messenger" title="Second messenger">secondary messengers</a> (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclic_AMP" title="Cyclic AMP">cyclic AMP</a>). Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_hormone" title="Protein hormone">protein hormones</a> also interact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intracellular" title="Intracellular">intracellular</a> receptors located in the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a> by an <a href="/wiki/Intracrine" title="Intracrine">intracrine</a> mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Steroid_hormone" title="Steroid hormone">steroid</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_hormone" title="Thyroid hormone">thyroid</a> hormones, their <a href="/wiki/Steroid_hormone_receptor" title="Steroid hormone receptor">receptors</a> are located <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intracellular" title="Intracellular">inside the cell</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> of the target cell. These receptors belong to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_receptor" title="Nuclear receptor">nuclear receptor</a> family of ligand-activated <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factor" title="Transcription factor">transcription factors</a>. To bind their receptors, these hormones must first cross the cell membrane. They can do so because they are lipid-soluble. The combined hormone-receptor <a href="/wiki/Protein_complex" title="Protein complex">complex</a> then moves across the nuclear membrane into the nucleus of the cell, where it binds to specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequences" title="DNA sequences">DNA sequences</a>, regulating the expression of certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genes" title="Genes">genes</a>, and thereby increasing the levels of the proteins encoded by these genes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beato_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-beato-26">[26]</a></sup> However, it has been shown that not all steroid receptors are located inside the cell. Some are associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_membrane" title="Plasma membrane">plasma membrane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hammes_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-hammes-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_in_humans">Effects in humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects in humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hormones have the following effects on the body:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>stimulation or inhibition of growth</li>
<li>wake-sleep cycle and other <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mood_swing" title="Mood swing">mood swings</a></li>
<li>induction or suppression of <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">apoptosis</a> (programmed cell death)</li>
<li>activation or inhibition of the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a></li>
<li>regulation of <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a></li>
<li>preparation of the body for <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fighting" title="Fighting">fighting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fight-or-flight_response" title="Fight-or-flight response">fleeing</a>, and other activity</li>
<li>preparation of the body for a new phase of life, such as <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">parenting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">menopause</a></li>
<li>control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reproductive_cycle" title="Reproductive cycle">reproductive cycle</a></li>
<li>hunger cravings</li></ul>
<p>A hormone may also regulate the production and release of other hormones. Hormone signals control the internal environment of the body through <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rate of hormone biosynthesis and secretion is often regulated by a <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> control mechanism. Such a mechanism depends on factors that influence the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Excretion" title="Excretion">excretion</a> of hormones. Thus, higher hormone concentration alone cannot trigger the negative feedback mechanism. Negative feedback must be triggered by overproduction of an "effect" of the hormone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="489" data-file-width="786" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif/220px-Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif/330px-Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif/440px-Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Negative_Feedback_Gif.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blood glucose levels are maintained at a constant level in the body by a negative feedback mechanism. When the blood glucose level is too high, the pancreas secretes insulin and when the level is too low, the pancreas then secretes glucagon. The flat line shown represents the homeostatic set point. The sinusoidal line represents the blood glucose level.</div></div></div>
<p>Hormone secretion can be stimulated and inhibited by:
</p>
<ul><li>Other hormones (<i>stimulating</i>- or <i>releasing</i> -hormones)</li>
<li>Plasma concentrations of ions or nutrients, as well as binding <a href="/wiki/Globulin" title="Globulin">globulins</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">Neurons</a> and mental activity</li>
<li>Environmental changes, e.g., of light or temperature</li></ul>
<p>One special group of hormones is the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_hormone" title="Tropic hormone">tropic hormones</a> that stimulate the hormone production of other <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine glands</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Thyroid-stimulating_hormone" title="Thyroid-stimulating hormone">thyroid-stimulating hormone</a> (TSH) causes growth and increased activity of another endocrine gland, the <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a>, which increases output of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_hormone" title="Thyroid hormone">thyroid hormones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shah,_Shilpa_Bhupatrai._2012_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shah,_Shilpa_Bhupatrai._2012-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>To release active hormones quickly into the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulation</a>, hormone biosynthetic cells may produce and store biologically inactive hormones in the form of <a href="/wiki/Prehormone" title="Prehormone">pre-</a> or <a href="/wiki/Prohormone" title="Prohormone">prohormones</a>. These can then be quickly converted into their active hormone form in response to a particular stimulus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shah,_Shilpa_Bhupatrai._2012_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shah,_Shilpa_Bhupatrai._2012-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eicosanoid" title="Eicosanoid">Eicosanoids</a> are considered to act as local hormones. They are considered to be "local" because they possess specific effects on target cells close to their site of formation. They also have a rapid degradation cycle, making sure they do not reach distant sites within the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hormones are also regulated by receptor agonists. Hormones are ligands, which are any kinds of molecules that produce a signal by binding to a receptor site on a protein. Hormone effects can be inhibited, thus regulated, by competing ligands that bind to the same target receptor as the hormone in question. When a competing ligand is bound to the receptor site, the hormone is unable to bind to that site and is unable to elicit a response from the target cell. These competing ligands are called antagonists of the hormone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Therapeutic_use">Therapeutic use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Therapeutic use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hormone_therapy" title="Hormone therapy">Hormone therapy</a></div>
<p>Many hormones and their <a href="/wiki/Structural_analog" title="Structural analog">structural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Functional_analog_(chemistry)" title="Functional analog (chemistry)">functional analogs</a> are used as <a href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">medication</a>. The most commonly prescribed hormones are <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progestogen" title="Progestogen">progestogens</a> (as methods of <a href="/wiki/Hormonal_contraception" title="Hormonal contraception">hormonal contraception</a> and as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormone_replacement_therapy_(menopause)" title="Hormone replacement therapy (menopause)">HRT</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroxine" title="Thyroxine">thyroxine</a> (as <a href="/wiki/Levothyroxine" title="Levothyroxine">levothyroxine</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a> (for <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_disease" title="Autoimmune disease">autoimmune diseases</a> and several <a href="/wiki/Pulmonology" title="Pulmonology">respiratory disorders</a>). <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">Insulin</a> is used by many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetics</a>. Local preparations for use in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otolaryngology" title="Otolaryngology">otolaryngology</a> often contain <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacologic</a> equivalents of <a href="/wiki/Adrenaline" title="Adrenaline">adrenaline</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a> creams are used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatological</a> practice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A "pharmacologic dose" or "supraphysiological dose" of a hormone is a medical usage referring to an amount of a hormone far greater than naturally occurs in a healthy body. The effects of pharmacologic doses of hormones may be different from responses to naturally occurring amounts and may be therapeutically useful, though not without potentially adverse side effects. An example is the ability of pharmacologic doses of <a href="/wiki/Glucocorticoid" title="Glucocorticoid">glucocorticoids</a> to suppress <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hormone-behavior_interactions">Hormone-behavior interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hormone-behavior interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the neurological level, behavior can be inferred based on hormone concentration, which in turn are influenced by hormone-release patterns; the numbers and locations of hormone receptors; and the efficiency of hormone receptors for those involved in gene transcription. Hormone concentration does not incite behavior, as that would undermine other external stimuli; however, it influences the system by increasing the probability of a certain event to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Not only can hormones influence behavior, but also behavior and the environment can influence hormone concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Thus, a feedback loop is formed, meaning behavior can affect hormone concentration, which in turn can affect behavior, which in turn can affect hormone concentration, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> For example, hormone-behavior feedback loops are essential in providing constancy to episodic hormone secretion, as the behaviors affected by episodically secreted hormones directly prevent the continuous release of said hormones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Three broad stages of reasoning may be used to determine if a specific hormone-behavior interaction is present within a system:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The frequency of occurrence of a hormonally dependent behavior should correspond to that of its hormonal source.</li>
<li>A hormonally dependent behavior is not expected if the hormonal source (or its types of action) is non-existent.</li>
<li>The reintroduction of a missing behaviorally dependent hormonal source (or its types of action) is expected to bring back the absent behavior.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_neurotransmitters">Comparison with neurotransmitters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Comparison with neurotransmitters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various clear distinctions between hormones and <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A hormone can perform functions over a larger spatial and temporal scale than can a neurotransmitter, which often acts in micrometer-scale distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Hormonal signals can travel virtually anywhere in the circulatory system, whereas neural signals are restricted to pre-existing <a href="/wiki/Nerve_tract" title="Nerve tract">nerve tracts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Assuming the travel distance is equivalent, neural signals can be transmitted much more quickly (in the range of milliseconds) than can hormonal signals (in the range of seconds, minutes, or hours). Neural signals can be sent at speeds up to 100 meters per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>Neural signalling is an all-or-nothing (digital) action, whereas hormonal signalling is an action that can be continuously variable as it is dependent upon hormone concentration.</li></ul>
<p>Neurohormones are a type of hormone that share a commonality with neurotransmitters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-43">[43]</a></sup> They are produced by endocrine cells that receive input from neurons, or neuroendocrine cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-43">[43]</a></sup> Both classic hormones and neurohormones are secreted by endocrine tissue; however, neurohormones are the result of a combination between endocrine reflexes and neural reflexes, creating a neuroendocrine pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup> While endocrine pathways produce chemical signals in the form of hormones, the neuroendocrine pathway involves the electrical signals of neurons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup> In this pathway, the result of the electrical signal produced by a neuron is the release of a chemical, which is the neurohormone<b>.</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup> Finally, like a classic hormone, the neurohormone is released into the bloodstream to reach its target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Binding_proteins">Binding proteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Binding proteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hormone transport and the involvement of binding proteins is an essential aspect when considering the function of hormones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The formation of a complex with a binding protein has several benefits: the effective half-life of the bound hormone is increased, and a reservoir of bound hormones is created, which evens the variations in concentration of unbound hormones (bound hormones will replace the unbound hormones when these are eliminated).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> An example of the usage of hormone-binding proteins is in the thyroxine-binding protein which carries up to 80% of all thyroxine in the body, a crucial element in regulating the metabolic rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
