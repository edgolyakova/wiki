<p>A <b>season</b> is a division of the year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> based on changes in <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, and the number of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> hours in a given region. On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, seasons are the result of <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">Earth's orbit</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and Earth's <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> relative to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> plane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SunModel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SunModel-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SunModel2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SunModel2-3">[3]</a></sup> In temperate and polar regions, the seasons are marked by changes in the intensity of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> that reaches the Earth's surface, variations of which may cause animals to undergo <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">hibernation</a> or to <a href="/wiki/Migration_(ecology)" title="Migration (ecology)">migrate</a>, and plants to be dormant. Various cultures define the number and nature of seasons based on regional variations, and as such there are a number of both modern and historical cultures whose number of seasons varies.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> experiences more direct sunlight during May, June, and July, as the hemisphere faces the Sun. The same is true of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> in November, December, and January. It is Earth's axial tilt that causes the Sun to be higher in the sky during the summer <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">months</a>, which increases the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flux" title="Solar flux">solar flux</a>. However, due to <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">seasonal lag</a>, June, July, and August are the warmest months in the Northern Hemisphere while December, January, and February are the warmest months in the Southern Hemisphere.
</p><p>
In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpolar_climate" title="Subpolar climate">sub-polar</a> regions, four seasons based on the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> are generally recognized: <i><a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a></i> (or <i>fall</i>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a></i>. Ecologists often use a six-season model for temperate <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> regions which are not tied to any fixed calendar dates: <i>prevernal</i>, <i>vernal</i>, <i>estival</i>, <i>serotinal</i>, <i>autumnal</i>, and <i>hibernal</i>. Many tropical regions have two seasons: the <i><a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">rainy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> season</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a></i>. Some have a third <i>cool</i>, <i>mild</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Harmattan" title="Harmattan">harmattan</a> season</i>. "Seasons" can also be dictated by the timing of important ecological events such as <i><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane season</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornado_season" title="Tornado season">tornado season</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> season</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This content does not appear anywhere in the article body. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some examples of historical importance are the ancient Egyptian seasons—<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akhet_(season)" title="Akhet (season)">flood</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Season_of_the_Emergence" title="Season of the Emergence">growth</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shemu" title="Shemu">low water</a></i>—which were previously defined by the <a href="/wiki/Flooding_of_the_Nile" title="Flooding of the Nile">former annual flooding</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1947" data-file-width="7569" decoding="async" height="67" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg/260px-MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg/390px-MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg/520px-MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MatheranPanoramaPointDrySeasonCrop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tropical dry season in <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, India</div></div></div>

<p>Seasons often hold special significance for agrarian societies, whose lives revolve around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planting" title="Planting">planting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a> times, and the change of seasons is often attended by <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a>. The definition of seasons is also cultural. In India, from ancient times to the present day, six seasons or <a href="/wiki/Ritu_(Indian_season)" title="Ritu (Indian season)">Ritu</a> based on south Asian religious or cultural calendars are recognised and identified for purposes such as agriculture and trade.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_and_effects">Causes and effects</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">Effect of sun angle on climate</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Axial_tilt">Axial tilt</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="11000" data-file-width="11000" decoding="async" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg/280px-Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg/420px-Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg/560px-Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_seasons_2021-2022.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illumination of Earth at each change of astronomical season</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:268px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seasons.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="170" data-file-width="266" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Seasons.svg/266px-Seasons.svg.png" width="266"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">This diagram shows how the tilt of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_axis" title="Earth's axis">Earth's axis</a> aligns with incoming <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. Regardless of the time of day (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">Earth's rotation</a> on its axis), the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> will be dark and the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> will be illuminated; see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_winter" title="Arctic winter">arctic winter</a>. In addition to the density of <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">incident</a> light, the <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> of light in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is greater when it falls at a shallow angle.</div></div></div>
<p>The seasons result from the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">axis of rotation</a> being <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt#Earth" title="Axial tilt">tilted</a> with respect to its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">orbital plane</a> by an angle of approximately 23.4 <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (This tilt is also known as "obliquity of the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>".)
</p><p>Regardless of the time of year, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">northern</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemispheres</a> always experience opposite seasons. This is because during <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, one part of the planet is more directly exposed to the rays of the Sun than the other, and this exposure alternates as the Earth revolves in its orbit. For approximately half of the year (from around March<span class="nowrap"> </span>20 to around September<span class="nowrap"> </span>22), the Northern Hemisphere tips toward the Sun, with the maximum amount occurring on about June<span class="nowrap"> </span>21. For the other half of the year, the same happens, but in the Southern Hemisphere instead of the Northern, with the maximum around December<span class="nowrap"> </span>21. The two instants when the Sun is directly overhead at the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>. Also at that moment, both the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> of the Earth are just on the <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">terminator</a>, and hence day and night are equally divided between the two hemispheres. Around the <a href="/wiki/March_equinox" title="March equinox">March equinox</a>, the Northern Hemisphere will be experiencing <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> as the hours of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> increase, and the Southern Hemisphere is experiencing <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a> as daylight hours shorten.
</p><p>The effect of axial tilt is observable as the change in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_length" title="Day length">day length</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)" title="Altitude (astronomy)">altitude</a> of the Sun at solar <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">noon</a> (the Sun's <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">culmination</a>) during the <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>. The low angle of Sun during the winter months means that incoming rays of solar radiation are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">spread over a larger area</a> of the Earth's surface, so the light received is more indirect and of lower intensity. Between this effect and the shorter daylight hours, the axial tilt of the Earth accounts for most of the seasonal variation in climate in both hemispheres.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elliptical_Earth_orbit">Elliptical Earth orbit</span></h3>
<p>Compared to axial tilt, other factors contribute little to seasonal temperature changes. The seasons are not the result of the variation in Earth's distance to the Sun because of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_orbit" title="Elliptical orbit">elliptical orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In fact, Earth reaches <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> (the point in its orbit closest to the Sun) in January, and it reaches <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a> (the point farthest from the Sun) in July, so the slight contribution of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> opposes the temperature trends of the seasons in the Northern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science@NASA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science@NASA-6">[6]</a></sup> In general, the effect of orbital eccentricity on Earth's seasons is a 7% variation in sunlight received.
</p><p>Orbital eccentricity can influence temperatures, but on Earth, this effect is small and is more than counteracted by other factors; research shows that the Earth as a whole is actually slightly warmer when <i>farther</i> from the sun. This is because the Northern Hemisphere has more land than the Southern, and land warms more readily than sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science@NASA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Science@NASA-6">[6]</a></sup>
Any noticeable intensification of southern winters and summers due to Earth's elliptical orbit is mitigated by the abundance of water in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_and_hemispheric">Maritime and hemispheric</span></h3>
<p>Seasonal weather fluctuations (changes) also depend on factors such as proximity to <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> or other large bodies of water, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents</a> in those oceans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a>/ENSO and other oceanic cycles, and prevailing <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>.
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions_of_Earth" title="Polar regions of Earth">polar regions</a>, seasons are marked by changes in the amount of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>, which in turn often causes <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">cycles</a> of dormancy in plants and <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">hibernation</a> in animals. These effects vary with <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and with proximity to bodies of water. For example, the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> is in the middle of the continent of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and therefore a considerable distance from the moderating influence of the southern oceans. The <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> is in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>, and thus its temperature extremes are buffered by the water. The result is that the South Pole is consistently colder during the southern winter than the North Pole during the northern winter.
</p><p>The seasonal cycle in the polar and temperate zones of one hemisphere is opposite to that of the other. When it is summer in the Northern Hemisphere, it is winter in the Southern, and vice versa.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropics">Tropics</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="432" data-file-width="864" decoding="async" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif/220px-BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif/330px-BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif/440px-BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BlueMarble_monthlies_animation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animation of seasonal differences especially <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> cover through the year</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> regions see little annual fluctuation of sunlight.  However, seasonal shifts occur along a rainy, low-pressure belt called the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ICZ). As a result, the amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> tends to vary more dramatically than the average temperature. When the Zone is north of the Equator, the northern tropics experience their wet season while the southern tropics have their dry season. This pattern reverses when the Zone migrates to a position south of the Equator.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-latitude_thermal_lag">Mid-latitude thermal lag</span></h3>
<p>In meteorological terms, the <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstices</a> (the maximum and minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>) do not fall in the middles of summer and winter. The heights of these seasons occur up to 7 weeks later because of <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">seasonal lag</a>. Seasons, though, are not always defined in meteorological terms.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> reckoning by hours of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> alone, the solstices and <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a> are in the <i>middle</i> of the respective seasons. Because of seasonal lag due to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_absorption&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thermal absorption (page does not exist)">thermal absorption</a> and release by the oceans, regions with a <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a>, which predominate in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, often consider these four dates to be the <i>start</i> of the seasons as in the diagram, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-quarter_day" title="Cross-quarter day">cross-quarter days</a> considered seasonal midpoints. The length of these seasons is not uniform because of Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_orbit" title="Elliptical orbit">elliptical orbit</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">different speeds along that orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Four-season_reckoning"><span class="anchor" id="Reckoning"></span>Four-season reckoning</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha,_circa_1897.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1934" data-file-width="3572" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha%2C_circa_1897.jpg/220px-Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha%2C_circa_1897.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha%2C_circa_1897.jpg/330px-Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha%2C_circa_1897.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha%2C_circa_1897.jpg/440px-Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha%2C_circa_1897.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Four_Seasons_by_Alfons_Mucha,_circa_1897.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Four Seasons by <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Mucha" title="Alphonse Mucha">Alphonse Mucha</a> (1897)</div></div></div>
<p>Most calendar-based partitions use a four-season model to demarcate the warmest and coldest seasons, which are further separated by two intermediate seasons. Calendar-based reckoning defines the seasons in relative rather than absolute terms, so the coldest quarter-year is considered winter even if floral activity is regularly observed during it, despite the traditional association of flowers with spring and summer. The major exception is in the tropics where, as already noted, the winter season is not observed.
</p><p>The four seasons have been in use since at least Roman times, as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rerum_rusticarum" title="Rerum rusticarum">Rerum rusticarum</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varro" title="Varro">Varro</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Varro_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Varro-9">[9]</a></sup> Varro says that spring, summer, autumn, and winter start on the 23rd day of the sun's passage through Aquarius, Taurus, Leo, and Scorpio, respectively. Nine years before he wrote, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> had reformed the calendar, so Varro was able to assign the dates of February 7, May 9, August 11, and November 10 to the start of spring, summer, autumn, and winter.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official">Official</span></h3>
<p>As noted, a variety of dates and even exact times are used in different countries or regions to mark changes of the calendar seasons. These observances are often declared "official" within their respective areas by the local or national media, even when the weather or climate is contradictory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_spring_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-official_spring-10">[10]</a></sup>  However, they are mainly a matter of custom only, and have not generally been proclaimed by governments north or south of the equator for civil purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_seasons_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-official_seasons-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological">Meteorological</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_winter" title="Meteorological winter">Meteorological winter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_spring" title="Meteorological spring">Meteorological spring</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_summer" title="Meteorological summer">Meteorological summer</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_fall" title="Meteorological fall">Meteorological fall</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3620" data-file-width="5120" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg/260px-B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg/390px-B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg/520px-B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:B%C3%A4ume_Jahreszeit_2013.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Four temperate and subpolar seasons: (above) winter, spring, (below) summer, autumn/fall</div></div></div>
<p>Meteorological seasons are reckoned by temperature, with summer being the hottest quarter of the year and winter the coldest quarter of the year. In 1780 the Societas Meteorologica Palatina (which became defunct in 1795), an early international organization for meteorology, defined seasons as groupings of three whole months as identified by the Gregorian calendar. Ever since, professional meteorologists all over the world have used this definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
Therefore, for temperate areas in the northern hemisphere, spring begins on 1 March, summer on 1 June, autumn on 1 September, and winter on 1 December. For the southern hemisphere temperate zone, spring begins on 1 September, summer on 1 December, autumn on 1 March, and winter on 1 June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> the meteorological terms for seasons apply to the temperate zone that occupies all of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, the south-eastern corner of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> and the south-west of Western Australia, and the south east <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> areas south of <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>.
</p>

<p>In Sweden and Finland, meteorologists and news outlets use the concept of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_season&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thermal season (page does not exist)">thermal seasons</a>, which are defined based on mean daily temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The beginning of spring is defined as when the mean daily temperature permanently rises above 0 °C. The beginning of summer is defined as when the temperature permanently rises above +10 °C, autumn as when the temperature permanently falls below +10 °C, and winter as when the temperature permanently falls below 0 °C. In Finland, "permanently" is defined as when the mean daily averaged temperature remains above or below the defined limit for seven consecutive days. (In Sweden the number of days ranges from 5 to 7 depending on the season.) This implies two things: 
</p>
<ul><li>the seasons do not begin on fixed dates and must be determined by observation and are known only after the fact,</li>
<li>the seasons begin on different dates in different parts of the country.</li></ul>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD) designates four climatological seasons:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Winter</b>, occurring from December to February. The year's coldest months are December and January, when temperatures average around 10–15 °C (50–59 °F) in the northwest; temperatures rise as one proceeds towards the equator, peaking around 20–25 °C (68–77 °F) in mainland India's southeast.</li>
<li><b>Summer</b> or <b>pre-monsoon</b> season, lasting from March to May. In western and southern regions, the hottest month is April; for northern regions of India, May is the hottest month. Temperatures average around 32–40 °C (90–104 °F) in most of the interior.</li>
<li><b>Monsoon</b> or <b>rainy</b> season, lasting from June to September. The season is dominated by the humid southwest summer monsoon, which slowly sweeps across the country beginning in late May or early June. Monsoon rains begin to recede from North India at the beginning of October. South India typically receives more rainfall.</li>
<li><b>Post-monsoon</b> or <b>autumn</b> season, lasting from October to November. In the northwest of India, October and November are usually cloudless. Tamil Nadu receives most of its annual precipitation in the northeast monsoon season.</li></ul>

<p>Astronomical timing as the basis for designating the temperate seasons dates back at least to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_Calendar" title="Julian Calendar">Julian Calendar</a> used by the ancient Romans. As mentioned above, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varro" title="Varro">Varro</a> wrote that spring, summer, autumn, and winter start on the 23rd day of the sun's passage through Aquarius, Taurus, Leo, and Scorpio, respectively, and that (in the Julian Calendar) these days were February 7, May 9, August 11, and November 10. He points out that the lengths are not equal, being 91 (in non-leap years), 94, 91, and 89 days for spring, summer, autumn, and winter, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Varro_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Varro-9">[9]</a></sup> The midpoints of these seasons were March 24 or 25, June 25, September 25 or 26, and December 24 or 25, which are near to the equinoxes and solstices of his day.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i>, mentions the two equinoxes and the two solstices and gives the lengths of the intervals (values which were fairly correct in his day but are no longer very correct because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> has moved from December into January). He then defines the seasons of autumn, winter, spring, and summer as starting half-way through these intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He gives "the eighth day to the Kalends of January" (December 25) as the date of the winter solstice, though actually it occurred on the 22nd or 23rd at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nowadays the astronomical timing has winter starting at the winter solstice, spring at the spring equinox, and so on. This is used worldwide, although some countries like Australia, New Zealand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Pakistan and Russia prefer to use meteorological reckoning. The precise timing of the seasons is determined by the exact times of the sun reaching the tropics of Cancer and Capricorn for the <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstices</a> and the times of the sun's transit over the equator for the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>, or a traditional date close to these times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The following diagram shows the relation between the line of solstice and the line of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apsides" title="Apsides">apsides</a> of Earth's elliptical orbit.  The orbital ellipse (with eccentricity exaggerated for effect) goes through each of the six Earth images, which are sequentially the <a href="/wiki/Apsis#Earth's_perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Apsis">perihelion</a> (periapsis—nearest point to the sun) on anywhere from 2 January to 5 January, the point of March equinox on 19, 20 or 21 March, the point of June solstice on 20 or 21 June, the <a href="/wiki/Apsis#Earth's_perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Apsis">aphelion</a> (apoapsis—farthest point from the sun) on anywhere from 3 July to 6 July, the September equinox on 22 or 23 September, and the December solstice on 21 or 22 December.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:662px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seasons1.svg"><img alt="Illustration of seasonal distances from Earth to the Sun" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1070" data-file-width="1826" decoding="async" height="387" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Seasons1.svg/660px-Seasons1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Seasons1.svg/990px-Seasons1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Seasons1.svg/1320px-Seasons1.svg.png 2x" width="660"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seasons1.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Note: Distances are exaggerated and not to scale</div></div></div></div>
<p>These "astronomical" seasons are not of equal length, because of the <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptical nature</a> of the orbit of the Earth, as discovered by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a>.  From the March equinox it currently takes 92.75 days until the June solstice, then 93.65 days until the September equinox, 89.85 days until the December solstice and finally 88.99 days until the March equinox. Thus the time from the March equinox to the September equinox is 7.56 days longer than from the September equinox to the March equinox.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_due_to_calendar_misalignment">Variation due to calendar misalignment</span></h4>
<p>The times of the equinoxes and solstices are not fixed with respect to the modern Gregorian calendar, but fall about six hours later every year, amounting to one full day in four years. They are reset by the occurrence of a leap year. The Gregorian calendar is designed to keep the March equinox no later than 21 March as accurately as is practical. <i>Also see: <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar#Calendar_seasonal_error" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar seasonal error</a>.</i>
</p><p>The calendar equinox (used in the calculation of Easter) is 21 March, the same date as in the Easter tables current at the time of the Council of Nicaea in AD 325. The calendar is therefore framed to prevent the astronomical equinox wandering onto 22 March. From Nicaea to the date of the reform, the years 500, 600, 700, 900, 1000, 1100, 1300, 1400, and 1500, which would not have been leap years in the Gregorian calendar, amount to nine extra days, but astronomers directed that ten days be removed.  Because of this, the (<a href="/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar" title="Proleptic Gregorian calendar">proleptic</a>) Gregorian calendar agrees with the Julian calendar in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_century" title="Third century">third century</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_era" title="Christian era">Christian era</a>, rather than in the fourth.
</p><p>Currently, the most common equinox and solstice dates are March 20, June 21, September 22 or 23, and December 21; the four-year average slowly shifts to earlier times as a century progresses. This shift is a full day in about 128 years (compensated mainly by the century "leap year" rules of the Gregorian calendar); as 2000 was a leap year, the current shift has been progressing since the beginning of the last century, when equinoxes and solstices were relatively late. This also means that in many years of the twentieth century, the dates March 21, June 22, September 23, and December 22 were much more common, so older books teach (and older people may still remember) these dates.
</p><p>Note that all the times are given in <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> (roughly speaking, the time at <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a>, ignoring <a href="/wiki/British_Summer_Time" title="British Summer Time">British Summer Time</a>). People living farther to the east (Asia and Australia), whose local times are in advance, see the astronomical seasons apparently start later; for example, in <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> (UTC+13), an equinox occurred on September 24, 1999, a date on which the equinox will not fall again until 2103. On the other hand, people living far to the west (America), whose clocks run behind UTC, may experience an equinox as early as March 19.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_over_time">Change over time</span></h4>
<p>Over thousands of years, the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> and orbital eccentricity vary (see <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>). The equinoxes and solstices move westward relative to the stars while the perihelion and aphelion move eastward. Thus, ten thousand years from now Earth's northern winter will occur at aphelion and northern summer at perihelion. The severity of seasonal change — the average temperature difference between summer and winter in location — will also change over time because the Earth's axial tilt fluctuates between 22.1 and 24.5 degrees.
</p><p>Smaller irregularities in the times are caused by perturbations of the Moon and the other planets.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phases_of_the_Sun_(NHemi).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="777" data-file-width="777" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png/220px-Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png/330px-Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png/440px-Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phases_of_the_Sun_(NHemi).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The annual cycle of insolation (Sun energy, shown in blue) with key points for seasons (middle), <a href="/wiki/Quarter_days" title="Quarter days">quarter days</a> (top) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-quarter_days" title="Cross-quarter days">cross-quarter days</a> (bottom) along with months (lower) and Zodiac houses (upper).  The cycle of temperature (shown in pink) is delayed by <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">seasonal lag</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Solar timing is based on insolation in which the solstices and equinoxes are seen as the midpoints of the seasons. This was the case with the seasons described by the Roman scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varro" title="Varro">Varro</a> (see above). It was the method for reckoning seasons in medieval Europe, especially by the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, and is still ceremonially observed in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and some East Asian countries. Summer is defined as the quarter of the year with the greatest insolation and winter as the quarter with the least.
</p><p>The solar seasons change at the cross-quarter days, which are about 3–4 weeks earlier than the meteorological seasons and 6–7 weeks earlier than seasons starting at equinoxes and solstices.  Thus, the day of greatest insolation is designated "midsummer" as noted in <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i>, which is set on the summer solstice.  On the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_calendar" title="Celtic calendar">Celtic calendar</a>, the start of the seasons corresponds to four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagan" title="Pagan">Pagan</a> agricultural festivals - the traditional first day of winter is 1 November (<a href="/wiki/Samhain" title="Samhain">Samhain</a>, the Celtic origin of <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>); spring starts 1 February (<a href="/wiki/Imbolc" title="Imbolc">Imbolc</a>, the Celtic origin of <a href="/wiki/Groundhog_Day" title="Groundhog Day">Groundhog Day</a>); summer begins 1 May (<a href="/wiki/Beltane" title="Beltane">Beltane</a>, the Celtic origin of <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>); the first day of autumn is 1 August (Celtic <a href="/wiki/Lughnasadh" title="Lughnasadh">Lughnasadh</a>).
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_terms">Solar terms</span></h4>
<p><span class="anchor" id="China"></span><span class="anchor" id="Japan"></span><span class="anchor" id="East_Asia"></span>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">traditional calendar in China</a> has 4 seasons based on 24 periods known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_terms" title="Solar terms">solar terms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The four seasons <i>chūn</i> (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%A5" title="wikt:春">春</a></span></span>), <i>xià</i> (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%8F" title="wikt:夏">夏</a></span></span>), <i>qiū</i> (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A7%8B" title="wikt:秋">秋</a></span></span>), and <i>dōng</i> (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%86%AC" title="wikt:冬">冬</a></span></span>)—universally translated as "spring", "summer", "autumn", and "winter"—each center around the respective solstice or equinox. Astronomically, the seasons are said to begin on Lichun (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AB%8B%E6%98%A5" title="wikt:立春">立春</a></span></span>, "the start of spring") on about 4 February, Lixia (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AB%8B%E5%A4%8F" title="wikt:立夏">立夏</a></span></span>) on about 6 May, Liqiu (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AB%8B%E7%A7%8B" title="wikt:立秋">立秋</a></span></span>) on about 8 August, and Lidong (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AB%8B%E5%86%AC" title="wikt:立冬">立冬</a></span></span>) on about 7 November. These dates were not part of the traditional lunar calendar, however, and moveable holidays such as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival" title="Mid-Autumn Festival">Mid-Autumn Festival</a> are more closely associated with the seasons. It forms the basis of other such systems in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar calendars</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Six-season_reckoning">Six-season reckoning</span></h2>

The six seasons are ascribed to two months each of the twelve months in the Hindu calendar. The rough correspondences are:

<p>Ecologically speaking, a season is a period of the year in which only certain types of floral and animal events happen (e.g.: flowers bloom—spring; <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a> hibernate—winter). So, if we can observe a change in daily floral and animal events, the season is changing.  In this sense, ecological seasons are defined in absolute terms, unlike calendar-based methods in which the seasons are relative. If specific conditions associated with a particular ecological season don't normally occur in a particular region, then that area cannot be said to experience that season on a regular basis.
</p><p>In Great Britain, the onset of spring used to be defined<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2021)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> as when the maximum daily temperature reached 50 °F (10 °C) in a defined sequence of days. This almost always occurred in March. However, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> this temperature is now not uncommon in the winter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_mid-latitude_ecological">Modern mid-latitude ecological</span></h3>
<p>Six ecological seasons can be distinguished without fixed calendar-based dates like the meteorological and astronomical seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic</a> regions tend to experience the beginning of the hibernal season up to a month later than <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification#Group_D:_Continental/microthermal_climates" title="Köppen climate classification">continental climates</a>. Conversely, prevernal and vernal seasons begin up to a month earlier near oceanic and coastal areas. For example, prevernal <a href="/wiki/Crocus" title="Crocus">crocus</a> blooms typically appear as early as February in coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, but generally don't appear until March or April in locations like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a> USA or parts of eastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The actual dates for each season vary by climate region and can shift from one year to the next.  Average dates listed here are for mild and cool temperate climate zones in the Northern Hemisphere:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Prevernal</b> (early or pre-spring): Begins February (mild temperate), to March (cool temperate). Deciduous tree buds begin to swell. Some types of migrating birds fly from winter to summer habitats.</li>
<li><b>Vernal</b> (spring): Begins mid March (mild temperate), to late April (cool temperate). Tree buds burst into leaves. Birds establish territories and begin mating and nesting.</li>
<li><b>Estival</b> (high summer): Begins June in most temperate climates. Trees in full leaf. Birds hatch and raise offspring.</li>
<li><b>Serotinal</b> (late summer): Generally begins mid to late August. Deciduous leaves begin to change color in higher latitude locations (above 45 north). Young birds reach maturity and join other adult birds preparing for autumn migration.  The traditional "harvest season" begins by early September.</li>
<li><b>Autumnal</b> (autumn): Generally begins mid to late September. Tree leaves in full color then turn brown and fall to the ground. Birds migrate back to wintering areas.</li>
<li><b>Hibernal</b> (winter): Begins December (mild temperate), November (cool temperate). Deciduous trees are bare and fallen leaves begin to decay. Migrating birds settled in winter habitats.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_ecological">Indigenous ecological</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australian_seasons" title="Indigenous Australian seasons">Indigenous Australian seasons</a></div>
<p>Indigenous people in polar, temperate and tropical climates of northern Eurasia, the Americas, Africa, Oceania, and Australia have traditionally defined the seasons ecologically by observing the activity of the plants, animals and weather around them. Each separate tribal group traditionally observes different seasons determined according to local criteria that can vary from the hibernation of polar bears on the arctic tundras to the <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing seasons</a> of plants in the tropical rainforests.  In Australia, some tribes have up to eight seasons in a year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons-14">[14]</a></sup> as do the Sami people in Scandinavia. Many indigenous people who no longer live directly off the land in traditional often nomadic styles, now observe modern methods of seasonal reckoning according to what is customary in their particular country or region.
</p><p>The North American <a href="/wiki/Cree" title="Cree">Cree</a> and possibly other <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a> speaking peoples used or still use a 6-season system. The extra two seasons denoting the freezing and breaking up of the ice on rivers and lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_seasons">Two seasons</span></h4>
<p>In the tropics, where seasonal dates also vary, it is more common to speak of the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">rainy</a> (or wet, or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>) season versus the <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> the dry season (November to April) is called "summer" and the rainy season (May to October) is called "winter", even though it is located in the northern hemisphere. There is no noticeable change in the amount of sunlight at different times of the year. However, many regions (such as the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_ocean" title="Indian ocean">Indian ocean</a>) are subject to <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> and wind cycles.
</p><p>Floral and animal activity variation near the equator depends more on wet/dry cycles than seasonal temperature variations, with different species flowering (or emerging from cocoons) at specific times before, during, or after the monsoon season. Thus, the tropics are characterized by numerous "mini-seasons" within the larger seasonal blocks of time.
</p><p>In the tropical parts of Australia in the northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>,  wet and dry seasons are observed in addition to or in place of temperate season names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar">Polar</span></h3>
<p>Any point north of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> or south of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a> will have one period in the summer called "polar day" when the sun does not set, and one period in the winter called 'polar night' when the sun does not rise. At progressively higher latitudes, the maximum periods of "<a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">midnight sun</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">polar night</a>" are progressively longer.
</p><p>For example, at the military and weather station <a href="/wiki/Alert,_Nunavut" title="Alert, Nunavut">Alert</a> located at 82°30′05″N and 62°20′20″W, on the northern tip of <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (about 450 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> or 830 km from the North Pole), the sun begins to peek above the horizon for minutes per day at the end of February and each day it climbs higher and stays up longer; by 21 March, the sun is up for over 12 hours. On 6 April the sun rises at 0522 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> and remains above the horizon until it sets below the horizon again on 6 September at 0335 UTC.  By October 13 the sun is above the horizon for only 1 hour 30 minutes, and on October 14 it does not rise above the horizon at all and remains below the horizon until it rises again on 27 February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>First light comes in late January because the sky has <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>, being a glow on the horizon, for increasing hours each day, for more than a month before the sun first appears with its disc above the horizon. From mid-November to mid-January, there is no twilight.
</p><p>In the weeks surrounding 21 June, in the northern polar region, the sun is at its highest elevation, appearing to circle the sky there without going below the horizon. Eventually, it does go below the horizon, for progressively longer periods each day until around the middle of October, when it disappears for the last time until the following February. For a few more weeks, "day" is marked by decreasing periods of twilight. Eventually, from mid-November to mid-January, there is no twilight and it is continuously dark. In mid January the first faint wash of twilight briefly touches the horizon (for just minutes per day), and then twilight increases in duration with increasing brightness each day until sunrise at end of February, then on 6 April the sun remains above the horizon until mid October.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_campaigning_seasons">Military campaigning seasons<span class="anchor" id="Military_campaigning_seasons"></span></span></h3>
<p>Seasonal weather and climate conditions can become important in the context of military operations.  Seasonal reckoning in the military of any country or region tends to be very fluid and based mainly on short to medium term weather conditions that are independent of the calendar.
</p><p>For navies, the presence of accessible ports and bases can allow naval operations during certain (variable) seasons of the year. The availability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-free_harbor" title="Ice-free harbor"> ice-free</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm-water_port" title="Warm-water port">warm-water ports</a> can make navies much more effective. Thus <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, historically navally constrained when confined to using <a href="/wiki/Arkhangelsk" title="Arkhangelsk">Arkhangelsk</a> (before the 18th century) and even <a href="/wiki/Kronstadt" title="Kronstadt">Kronstadt</a>, has particular interests in maintaining and in preserving access to <a href="/wiki/Baltiysk" title="Baltiysk">Baltiysk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sevastopol" title="Sevastopol">Sevastopol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
Storm seasons or polar winter-weather conditions can inhibit surface warships at sea.
</p><p>Pre-modern armies, especially in Europe, tended to campaign in the summer months - peasant <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription"> conscripts</a> tended to melt away at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvest_time" title="Harvest time">harvest time</a>, nor did it make economic sense in an agricultural society to neglect the <a href="/wiki/Sowing" title="Sowing">sowing</a> season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
Any modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manoeuvre_warfare" title="Manoeuvre warfare"> war of manoeuvre</a> profits from firm ground - summer can provide dry conditions suitable for marching and transport, frozen snow in winter can also offer a reliable surface for a period, but <a href="/wiki/Rasputitsa" title="Rasputitsa"> spring thaws or autumn rains</a> can inhibit campaigning. Rainy-season floods may make rivers temporarily impassable, and winter snow tends to block mountain passes. Taliban offensives are usually confined to the <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan_fighting_season" title="Afghanistan fighting season">Afghanistan fighting season</a>.
</p>

<li id="cite_note-26"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-26">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In a space of four yeaers, the solstice occurs latest in the Julian Calendar in the year before a leap year. In 2019, it occurred on the 22nd in the Gregorian Calendar, or December 9 in the Julian, at 4:19 AM, according to <a class="external text" href="http://aa.usno.navy.mil/data/docs/EarthSeasons.php" rel="nofollow">Earth's Seasons Equinoxes, Solstices, Perihelion, and Aphelion</a>. The number of days betwwen successive winter solstices varied from 365.242883 to 365.242740 between the year 1 BC and AD 2000, according to <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1067248974" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><cite class="citation journal cs1" id="CITEREFMeeusSavoie1992"><a href="/wiki/Jean_Meeus" title="Jean Meeus">Meeus, J.</a>; Savoie, D. (1992). "The history of the tropical year". <i>Journal of the British Astronomical Association</i>. <b>102</b> (1): 40–42. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibcode_(identifier)" title="Bibcode (identifier)">Bibcode</a>:<a class="external text" href="https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1992JBAA..102...40M" rel="nofollow">1992JBAA..102...40M</a>.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft.jtitle=Journal+of+the+British+Astronomical+Association&amp;rft.atitle=The+history+of+the+tropical+year&amp;rft.volume=102&amp;rft.issue=1&amp;rft.pages=40-42&amp;rft.date=1992&amp;rft_id=info%3Abibcode%2F1992JBAA..102...40M&amp;rft.aulast=Meeus&amp;rft.aufirst=J.&amp;rft.au=Savoie%2C+D.&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ASeason"></span>. Therefore, the average value over the last 2000 years has been 365.24281 days, 0.00719 days less than an average Julian year. This means the solstice was 2000×0.00719=14.38 days later, that is, on December 23 in the middle of the day.A hundred years earlier it would have been on the 24th.</span>
