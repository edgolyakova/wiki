<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1086" data-file-width="1417" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg/230px-Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg/345px-Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg/460px-Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bian_V%27nu_Saint_Pi%C3%A8rre_Port_Dg%C3%A8rn%C3%A9sy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A family support centre in Saint Peter Port, <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, which provides assistance to families with children.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Welfare</b>, or commonly <b>social welfare</b>, is a type of government support intended to ensure that members of a society can meet <a href="/wiki/Basic_needs" title="Basic needs">basic human needs</a> such as food and shelter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>Social security</b> may either be synonymous with welfare,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[a]</a></sup> or refer specifically to <a href="/wiki/Social_insurance" title="Social insurance">social insurance</a> programs which provide support only to those who have previously contributed (e.g. most pension systems), as opposed to <i>social assistance</i> programs which provide support on the basis of need alone (e.g. most disability benefits).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeissbrodtVega2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WeissbrodtVega2007-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robert2004-8">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Labour_Organization" title="International Labour Organization">International Labour Organization</a> defines social security as covering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_age_pension" title="Old age pension">support for those in old age</a>, <a href="/wiki/Child_benefit" title="Child benefit">support for the maintenance of children</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_healthcare" title="Universal healthcare">medical treatment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parental_leave" title="Parental leave">parental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sick_leave" title="Sick leave">sick leave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_benefits" title="Unemployment benefits">unemployment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disability_benefits" title="Disability benefits">disability benefits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_compensation" title="Workers' compensation">support for sufferers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Occupational_injury" title="Occupational injury">occupational injury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pennings2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pennings2006-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>More broadly, welfare may also encompass efforts to provide a basic level of <a href="/wiki/Well-being" title="Well-being">well-being</a> through free or <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidized</a> <i>social services</i> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_healthcare" title="Universal healthcare">healthcare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Free_education" title="Free education">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocational_training" title="Vocational training">vocational training</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_housing" title="Public housing">public housing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vrooman2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vrooman2009-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919-12">[11]</a></sup> In a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>, the state assumes responsibility for the health, education, infrastructure and welfare of society, providing a range of social services such as those described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some historians view systems of codified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almsgiving" title="Almsgiving">almsgiving</a>, like the <i>zakat</i> policy of the 7th century (634 CE) <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate" title="Rashidun Caliphate">Rashidun caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Umar</a>, as early examples of universal government welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>  The first welfare state was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Germany" title="Imperial Germany">Imperial Germany</a> (1871–1918), where the <a href="/wiki/Social_security_in_Germany" title="Social security in Germany">Bismarck government introduced social security</a> in 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> In the early 20th century, the United Kingdom introduced social security around 1913, and adopted the welfare state with the <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance_Act_1946" title="National Insurance Act 1946">National Insurance Act 1946</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Attlee government</a> (1945–51).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919-12">[11]</a></sup> In the countries of western Europe, Australia, and New Zealand, social welfare is mainly provided by the government out of the national <a href="/wiki/Tax_revenue" title="Tax revenue">tax revenues</a>, and to a lesser extent by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGO" title="NGO">non-government organizations</a> (NGOs), and <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">charities</a> (social and religious).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Thought_Third_Edition_1999_p._919-12">[11]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Right_to_social_security" title="Right to social security">right to social security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Right_to_an_adequate_standard_of_living" title="Right to an adequate standard of living">an adequate standard of living</a> is asserted in Articles 22 and 25 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeissbrodtVega2007_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WeissbrodtVega2007-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[b]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state#History" title="Welfare state">Welfare state § History</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abbey_of_Port-Royal,_Distributing_Alms_to_the_Poor_by_Louise-Magdeleine_Hortemels_c._1710.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="306" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Abbey_of_Port-Royal%2C_Distributing_Alms_to_the_Poor_by_Louise-Magdeleine_Hortemels_c._1710.jpg/240px-Abbey_of_Port-Royal%2C_Distributing_Alms_to_the_Poor_by_Louise-Magdeleine_Hortemels_c._1710.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Abbey_of_Port-Royal%2C_Distributing_Alms_to_the_Poor_by_Louise-Magdeleine_Hortemels_c._1710.jpg 1.5x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abbey_of_Port-Royal,_Distributing_Alms_to_the_Poor_by_Louise-Magdeleine_Hortemels_c._1710.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distributing <a href="/wiki/Alms" title="Alms">alms</a> to the poor, abbey of Port-Royal des Champs c. 1710.</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, the first emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> provided the <i><a href="/wiki/Cura_Annonae" title="Cura Annonae">Cura Annonae</a></i> or grain dole for citizens who could not afford to buy food every month. Social welfare was enlarged by the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> Trajan's program brought acclaim from many, including <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger" title="Pliny the Younger">Pliny the Younger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> government (960 CE) supported multiple programs which could be classified as social welfare, including the establishment of retirement homes, public clinics, and paupers' graveyards. According to economist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nelson_(economist)" title="Robert Nelson (economist)">Robert Henry Nelson</a>, "The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> operated a far-reaching and comprehensive welfare system for the poor<span class="nowrap"> </span>..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> From the 14th century onward the governments of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_city-states" title="Italian city-states">Italian city-states</a> began to partner with the church to provide welfare and education to the lower classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1997_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1997-21">[19]</a></sup>  In later Protestant European nations such as the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> welfare was managed by local <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a> until the abolition of the guild system in the early 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leeuwen2016_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leeuwen2016-22">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HarrisBridgen2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HarrisBridgen2012-23">[21]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_imperial_cities" title="Free imperial cities">free imperial cities</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> the city governments in cities like <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> could take control of the collection and distribution of public welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holborn1982_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holborn1982-24">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CollinsTaylor2008_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-CollinsTaylor2008-25">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 7th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Umar</a> implemented a form of <i><a href="/wiki/Zakat" title="Zakat">zakat</a></i>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Pillars_of_Islam" title="Five Pillars of Islam">Five Pillars of Islam</a>, as a codified universal social security tax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> Traditionally estimated at 2.5% of an individual's assets, government <i>zakat</i> funds were distributed to various groups of Muslims, including <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">impoverished people</a> and those in severe debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> The collection of <i>zakat</i> increased during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> caliphates, though the <i>zakat</i> system was frequently inefficient and corrupt; Islamic jurists often instructed Muslims to distribute money to the needy directly instead to maximize its impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Likewise, in Jewish tradition, charity (represented by <a href="/wiki/Tzedakah" title="Tzedakah">tzedakah</a>) is a matter of religious obligation rather than benevolence. Contemporary charity is regarded as a continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maaser_Ani" title="Maaser Ani">Maaser Ani</a>, or poor-<a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithe</a>, as well as Biblical practices, such as permitting the poor to glean the corners of a field and harvest during the <a href="/wiki/Shmita" title="Shmita">Shmita</a> (Sabbatical year).
</p><p>There is relatively little <a href="/wiki/Statistic" title="Statistic">statistical</a> data on <a href="/wiki/Transfer_payment" title="Transfer payment">transfer payments</a> before the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> period and until the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, the function of welfare payments in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> was achieved through private giving or <a href="/wiki/Charity_(practice)" title="Charity (practice)">charity</a>, through numerous <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">confraternities</a> and activities of different <a href="/wiki/Religious_order" title="Religious order">religious orders</a>. Early welfare programs in Europe included the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Act_for_the_Relief_of_the_Poor_1601" title="Act for the Relief of the Poor 1601">Poor Law of 1601</a>, which gave <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> the responsibility for providing welfare payments to the poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Poor_relief" title="Poor relief">This system</a> was substantially modified by the 19th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poor_Law_Amendment_Act" title="Poor Law Amendment Act">Poor Law Amendment Act</a>, which introduced the system of <a href="/wiki/Workhouse" title="Workhouse">workhouses</a>.
</p><p>It was predominantly in the late 19th and early 20th centuries that an organized system of state welfare provision was introduced in many countries. <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_German_Empire" title="Chancellor of the German Empire">Chancellor</a> of Germany, introduced one of the first welfare systems for the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working classes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WB_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-WB-31">[29]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Henry Campbell-Bannerman</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance" title="National Insurance">National Insurance</a> system in 1911,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> a system later expanded by <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>.
</p><p>Modern welfare states include Germany, France, the Netherlands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>, such as Iceland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, and Finland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> which employ a system known as the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_model" title="Nordic model">Nordic model</a>. Esping-Andersen classified the most developed welfare state systems into three categories; Social Democratic, Conservative, and Liberal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>A report published by the ILO in 2014 estimated that only 27% of the world's population has access to comprehensive social security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>'s 2019 <a href="/wiki/World_Development_Report" title="World Development Report">World Development Report</a> argues that the traditional payroll-based model of many kinds of social insurance are "increasingly challenged by working arrangements outside standard employment contracts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WB_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-WB-31">[29]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Welfare can take a variety of forms, such as monetary payments, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsidies" title="Subsidies">subsidies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voucher" title="Voucher">vouchers</a>, or housing assistance. Welfare systems differ from country to country, but welfare is commonly provided to individuals who are <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployed</a>, those with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illness" title="Illness">illness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elderly" title="Elderly">elderly</a>, those with dependent children, and <a href="/wiki/Veteran" title="Veteran">veterans</a>. Programs may have a variety of conditions for a person to receive welfare:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Social_insurance" title="Social insurance">Social insurance</a>, state-sponsored programs based partly on individual contributions towards benefits such as healthcare, unemployment payments, and old-age pensions.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Means-tested" title="Means-tested">Means-tested</a> benefits, financial assistance provided for those who are unable to cover basic needs, such as food, clothing and housing, due to <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> or lack of income because of unemployment, sickness, disability, or caring for children. While assistance is often in the form of financial payments, those eligible for social welfare can usually access health and educational services free of charge. The amount of support is enough to cover basic needs and eligibility is often subject to a comprehensive and complex assessment of an applicant's social and financial situation. See also <a href="/wiki/Income_Support" title="Income Support">Income Support</a>.</li>
<li>Non-contributory benefits. Several countries have special schemes, administered with no requirement for contributions and no means test, for people in certain categories of need, such as veterans of armed forces, people with disabilities, and very old people.</li>
<li>Discretionary benefits. Some schemes are based on the decision of an official, such as a social worker.</li>
<li>Universal or categorical benefits, also known as demogrants. These are non-contributory benefits given for whole sections of the population without a means test, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_allowance" title="Family allowance">family allowances</a> or the public pension in New Zealand (known as <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Superannuation_Fund" title="New Zealand Superannuation Fund">New Zealand Superannuation</a>). See also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alaska_Permanent_Fund_Dividend" title="Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend">Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_protection">Social protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_protection" title="Social protection">Social protection</a></div>
<p>In developing countries, formal social security arrangements are often absent for the vast majority of the working population, in part due to reliance on the <a href="/wiki/Informal_economy" title="Informal economy">informal economy</a>. Additionally, the state's capacity to reach people may be limited because of its limited infrastructure and resources. In this context, <i>social protection</i> is often referred to instead of social security, encompassing a broader set of means, such as <a href="/wiki/Active_labour_market_policies" title="Active labour market policies">labour market intervention</a> and local community-based programs, to alleviate poverty and provide security against things like unemployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_security_in_Australia" title="Social security in Australia">Social security in Australia</a></div>
<p>Prior to 1900 in Australia, charitable assistance from benevolent societies, sometimes with financial contributions from the authorities, was the primary means of relief for people not able to support themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abs-1988_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-abs-1988-40">[38]</a></sup> The 1890s economic depression and the rise of the trade unions and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party#History" title="Australian Labor Party">Labor parties</a> during this period led to a movement for welfare reform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dict-syd-2008_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-dict-syd-2008-41">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1900, the states of New South Wales and Victoria enacted legislation introducing non-contributory pensions for those aged 65 and over. Queensland legislated a similar system in 1907 before the Australian labor Commonwealth government led by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Fisher" title="Andrew Fisher">Andrew Fisher</a> introduced a national aged pension under the Invalid and Old-Aged Pensions Act 1908. A national invalid disability pension was started in 1910, and a national maternity allowance was introduced in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abs-1988_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-abs-1988-40">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia-wr-2000_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-australia-wr-2000-42">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Second World War, Australia under a labor government created a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> by enacting national schemes for: child endowment in 1941 (superseding the 1927 New South Wales scheme); a widows’ pension in 1942 (superseding the New South Wales 1926 scheme); a wife's allowance in 1943; additional allowances for the children of pensioners in 1943; and unemployment, sickness, and special benefits in 1945 (superseding the Queensland 1923 scheme).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abs-1988_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-abs-1988-40">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia-wr-2000_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-australia-wr-2000-42">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_programs_in_Canada" title="Social programs in Canada">Social programs in Canada</a></div>
<p>Canada has a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> in the European tradition; however, it is not referred to as "welfare", but rather as "social programs". In Canada, "welfare" usually refers specifically to direct payments to poor individuals (as in the American usage) and not to healthcare and education spending (as in the European usage).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-43">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Canadian <a href="/wiki/Social_safety_net" title="Social safety net">social safety net</a> covers a broad spectrum of programs, and because Canada is a <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federalism" title="Canadian federalism">federation</a>, many are run by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Canada" title="Provinces of Canada">provinces</a>. Canada has a wide range of government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transfer_payments" title="Transfer payments">transfer payments</a> to individuals, which totaled $145<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-govtransfers_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-govtransfers-44">[42]</a></sup> Only social programs that direct funds to individuals are included in that cost; programs such as <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(Canada)" title="Medicare (Canada)">medicare</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_education" title="Public education">public education</a> are additional costs.
</p><p>Generally speaking, before the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_Canada" title="Great Depression in Canada">Great Depression</a>, most social services were provided by religious charities and other private groups. Changing government policy between the 1930s and 1960s saw the emergence of a welfare state, similar to many <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western European</a> countries. Most programs from that era are still in use, although many were scaled back during the 1990s as government priorities shifted towards reducing <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_budget_deficit" title="Government budget deficit">deficits</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">Czech Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Social welfare in <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> is outlined in a series of <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">social policies</a>, as is the tradition in Europe. Their goal is primarily preventing, but also mitigating social situations individuals may find themselves in through their lives. The social welfare is provided through social (including <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a>) insurance, sick insurance (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Health_insurance" title="Health insurance">health insurance</a>), public policy related to unemployment and low income benefits, which are financed through the government budget, and health insurance, which is financed through an array of insurance companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> Therefore, the social welfare program is usually separated into three categories: health insurance, social insurance and social benefits support.
</p><p>Social insurance is a type of <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statutory</a> insurance that provides citizens for a future unforeseen social event, such as unemployment or disability that would prevent an individual from working, but also planned retirement. Social insurance is therefore compulsory for all self-employed individuals, employees and employers operating in the Czech Republic and gets deducted from a salary in a similar manner to taxes. This insurance serves to finance unemployment support, disability benefits and covers a part of an employer's salary in cases of long-term illness that obstructs them from participating in the workforce.
</p><p>The sickness insurance system is intended for gainfully employed individuals who, in cases of short-term social events, are provided with health insurance benefits. These are provided through financial benefits. Sickness insurance participants are employees and self-employed individuals. Employees are compulsorily covered by sickness insurance, unlike the self-employed, whose sickness insurance is voluntary (and there is no penalisation for opting out of the insurance). It is calculated through a series of reductions of an assessment base. Employees' sickness insurance provides 4 types of sickness benefits: benefits for caring for a family member, pregnancy and maternity allowance, maternity allowance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup> It is standard to go on paid maternity leave in the Czech Republic, which ordinarily lasts 28 weeks. This period is prolonged to 37 weeks in the case of twins. Furthermore, there are special circumstances, such as the birth of a disabled child, when this period is extended even more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup> The beginning of maternity leave is usually 6 weeks before expected due date, and after maternity leave, it is possible to request further parental leave, which is optional and can be performed by either parent, regardless of gender. Parental leave lasts upto 4 years, depending on individual preferences.
</p><p>Pension insurance is part of social insurance, in addition to sickness insurance and serves as a contribution to the state employment policy. It is a premium for old age retirement, disability retirement or benefits in the event of the death of the primary provider in a household. Participation in pension insurance is mandatory for economically active individuals. For those on various forms of benefits, or currently registered as a care provider, this insurance is provided by the state. The basic pension insurance provides, in time old-age pension (includes regular, proportional, early and other variants of old-age pension), disability benefits, widower's and orphan's pension. Pension insurance is applied for at the District Social Security Administration in the applicant's place of permanent residence. They can apply in person or by proxy, on the basis of a notarized power of attorney. The amount of the pension consists of two component - the basic acreage and the percentage acreage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another form of pension is the disability pension. This is provided to individuals that are unable to participate in the workforce to the same degree as their able-bodied counterparts, due to their disability contributing to the decline of their ability to work (be it partially or entirely). There is a distinction between disability pension for individuals with first, second and third degree disability. The amount of the pension depends on the rate of decline of the person's ability to work, with the categories being divided between the 1st degree (a decrease of 35-49%), the 2nd degree (a decrease of 50-69%), and the 3rd degree (a decrease of 70% and above).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, there is a so-called widow's / widower's pension. Provided to widowed individuals from the Czech pension system, its aim is to compensate households for losing income that they would in this situation be lacking. The widowed individual is entitled to a pension from a deceased person who has received an old-age or disability pension or has fulfilled the statutory condition on the required insurance period at the date of death. The amount of the assessment is 10% of the average wage and the amount of the percentage assessment is 50% of the percentage of the old-age or disability pension to which the deceased was entitled at the time of their death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="extiw" href="https://da.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kontanthj%C3%A6lp" title="da:Kontanthjælp">da:Kontanthjælp</a></div>
<p>Danish welfare is handled by the state through a series of policies (and the like) that seeks to provide welfare services to citizens, hence the term welfare state. This refers not only to social benefits, but also tax-funded education, public child care, medical care, etc. A number of these services are not provided by the state directly, but administered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities" title="Municipalities">municipalities</a>, regions or private providers through outsourcing. This sometimes gives a source of tension between the state and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities" title="Municipalities">municipalities</a>, as there is not always consistency between the promises of welfare provided by the state (i.e. parliament) and local perception of what it would cost to fulfill these promises.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Programs_in_India" title="Social Programs in India">Social Programs in India</a></div>
<p>The Central Government of India's social programmes and welfare expenditures are a substantial portion of the official budget, and state and local governments play roles in developing and implementing social security policies. Additional welfare measure systems are also uniquely operated by various state governments. The government uses the unique identity number (Aadhar) that every Indian possesses to distribute welfare measures in India. As of 2020, the government's expenditure on social programme and welfare (direct cash transfers, financial inclusion, benefits, health and other insurances, subsidies, free school meals, rural employment guarantee), was approximately fourteen <a href="/wiki/Crore#Money" title="Crore">lakh crore</a> rupees ($192<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion), which was 7.3% of gross domestic product (GDP).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Poverty_in_France" title="Poverty in France">Poverty in France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_protection_in_France" title="Social protection in France">Social protection in France</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Fifth_Risk_Plan" title="French Fifth Risk Plan">French Fifth Risk Plan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revenu_de_solidarit%C3%A9_active" title="Revenu de solidarité active">Revenu de solidarité active</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Revenu_minimum_d%27insertion" title="Revenu minimum d'insertion">Revenu minimum d'insertion</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solidarity" title="Solidarity">Solidarity</a> is a strong value of the French Social Protection system. The first article of the French Code of Social Security describes the principle of solidarity. Solidarity is commonly comprehended in relations of similar work, shared responsibility and common risks. Existing solidarities in France caused the expansion of health and social security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[51]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welfare_in_Germany" title="Welfare in Germany">Welfare in Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hartz_concept#Hartz_IV" title="Hartz concept">Hartz_concept § Hartz_IV</a></div>
<p>The welfare state has a long tradition in Germany dating back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>. Due to the pressure of the workers' movement in the late 19th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reichskanzler" title="Reichskanzler">Reichskanzler</a> <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a> introduced the first rudimentary state <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck#Warfare_state" title="Otto von Bismarck">social insurance scheme</a>. Under <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_Program" title="National Socialist Program">National Socialist Program</a> stated "We demand an expansion on a large scale of old age welfare." Today, the social protection of all its citizens is considered a central pillar of German national policy. 27.6 percent of Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a> is channeled into an all-embracing system of health, <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_Statutory_Accident_Insurance" title="German Statutory Accident Insurance">accident</a>, longterm care and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unemployment_insurance" title="Unemployment insurance">unemployment insurance</a>, compared to 16.2 percent in the US. In addition, there are tax-financed services such as child benefits (<i>Kindergeld</i>, beginning at €192 per month for the first and second child, €198 for the third and €223 for each child thereafter, until they attain 25 years or receive their first professional qualification),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[52]</a></sup> and basic provisions for those unable to work or anyone with an income below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 2005, reception of full unemployment pay (60–67% of the previous net <a href="/wiki/Salary" title="Salary">salary</a>) has been restricted to 12 months in general and 18 months for those over 55. This is now followed by (usually much lower) <i>Arbeitslosengeld II (ALG II)</i> or <i>Sozialhilfe</i>, which is independent of previous employment (<a href="/wiki/Hartz_concept#Hartz_IV" title="Hartz concept">Hartz IV</a> concept).
</p><p>As of 2022<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, under ALG II, single adults receive up to €449 per month plus the cost of 'adequate' housing. ALG II can also be paid partially to employed persons to supplement a low work income.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_welfare_state" title="Italian welfare state">Italian welfare state</a></div>
<p>The Italian welfare state's foundations were laid along the lines of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporatist" title="Corporatist">corporatist</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservative" title="Conservative">conservative</a> model, or of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> variant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Later, in the 1960s and 1970s, increases in public spending and a major focus on universality brought it on the same path as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social-democratic" title="Social-democratic">social-democratic</a> systems. In 1978, a universalistic welfare model was introduced in Italy, offering a number of universal and free services such as a National Health Fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Welfare_in_Japan" title="Welfare in Japan">Welfare in Japan</a></div>
<p>Social welfare, assistance for the ill or otherwise disabled and for the old, has long been provided in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> by both the government and private companies. Beginning in the 1920s, the government enacted a series of welfare programs, based mainly on European models, to provide medical care and financial support. During the postwar period, a comprehensive system of social security was gradually established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The 1980s marked a change in the structure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American" title="Latin American">Latin American</a> social protection programs. Social protection embraces three major areas: social insurance, financed by workers and employers; social assistance to the population's poorest, financed by the state; and labor market regulations to protect worker rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-59">[57]</a></sup> Although diverse, recent Latin American social policy has tended to concentrate on social assistance.
</p><p>The 1980s, had a significant effect on social protection policies. Prior to the 1980s, most Latin American countries focused on social insurance policies involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_sector" title="Formal sector">formal sector</a> workers, assuming that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informal_sector" title="Informal sector">informal sector</a> would disappear with <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>. The economic crisis of the 1980s and the <a href="/wiki/Liberalization" title="Liberalization">liberalization</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_market" title="Labor market">labor market</a> led to a growing informal sector and a rapid increase in poverty and inequality. Latin American countries did not have the institutions and funds to properly handle such a crisis, both due to the structure of the social security system, and to the previously implemented <a href="/wiki/Structural_adjustment" title="Structural adjustment">structural adjustment</a> policies (SAPs) that had decreased the size of the state.
</p><p>New Welfare programs have integrated the multidimensional, <a href="/wiki/Social_risk_management" title="Social risk management">social risk management</a>, and capabilities approaches into poverty alleviation. They focus on income transfers and service provisions while aiming to alleviate both long- and short-term poverty through, among other things, education, health, security, and housing. Unlike previous programs that targeted the working class, new programs have successfully focused on locating and targeting the very poorest.
</p><p>The impacts of social assistance programs vary between countries, and many programs have yet to be fully evaluated. According to Barrientos and Santibanez, the programs have been more successful in increasing investment in <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">human capital</a> than in bringing households above the poverty line. Challenges still exist, including the extreme inequality levels and the mass scale of poverty; locating a financial basis for programs; and deciding on <a href="/wiki/Exit_strategy" title="Exit strategy">exit strategies</a> or on the long-term establishment of programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-test-59">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s_impacts">1980s impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 1980s impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The economic crisis of the 1980s led to a shift in social policies, as understandings of poverty and social programs evolved (24). New, mostly short-term programs emerged. These include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test1_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-test1-60">[58]</a></sup>
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>: <i>Bonosol</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>: <i>Bolsa Escola and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolsa_Familia" title="Bolsa Familia">Bolsa Familia</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>: <i>Chile Solidario</i></li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_aspects_of_current_social_assistance_programs">Major aspects of current social assistance programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Major aspects of current social assistance programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Conditional_cash_transfer" title="Conditional cash transfer">Conditional cash transfer</a> (CCT) combined with service provisions</b>. Transfer cash directly to households, most often through the women of the household, if certain conditions are met (e.g. children's school attendance or doctor visits) (10). Providing <a href="/wiki/Free_education" title="Free education">free schooling</a> or healthcare is often not sufficient, because there is an opportunity cost for the parents in, for example, sending children to school (lost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_power" title="Labor power">labor power</a>), or in paying for the transportation costs of getting to a health clinic.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">Household</a></b>. The household has been the focal point of social assistance programs.</li>
<li><b>Target the poorest</b>. Recent programs have been more successful than past ones in targeting the poorest. Previous programs often targeted the working class.</li>
<li><b>Multidimensional</b>. Programs have attempted to address many dimensions of poverty at once. Chile Solidario is the best example.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Welfare_in_New_Zealand" title="Welfare in New Zealand">Welfare in New Zealand</a></div>
<p>New Zealand is often regarded as having one of the first comprehensive welfare systems in the world. During the 1890s a Liberal government adopted many social programmes to help the poor who had suffered from a long economic depression in the 1880s. One of the most far reaching was the passing of tax legislation that made it difficult for wealthy sheep farmers to hold onto their large land holdings. This and the invention of refrigeration led to a farming revolution where many sheep farms were broken up and sold to become smaller dairy farms. This enabled thousands of new farmers to buy land and develop a new and vigorous industry that has become the backbone of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand%27s_economy" title="New Zealand's economy">New Zealand's economy</a> to this day. This liberal tradition flourished with increased enfranchisement for indigenous <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Maori</a> in the 1880s and women. Pensions for the elderly, the poor and war casualties followed, with State-run schools, hospitals and subsidized medical and dental care. By 1960, New Zealand was able to afford one of the best-developed and most comprehensive welfare systems in the world, supported by a well-developed and stable economy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_System_(Philippines)" title="Social Security System (Philippines)">Social Security System (Philippines)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_Service_Insurance_System" title="Government Service Insurance System">Government Service Insurance System</a></div>
<p>In Philippines, social welfare is divided into two. The first one is called Social Security System (SSS) and the other one is called Government Service Insurance System (GSIS). Social Security System or SSS is a social insurance program for workers in private sector. Individuals who are self-employed or not working can also apply to be protected under SSS programme. SSS provide benefits for unemployment, death, funeral, maternity leave, disabilities and many more. Members of SSS who experienced accident or death due to work activity, can also claim for double compensation under the Employees'  Compensation (EC) programme. SSS also allows its members to apply for salary loans, based on their monthly salary and calamity loan, if their place is declared as "state of calamity" by the government. SSS also have two voluntary saving programs, PESO Fund and Flexi Fund to help prepare their members with a stable income for retirement. Government servants are protected under the Government Service Insurance System or GSIS. Just like SSS, GSIS also provide their members with retirement benefits, life insurance and employee compensation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Citation_style plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-citation_style" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>has an unclear citation style</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> The references used may be made clearer with a different or consistent style of <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">citation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Help:Footnotes" title="Help:Footnotes">footnoting</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2021</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Welfare_in_Sweden" title="Welfare in Sweden">Welfare in Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_security_in_Sweden" title="Social security in Sweden">Social security in Sweden</a>, and <a class="extiw" href="https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%B6rs%C3%B6rjningsst%C3%B6d" title="sv:Försörjningsstöd">sv:Försörjningsstöd</a></div>
<p>Social welfare in Sweden is made up of several organizations and systems dealing with welfare. It is mostly funded by taxes, and executed by the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a> on all levels of government as well as private organizations. It can be separated into three parts falling under three different ministries; social welfare, falling under the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health_and_Social_Affairs_(Sweden)" title="Ministry of Health and Social Affairs (Sweden)">Ministry of Health and Social Affairs</a>; education, under the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education_and_Research_(Sweden)" title="Ministry of Education and Research (Sweden)">Ministry of Education and Research</a> and labor market, under the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Employment_(Sweden)" title="Ministry of Employment (Sweden)">Ministry of Employment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-departementen_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-departementen-61">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Government pension payments are financed through an 18.5% pension tax on all taxed incomes in the country, which comes partly from a <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">tax</a> category called a public pension fee (7% on <a href="/wiki/Gross_income" title="Gross income">gross income</a>), and 30% of a tax category called employer fees on salaries (which is 33% on a netted income). Since January 2001, the 18.5% is divided in two parts: 16% goes to current payments, and 2.5% goes into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Individual_retirement_accounts" title="Individual retirement accounts">individual retirement accounts</a>, which were introduced in 2001. Money saved and invested in government funds, and <a href="/wiki/Individual_retirement_account" title="Individual retirement account">IRAs</a> for future pension costs, are roughly five times annual government pension expenses (725/150).
</p><p>Viewing Swedish welfare more broadly, it emerges as highly rated in many standard international comparisons of welfare or well-being (e.g. World Economic Forum 2020).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[60]</a></sup> However, some Nordic-based welfare and gender researchers have argued that such assessments, based on conventional welfare/well-being criteria, may to some extent over-privilege Sweden (and other Nordic countries) in terms of, for instance, gender and racial equality. For example, they suggest that if one takes a broader perspective on well-being incorporating issues associated with bodily integrity or bodily citizenship (Pringle 2011),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[61]</a></sup> then some major forms of men's domination and/or white privilege can be seen to still stubbornly persist in the Nordic countries, e.g. business, violence to women, sexual violence to children, the military, academia and religion (Hearn and Pringle 2006; Hearn et al. 2018; Pringle 2016).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Welfare state in the United Kingdom">Welfare state in the United Kingdom</a></div>

<dd></dd></dl>

<p>The United Kingdom has a long history of welfare, notably including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Poor_laws" title="English Poor laws">English Poor laws</a> which date back to 1536. After various reforms to the program, which involved <a href="/wiki/Workhouse" title="Workhouse">workhouses</a>, it was eventually abolished and replaced with a modern system by laws such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Assistance_Act_1948" title="National Assistance Act 1948">National Assistance Act 1948</a>.
</p><p>In more recent times, comparing the <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron–Clegg coalition">Cameron–Clegg coalition</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_government_austerity_programme" title="United Kingdom government austerity programme">austerity measures</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Loyal_Opposition_(United_Kingdom)" title="Her Majesty's Most Loyal Opposition (United Kingdom)">the Opposition</a>'s, the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> commentator <a href="/wiki/Martin_Wolf" title="Martin Wolf">Martin Wolf</a> commented that the "big shift from Labour<span class="nowrap"> </span>... is the cuts in welfare benefits."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_28102010_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT_28102010-68">[66]</a></sup> The government's austerity programme, which involves reduction in government policy, has been linked to a rise in food banks. A study published in the <i>British Medical Journal</i> in 2015 found that each one percentage point increase in the rate of <a href="/wiki/Jobseeker%27s_Allowance" title="Jobseeker's Allowance">Jobseeker's Allowance</a> claimants sanctioned was associated with a 0.09 percentage point rise in food bank use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[67]</a></sup> The austerity programme has faced opposition from disability rights groups for disproportionately affecting disabled people. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Under-occupancy_penalty" title="Under-occupancy penalty">"bedroom tax"</a> is an austerity measure that has attracted particular criticism, with activists arguing that two-thirds of council houses affected by the policy are occupied with a person with a disability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_programs_in_the_United_States" title="Social programs in the United States">Social programs in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4130" decoding="async" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg/250px-Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg/375px-Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg/500px-Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Signing_Of_The_Social_Security_Act.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. <div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Welfare_in_America.png" title="Welfare in America"><img alt="Welfare in America" data-file-height="2325" data-file-width="3698" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Welfare_in_America.png/240px-Welfare_in_America.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Welfare_in_America.png/360px-Welfare_in_America.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Welfare_in_America.png/480px-Welfare_in_America.png 2x" width="240"/></a></div>
<p>In the United States, depending on the context, the term "welfare" can be used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Means_test" title="Means test">means-tested</a> cash benefits, especially the <a href="/wiki/Aid_to_Families_with_Dependent_Children" title="Aid to Families with Dependent Children">Aid to Families with Dependent Children</a> (AFDC) program and its successor, the <a href="/wiki/Temporary_Assistance_for_Needy_Families" title="Temporary Assistance for Needy Families">Temporary Assistance for Needy Families</a> <a href="/wiki/Block_grant" title="Block grant">block grants</a>, or it can be used to refer to all means-tested programs that help individuals or families meet basic needs, including, for example, health care through <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Security_Income" title="Supplemental Security Income">Supplemental Security Income</a> (SSI) benefits and <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">food and nutrition programs (SNAP)</a>. It can also include <a href="/wiki/Social_insurance" title="Social insurance">social insurance</a> programs such as <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_benefits" title="Unemployment benefits">unemployment insurance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a>.
</p><p>AFDC (originally called Aid to Dependent Children) was created during the Great Depression to alleviate the burden of poverty for families with children and allow widowed mothers to maintain their households. The New Deal employment program such as the <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a> primarily served men. Prior to the New Deal, <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">anti-poverty programs</a> were primarily operated by private charities or state or local governments; however, these programs were overwhelmed by the depth of need during the Depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[69]</a></sup> The United States has no national program of cash assistance for non-disabled poor individuals who are not raising children.
</p><p>Until early in the year 1965, the news media conveyed the idea that only whites lived in poverty, but then Blacks were presented more often than Whites in this plight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-72">[70]</a></sup> Some of the influences on this shift may have been the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights movement</a> and urban riots from the mid 60s. Welfare had by then shifted from being a White issue to being a Black issue and during this time frame the war on poverty had already begun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-72">[70]</a></sup> Subsequently, news media portrayed stereotypes of Blacks as lazy and undeserving and as <a href="/wiki/Welfare_queen" title="Welfare queen">welfare queens</a>. These shifts in media presentation didn't necessarily reflect a decrease in the percentage of the population living in poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-72">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="809" decoding="async" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif/250px-Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif/375px-Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif/500px-Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Welfare_Benefits_Payments_Graph.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A chart showing the overall decline of average monthly welfare benefits (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFDC" title="AFDC">AFDC</a> then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TANF" title="TANF">TANF</a>) per recipient 1962–2006 (in 2006 dollars).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-73">[71]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In 1996, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_Responsibility_and_Work_Opportunity_Reconciliation_Act" title="Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act">Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act</a> changed the structure of Welfare payments and added new criteria to states that received Welfare funding. After reforms that President Clinton said would "end Welfare as we know it",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_tanf_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_tanf-74">[72]</a></sup> amounts from the federal government were given out in a <a href="/wiki/Flat_rate" title="Flat rate">flat rate</a> per state based on <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_review_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-national_review-75">[73]</a></sup> Each state must meet certain criteria to ensure recipients are being encouraged to work themselves out of Welfare. The new program is called <a href="/wiki/Temporary_Assistance_for_Needy_Families" title="Temporary Assistance for Needy Families">Temporary Assistance for Needy Families</a> (TANF).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritage_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-heritage-76">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TANF_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-TANF-77">[75]</a></sup> It encourages states to require some sort of employment search in exchange for providing funds to individuals, and imposes a five-year lifetime limit on cash assistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_tanf_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_tanf-74">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritage_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-heritage-76">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_clinton_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_clinton-78">[76]</a></sup> In FY 2010, 31.8% of TANF families were white, 31.9% were African-American, and 30.0% were Hispanic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TANF_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-TANF-77">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a> data released 13 September 2011, the nation's <a href="/wiki/Poverty_in_the_United_States" title="Poverty in the United States">poverty rate</a> rose to 15.1% (46.2 million) in 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[77]</a></sup> up from 14.3% (approximately 43.6 million) in 2009 and to its highest level since 1993. In 2008, 13.2% (39.8 million) Americans lived in relative poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009_Poverty_Rate_Reuters_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-2009_Poverty_Rate_Reuters-80">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2011 op-ed in <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i>, Peter Ferrara stated that, "The best estimate of the cost of the 185 federal means tested Welfare programs for 2010 for the federal government alone is nearly $700<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion, up a third since 2008, according to the Heritage Foundation. Counting state spending, total Welfare spending for 2010 reached nearly $900<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion, up nearly one-fourth since 2008 (24.3%)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, with 12% of the U.S. population, has one-third of the nation's welfare recipients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>In FY 2011, federal spending on means-tested welfare, plus state contributions to federal programs, reached $927<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion per year. Roughly half went to families with children, most of which are headed by a single parent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMG_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMG-83">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States has also typically relied on charitable giving through non-profit agencies and fundraising instead of direct monetary assistance from the government itself. According to Giving USA, Americans gave $358.38<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion to charity in 2014. This is rewarded by the United States government through tax credits for individuals and companies that are not typically seen in other countries. Since these per capita charity sums are much higher than in all other countries, this means that private people and companies have a strong say in how social assistance is carried out. This means that public funds in the form of unpaid taxes are distributed according to less professional, less objective, and less unbiased criteria than in all other countries.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Welfare%27s_effect_on_poverty" title="Welfare's effect on poverty">Welfare's effect on poverty</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_welfare" title="Criticisms of welfare">Criticisms of welfare</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state#Effects" title="Welfare state">Welfare state § Effects</a></div>
<p>The welfare-to-work intervention programme is unlikely to have any impacts on the mental and physical health of lone parents and children. Even when the employment and income rates were higher in this group of people, the poverty rate was high which could lead to persistently high rates of depression whether they were in the programme or not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>Income transfers can be either <a href="/wiki/Conditional_cash_transfer" title="Conditional cash transfer">conditional</a> or <a href="/wiki/Unconditional_cash_transfer" title="Unconditional cash transfer">unconditional</a>. 
Conditionalities are sometimes criticized as being <a href="/wiki/Paternalism" title="Paternalism">paternalistic</a> and unnecessary, and research in different contexts shows enforcing conditionalities can result in additional burdens for recipients and social service providers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2008 study by welfare economist and Brown University Professor Allan M. Feldman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[84]</a></sup> suggests that welfare can achieve both <a href="/wiki/Competitive_equilibrium" title="Competitive equilibrium">competitive equilibrium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pareto_efficiency" title="Pareto efficiency">Pareto efficiency</a> in the market,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[85]</a></sup> although different points of Pareto efficiency are more fair to some than others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some opponents of welfare argue that it affects work incentives.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Perception">Perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a 2012 review study, whether a welfare program generates public support depends on:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>whether the program is universal or targeted towards certain groups</li>
<li>the size of the social program benefits (larger benefits incentivize greater mobilization to defend a social program)</li>
<li>the visibility and traceability of the benefits (whether recipients know where the benefits come from)</li>
<li>the proximity and concentration of the beneficiaries (this affects the ease by which beneficiaries can organize to protect a social program)</li>
<li>the duration of the benefits (longer benefits incentivize greater mobilization to defend a social program)</li>
<li>the manner in which a program is administered (whether the program  is inclusive and follow principles)</li></ul>
