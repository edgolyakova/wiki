<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Garden</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Planned space for displaying plants and other forms of nature</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Garden_(disambiguation)" title="Garden (disambiguation)">Garden (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" decoding="async" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg/330px-Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg/495px-Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg/660px-Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden_New_York_May_2015_010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A section of the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Botanic_Garden" title="Brooklyn Botanic Garden">Brooklyn Botanic Garden</a> that has pink <a href="/wiki/Prunus_%27Kanzan%27" title="Prunus 'Kanzan'"><i>Prunus</i> 'Kanzan'</a> cherry trees</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>garden</b> is a planned space, usually outdoors, set aside for the cultivation, display, and enjoyment of plants and other forms of nature. The single feature identifying even the wildest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_garden" title="Wild garden">wild garden</a> is <i>control</i>. The garden can incorporate both natural and artificial materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gardens often have design features including statuary, <a href="/wiki/Folly" title="Folly">follies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pergola" title="Pergola">pergolas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trellis_(architecture)" title="Trellis (architecture)">trellises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stumpery" title="Stumpery">stumperies</a>, dry creek beds, and <a href="/wiki/Water_feature" title="Water feature">water features</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fountain" title="Fountain">fountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garden_pond" title="Garden pond">ponds</a> (with or without <a href="/wiki/Koi_pond" title="Koi pond">fish</a>), <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfalls</a> or creeks. Some gardens are for ornamental purposes only, while others also produce food crops, sometimes in separate areas, or sometimes intermixed with the <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plants</a>. Food-producing gardens are distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farms</a> by their smaller scale, more labor-intensive methods, and their purpose (enjoyment of a hobby or self-sustenance rather than producing for sale, as in a  <a href="/wiki/Market_garden" title="Market garden">market garden</a>). <a href="/wiki/Flower_garden" title="Flower garden">Flower gardens</a> combine plants of different heights, colors, textures, and fragrances to create interest and delight the senses.
</p><p>The most common form today is a residential or public garden, but the term <i>garden</i> has traditionally been a more general one. <a href="/wiki/Zoo" title="Zoo">Zoos</a>, which display <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wild animals</a> in simulated natural habitats, were formerly called zoological gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klindienst_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klindienst-3">[3]</a></sup> Western gardens are almost universally based on plants, with <i>garden</i>, which etymologically implies <i>enclosure</i>, often signifying a shortened form of <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>. Some traditional types of eastern gardens, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_rock_garden" title="Japanese rock garden">Zen gardens</a>, however, use plants sparsely or not at all. Landscape gardens, on the other hand, such as the <a href="/wiki/English_landscape_garden" title="English landscape garden">English landscape gardens</a> first developed in the 18th century, may omit flowers altogether.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Landscape_architecture" title="Landscape architecture">Landscape architecture</a> is a related professional activity with <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architect" title="Landscape architect">landscape architects</a> tending to engage in design at many scales and working on both public and private projects.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The etymology of the word <i>gardening</i> refers to <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">enclosure</a>: it is from Middle English <i>gardin</i>, from Anglo-French <i>gardin</i>, <i>jardin</i>, of Germanic origin; akin to Old High German <i>gard</i>, <i>gart</i>, an enclosure or compound, as in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grad_(Slavic_settlement)" title="Grad (Slavic settlement)">Grad (Slavic settlement)</a> for more complete etymology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The words <i>yard</i>, <i>court</i>, and Latin <i>hortus</i> (meaning "garden", hence horticulture and orchard), are cognates—all referring to an enclosed space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "garden" in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> refers to a small enclosed area of land, usually adjoining a building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This would be referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Yard_(land)" title="Yard (land)">yard</a> in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A garden can have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesthetic" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a>, functional, and recreational uses:
</p>
<ul><li>Cooperation with nature
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_cultivation" title="Plant cultivation">Plant cultivation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Garden-based_learning" title="Garden-based learning">Garden-based learning</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Observation of nature
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">Bird</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a>-watching</li>
<li>Reflection on the changing <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Relaxation
<ul><li>Family dinners on the terrace</li>
<li>Children playing in the garden</li>
<li>Reading and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relaxing" title="Relaxing">relaxing</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Hammock" title="Hammock">hammock</a></li>
<li>Maintaining the flowerbeds</li>
<li>Pottering in the <a href="/wiki/Shed" title="Shed">shed</a></li>
<li>Cottaging in the bushes</li>
<li>Basking in warm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunshine" title="Sunshine">sunshine</a></li>
<li>Escaping oppressive sunlight and heat</li></ul></li>
<li>Growing useful produce
<ul><li>Flowers to cut and bring inside for indoor beauty</li>
<li>Fresh herbs and vegetables for cooking</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_gardening" title="History of gardening">History of gardening</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Garden_in_NPM.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="872" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Garden_in_NPM.JPG/220px-Garden_in_NPM.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Garden_in_NPM.JPG/330px-Garden_in_NPM.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Garden_in_NPM.JPG/440px-Garden_in_NPM.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Garden_in_NPM.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Naturalistic design of a Chinese garden incorporated into the landscape, including a pavilion</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_garden" title="Chinese garden">Chinese garden</a></div>
<p>The earliest recorded Chinese gardens were created in the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">Shang Dynasty</a> (1600–1046 BC). These gardens were large enclosed parks where the kings and nobles hunted game, or where fruit and vegetables were grown. Early inscriptions from this period, carved on tortoise shells, have three Chinese characters for garden, <i>you</i>, <i>pu</i> and <i>yuan</i>. <i>You</i> was a royal garden where birds and animals were kept, while <i>pu</i> was a garden for plants. During the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> (221–206 BC), <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9C%92" title="wiktionary:園">yuan</a></i> became the character for all gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The old character for <i>yuan</i> is a small picture of a garden; it is enclosed in a square which can represent a wall, and has symbols which can represent the plan of a structure, a small square which can represent a pond, and a symbol for a plantation or a pomegranate tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>A famous royal garden of the late Shang dynasty was the <i>Terrace, Pond and Park</i> of the Spirit (<i>Lingtai, Lingzhao Lingyou</i>) built by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Wenwang" title="King Wenwang">King Wenwang</a> west of his capital city, <a href="/wiki/Yinxu" title="Yinxu">Yin</a>. The park was described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Poetry" title="Classic of Poetry">Classic of Poetry</a></i> this way:
</p>
<dl><dd>The King makes his promenade in the Park of the Spirit,</dd>
<dd>The deer are kneeling on the grass, feeding their fawns,</dd>
<dd>The deer are beautiful and resplendent.</dd>
<dd>The immaculate cranes have plumes of a brilliant white.</dd>
<dd>The King makes his promenade to the Pond of the Spirit,</dd>
<dd>The water is full of fish, who wriggle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Another early royal garden was <i>Shaqui</i>, or the <i>Dunes of Sand</i>, built by the last Shang ruler, <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou</a> (1075–1046 BC). It was composed of an earth terrace, or <i>tai</i>, which served as an observation platform in the center of a large square park. It was described in one of the early classics of Chinese literature, the <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i> (<i>Shiji</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> According to the <i>Shiji</i>, one of the most famous features of this garden was the <i>Wine Pool and Meat Forest</i> (酒池肉林). A large pool, big enough for several small boats, was constructed on the palace grounds, with inner linings of polished oval shaped stones from the seashore. The pool was then filled with wine. A small island was constructed in the middle of the pool, where trees were planted, which had skewers of roasted meat hanging from their branches. King Zhou and his friends and concubines drifted in their boats, drinking the wine with their hands and eating the roasted meat from the trees. Later Chinese philosophers and historians cited this garden as an example of decadence and bad taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (722–481 BC), in 535 BC, the <i>Terrace of Shanghua</i>, with lavishly decorated palaces, was built by <a href="/wiki/King_Jing_of_Zhou_(Gui)" title="King Jing of Zhou (Gui)">King Jing</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>. In 505 BC, an even more elaborate garden, the <i>Terrace of Gusu</i>, was begun. It was located on the side of a mountain, and included a series of terraces connected by galleries, along with a lake where boats in the form of blue dragons navigated. From the highest terrace, a view extended as far as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tai" title="Lake Tai">Lake Tai</a>, the Great Lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Manasollasa" title="Manasollasa">Manasollasa</a></i> is a twelfth century <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> text that offers details on garden design and a variety of other subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sadhalenenebim_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sadhalenenebim-14">[14]</a></sup> Both public parks and woodland gardens are described, with about 40 types of trees recommended for the park in the <i>Vana-krida</i> chapter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sadhalenenebim_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-sadhalenenebim-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShrigondekar1961_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShrigondekar1961-15">[15]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Shilparatna" title="Shilparatna">Shilparatna</a></i>, a text from the sixteenth century, states that flower gardens or public parks should be located in the northern portion of a town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg/220px-Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg/330px-Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg/440px-Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saihouji-kokedera01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moss_garden" title="Moss garden">moss garden</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saih%C5%8D-ji" title="Saihō-ji">Saihō-ji</a> temple in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, started in 1339.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_garden" title="Japanese garden">Japanese garden</a></div>
<p>The earliest recorded Japanese gardens were the <a href="/wiki/Pleasure_garden" title="Pleasure garden">pleasure gardens</a> of the Emperors and nobles. They are mentioned in several brief passages of the <span title="Japanese-language romanization"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i></span>, the first chronicle of Japanese history, published in 720 CE. In spring 74 CE, the chronicle recorded: "The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Keik%C5%8D" title="Emperor Keikō">Emperor Keikō</a> put a few carp into a pond, and rejoiced to see them morning and evening". The following year, "The Emperor launched a double-hulled boat in the pond of Ijishi at Ihare, and went aboard with his imperial concubine, and they feasted sumptuously together". In 486, the chronicle recorded that "The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Kenz%C5%8D" title="Emperor Kenzō">Emperor Kenzō</a> went into the garden and feasted at the edge of a winding stream".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Korea">Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Korean_garden" title="Korean garden">Korean garden</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> gardens are a type of garden described as being natural, informal, simple and unforced, seeking to merge with the natural world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> They have a history that goes back more than two thousand years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> but are little known in the west. The oldest records date to the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms</a> period (57 BC – 668 AD) when architecture and palace gardens showed a development noted in the Korean <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">History of the Three Kingdoms</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_(mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli,_2007)_01.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_%28mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli%2C_2007%29_01.JPG/220px-Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_%28mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli%2C_2007%29_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_%28mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli%2C_2007%29_01.JPG/330px-Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_%28mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli%2C_2007%29_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_%28mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli%2C_2007%29_01.JPG/440px-Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_%28mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli%2C_2007%29_01.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ricostruzione_del_giardino_della_casa_dei_vetii_di_pompei_(mostra_al_giardino_di_boboli,_2007)_01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of the garden at the <a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Vettii" title="House of the Vettii">House of the Vettii</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">Gardening</a> was not recognized as an art form in Europe until the mid 16th century when it entered the political discourse, as a symbol of the concept of the "ideal republic". Evoking utopian imagery of the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a>, a time of abundance and plenty where humans didn't know hunger or the conflicts that arose from property disputes. <a href="/wiki/John_Evelyn" title="John Evelyn">John Evelyn</a> wrote in the early 17th century, "there is not a more laborious life then is that of a good Gard'ners; but a labour full of tranquility and satisfaction; Natural and Instructive, and such as (if any) contributes to Piety and Contemplation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> During the era of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enclosures" title="Enclosures">Enclosures</a>, the agrarian collectivism of the <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal age</a> was idealized in literary "fantasies of liberating regression to garden and wilderness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following his campaign in Italy in 1495, where he saw the gardens and castles of Naples, King <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII</a>  brought Italian craftsmen and <a href="/wiki/Garden_designer" title="Garden designer">garden designers</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pacello_da_Mercogliano" title="Pacello da Mercogliano">Pacello da Mercogliano</a>, from Naples and ordered the construction of Italian-style gardens at his residence at the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Amboise" title="Château d'Amboise">Château d'Amboise</a> and at Château Gaillard, another private résidence in Amboise. His successor <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">Henry II</a>, who had also travelled to Italy and had met <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, created an Italian nearby at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Blois" title="Château de Blois">Château de Blois</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Beginning in 1528, King <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a> created new gardens at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Fontainebleau" title="Château de Fontainebleau">Château de Fontainebleau</a>, which featured fountains, parterres, a forest of pine trees brought from <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a>, and the first artificial grotto in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Chenonceau" title="Château de Chenonceau">Château de Chenonceau</a> had two gardens in the new style, one created for <a href="/wiki/Diane_de_Poitiers" title="Diane de Poitiers">Diane de Poitiers</a> in 1551, and a second for <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de' Medici">Catherine de' Medici</a> in 1560.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1536, the architect <a href="/wiki/Philibert_de_l%27Orme" title="Philibert de l'Orme">Philibert de l'Orme</a>, upon his return from Rome, created the gardens of the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Anet" title="Château d'Anet">Château d'Anet</a> following the Italian rules of proportion. The carefully prepared harmony of Anet, with its parterres and surfaces of water integrated with sections of greenery, became one of the earliest and most influential examples of the classic French garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeannel_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-jeannel-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/French_formal_garden" title="French formal garden">French formal garden</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">jardin à la française</i>) contrasted with the design principles of the English landscape garden (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">jardin à la anglaise</i>); namely, to "force nature" instead of leaving it undisturbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> By the mid-17th century <a href="/wiki/Axial_symmetry" title="Axial symmetry">axial symmetry</a> had ascended to prominence in the French gardening traditions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andre_Mollet" title="Andre Mollet">Andre Mollet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Boyceau" title="Jacques Boyceau">Jacques Boyceau</a>, the latter who wrote: "All things, however beautiful they may be chosen, will be defective if they are not ordered and placed in proper symmetry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayes_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-hayes-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/French_landscape_garden" title="French landscape garden">French landscape garden</a> was influenced by the English landscape garden and gained prominence in the late eighteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Manner" title="Grand Manner">Grand Manner</a> era, what few significant gardens could be found in Britain had developed under influence from the continent. Britain's homegrown domestic gardening traditions were mostly practical in purpose, rather than aesthetic, unlike the grand gardens found mostly on castle grounds, and less commonly at universities. Tudor gardens emphasized contrast rather than transitions, distinguished by color and illusion. They were not intended as a complement to home or architecture, but conceived as independent spaces, arranged to grow and display flowers and ornamental plants. Gardeners demonstrated their artistry in <a href="/wiki/Knot_garden" title="Knot garden">knot gardens</a>, with complex arrangements most commonly included interwoven <a href="/wiki/Buxus" title="Buxus">box hedges</a>, and less commonly fragrant herbs like <a href="/wiki/Rosemary" title="Rosemary">rosemary</a>. Sanded paths run between the hedgings of open knots whereas closed knots were filled with single colored flowers. The knot and <a href="/wiki/Parterre" title="Parterre">parterre</a> gardens were always placed on level ground, and elevated areas reserved for terraces from which the intricacy of the gardens could be viewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayes_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-hayes-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacobean gardens were described as "a delightful confusion" by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wotton" title="Henry Wotton">Henry Wotton</a> in 1624. Under the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>, Caroline gardens began to shed some of the chaos of earlier designs, marking the beginning of a trends towards symmetrical unified designs that took the building architecture into account, and featuring an elevated terrace from which home and garden could be viewed. The only surviving Caroline garden is located at <a href="/wiki/Bolsover_Castle" title="Bolsover Castle">Bolsover Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, but is too simple to attract much interest. During the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England,_Scotland_and_Ireland" title="Charles II of England, Scotland and Ireland">Charles II</a>, many new <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> style country houses were built; while in England <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cromwell" title="Thomas Cromwell">Thomas Cromwell</a> sought to destroy many Tudor, Jacobean and Caroline style gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayes_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-hayes-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Garden_design" title="Garden design">Garden design</a></div>
<p>Garden design is the process of creating plans for the layout and planting of gardens and landscapes. Gardens may be designed by garden owners themselves, or by professionals. Professional garden designers tend to be trained in principles of design and horticulture, and have a knowledge and experience of using plants. Some professional garden designers are also <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architect" title="Landscape architect">landscape architects</a>, a more formal level of training that usually requires an advanced degree and often an occupational <a href="/wiki/Licensure" title="Licensure">license</a>.
</p><p>Elements of garden design include the layout of hard landscape, such as paths, rockeries, walls, water features, sitting areas and decking, as well as the plants themselves, with consideration for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horticultural" title="Horticultural">horticultural</a> requirements, their season-to-season appearance, lifespan, <a href="/wiki/Habit_(biology)" title="Habit (biology)">growth habit</a>, size, speed of growth, and combinations with other plants and landscape features. Most gardens consist of a mix of natural and constructed elements, although even very 'natural' gardens are always an inherently artificial creation. Natural elements present in a garden principally comprise flora (such as trees and <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a>), fauna (such as <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and birds), soil, water, air and light. Constructed elements include paths, <a href="/wiki/Patio" title="Patio">patios</a>, decking, sculptures, <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a> systems, lights and buildings (such as <a href="/wiki/Shed" title="Shed">sheds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gazebo" title="Gazebo">gazebos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pergola" title="Pergola">pergolas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folly" title="Folly">follies</a>), but also living constructions such as <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_border" title="Herbaceous border">flower beds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garden_pond" title="Garden pond">ponds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">lawns</a>.
</p><p>Consideration is also given to the maintenance needs of the garden.  Including the time or funds available for regular maintenance,  (this can affect the choices of plants regarding speed of growth) spreading or self-seeding of the plants (annual or perennial), bloom-time, and many other characteristics. Garden design can be roughly divided into two groups, formal and naturalistic gardens. The most important consideration in any garden design is how the garden will be used, followed closely by the desired stylistic genres, and the way the garden space will connect to the home or other structures in the surrounding areas. All of these considerations are subject to the limitations of the budget. Budget limitations can be addressed by a simpler garden style with fewer plants and less costly hard landscape materials, seeds rather than sod for lawns, and plants that grow quickly; alternatively, garden owners may choose to create their garden over time, area by area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChen20103_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChen20103-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_gardening" title="Sustainable gardening">Sustainable gardening</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_landscaping" title="Sustainable landscaping">Sustainable landscaping</a></div>
<p>Gardeners may cause environmental damage by the way they garden, or they may enhance their local environment.
Damage by gardeners can include direct <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">destruction of natural habitats</a> when houses and gardens are created; indirect <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and damage to provide garden materials such as <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> rock for rock gardens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and by the use of tapwater to <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigate</a> gardens; the death of living beings in the garden itself, such as the killing not only of <a href="/wiki/Slug" title="Slug">slugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> but also their predators such as <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrushes</a> by <a href="/wiki/Metaldehyde" title="Metaldehyde">metaldehyde</a> slug killer; the death of living beings outside the garden, such as local species extinction by indiscriminate <a href="/wiki/Plant_collecting" title="Plant collecting">plant collectors</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> produced by gardening.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gardeners can help to prevent climate change in many ways, including the use of trees, shrubs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_cover" title="Ground cover">ground cover plants</a> and other perennial plants in their gardens, turning garden waste into <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a> instead of burning it, keeping soil and compost heaps aerated, avoiding peat, switching from power tools to hand tools or changing their garden design so that power tools are not needed, and using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen-fixing#Root_nodule_symbioses" title="Nitrogen-fixing">nitrogen-fixing</a> plants instead of nitrogen fertiliser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_S.Ingram,_Daphne_Vince-Prue_and_Peter_J._Gregory_(2008)_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_S.Ingram,_Daphne_Vince-Prue_and_Peter_J._Gregory_(2008)-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change will have many impacts on gardens; some studies suggest most of them will be negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bisgrove_and_Hadley_(2002)_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bisgrove_and_Hadley_(2002)-33">[33]</a></sup> Gardens also contribute to climate change. Greenhouse gases can be produced by gardeners in many ways. The three main <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>. Gardeners produce carbon dioxide directly by <a href="/wiki/Tillage#Negative" title="Tillage">overcultivating</a> soil and destroying <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a>, by burning garden waste on <a href="/wiki/Bonfire#Farm_and_garden_bonfires" title="Bonfire">bonfires</a>, by using power tools which burn <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> or use electricity generated by fossil fuels, and by using <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>. Gardeners produce methane by compacting the soil and making it anaerobic, and by allowing their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compost_heap" title="Compost heap">compost heaps</a> to become compacted and anaerobic. Gardeners produce nitrous oxide by applying excess <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer#Inorganic_fertilizers_(mineral_fertilizer)" title="Fertilizer">nitrogen fertiliser</a> when plants are not actively growing so that the nitrogen in the fertiliser is converted by <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide#Soil" title="Nitrous oxide">soil bacteria</a> to nitrous oxide.
</p>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_sprinkler" title="Irrigation sprinkler">Irrigation sprinkler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">drip irrigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hand_pump" title="Hand pump">hand pump</a></div>
<p>Some gardeners manage their gardens without using any water from outside the garden. Examples in Britain include <a href="/wiki/Ventnor_Botanic_Garden" title="Ventnor Botanic Garden">Ventnor Botanic Garden</a> on the Isle of Wight, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Beth_Chatto" title="Beth Chatto">Beth Chatto</a>'s garden in Essex, Sticky Wicket garden in Dorset, and the Royal Horticultural Society's gardens at <a href="/wiki/RHS_Garden_Harlow_Carr" title="RHS Garden Harlow Carr">Harlow Carr</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RHS_Hyde_Hall" title="RHS Hyde Hall">Hyde Hall</a>.
<a href="/wiki/Rain_garden" title="Rain garden">Rain gardens</a> absorb rainfall falling onto nearby hard surfaces, rather than sending it into stormwater drains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
