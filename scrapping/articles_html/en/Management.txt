<p><b>Management</b> (or <b>managing</b>) is the administration of an <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a>, whether it is a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Not-for-profit" title="Not-for-profit">non-profit</a> organization, or a <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government body</a>. It is the art and science of managing resources of the business. 
</p><p>Management includes the activities of setting the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_management" title="Strategic management">strategy</a> of an organization and coordinating the efforts of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Employee" title="Employee">employees</a> (or of volunteers) to accomplish its <a href="/wiki/Goal" title="Goal">objectives</a> through the application of available <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_(economics)" title="Resource (economics)">resources</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial" title="Financial">financial</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technological" title="Technological">technological</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a>. "Run the business"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and "Change the business" are two concepts that are used in management to differentiate between the continued delivery of goods or services and adapting of goods or services to meet the changing needs of customers - see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Trend_(disambiguation)" title="Trend (disambiguation)">trend</a>. The term "management" may also refer to those people who manage an organization—<b>managers</b>. 
</p><p>Some people study management at colleges or universities; major degrees in management includes the <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Commerce" title="Bachelor of Commerce">Bachelor of Commerce</a> (B.Com.), <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Business_Administration" title="Bachelor of Business Administration">Bachelor of Business Administration</a> (BBA.), <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> (MBA.), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_in_Management" title="Master in Management">Master in Management</a> (MSM or MIM) and, for the public sector, the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Public_Administration" title="Master of Public Administration">Master of Public Administration</a> (MPA) degree. Individuals who aim to become management specialists or experts, management researchers, or professors may complete the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Management" title="Doctor of Management">Doctor of Management</a> (DM), the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Business_Administration" title="Doctor of Business Administration">Doctor of Business Administration</a> (DBA), or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in Business Administration or Management. In the past few decades, there has been a movement for <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_management" title="Evidence-based management">evidence-based management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Larger organizations generally have three <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchical</a> levels of managers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in a pyramid structure:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Senior_management" title="Senior management">Senior managers</a> such as members of a <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> (CEO) or a <a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">president</a> of an organization sets the strategic goals of the organization and make decisions on how the overall organization will operate. Senior managers are generally executive-level professionals who provide direction to middle management, and directly or indirectly report to them.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Middle_management" title="Middle management">Middle managers</a> such as branch managers, regional managers, department managers, and section managers, who provide direction to the front-line managers. They communicate the strategic goals of senior management to the front-line managers.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Line_management" title="Line management">Line managers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Supervisor" title="Supervisor">supervisors</a> and front-line <a href="/wiki/Team_leader" title="Team leader">team leaders</a>, oversee the work of regular employees (or volunteers, in some voluntary organizations) and provide direction on their work. Line managers often perform the traditional functions of management. They are usually considered part of the workforce and not a proper part of the organization's management.</li></ul>
<p>In smaller organizations, a manager may have a much wider scope and may perform several roles or even all of the roles commonly observed in a large organization.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">Social scientists</a> study management as an <a href="/wiki/Academic_discipline" title="Academic discipline">academic discipline</a>, investigating areas such as <a href="/wiki/Social_organization" title="Social organization">social organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organizational_adaptation" title="Organizational adaptation">organizational adaptation</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organizational_leadership" title="Organizational leadership">organizational leadership</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English verb "manage" has its roots by the XV century <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> verb 'mesnager', which often referred in <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">equestrian</a> language "to hold in hand the reins of a horse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Also the <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> term <i>maneggiare</i> (to handle, especially tools or a horse) is possible. In <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <i>manejar</i> can also mean to rule the horses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> These three terms derive from the two <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words <i>manus</i> (hand) and <i>agere</i> (to act). 
</p><p>The French word for <a href="/wiki/Housekeeping" title="Housekeeping">housekeeping</a>, <i>ménagerie</i>, derived from <i>ménager</i> ("to keep house"; compare <i>ménage</i> for "household"), also encompasses taking care of domestic animals. <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/menagerie" title="wikt:menagerie"><i>Ménagerie</i></a> is the French translation of <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a>'s famous book <i><a href="/wiki/Oeconomicus" title="Oeconomicus">Oeconomicus</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Οἰκονομικός</span></span>) on household matters and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Husbandry" title="Husbandry">husbandry</a>. The French word <i>mesnagement</i> (or <i>ménagement</i>) influenced the semantic development of the English word <i>management</i> in the 17th and 18th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Views on the definition and scope of management include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Henri_Fayol" title="Henri Fayol">Henri Fayol</a> (1841–1925) stated: "to manage is to forecast and to plan, to organise, to command, to co-ordinate and to control."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulshan_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gulshan-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fredmund_Malik" title="Fredmund Malik">Fredmund Malik</a> (1944– ) defines management as "the transformation of resources into utility".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Management is included<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2020)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> as one of the <a href="/wiki/Factors_of_production" title="Factors of production">factors of production</a> – along with machines, materials and money.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ghislain_Deslandes" title="Ghislain Deslandes">Ghislain Deslandes</a> defines management as "a vulnerable force, under pressure to achieve results and endowed with the triple power of constraint, imitation and imagination, operating on subjective, interpersonal, institutional and environmental levels".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>

Nevertheless, innovation is also linked to marketing (product innovation is a central strategic marketing issue).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_scope">Theoretical scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Management involves identifying the <a href="/wiki/Mission_statement" title="Mission statement">mission</a>, objective, procedures, rules and manipulation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>of the <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">human capital</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">enterprise</a> to contribute to the success of the enterprise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Scholars have focused on the management of individual,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> organizational,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and inter-organizational relationships. This implies effective <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>: an enterprise environment (as opposed to a physical or mechanical mechanism) implies human <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a> and implies some sort of successful progress or <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> outcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As such, management is not the manipulation of a mechanism (machine or automated program), not the herding of animals, and can occur either in a legal or in an illegal enterprise or environment. From an individual's perspective, management does not need to be seen solely from an enterprise point of view, because management is an essential<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This term requires quantification. (January 2020)">quantify</span></a></i>]</sup> function in improving one's <a href="/wiki/Personal_life" title="Personal life">life</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_relationship" title="Social relationship">relationships</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Management is therefore everywhere<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and it has a wider range of application.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Communication and a positive endeavor are two main aspects of it either through enterprise or through independent pursuit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Plans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measurements" title="Measurements">measurements</a>, motivational psychological tools, goals, and economic measures (profit, etc.) may or may not be necessary components for there to be management. At first, one views management functionally, such as measuring quantity, adjusting <a href="/wiki/Plan" title="Plan">plans</a>, and meeting <a href="/wiki/Goal" title="Goal">goals</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but this applies even in situations where planning does not take place. From this perspective, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Fayol" title="Henri Fayol">Henri Fayol</a> (1841–1925)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2015)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup>considers management to consist of five <a href="/wiki/Function_(engineering)" title="Function (engineering)">functions</a>:
</p>
<ol><li>planning (forecasting)</li>
<li>organizing</li>
<li>commanding</li>
<li>coordinating</li>
<li>controlling</li></ol>
<p>In another way of thinking, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Parker_Follett" title="Mary Parker Follett">Mary Parker Follett</a> (1868–1933), allegedly defined management as "the art of getting things done through people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>She described management as a philosophy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2015)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup>, however, find this definition useful but far too narrow. The phrase "management is what managers do" occurs widely,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>suggesting the difficulty of defining management without <a href="/wiki/Circular_definition" title="Circular definition">circularity</a>, the shifting nature of definitions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the connection of <a href="/wiki/Managerialism" title="Managerialism">managerial practices</a> with the existence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Managerial_class" title="Managerial class">managerial cadre</a> or of a <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a>.
</p><p>One habit of thought regards management as equivalent to "<a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a>" and thus excludes management in places outside <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a>, as for example in <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">charities</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a>. More broadly, every organization must "manage" its work, people, processes, technology, etc. to maximize effectiveness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Nonetheless, many people refer to university departments that teach management as "<a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">business schools</a>". Some such institutions (such as the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>) use that name, while others (such as the <a href="/wiki/Yale_School_of_Management" title="Yale School of Management">Yale School of Management</a>) employ the broader term "management".
</p><p>English-speakers may also use the term "management" or "the management" as a collective word describing the managers of an organization, for example of a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
Historically this use of the term often contrasted with the term <a href="/wiki/Industrial_relations" title="Industrial relations">"labor"</a> – referring to those being managed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>But in the present era<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the concept of management is identified<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> in the wide areas<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> and its frontiers have been pushed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2020)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to a broader range.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Apart from profitable organizations, even <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">non-profit organizations</a> apply management concepts. The concept and its uses are not constrained<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>. Management as a whole is the process of planning, organizing, directing, <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leading</a> and controlling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Levels">Levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USCG_Org_Chart.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="506" data-file-width="429" decoding="async" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/USCG_Org_Chart.jpg/200px-USCG_Org_Chart.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/USCG_Org_Chart.jpg/300px-USCG_Org_Chart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/USCG_Org_Chart.jpg/400px-USCG_Org_Chart.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USCG_Org_Chart.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organization_chart" title="Organization chart">organization chart</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> shows the hierarchy of managerial roles in that organization.</div></div></div>
<p>Most organizations have three management levels: first-level, middle-level, and top-level managers. First-line managers are the lowest level of management and manage the work of non-managerial individuals who are directly involved with the production or creation of the organization's products. First-line managers are often called supervisors, but may also be called line managers, office managers, or even foremen. Middle managers include all levels of management between the first-line level and the top level of the organization. These managers manage the work of first-line managers and may have titles such as department head, project leader, plant manager, or division manager. Top managers are responsible for making organization-wide decisions and establishing the plans and goals that affect the entire organization. These individuals typically have titles such as executive vice president, president, managing director, chief operating officer, chief executive officer, or chairman of the board.
</p><p>These managers are classified in a hierarchy of authority, and perform different tasks. In many organizations, the number of managers in every level resembles a pyramid. Each level is explained below in specifications of their different responsibilities and likely job titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Top_management">Top management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Top management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The top or senior layer of management is a small group which consists of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Non-executive_director" title="Non-executive director">non-executive directors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Executive_director" title="Executive director">executive directors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independent_director" title="Independent director">independent directors</a>), president, vice-president, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CEO" title="CEO">CEOs</a> and other members of the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_title" title="Corporate title">C-level</a> executives. Different organizations have various members in their C-suite, which may include a <a href="/wiki/Chief_financial_officer" title="Chief financial officer">chief financial officer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chief_technology_officer" title="Chief technology officer">chief technology officer</a>, and so on. They are responsible for controlling and overseeing the operations of the entire organization. They set a "<a href="/wiki/Tone_at_the_top" title="Tone at the top">tone at the top</a>" and develop <a href="/wiki/Strategic_planning" title="Strategic planning">strategic plans</a>, company policies, and make decisions on the overall direction of the organization. In addition, top-level managers play a significant role in the mobilization of outside resources. Senior managers are accountable to the shareholders, the general public and to public bodies that oversee corporations and similar organizations. Some members of the senior management may serve as the public face of the organization, and they may make speeches to introduce new strategies or appear in marketing.
</p><p>The board of directors is typically primarily composed of non-executives who owe a <a href="/wiki/Fiduciary" title="Fiduciary">fiduciary</a> duty to shareholders and are not closely involved in the day-to-day activities of the organization, although this varies depending on the type (e.g., public versus private), size and culture of the organization. These directors are theoretically liable for breaches of that duty and typically insured under <a href="/wiki/Directors_and_officers_liability_insurance" title="Directors and officers liability insurance">directors and officers liability insurance</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> directors are estimated to spend 4.4 hours per week on board duties, and median compensation was $212,512 in 2010. The board sets corporate strategy, makes major decisions such as major acquisitions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and hires, evaluates, and fires the top-level manager (<a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> or CEO). The CEO typically hires other positions. However, board involvement in the hiring of other positions such as the <a href="/wiki/Chief_financial_officer" title="Chief financial officer">chief financial officer</a> (CFO) has increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2013, a survey of over 160 CEOs and directors of public and private companies found that the top weaknesses of CEOs were "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mentoring" title="Mentoring">mentoring</a> skills" and "board engagement", and 10% of companies never evaluated the CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The board may also have certain employees (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Internal_auditor" title="Internal auditor">internal auditors</a>) report to them or directly hire independent contractors; for example, the board (through the <a href="/wiki/Audit_committee" title="Audit committee">audit committee</a>) typically selects the <a href="/wiki/Auditor" title="Auditor">auditor</a>.
</p><p>Helpful skills of top management vary by the type of organization but typically include<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> a broad understanding of competition, world economies, and politics. In addition, the CEO is responsible for implementing and determining (within the board's framework) the broad policies of the organization. Executive management accomplishes the day-to-day details, including: instructions for preparation of department budgets, procedures, schedules; appointment of middle level executives such as department managers; coordination of departments; media and governmental relations; and <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholder</a> communication.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_management">Middle management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Middle management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Consist of <a href="/wiki/General_manager" title="General manager">general managers</a>, branch managers and department managers. They are accountable to the top management for their department's function. They devote more time to organizational and directional functions. Their roles can be emphasized as executing organizational plans in conformance with the company's policies and the objectives of the top management, they define and discuss information and policies from top management to lower management, and most importantly they inspire and provide guidance to lower-level managers towards better performance.
</p><p>Middle management is the midway management of a categorized organization, being secondary to the senior management but above the deepest levels of operational members. An operational manager may be well-thought-out by middle management or may be categorized as non-management operate, liable to the policy of the specific organization. The efficiency of the middle level is vital in any organization since they bridge the gap between top level and bottom level staffs.
</p><p>Their functions include:
</p>
<ul><li>Design and implement effective group and inter-group work and information systems.</li>
<li>Define and monitor group-level performance indicators.</li>
<li>Diagnose and resolve problems within and among workgroups.</li>
<li>Design and implement reward systems that support cooperative behavior. They also make decisions and share ideas with top managers.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Line_management">Line management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Line management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Line managers include <a href="/wiki/Supervisor" title="Supervisor">supervisors</a>, section leaders, forepersons and team leaders. They focus on controlling and directing regular employees. They are usually responsible for assigning employees' tasks, guiding and supervising employees on day-to-day activities, ensuring the quality and quantity of production and/or service, making recommendations and suggestions to employees on their work, and channeling employee concerns that they cannot resolve to mid-level managers or other administrators. First-level or "front line" managers also act as role models for their employees. In some types of work, front line managers may also do some of the same tasks that employees do, at least some of the time. For example, in some restaurants, the front line managers will also serve customers during a very busy period of the day. In general, line managers are considered part of the workforce and not part of the organization's proper management despite performing traditional management functions.
</p><p>Front-line managers typically provide:
</p>
<ul><li>Training for new employees</li>
<li>Basic supervision</li>
<li>Motivation</li>
<li>Performance feedback and guidance</li></ul>
<p>Some front-line managers may also provide career planning for employees who aim to rise within the organization.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Colleges and universities around the world offers bachelor's degrees, graduate degrees, diplomas and certificates in management; generally within their colleges of business, business schools or faculty of management but also in other related departments. In the 2010s era, there has been an increase in online management education and training in the form of electronic <a href="/wiki/Educational_technology" title="Educational technology">educational technology</a> (also called e-learning). Online education has increased the accessibility of management training to people who do not live near a college or university, or who cannot afford to travel to a city where such training is available.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Requirement">Requirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Requirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While some professions require academic credentials in order to work in the profession (e.g., law, medicine, engineering, which require, respectively the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Law" title="Bachelor of Law">Bachelor of Law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">Doctor of Medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Engineering" title="Bachelor of Engineering">Bachelor of Engineering</a> degrees), management and administration positions do not necessarily require the completion of academic degrees. Some well-known senior executives in the US who did not complete a degree include <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Zuckerberg" title="Mark Zuckerberg">Mark Zuckerberg</a>. However, many managers and executives have completed some type of business or management training, such as a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Commerce" title="Bachelor of Commerce">Bachelor of Commerce</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> degree. Some major organizations, including companies, non-profit organizations and governments, require applicants to managerial or executive positions to hold at minimum <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in a field related to administration or management, or in the case of business jobs, a Bachelor of Commerce or a similar degree.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Undergraduate">Undergraduate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Undergraduate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Business_education#Undergraduate_education" title="Business education">Business education § Undergraduate education</a></div>
<p>At the undergraduate level, the most common business programs are the <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Business_Administration" title="Bachelor of Business Administration">Bachelor of Business Administration</a> (BBA) and <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Commerce" title="Bachelor of Commerce">Bachelor of Commerce</a> (B.Com.).
These typically comprise a four-year program designed to give students an overview of the role of managers in planning and directing within an organization. 
Course topics include accounting, financial management, statistics, marketing, strategy, and other related areas.
</p><p>There are many other undergraduate degrees that include the study of management, such as <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degrees with a major in <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a> or management and Bachelor of Public Administration (B.P.A), a degree designed for individuals aiming to work as <a href="/wiki/Bureaucrat" title="Bureaucrat">bureaucrats</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">government jobs</a>. 
Many colleges and universities also offer certificates and diplomas in business administration or management, which typically require one to two years of full-time study.
</p><p>Note that to manage technological areas, one often needs an undergraduate degree in a <a href="/wiki/Science,_technology,_engineering,_and_mathematics#Immigration_policy" title="Science, technology, engineering, and mathematics">STEM area</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduate">Graduate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Graduate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Business_education#Postgraduate_education" title="Business education">Business education § Postgraduate education</a></div>
<p>At the graduate level students aiming at careers as managers or executives may choose to specialize in major subareas of management or business administration such as <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurship" title="Entrepreneurship">entrepreneurship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_business" title="International business">international business</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organizational_behavior" title="Organizational behavior">organizational behavior</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organizational_theory" title="Organizational theory">organizational theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strategic_management" title="Strategic management">strategic management</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_finance" title="Corporate finance">corporate finance</a>, entertainment, global management, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healthcare_management" title="Healthcare management">healthcare management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a>, sustainability and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> (MBA) is the most popular professional degree at the master's level and can be obtained from many universities in the United States. MBA programs provide further education in management and leadership for graduate students. Other master's degrees in business and management include <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Management" title="Master of Management">Master of Management</a> (MM) and the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> (M.Sc.) in business administration or management, which is typically taken by students aiming to become researchers or professors.
</p><p>There are also specialized master's degrees in administration for individuals aiming at careers outside of business, such as the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Public_Administration" title="Master of Public Administration">Master of Public Administration</a> (MPA) degree (also offered as a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Administration" title="Public Administration">Public Administration</a> in some universities), for students aiming to become managers or executives in the public service and the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Health_Administration" title="Master of Health Administration">Master of Health Administration</a>, for students aiming to become managers or executives in the health care and hospital sector.
</p><p>Management doctorates are the most advanced <a href="/wiki/Terminal_degree" title="Terminal degree">terminal degrees</a> in the field of business and management. Most individuals obtaining management doctorates take the programs to obtain the training in research methods, statistical analysis and writing academic papers that they will need to seek careers as researchers, senior consultants and/or professors in business administration or management. There are three main types of management doctorates: the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Management" title="Doctor of Management">Doctor of Management</a> (D.M.), the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Business_Administration" title="Doctor of Business Administration">Doctor of Business Administration</a> (D.B.A.), and the Doctor of Philosophy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> or DPhil) in Business Administration or Management. In the 2010s, doctorates in business administration and management are available with many specializations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Good_practices">Good practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Good practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While management trends can change so fast, the long-term trend in management has been defined by a market embracing diversity and a rising service industry. Managers are currently being trained to encourage greater equality for minorities and women in the workplace, by offering increased flexibility in working hours, better retraining, and innovative (and usually industry-specific) performance markers. Managers destined for the service sector are being trained to use unique measurement techniques, better worker support and more charismatic leadership styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Human resources finds itself increasingly working with management in a training capacity to help collect management data on the success (or failure) of management actions with employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence-based_management">Evidence-based management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Evidence-based management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_management" title="Evidence-based management">Evidence-based management</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_management" title="Evidence-based management">Evidence-based management</a> is an emerging movement to use the current, best evidence in management and <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a>. It is part of the larger movement towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evidence-based_practices" title="Evidence-based practices">evidence-based practices</a>. Evidence-based management entails managerial decisions and organizational practices informed by the best available evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pfeffer_2006_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pfeffer_2006-34">[34]</a></sup> As with other evidence-based practice, this is based on the three principles of: 1) published peer-reviewed (often in management or social science journals) research evidence that bears on whether and why a particular management practice works; 2) judgement and experience from contextual management practice, to understand the organization and interpersonal dynamics in a situation and determine the risks and benefits of available actions; and 3) the preferences and values of those affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some see management as a late-modern (in the sense of late <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>) conceptualization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On those terms it cannot have a pre-modern history – only harbingers (such as <a href="/wiki/Steward_(office)" title="Steward (office)">stewards</a>). Others, however, detect management-like thought among ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> traders and the builders of the pyramids of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>. Slave-owners through the centuries faced the problems of exploiting/motivating a dependent but sometimes unenthusiastic or recalcitrant workforce, but many pre-industrial <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">enterprises</a>, given their small scale, did not feel compelled to face the issues of management systematically. However, <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovations</a> such as the spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_numeral_system" title="Arabic numeral system">Arabic numerals</a> (5th to 15th centuries) and the codification of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping_system" title="Double-entry bookkeeping system">double-entry book-keeping</a> (1494) provided <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Management_tool&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Management tool (page does not exist)">tools</a> for management assessment, planning and control.
</p>
<ul><li>An organisation is more stable if members have the right to express their differences and solve their conflicts within it.</li>
<li>While one person can begin an organisation, "it is lasting when it is left in the care of many and when many desire to maintain it".</li>
<li>A weak manager can follow a strong one, but not another weak one, and maintain authority.</li>
<li>A manager seeking to change an established organization "should retain at least a shadow of the ancient customs".</li></ul>
<p>With the changing workplaces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolutions</a> in the 18th and 19th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> theory and practice contributed approaches to managing the newly popular <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Given the scale of most commercial operations and the lack of mechanized record-keeping and recording before the industrial revolution, it made sense for most <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">owners</a> of enterprises in those times to carry out management functions by and for themselves. But with growing size and complexity of organizations, a distinction between owners (individuals, industrial dynasties or groups of <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a>) and day-to-day managers (independent specialists in planning and control) gradually became more common.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_writing">Early writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Early writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The field of management originated in ancient China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ewan_Ferlie_p.30_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ewan_Ferlie_p.30-39">[39]</a></sup> including possibly the first highly centralized <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucratic</a> state, and the earliest (by the second century BC) example of an <a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">administration based on merit</a> through <a href="/wiki/Imperial_examination" title="Imperial examination">testing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APHq_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-APHq-40">[40]</a></sup> Some theorists have cited <a href="/wiki/Category:Ancient_military_books" title="Category:Ancient military books">ancient military texts</a> as providing lessons for civilian managers.  For example, Chinese general <a href="/wiki/Sun_Tzu" title="Sun Tzu">Sun Tzu</a> in his 6th-century BC work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_War" title="The Art of War">The Art of War</a></i> recommends<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (when re-phrased in modern terminology) being aware of and acting on strengths and weaknesses of both a manager's organization and a foe's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-management_gomez-mejia_p19_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-management_gomez-mejia_p19-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (July 2018)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> The writings of influential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Legalist" title="Chinese Legalist">Chinese Legalist</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Shen_Buhai" title="Shen Buhai">Shen Buhai</a> may be considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to embody a rare premodern example of abstract theory of administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> American philosopher <a href="/wiki/Herrlee_G._Creel" title="Herrlee G. Creel">Herrlee G. Creel</a> and other scholars find the influence of Chinese administration in Europe by the 12th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  Thomas Taylor Meadows, Britain's consul in <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, argued in his <i>Desultory Notes on the Government and People of China</i> (1847) that "the long duration of the Chinese empire is solely and altogether owing to the good government which consists in the advancement of men of talent and merit only," and that the British must reform their civil service by making the institution <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meritocratic" title="Meritocratic">meritocratic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodde_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bodde-48">[48]</a></sup> Influenced by the ancient Chinese <a href="/wiki/Imperial_examination" title="Imperial examination">imperial examination</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northcote%E2%80%93Trevelyan_Report" title="Northcote–Trevelyan Report">Northcote–Trevelyan Report</a> of 1854 recommended that recruitment should be on the basis of merit determined through competitive examination, candidates should have a solid general education to enable inter-departmental transfers, and promotion should be through achievement rather than "preferment, patronage, or purchase".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodde_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bodde-48">[48]</a></sup> This led to implementation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" title="Her Majesty's Civil Service">Her Majesty's Civil Service</a> as a systematic, meritocratic civil service bureaucracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Like the British, the development of French bureaucracy was influenced by the Chinese system.  <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a> claimed that the Chinese had "perfected moral science" and <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Quesnay" title="François Quesnay">François Quesnay</a> advocated an economic and political system modeled after that of the Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> French civil service examinations adopted in the late 19th century were also heavily based on general cultural studies. These features have been likened to the earlier Chinese model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rung_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-rung-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Various ancient and medieval civilizations produced "<a href="/wiki/Mirrors_for_princes" title="Mirrors for princes">mirrors for princes</a>" books, which aimed to advise new monarchs on how to govern. <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> described job specialization in 350 BC, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfarabi" title="Alfarabi">Alfarabi</a> listed several leadership traits in AD 900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Other examples include the Indian <i><a href="/wiki/Arthashastra" title="Arthashastra">Arthashastra</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Chanakya" title="Chanakya">Chanakya</a> (written around 300 BC), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i> by Italian author
<a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> (c. 1515).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-management_gomez-mejia_p20_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-management_gomez-mejia_p20-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mirrors_for_princes" title="Mirrors for princes">Mirrors for princes</a></div>
<p>Written in 1776 by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">moral philosopher</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i> discussed efficient organization of work through <a href="/wiki/Division_of_labour" title="Division of labour">division of labour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-management_gomez-mejia_p20_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-management_gomez-mejia_p20-54">[54]</a></sup>
Smith described how changes in processes could boost productivity in the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pin_(device)" title="Pin (device)">pins</a>. While individuals could produce 200 pins per day, Smith analyzed the steps involved in manufacture and, with 10 specialists, enabled production of 48,000 pins per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-management_gomez-mejia_p20_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-management_gomez-mejia_p20-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2017)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Classical economists such as <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> (1723–1790) and <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a> (1806–1873) provided a theoretical background to <a href="/wiki/Resource_allocation" title="Resource allocation">resource allocation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production (economics)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pricing" title="Pricing">pricing</a> issues. About the same time, innovators like <a href="/wiki/Eli_Whitney" title="Eli Whitney">Eli Whitney</a> (1765–1825), <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> (1736–1819), and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Boulton" title="Matthew Boulton">Matthew Boulton</a> (1728–1809) developed elements of technical production such as <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standardization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality-control</a> procedures, <a href="/wiki/Cost_accounting" title="Cost accounting">cost-accounting</a>, interchangeability of parts, and <a href="/wiki/Plan" title="Plan">work-planning</a>. Many of these aspects of management existed in the pre-1861 slave-based sector of the US economy. That environment saw 4 million people, as the contemporary usages had it, "managed" in profitable quasi-<a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
before <a href="/wiki/Wage_slavery" title="Wage slavery">wage slavery</a> eclipsed chattel slavery.
</p><p>Salaried managers as an identifiable group first became prominent in the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> As large corporations began to overshadow small family businesses the need for personnel management positions became more necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Businesses grew into large corporations and the need for clerks, bookkeepers, secretaries and managers expanded. The demand for trained managers led college and university administrators to consider and move forward with plans to create the first schools of business on their campuses.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the turn of the twentieth century the need for skilled and trained managers had become increasingly apparent. The demand occurred as personnel departments began to expand rapidly. In 1915, less than one in twenty manufacturing firms had a dedicated personnel department. By 1929 that number had grown to over one-third.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Formal management education became standardized at colleges and universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Colleges and universities capitalized on the needs of corporations by forming business schools and corporate placement departments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> This shift toward formal business education marked the creation of a corporate elite in the US.
</p><p>By about 1900 one finds managers trying to place their theories on what they regarded as a thoroughly scientific basis (see <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">scientism</a> for perceived limitations of this belief). Examples include <a href="/wiki/Henry_R._Towne" title="Henry R. Towne">Henry R. Towne</a>'s <i>Science of management</i> in the 1890s, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Winslow_Taylor" title="Frederick Winslow Taylor">Frederick Winslow Taylor</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Principles_of_Scientific_Management" title="The Principles of Scientific Management">The Principles of Scientific Management</a></i> (1911), <a href="/wiki/Lillian_Moller_Gilbreth" title="Lillian Moller Gilbreth">Lillian Gilbreth</a>'s <i>Psychology of Management</i> (1914),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Bunker_Gilbreth" title="Frank Bunker Gilbreth">Frank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lillian_Moller_Gilbreth" title="Lillian Moller Gilbreth">Lillian Gilbreth</a>'s <i>Applied motion study</i> (1917), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_L._Gantt" title="Henry L. Gantt">Henry L. Gantt</a>'s charts (1910s). J. Duncan wrote the first <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> management <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbook</a> in 1911. In 1912 <a href="/wiki/Yoichi_Ueno" title="Yoichi Ueno">Yoichi Ueno</a> introduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taylorism" title="Taylorism">Taylorism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and became the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Management_consultant" title="Management consultant">management consultant</a> of the "<a href="/wiki/Japanese_management_culture" title="Japanese management culture">Japanese management style</a>". His son Ichiro Ueno pioneered Japanese <a href="/wiki/Quality_assurance" title="Quality assurance">quality assurance</a>.
</p><p>The first comprehensive theories of management appeared around 1920.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a> offered the first <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> degree (MBA) in 1921. People like <a href="/wiki/Henri_Fayol" title="Henri Fayol">Henri Fayol</a> (1841–1925) and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton_Church" title="Alexander Hamilton Church">Alexander Church</a> (1866–1936) described the various branches of management and their inter-relationships. In the early 20th century, people like Ordway Tead (1891–1973), <a href="/wiki/Walter_Dill_Scott" title="Walter Dill Scott">Walter Scott</a> (1869–1955) and J. Mooney applied the principles of <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> to management. Other writers, such as <a href="/wiki/Elton_Mayo" title="Elton Mayo">Elton Mayo</a> (1880–1949), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Follett" title="Mary Follett">Mary Parker Follett</a> (1868–1933), <a href="/wiki/Chester_Barnard" title="Chester Barnard">Chester Barnard</a> (1886–1961), <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> (1864–1920), who saw what he called the "administrator" as <a href="/wiki/Bureaucrat" title="Bureaucrat">bureaucrat</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rensis_Likert" title="Rensis Likert">Rensis Likert</a> (1903–1981), and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Argyris" title="Chris Argyris">Chris Argyris</a> (born 1923) approached the phenomenon of management from a <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> perspective.
</p><p>The 1930s and 1940s saw the development of a <a href="/wiki/Militarization" title="Militarization">militarization</a> trend in management in parts of Eurasia – both the <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> (in the Soviet Union) and the <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel"> SS</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Germanic_Reich" title="Greater Germanic Reich">Greater Germanic Reich</a>), for example, managed <a href="/wiki/Labor_camp" title="Labor camp">labor camps</a> as industrial enterprises using slave labor supervised by uniformed cadres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
Military habits persisted in some management circles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Drucker" title="Peter Drucker">Peter Drucker</a> (1909–2005) wrote one of the earliest books on applied management: <i><a href="/wiki/Concept_of_the_Corporation" title="Concept of the Corporation">Concept of the Corporation</a></i> (published in 1946). It resulted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Sloan" title="Alfred Sloan">Alfred Sloan</a> (chairman of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation" title="General Motors Corporation">General Motors</a> until 1956) commissioning a study of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation" title="Organisation">organisation</a>. Drucker went on to write 39 books, many in the same vein.
</p><p>H. Dodge, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a> (1890–1962), and Thornton C. Fry introduced statistical techniques into management-studies. In the 1940s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrick_Maynard_Stuart_Blackett" title="Patrick Maynard Stuart Blackett">Patrick Blackett</a> worked in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">applied-mathematics</a> <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> of <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">operations research</a>, initially for military operations. Operations research, sometimes known as "<a href="/wiki/Management_science" title="Management science">management science</a>" (but distinct from Taylor's <a href="/wiki/Scientific_management" title="Scientific management">scientific management</a>), attempts to take a <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> approach to solving decision-problems, and can apply directly to multiple management problems, particularly in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> and operations.
</p><p>Some of the later 20th-century developments include the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_constraints" title="Theory of constraints">theory of constraints</a> (introduced in 1984), <a href="/wiki/Management_by_objectives" title="Management by objectives">management by objectives</a> (systematised in 1954), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_process_reengineering" title="Business process reengineering">re-engineering</a> (early 1990s), <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">Six Sigma</a> (1986), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Management_by_walking_around" title="Management by walking around">management by walking around</a> (1970s), the <a href="/wiki/Viable_system_model" title="Viable system model">Viable system model</a> (1972), and various <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology"> information-technology</a>-driven theories such as <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile software development</a> (so-named from 2001), as well as group-management theories such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cog%27s_Ladder" title="Cog's Ladder">Cog's Ladder</a> (1972) and the notion of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Peters" title="Tom Peters">"thriving on chaos"</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> (1987).
</p><p>As the general recognition of managers as a class solidified during the 20th century and gave perceived practitioners of the art/science of management a certain amount of prestige, so the way opened for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_philosophies_and_popular_management_theories" title="Business philosophies and popular management theories">popularised systems of management ideas</a> to peddle their wares. In this context many <a href="/wiki/Management_fad" title="Management fad">management fads</a> may have had more to do with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_psychology" title="Pop psychology">pop psychology</a> than with scientific theories of management.
</p><p>Business management<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2021)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> includes the following branches:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">financial management</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Human_resource_management" title="Human resource management">human resource management</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Management_cybernetics" title="Management cybernetics">Management cybernetics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_technology_management" title="Information technology management">information technology management</a> (responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Management_information_systems" title="Management information systems">management information systems</a> )</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marketing_management" title="Marketing management">marketing management</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Operations_management" title="Operations management">operations management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">production</a> management</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strategic_management" title="Strategic management">strategic management</a></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 21st century observers find it increasingly difficult to subdivide management into functional categories in this way. More and more processes simultaneously involve several categories. Instead, one tends to think in terms of the various processes, tasks, and objects subject to management.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Branches of management theory also exist relating to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">nonprofits</a> and to government: such as <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_management" title="Public management">public management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Educational_management" title="Educational management">educational management</a>. Further, management programs related to <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil-society</a> organizations have also spawned programs in nonprofit management and <a href="/wiki/Social_entrepreneurship" title="Social entrepreneurship">social entrepreneurship</a>.
</p><p>Note that many of the assumptions made by management have come under attack from <a href="/wiki/Business_ethics" title="Business ethics">business-ethics</a> viewpoints, <a href="/wiki/Critical_management_studies" title="Critical management studies">critical management studies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anti-corporate_activism" title="Anti-corporate activism">anti-corporate activism</a>.
</p><p>As one consequence, <a href="/wiki/Workplace_democracy" title="Workplace democracy">workplace democracy</a> (sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_self-management" title="Workers' self-management">Workers' self-management</a>) has become both more common and more advocated, in some places distributing all management functions among workers, each of whom takes on a portion of the work. However, these models predate any current political issue, and may occur more naturally than does a <a href="/wiki/Command_hierarchy" title="Command hierarchy">command hierarchy</a>. All management embraces to some degree a democratic principle—in that in the long term, the majority of workers must support management. Otherwise, they leave to find other work or go on strike. Despite the move toward workplace <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>, command-and-control organization structures remain commonplace as <i>de facto</i> organization structures. Indeed, the entrenched nature of command-and-control is evident in the way that recent<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (July 2018)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> layoffs have been conducted with management ranks affected far less than employees at the lower levels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In some cases, management has even rewarded itself with bonuses after laying off lower-level workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to leadership-academic <a href="/wiki/Manfred_F.R._Kets_de_Vries" title="Manfred F.R. Kets de Vries">Manfred F.R. Kets de Vries</a>, a contemporary senior-management team will almost inevitably have some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personality_disorders" title="Personality disorders">personality disorders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_work">Nature of work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Nature of work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In profitable organizations, management's primary function is the satisfaction of a range of <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a>. This typically involves making a profit (for the shareholders), creating valued products at a reasonable cost (for customers), and providing great employment opportunities for employees. In case of nonprofit management, one of the main functions is, keeping the faith of donors. In most models of management and <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a>, shareholders vote for the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a>, and the board then hires senior management. Some organizations have experimented with other methods (such as employee-voting models) of selecting or reviewing managers, but this is rare.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topics">Topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basics">Basics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Basics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Henri_Fayol" title="Henri Fayol">Fayol</a>, management operates through five basic functions: planning, organizing, commanding, coordinating and controlling.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Planning</b>: Deciding what needs to happen in the future and generating plans for action (deciding in advance).</li>
<li><b>Organizing</b> (or staffing): Making sure the human and nonhuman resources are put into place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peaucelle2015_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peaucelle2015-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Commanding</b> (or leading): Determining what must be done in a situation and getting people to do it.</li>
<li><b>Coordinating</b>: Creating a structure through which an organization's goals can be accomplished.</li>
<li><b>Controlling</b>: Checking progress against plans.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_roles">Basic roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Basic roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Interpersonal</b>: roles that involve coordination and interaction with employees.</li></ul>
<p>Figurehead, leader
</p>
<ul><li><b>Informational</b>: roles that involve handling, sharing, and analyzing information.</li></ul>
<p>Nerve centre, disseminator
</p>
<ul><li><b>Decision</b>: roles that require decision-making.</li></ul>
<p>Entrepreneur, negotiator, allocator, disturbance handler
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skills">Skills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Skills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Management skills include:
</p>
<ul><li>political: used to build a power base and to establish <a href="/wiki/Personal_network" title="Personal network">connections.</a></li>
<li>conceptual: used to analyze complex situations.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpersonal" title="Interpersonal">interpersonal</a>: used to communicate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motivate" title="Motivate">motivate</a>, mentor and delegate.</li>
<li>diagnostic: ability to <a href="/wiki/Mental_image" title="Mental image">visualize</a> appropriate responses to a situation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leadership</a>: ability to communicate a vision and inspire people to embrace that vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cross-cultural_leadership" title="Cross-cultural leadership">cross-cultural leadership</a>: ability to understand the effects of culture on leadership style.</li></ul></li>
<li>technical: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expertise" title="Expertise">expertise</a> in one's particular functional area.</li>
<li>behavioral: perception towards others, conflict resolution, time-management, self-improvement, stress management and resilience, patience, clear communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation_of_policies_and_strategies">Implementation of policies and strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Implementation of policies and strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>All policies and strategies must be discussed with all managerial personnel and staff.</li>
<li>Managers must understand where and how they can implement their policies and strategies.</li>
<li>An action plan must be devised for each department.</li>
<li>Policies and strategies must be reviewed regularly.</li>
<li>Contingency plans must be devised in case the environment changes.</li>
<li>Top-level managers should carry out regular progress assessments.</li>
<li>The business requires team spirit and a good environment.</li>
<li>The missions, objectives, strengths and weaknesses of each department must be analyzed to determine their roles in achieving the business's mission.</li>
<li>The forecasting method develops a reliable picture of the business's future environment.</li>
<li>A planning unit must be created to ensure that all plans are consistent and that policies and strategies are aimed at achieving the same mission and objectives.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies_and_strategies_in_the_planning_process">Policies and strategies in the planning process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Policies and strategies in the planning process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>They give mid and lower-level managers a good idea of the future plans for each department in an organization.</li>
<li>A framework is created whereby plans and decisions are made.</li>
<li>Mid and lower-level management may add their own plans to the business's strategies.</li></ul>
