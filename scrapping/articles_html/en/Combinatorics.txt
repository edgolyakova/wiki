<p><b>Combinatorics</b> is an area of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> primarily concerned with <a href="/wiki/Counting" title="Counting">counting</a>, both as a means and an end in obtaining results, and certain properties of <a href="/wiki/Finite_set" title="Finite set">finite</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_structure" title="Mathematical structure">structures</a>.  It is closely related to many other areas of mathematics and has many applications ranging from <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Statistical_physics" title="Statistical physics">statistical physics</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a> to <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>.
</p><p>Combinatorics is well known for the breadth of the problems it tackles. Combinatorial problems arise in many areas of <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>, notably in <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> as well as in its many application areas. Many combinatorial questions have historically been considered in isolation, giving an <i>ad hoc</i> solution to a problem arising in some mathematical context. In the later twentieth century, however, powerful and general theoretical methods were developed, making combinatorics into an independent branch of mathematics in its own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> One of the oldest and most accessible parts of combinatorics is <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>, which by itself has numerous natural connections to other areas. Combinatorics is used frequently in computer science to obtain formulas and estimates in the <a href="/wiki/Analysis_of_algorithms" title="Analysis of algorithms">analysis of algorithms</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> who studies combinatorics is called a <i><dfn>combinatorialist</dfn></i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The full scope of combinatorics is not universally agreed upon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.J._Ryser" title="H.J. Ryser">H.J. Ryser</a>, a definition of the subject is difficult because it crosses so many mathematical subdivisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Insofar as an area can be described by the types of problems it addresses, combinatorics is involved with:
</p>
<ul><li>the <i>enumeration</i> (counting) of specified structures, sometimes referred to as arrangements or configurations in a very general sense, associated with finite systems,</li>
<li>the <i>existence</i> of such structures that satisfy certain given criteria,</li>
<li>the <i>construction</i> of these structures, perhaps in many ways, and</li>
<li><i>optimization</i>: finding the "best" structure or solution among several possibilities, be it the "largest",  "smallest" or satisfying some other <i>optimality criterion</i>.</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leon_Mirsky" title="Leon Mirsky">Leon Mirsky</a> has said: "combinatorics is a range of linked studies which have something in common and yet diverge widely in their objectives, their methods, and the degree of coherence they have attained."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> One way to define combinatorics is, perhaps, to describe its subdivisions with their problems and techniques. This is the approach that is used below. However, there are also purely historical reasons for including or not including some topics under the combinatorics umbrella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Although primarily concerned with finite systems, some combinatorial questions and techniques can be extended to an infinite (specifically, <a href="/wiki/Countable_set" title="Countable set">countable</a>) but <a href="/wiki/Discrete_mathematics" title="Discrete mathematics">discrete</a> setting.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plain-bob-minor_2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="295" decoding="async" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Plain-bob-minor_2.png/150px-Plain-bob-minor_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Plain-bob-minor_2.png/225px-Plain-bob-minor_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/Plain-bob-minor_2.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plain-bob-minor_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of <a href="/wiki/Change_ringing" title="Change ringing">change ringing</a> (with six bells), one of the earliest nontrivial results in <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_combinatorics" title="History of combinatorics">History of combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Basic combinatorial concepts and enumerative results appeared throughout the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient world</a>. In the 6th century BCE, <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Indian_history" title="Timeline of Indian history">ancient Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sushruta" title="Sushruta">Sushruta</a> asserts in <a href="/wiki/Sushruta_Samhita" title="Sushruta Samhita">Sushruta Samhita</a> that 63 combinations can be made out of 6 different tastes, taken one at a time, two at a time, etc., thus computing all 2<sup>6</sup> − 1 possibilities.  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> discusses an argument between <a href="/wiki/Chrysippus" title="Chrysippus">Chrysippus</a> (3rd century BCE) and <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">Hipparchus</a> (2nd century BCE) of a rather delicate enumerative problem, which was later shown to be related to <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6der%E2%80%93Hipparchus_number" title="Schröder–Hipparchus number">Schröder–Hipparchus numbers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Earlier, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Ostomachion" title="Ostomachion">Ostomachion</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> (3rd century BCE) may have considered the number of configurations of a <a href="/wiki/Tiling_puzzle" title="Tiling puzzle">tiling puzzle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> while combinatorial interests possibly were present in lost works by <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga" title="Apollonius of Perga">Apollonius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, combinatorics continued to be studied, largely outside of the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Europe" title="Culture of Europe">European civilization</a>. The <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81v%C4%ABra_(mathematician)" title="Mahāvīra (mathematician)">Mahāvīra</a> (c. 850) provided formulae for the number of <a href="/wiki/Permutation" title="Permutation">permutations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Combination" title="Combination">combinations</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and these formulas may have been familiar to Indian mathematicians as early as the 6th century CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Abraham_ibn_Ezra" title="Abraham ibn Ezra">Abraham ibn Ezra</a> (c. 1140) established the symmetry of <a href="/wiki/Binomial_coefficient" title="Binomial coefficient">binomial coefficients</a>, while a closed formula was obtained later by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talmudist" title="Talmudist">talmudist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levi_ben_Gerson" title="Levi ben Gerson">Levi ben Gerson</a> (better known as Gersonides), in 1321.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
The arithmetical triangle—a graphical diagram showing relationships among the binomial coefficients—was presented by mathematicians in treatises dating as far back as the 10th century, and would eventually become known as <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_triangle" title="Pascal's triangle">Pascal's triangle</a>. Later, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_England" title="Medieval England">Medieval England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Campanology" title="Campanology">campanology</a> provided examples of what is now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_cycle" title="Hamiltonian cycle">Hamiltonian cycles</a> in certain <a href="/wiki/Cayley_graph" title="Cayley graph">Cayley graphs</a> on permutations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, together with the rest of mathematics and the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a>, combinatorics enjoyed a rebirth. Works of <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Pascal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Bernoulli" title="Jacob Bernoulli">Jacob Bernoulli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Euler</a> became foundational in the emerging field. In modern times, the works of <a href="/wiki/James_Joseph_Sylvester" title="James Joseph Sylvester">J.J. Sylvester</a> (late 19th century) and <a href="/wiki/Percy_Alexander_MacMahon" title="Percy Alexander MacMahon">Percy MacMahon</a> (early 20th century) helped lay the foundation for <a href="/wiki/Enumerative_combinatorics" title="Enumerative combinatorics">enumerative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics" title="Algebraic combinatorics">algebraic combinatorics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">Graph theory</a> also enjoyed an increase of interest at the same time, especially in connection with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_color_problem" title="Four color problem">four color problem</a>.
</p><p>In the second half of the 20th century, combinatorics enjoyed a rapid growth, which led to establishment of dozens of new journals and conferences in the subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In part, the growth was spurred by new connections and applications to other fields, ranging from algebra to probability, from <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, etc.  These connections shed the boundaries between combinatorics and parts of mathematics and theoretical computer science, but at the same time led to a partial fragmentation of the field.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches_and_subfields_of_combinatorics">Approaches and subfields of combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Approaches and subfields of combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enumerative_combinatorics">Enumerative combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Enumerative combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="90" data-file-width="650" decoding="async" height="44" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg/320px-Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg/480px-Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg/640px-Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Catalan_4_leaves_binary_tree_example.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Five <a href="/wiki/Binary_tree" title="Binary tree">binary trees</a> on three <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(graph_theory)" title="Vertex (graph theory)">vertices</a>, an example of <a href="/wiki/Catalan_number" title="Catalan number">Catalan numbers</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enumerative_combinatorics" title="Enumerative combinatorics">Enumerative combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Enumerative combinatorics is the most classical area of combinatorics and concentrates on counting the number of certain combinatorial objects.  Although counting the number of elements in a set is a rather broad <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_problem" title="Mathematical problem">mathematical problem</a>, many of the problems that arise in applications have a relatively simple combinatorial description. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibonacci_numbers" title="Fibonacci numbers">Fibonacci numbers</a> is the basic example of a problem in enumerative combinatorics.  The <a href="/wiki/Twelvefold_way" title="Twelvefold way">twelvefold way</a> provides a unified framework for counting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permutations" title="Permutations">permutations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combinations" title="Combinations">combinations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_a_set" title="Partition of a set">partitions</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytic_combinatorics">Analytic combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Analytic combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic_combinatorics" title="Analytic combinatorics">Analytic combinatorics</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic_combinatorics" title="Analytic combinatorics">Analytic combinatorics</a> concerns the enumeration of combinatorial structures using tools from <a href="/wiki/Complex_analysis" title="Complex analysis">complex analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>.  In contrast with enumerative combinatorics, which uses explicit combinatorial formulae and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generating_functions" title="Generating functions">generating functions</a> to describe the results, analytic combinatorics aims at obtaining <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_analysis" title="Asymptotic analysis">asymptotic formulae</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Partition_theory">Partition theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Partition theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Partition3D.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="432" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Partition3D.svg/150px-Partition3D.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Partition3D.svg/225px-Partition3D.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Partition3D.svg/300px-Partition3D.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Partition3D.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Plane_partition" title="Plane partition">plane partition</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Partition_(number_theory)" title="Partition (number theory)">Partition theory</a></div>
<p>Partition theory studies various enumeration and asymptotic problems related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integer_partition" title="Integer partition">integer partitions</a>, and is closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q-series" title="Q-series">q-series</a>, <a href="/wiki/Special_functions" title="Special functions">special functions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomials" title="Orthogonal polynomials">orthogonal polynomials</a>.  Originally a part of <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Analysis" title="Analysis">analysis</a>, it is now considered a part of combinatorics or an independent field.  It incorporates the <a href="/wiki/Bijective_proof" title="Bijective proof">bijective approach</a> and various tools in analysis and <a href="/wiki/Analytic_number_theory" title="Analytic number theory">analytic number theory</a> and has connections with <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>. Partitions can be graphically visualized with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_diagram" title="Young diagram">Young diagrams</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrers_diagram" title="Ferrers diagram">Ferrers diagrams</a>. They occur in a number of branches of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, including the study of <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_polynomial" title="Symmetric polynomial">symmetric polynomials</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_group" title="Symmetric group">symmetric group</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Group_representation" title="Group representation">group representation theory</a> in general.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graph_theory">Graph theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Graph theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Petersen1_tiny.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="220" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Petersen1_tiny.svg/150px-Petersen1_tiny.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Petersen1_tiny.svg/225px-Petersen1_tiny.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Petersen1_tiny.svg/300px-Petersen1_tiny.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Petersen1_tiny.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Petersen_graph" title="Petersen graph">Petersen graph</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">Graph theory</a></div>
<p>Graphs are fundamental objects  in combinatorics.  Considerations of graph theory range from enumeration (e.g., the number of graphs on <i>n</i> vertices with <i>k</i> edges) to existing structures (e.g., Hamiltonian cycles) to algebraic representations (e.g., given a graph <i>G</i> and two numbers <i>x</i> and <i>y</i>, does the <a href="/wiki/Tutte_polynomial" title="Tutte polynomial">Tutte polynomial</a> <i>T</i><sub><i>G</i></sub>(<i>x</i>,<i>y</i>) have a combinatorial interpretation?).  Although there are very strong connections between graph theory and combinatorics, they are sometimes thought of as separate subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  While combinatorial methods apply to many graph theory problems, the two disciplines are generally used to seek solutions to different types of problems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_theory">Design theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Design theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_design" title="Combinatorial design">Combinatorial design</a></div>
<p>Design theory is a study of <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_design" title="Combinatorial design">combinatorial designs</a>, which are collections of subsets with certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_intersection" title="Set intersection">intersection</a> properties. <a href="/wiki/Block_design" title="Block design">Block designs</a> are combinatorial designs of a special type. This area is one of the oldest parts of combinatorics, such as in <a href="/wiki/Kirkman%27s_schoolgirl_problem" title="Kirkman's schoolgirl problem">Kirkman's schoolgirl problem</a> proposed in 1850. The solution of the problem is a special case of a <a href="/wiki/Steiner_system" title="Steiner system">Steiner system</a>, which systems play an important role in the <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups" title="Classification of finite simple groups">classification of finite simple groups</a>. The area has further connections to <a href="/wiki/Coding_theory" title="Coding theory">coding theory</a> and geometric combinatorics. 
</p><p>Combinatorial design theory can be applied to the area of <a href="/wiki/Design_of_experiments" title="Design of experiments">design of experiments</a>.  Some of the basic theory of combinatorial designs originated in the statistician <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a>'s work on the design of biological experiments. Modern applications are also found in a wide gamut of areas including <a href="/wiki/Finite_geometry" title="Finite geometry">finite geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tournament" title="Tournament">tournament scheduling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lottery" title="Lottery">lotteries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_chemistry" title="Mathematical chemistry">mathematical chemistry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_biology" title="Mathematical biology">mathematical biology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algorithm_design" title="Algorithm design">algorithm design and analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">networking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Group_testing" title="Group testing">group testing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finite_geometry">Finite geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Finite geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Finite_geometry" title="Finite geometry">Finite geometry</a></div>
<p>Finite geometry is the study of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometric systems</a> having only a finite number of points. Structures analogous to those found in continuous geometries (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euclidean_plane" title="Euclidean plane">Euclidean plane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Real_projective_space" title="Real projective space">real projective space</a>, etc.) but defined combinatorially are the main items studied. This area provides a rich source of examples for <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_design" title="Combinatorial design">design theory</a>. It should not be confused with discrete geometry (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combinatorial_geometry" title="Combinatorial geometry">combinatorial geometry</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Order_theory">Order theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Order theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="325" data-file-width="429" decoding="async" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg/150px-Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg/225px-Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg/300px-Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hasse_diagram_of_powerset_of_3.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hasse_diagram" title="Hasse diagram">Hasse diagram</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Power_set" title="Power set">powerset</a> of {x,y,z} ordered by <a href="/wiki/Inclusion_map" title="Inclusion map">inclusion</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Order_theory" title="Order theory">Order theory</a></div>
<p>Order theory is the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partially_ordered_sets" title="Partially ordered sets">partially ordered sets</a>, both finite and infinite. It provides a formal framework for describing statements such as "this is less than that" or "this precedes that". Various examples of partial orders appear in <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">algebra</a>, geometry, number theory and throughout combinatorics and graph theory.  Notable classes and examples of partial orders include <a href="/wiki/Lattice_(order)" title="Lattice (order)">lattices</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boolean_algebras" title="Boolean algebras">Boolean algebras</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Matroid_theory">Matroid theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Matroid theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matroid_theory" title="Matroid theory">Matroid theory</a></div>
<p>Matroid theory abstracts part of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>. It studies the properties of sets (usually, finite sets) of vectors in a <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vector space</a> that do not depend on the particular coefficients in a <a href="/wiki/Linear_independence" title="Linear independence">linear dependence</a> relation.  Not only the structure but also enumerative properties belong to matroid theory.  Matroid theory was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Hassler_Whitney" title="Hassler Whitney">Hassler Whitney</a> and studied as a part of order theory.  It is now an independent field of study with a number of connections with other parts of combinatorics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extremal_combinatorics">Extremal combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extremal combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extremal_combinatorics" title="Extremal combinatorics">Extremal combinatorics</a></div><p>Extremal combinatorics studies how large or how small a collection of finite objects (<a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)" title="Graph (discrete mathematics)">graphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vectors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">sets</a>, etc.) can be, if it has to satisfy certain restrictions. Much of extremal combinatorics concerns <a href="/wiki/Class_(set_theory)" title="Class (set theory)">classes</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_system" title="Set system">set systems</a>; this is called extremal set theory. For instance, in an <i>n</i>-element set, what is the largest number of <i>k</i>-element <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subsets</a> that can pairwise intersect one another?  What is the largest number of subsets of which none contains any other?  The latter question is answered by <a href="/wiki/Sperner_family#Sperner's_theorem" title="Sperner family">Sperner's theorem</a>, which gave rise to much of extremal set theory.
</p><p>The types of questions addressed in this case are about the largest possible graph which satisfies certain properties.  For example, the largest <a href="/wiki/Triangle-free_graph" title="Triangle-free graph">triangle-free graph</a> on <i>2n</i> vertices is a <a href="/wiki/Complete_bipartite_graph" title="Complete bipartite graph">complete bipartite graph</a> <i>K<sub>n,n</sub></i>. Often it is too hard even to find the extremal answer <i>f</i>(<i>n</i>) exactly and one can only give an <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_analysis" title="Asymptotic analysis">asymptotic estimate</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ramsey_theory" title="Ramsey theory">Ramsey theory</a> is another part of extremal combinatorics.  It states that any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sufficiently_large" title="Sufficiently large">sufficiently large</a> configuration will contain some sort of order. It is an advanced generalization of the <a href="/wiki/Pigeonhole_principle" title="Pigeonhole principle">pigeonhole principle</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Probabilistic_combinatorics">Probabilistic combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Probabilistic combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Self_avoiding_walk.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Self_avoiding_walk.svg/150px-Self_avoiding_walk.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Self_avoiding_walk.svg/225px-Self_avoiding_walk.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Self_avoiding_walk.svg/300px-Self_avoiding_walk.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Self_avoiding_walk.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Self-avoiding_walk" title="Self-avoiding walk">Self-avoiding walk</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Lattice_graph" title="Lattice graph">square grid graph</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Probabilistic_method" title="Probabilistic method">Probabilistic method</a></div>
<p>In probabilistic combinatorics, the questions are of the following type: what is the probability of a certain property for a random discrete object, such as a <a href="/wiki/Random_graph" title="Random graph">random graph</a>? For instance, what is the average number of triangles in a random graph? Probabilistic methods are also used to determine the existence of combinatorial objects with certain prescribed properties (for which explicit examples might be difficult to find), simply by observing that the probability of randomly selecting an object with those properties is greater than 0. This approach (often referred to as <i>the</i> <a href="/wiki/Probabilistic_method" title="Probabilistic method">probabilistic method</a>) proved highly effective in applications to extremal combinatorics and graph theory. A closely related area is the study of finite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markov_chains" title="Markov chains">Markov chains</a>, especially on combinatorial objects. Here again probabilistic tools are used to estimate the <a href="/wiki/Markov_chain_mixing_time" title="Markov chain mixing time">mixing time</a>.
</p><p>Often associated with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Erd%C5%91s" title="Paul Erdős">Paul Erdős</a>, who did the pioneering work on the subject, probabilistic combinatorics was traditionally viewed as a set of tools to study problems in other parts of combinatorics. However, with the growth of applications to <a href="/wiki/Analysis_of_algorithms" title="Analysis of algorithms">analyze algorithms</a> in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, as well as classical probability, <a href="/wiki/Additive_number_theory" title="Additive number theory">additive number theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Probabilistic_number_theory" title="Probabilistic number theory">probabilistic number theory</a>, the area recently grew to become an independent field of combinatorics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algebraic_combinatorics">Algebraic combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Algebraic combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="108" data-file-width="180" decoding="async" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg/150px-Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg/225px-Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg/300px-Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Young_diagram_for_541_partition.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_diagram" title="Young diagram">Young diagram</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Partition_(number_theory)" title="Partition (number theory)">partition</a> (5,4,1).</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics" title="Algebraic combinatorics">Algebraic combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Algebraic combinatorics is an area of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> that employs methods of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a>, notably <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Representation_theory" title="Representation theory">representation theory</a>, in various combinatorial contexts and, conversely, applies combinatorial techniques to problems in <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">algebra</a>. Algebraic combinatorics has come to be seen more expansively as an area of mathematics where the interaction of combinatorial and algebraic methods is particularly strong and significant. Thus the combinatorial topics may be <a href="/wiki/Enumerative_combinatorics" title="Enumerative combinatorics">enumerative</a> in nature or involve <a href="/wiki/Matroid" title="Matroid">matroids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partially_ordered_set" title="Partially ordered set">partially ordered sets</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Finite_geometry" title="Finite geometry">finite geometries</a>. On the algebraic side, besides group and representation theory, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_theory" title="Lattice theory">lattice theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">commutative algebra</a> are common.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combinatorics_on_words">Combinatorics on words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Combinatorics on words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Morse-Thue_sequence.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="57" data-file-width="318" decoding="async" height="38" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Morse-Thue_sequence.gif/210px-Morse-Thue_sequence.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Morse-Thue_sequence.gif/315px-Morse-Thue_sequence.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Morse-Thue_sequence.gif 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Morse-Thue_sequence.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Construction of a <a href="/wiki/Thue%E2%80%93Morse_sequence" title="Thue–Morse sequence">Thue–Morse infinite word</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Combinatorics_on_words" title="Combinatorics on words">Combinatorics on words</a></div>
<p>Combinatorics on words deals with <a href="/wiki/Formal_language" title="Formal language">formal languages</a>.  It  arose independently within several branches of mathematics, including <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>. It has applications to enumerative combinatorics, <a href="/wiki/Fractal_analysis" title="Fractal analysis">fractal analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_computer_science" title="Theoretical computer science">theoretical computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Automata_theory" title="Automata theory">automata theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>. While many applications are new, the classical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chomsky%E2%80%93Sch%C3%BCtzenberger_hierarchy" title="Chomsky–Schützenberger hierarchy">Chomsky–Schützenberger hierarchy</a> of classes of <a href="/wiki/Formal_grammar" title="Formal grammar">formal grammars</a> is perhaps the best-known result in the field.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geometric_combinatorics">Geometric combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geometric combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Icosahedron.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Icosahedron.svg/150px-Icosahedron.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Icosahedron.svg/225px-Icosahedron.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Icosahedron.svg/300px-Icosahedron.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Icosahedron.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">icosahedron</a>.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geometric_combinatorics" title="Geometric combinatorics">Geometric combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Geometric combinatorics is related to <a href="/wiki/Convex_geometry" title="Convex geometry">convex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Discrete_geometry" title="Discrete geometry">discrete geometry</a>. It asks, for example, how many faces of each dimension a <a href="/wiki/Convex_polytope" title="Convex polytope">convex polytope</a> can have.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_geometry" title="Metric geometry">Metric</a> properties of polytopes play an important role as well, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Cauchy%27s_theorem_(geometry)" title="Cauchy's theorem (geometry)">Cauchy theorem</a> on the rigidity of convex polytopes.  Special polytopes are also considered, such as <a href="/wiki/Permutohedron" title="Permutohedron">permutohedra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Associahedron" title="Associahedron">associahedra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birkhoff_polytope" title="Birkhoff polytope">Birkhoff polytopes</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combinatorial_geometry" title="Combinatorial geometry">Combinatorial geometry</a> is a historical name for discrete geometry.
</p><p>It includes a number of subareas such as <a href="/wiki/Polyhedral_combinatorics" title="Polyhedral combinatorics">polyhedral combinatorics</a> (the study of <a href="/wiki/Face_(geometry)" title="Face (geometry)">faces</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convex_polyhedron" title="Convex polyhedron">convex polyhedra</a>), <a href="/wiki/Convex_geometry" title="Convex geometry">convex geometry</a> (the study of <a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">convex sets</a>, in particular combinatorics of their intersections), and <a href="/wiki/Discrete_geometry" title="Discrete geometry">discrete geometry</a>, which in turn has many applications to <a href="/wiki/Computational_geometry" title="Computational geometry">computational geometry</a>. The study of <a href="/wiki/Regular_polytope" title="Regular polytope">regular polytopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archimedean_solid" title="Archimedean solid">Archimedean solids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kissing_number" title="Kissing number">kissing numbers</a> is also a part of geometric combinatorics. Special polytopes are also considered, such as the <a href="/wiki/Permutohedron" title="Permutohedron">permutohedron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Associahedron" title="Associahedron">associahedron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birkhoff_polytope" title="Birkhoff polytope">Birkhoff polytope</a>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topological_combinatorics">Topological combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Topological combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Topological_combinatorics" title="Topological combinatorics">Topological combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Combinatorial analogs of concepts and methods in <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a> are used to study <a href="/wiki/Graph_coloring" title="Graph coloring">graph coloring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fair_division" title="Fair division">fair division</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_a_set" title="Partition of a set">partitions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partially_ordered_set" title="Partially ordered set">partially ordered sets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decision_tree" title="Decision tree">decision trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Necklace_problem" title="Necklace problem">necklace problems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Discrete_Morse_theory" title="Discrete Morse theory">discrete Morse theory</a>.  It should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_topology" title="Combinatorial topology">combinatorial topology</a> which is an older name for <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arithmetic_combinatorics">Arithmetic combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Arithmetic combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_combinatorics" title="Arithmetic combinatorics">Arithmetic combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Arithmetic combinatorics arose out of the interplay between <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, combinatorics, <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodic theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_analysis" title="Harmonic analysis">harmonic analysis</a>. It is about combinatorial estimates associated with arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division). <a href="/wiki/Additive_number_theory" title="Additive number theory">Additive number theory</a> (sometimes also called additive combinatorics) refers to the special case when only the operations of addition and subtraction are involved.  One important technique in arithmetic combinatorics is the <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodic theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical systems</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infinitary_combinatorics">Infinitary combinatorics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Infinitary combinatorics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infinitary_combinatorics" title="Infinitary combinatorics">Infinitary combinatorics</a></div>
<p>Infinitary combinatorics, or combinatorial set theory, is an extension of ideas in combinatorics to infinite sets.  It is a part of <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a>, an area of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_logic" title="Mathematical logic">mathematical logic</a>, but uses tools and ideas from both set theory and extremal combinatorics. Some of the things studied include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_graph" title="Continuous graph">continuous graphs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tree_(set_theory)" title="Tree (set theory)">trees</a>, extensions of <a href="/wiki/Ramsey%27s_theorem" title="Ramsey's theorem">Ramsey's theorem</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Martin%27s_axiom" title="Martin's axiom">Martin's axiom</a>. Recent developments concern combinatorics of the <a href="/wiki/Continuum_(set_theory)" title="Continuum (set theory)">continuum</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and combinatorics on successors of singular cardinals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gian-Carlo_Rota" title="Gian-Carlo Rota">Gian-Carlo Rota</a> used the name <i>continuous combinatorics</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> to describe <a href="/wiki/Geometric_probability" title="Geometric probability">geometric probability</a>, since there are many analogies between <i>counting</i> and <i>measure</i>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combinatorial_optimization">Combinatorial optimization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Combinatorial optimization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_optimization" title="Combinatorial optimization">Combinatorial optimization</a> is the study of optimization on discrete and combinatorial objects. It started as a part of combinatorics and graph theory, but is now viewed as a branch of applied mathematics and computer science, related to <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">operations research</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analysis_of_algorithms" title="Analysis of algorithms">algorithm theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory" title="Computational complexity theory">computational complexity theory</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coding_theory">Coding theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Coding theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coding_theory" title="Coding theory">Coding theory</a> started as a part of design theory with early combinatorial constructions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Error-correcting_code" title="Error-correcting code">error-correcting codes</a>. The main idea of the subject is to design efficient and reliable methods of data transmission. It is now a large field of study, part of <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information theory</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discrete_and_computational_geometry">Discrete and computational geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Discrete and computational geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Discrete_geometry" title="Discrete geometry">Discrete geometry</a> (also called combinatorial geometry) also began as a part of combinatorics, with early results on <a href="/wiki/Convex_polytope" title="Convex polytope">convex polytopes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kissing_number" title="Kissing number">kissing numbers</a>.  With the emergence of applications of discrete geometry to <a href="/wiki/Computational_geometry" title="Computational geometry">computational geometry</a>, these two fields partially merged and became a separate field of study.  There remain many connections with geometric and topological combinatorics, which themselves can be viewed as outgrowths of the early discrete geometry.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combinatorics_and_dynamical_systems">Combinatorics and dynamical systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Combinatorics and dynamical systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Combinatorics_and_dynamical_systems" title="Combinatorics and dynamical systems">Combinatorial aspects of dynamical systems</a> is another emerging field.  Here dynamical systems can be defined on combinatorial objects.  See for example
<a href="/wiki/Graph_dynamical_system" title="Graph dynamical system">graph dynamical system</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combinatorics_and_physics">Combinatorics and physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combinatorics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Combinatorics and physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are increasing interactions between <a href="/wiki/Combinatorics_and_physics" title="Combinatorics and physics">combinatorics and physics</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Statistical_physics" title="Statistical physics">statistical physics</a>. Examples include an exact solution of the <a href="/wiki/Ising_model" title="Ising model">Ising model</a>, and a connection between the <a href="/wiki/Potts_model" title="Potts model">Potts model</a> on one hand, and the <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_polynomial" title="Chromatic polynomial">chromatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tutte_polynomial" title="Tutte polynomial">Tutte polynomials</a> on the other hand.
</p>
