<p>The <b>Iron Age</b> is the final epoch of the <a href="/wiki/Three-age_system" title="Three-age system">three-age division</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">protohistory</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">humanity</a>. It was preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> (<a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a>). The concept has been mostly applied to <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Europe" title="Iron Age Europe">Iron Age Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a>, but also, by analogy, to other parts of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>.
</p><p>The duration of the Iron Age varies depending on the region under consideration. It is defined by archaeological convention. The "Iron Age" begins locally when the production of iron or <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> has advanced to the point where iron tools and weapons replace their <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> equivalents in common use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a>, this transition took place in the wake of the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a>, in the 12th century BCE. The technology soon spread throughout the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a> region and to <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_in_India" title="Iron Age in India">Iron Age in India</a>) between the 12th and 11th century BCE. Its further spread to <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> is somewhat delayed, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> was not reached until around the start of the 5th century BCE.
</p><p>The Iron Age is taken to end, also by convention, with the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historiographical_record" title="Historiographical record">historiographical record</a>.
This usually does not represent a clear break in the archaeological record; for the Ancient Near East, the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> c. 550 BCE is traditionally and still usually taken as a cut-off date, later dates being considered historical by virtue of the record by <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, despite considerable written records from far earlier (well back into the Bronze Age) now being known. In Central and Western Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman conquests</a> of the 1st century BC serve as marking for the end of the Iron Age. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_Iron_Age" title="Germanic Iron Age">Germanic Iron Age</a> of Scandinavia is taken to end c. AD 800, with the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking Age</a>.
</p><p>In the Indian sub-continent, the Iron Age is taken to begin with the ironworking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painted_Gray_Ware" title="Painted Gray Ware">Painted Gray Ware</a> culture. Recent estimates suggest that it ranges from the 15th century BCE, through to the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> in the 3rd century BCE. The use of the term "Iron Age" in the archaeology of South, East, and Southeast Asia is more recent and less common than for Western Eurasia. In China, <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">written history</a> started before iron-working arrived, so the term is infrequently used. The <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudan_region" title="Sudan region">Sudan region</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> are outside of the three-age system, there being no Bronze Age, but the term "Iron Age" is sometimes used in reference to early cultures practicing ironworking, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nok_culture" title="Nok culture">Nok culture</a> of Nigeria.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">History of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three-age system was introduced in the first half of the 19th century for the archaeology of Europe in particular, and by the later 19th century expanded to the archaeology of the Ancient Near East. Its name harks back to the mythological "<a href="/wiki/Ages_of_Man" title="Ages of Man">Ages of Man</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>. As an archaeological era, it was first introduced for Scandinavia by <a href="/wiki/Christian_J%C3%BCrgensen_Thomsen" title="Christian Jürgensen Thomsen">Christian Jürgensen Thomsen</a> in the 1830s. By the 1860s, it was embraced as a useful division of the "earliest history of mankind" in general<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and began to be applied in <a href="/wiki/Assyriology" title="Assyriology">Assyriology</a>. The development of the now-conventional <a href="/wiki/Periodization" title="Periodization">periodization</a> in the archaeology of the Ancient Near East was developed in the 1920s to 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
As its name suggests, Iron Age technology is characterized by the production of tools and weaponry by <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy" title="Ferrous metallurgy">ferrous metallurgy</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_iron_production" title="Ancient iron production">ironworking</a>), more specifically from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<p>Increasingly the Iron Age in Europe is being seen as a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">ancient Near East</a>, in <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">ancient India</a> (with the post-<a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">Rigvedic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vedic_civilization" title="Vedic civilization">Vedic civilization</a>), <a href="/wiki/Greater_Iran" title="Greater Iran">ancient Iran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> (with the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Dark_Ages" title="Greek Dark Ages">Greek Dark Ages</a>). In other regions of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> the Iron Age began in the 8th century BC in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halstatt_culture" title="Halstatt culture">Central Europe</a> and the 6th century BC in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Roman_Iron_Age" title="Pre-Roman Iron Age">Northern Europe</a>. The Near Eastern Iron Age is divided into two subsections, Iron I and Iron II. Iron I (1200–1000 BC) illustrates both continuity and discontinuity with the previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age" title="Late Bronze Age">Late Bronze Age</a>. There is no definitive cultural break between the 13th and 12th centuries BC throughout the entire region, although certain new features in the hill country, <a href="/wiki/Transjordan_(region)" title="Transjordan (region)">Transjordan</a> and coastal region may suggest the appearance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaean" title="Aramaean">Aramaean</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_People" title="Sea People">Sea People</a> groups. There is evidence, however, of strong continuity with Bronze Age culture, although as one moves later into Iron Age the culture begins to diverge more significantly from that of the late 2nd millennium.
</p><p>The Iron Age as an archaeological period is roughly defined as that part of the prehistory of a culture or region during which <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy" title="Ferrous metallurgy">ferrous metallurgy</a> was the dominant technology of metalworking.
</p><p>The characteristic of an Iron Age culture is the mass production of tools and weapons made from <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, typically alloys with a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> content between approximately 0.30% and 1.2% by weight.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
Only with the capability of the production of carbon steel does ferrous metallurgy result in tools or weapons that are equal or superior to <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>. The use of steel has been based as much on economics as on metallurgical advancements. Early steel was made by <a href="/wiki/Smelting#Early_iron_smelting" title="Smelting">smelting iron</a>.
</p><p>By convention, the Iron Age in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a> is taken to last from c. 1200 BC (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a>) to c. 550 BC (or <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Opis" title="Battle of Opis">539 BC</a>), roughly the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a> with <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>; the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-historical" title="Proto-historical">proto-historical</a> period. In Central and Western Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Iron</a> Age is taken to last from c. 800 BC to c. 1 BC, in <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Scandinavia" title="Iron Age Scandinavia">Northern</a> Europe from c. 500 BC to AD 800.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">China</a>, there is no recognizable prehistoric period characterized by ironworking, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_China" title="Bronze Age China">Bronze Age China</a> transitions almost directly into the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> of imperial China; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_Age_China" title="Iron Age China">"Iron Age"</a> in the context of China is sometimes used for the transitional period of c. 900 BC to 100 BC during which ferrous metallurgy was present even if not dominant.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_ferrous_metallurgy">Early ferrous metallurgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early ferrous metallurgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy#Iron_smelting_and_the_Iron_Age" title="Ferrous metallurgy">Ferrous metallurgy § Iron smelting and the Iron Age</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_iron_production" title="Ancient iron production">Ancient iron production</a></div>
<p>The earliest-known iron artifacts are nine small beads dated to 3200 BC, which were found in burials at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerzeh" title="Gerzeh">Gerzeh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>. They have been identified as <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">meteoric iron</a> shaped by careful hammering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Meteoric iron, a characteristic iron–nickel alloy, was used by various ancient peoples thousands of years before the Iron Age. Such iron, being in its native metallic state, required no <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> of ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Smelted iron appears sporadically in the archeological record from the middle <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. Whilst terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> is naturally abundant, the high temperatures required to smelting iron ore 1,250 °C (2,280 °F) placed it out of reach of common use until the end of the second millennium BC. Tin's low melting point of 231.9 °C (449.4 °F) and copper's relatively moderate melting point of 1,085 °C (1,985 °F) placed them within the capabilities of the Neolithic <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kilns</a>, which date back to 6000 BC and were able to produce temperatures greater than 900 °C (1,650 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IIIDorn2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IIIDorn2006-7">[7]</a></sup> In addition to specially designed furnaces, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_iron_production" title="Ancient iron production">ancient iron production</a> needed to develop complex procedures for the removal of impurities, the regulation of the admixture of carbon, and for hot-working to achieve a useful balance of hardness and strength in <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>.
</p><p>The earliest tentative evidence for iron-making is a small number of iron fragments with the appropriate amounts of carbon admixture found in the Proto-Hittite layers at <a href="/wiki/Kaman-Kaleh%C3%B6y%C3%BCk" title="Kaman-Kalehöyük">Kaman-Kalehöyük</a> and dated to 2200–2000 BC. Akanuma (2008) concludes that "The combination of carbon dating, archaeological context, and <a href="/wiki/Archaeometallurgy" title="Archaeometallurgy">archaeometallurgical</a> examination indicates that it is likely that the use of ironware made of steel had already begun in the third millennium BC in Central Anatolia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Souckova-Siegolová (2001) shows that iron implements were made in Central Anatolia in very limited quantities around 1800 BC and were in general use by elites, though not by commoners, during the New Hittite Empire (∼1400–1200 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, recent archaeological remains of iron-working in the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> Valley in India have been tentatively dated to 1800 BC. Tewari (2003) concludes that "knowledge of iron smelting and manufacturing of iron artifacts was well known in the Eastern Vindhyas and iron had been in use in the Central Ganga Plain, at least from the early second millennium BC".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tewari_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tewari-10">[10]</a></sup> By the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age" title="Middle Bronze Age">Middle Bronze Age</a> increasing numbers of smelted iron objects (distinguishable from meteoric iron by the lack of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> in the product) appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. African sites are turning up dates as early as 2000-1200 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millermintz_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-millermintz-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eze-Uzomaka_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eze-Uzomaka-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern archaeological evidence identifies the start of large-scale iron production in around 1200 BC, marking the end of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. Between 1200 BC and 1000 BC diffusion in the understanding of iron metallurgy and the use of iron objects was fast and far-flung. <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Snodgrass" title="Anthony Snodgrass">Anthony Snodgrass</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> suggests that a shortage of tin, as a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age Collapse</a> and trade disruptions in the Mediterranean around 1300 BC, forced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smith_(metalwork)" title="Smith (metalwork)">metalworkers</a> to seek an alternative to bronze. As evidence, many bronze implements were recycled into weapons during that time. More widespread use of iron led to improved steel-making technology at a lower cost. Thus, even when tin became available again, iron was cheaper, stronger and lighter, and forged iron implements superseded cast bronze tools permanently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">Ancient Near East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient Near East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Iron Age in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a> is believed to have begun with the discovery of iron smelting and smithing techniques in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> in the late <a href="/wiki/2nd_millennium_BC" title="2nd millennium BC">2nd millennium BC</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1300 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The earliest <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomery</a> smelting of iron is found at <a href="/wiki/Tell_Hammeh" title="Tell Hammeh">Tell Hammeh</a>, Jordan around 930 BC (determined from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C14_dating" title="C14 dating"><sup>14</sup>C dating</a>).
</p><p>The Early Iron Age in the Caucasus area is conventionally divided into two periods, Early Iron I, dated to around 1100 BC, and the Early Iron II phase from the tenth to ninth centuries BC. Many of the material culture traditions of the Late Bronze Age continued into the Early Iron Age. Thus, there is a sociocultural continuity during this transitional period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Iran, the earliest actual iron artifacts were unknown until the 9th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For Iran, the best studied archaeological site during this time period is <a href="/wiki/Teppe_Hasanlu" title="Teppe Hasanlu">Teppe Hasanlu</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Asia">West Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: West Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian" title="Mesopotamian">Mesopotamian</a> states of <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_Empire" title="Akkadian Empire">Akkad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>, the initial use of iron reaches far back, to perhaps 3000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1910CH_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1910CH-21">[21]</a></sup> One of the earliest smelted iron artifacts known was a dagger with an iron blade found in a <a href="/wiki/Hattians" title="Hattians">Hattic</a> tomb in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, dating from 2500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cowen_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cowen-22">[22]</a></sup> The widespread use of iron weapons which replaced bronze weapons rapidly disseminated throughout the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> (North Africa, southwest <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>) by the beginning of the 1st millennium BC.
</p><p>The development of iron smelting was once attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> of Anatolia during the Late Bronze Age.
As part of the Late Bronze Age-Early Iron Age, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a> saw the slow, comparatively continuous spread of iron-working technology in the region.
It was long held that the success of the Hittite Empire during the Late Bronze Age had been based on the advantages entailed by the "monopoly" on ironworking at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muhly,_James_D._2003_pp._179-180_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muhly,_James_D._2003_pp._179-180-23">[23]</a></sup> Accordingly, the invading <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">Sea Peoples</a> would have been responsible for spreading the knowledge through that region.
The view of such a "Hittite monopoly" has come under scrutiny and no longer represents a scholarly consensus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muhly,_James_D._2003_pp._179-180_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Muhly,_James_D._2003_pp._179-180-23">[23]</a></sup>
While there are some iron objects from Bronze Age Anatolia, the number is comparable to iron objects found in Egypt and other places of the same time period; and only a small number of these objects are weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Third_Intermediate_Period_of_Egypt" title="Third Intermediate Period of Egypt">Third Intermediate Period of Egypt</a></div>
<p>The Iron Age in Egyptian archaeology essentially corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Third_Intermediate_Period_of_Egypt" title="Third Intermediate Period of Egypt">Third Intermediate Period of Egypt</a>.
</p><p>Iron metal is singularly scarce in collections of Egyptian antiquities. Bronze remained the primary material there until the conquest by <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Neo-Assyrian Empire</a> in 671 BC. The explanation of this would seem to be that the relics are in most cases the paraphernalia of tombs, the funeral vessels and vases, and iron being considered an impure metal by the ancient Egyptians it was never used in their manufacture of these or for any religious purposes. It was attributed to Seth, the spirit of evil who according to Egyptian tradition governed the central deserts of Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1910CH_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1910CH-21">[21]</a></sup>
In the <a href="/wiki/Abusir" title="Abusir">Black Pyramid of Abusir</a>, dating before 2000 BC, <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Maspero" title="Gaston Maspero">Gaston Maspero</a> found some pieces of iron. In the funeral text of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepi_I" title="Pepi I">Pepi I</a>, the metal is mentioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1910CH_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1910CH-21">[21]</a></sup> A sword bearing the name of pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Merneptah" title="Merneptah">Merneptah</a> as well as a battle axe with an iron blade and gold-decorated bronze shaft were both found in the excavation of Ugarit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cowen_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-cowen-22">[22]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Tutankhamun%27s_meteoric_iron_dagger" title="Tutankhamun's meteoric iron dagger">dagger with an iron blade found in Tutankhamun's tomb</a>, 13th century BC, was recently examined and found to be of meteoric origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xxwalshx_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-xxwalshx-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pankobe_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-pankobe-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Europe" title="Iron Age Europe">Iron Age Europe</a></div>

More than 2,000 Iron Age <a href="/wiki/Hillforts_in_Britain" title="Hillforts in Britain">hillforts</a> are known in Britain.</div></div></div>
<p>In Europe, the Iron Age is the last stage of <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Europe" title="Prehistoric Europe">prehistoric Europe</a> and the first of the <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">protohistoric</a> periods, which initially means descriptions of a particular area by Greek and Roman writers. For much of Europe, the period came to an abrupt local end after conquest by the Romans, though ironworking remained the dominant technology until recent times. Elsewhere it may last until the early centuries AD, and either Christianization or a new conquest in the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy" title="Ferrous metallurgy">Iron working</a> was introduced to Europe in the late 11th century BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> probably from the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, and slowly spread northwards and westwards over the succeeding 500 years. The Iron Age did not start when iron first appeared in Europe but it began to replace bronze in the preparation of tools and weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It did not happen at the same time all around Europe; local cultural developments played a role in the transition to the Iron Age. For example, the Iron Age of <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Ireland" title="Prehistoric Ireland">Prehistoric Ireland</a> begins around 500 BC (when the Greek Iron Age had already ended) and finishes around AD 400. The widespread use of the technology of iron was implemented in Europe simultaneously with Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Collis_1989_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Collis_1989-31">[31]</a></sup> The prehistoric Iron Age in Central Europe divided into two periods based on historical events – <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> (early Iron Age) and <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène</a> (late Iron Age) cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Material cultures of Hallstatt and La Tène consist of 4 phases (A, B, C, D phases).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Iron Age in Europe is characterized by an elaboration of designs in weapons, implements, and utensils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1910CH_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1910CH-21">[21]</a></sup> These are no longer cast but hammered into shape, and decoration is elaborate and curvilinear rather than simple rectilinear; the forms and character of the ornamentation of the northern European weapons resemble in some respects Roman arms, while in other respects they are peculiar and evidently representative of northern art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cit%C3%A2nia_de_Briteiros" title="Citânia de Briteiros">Citania de Briterios</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es" title="Guimarães">Guimarães</a>, Portugal is one of the examples of archaeological sites of the Iron Age. This settlement (fortified villages) covered an area of 3.8 hectares (9.4 acres), and served as a Celtiberian stronghold against Roman invasions. İt dates more than 2500 years back. The site was researched by Francisco Martins Sarmento starting from 1874. A number of <a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">amphoras</a> (containers usually for wine or olive oil), coins, fragments of pottery, weapons, pieces of jewelry, as well as ruins of a bath and its <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">pedra formosa</i></span> (lit.<span style="white-space: nowrap;"> </span>'handsome stone') revealed here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Asia">Central Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Central Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Iron Age in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> began when iron objects appear among the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Saka</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> (China) between the 10th century BC and the 7th century BC, such as those found at the cemetery site of Chawuhukou.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pazyryk_culture" title="Pazyryk culture">Pazyryk culture</a> is an Iron Age <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_culture" title="Archaeological culture">archaeological culture</a> (c. 6th to 3rd centuries BC) identified by excavated artifacts and mummified humans found in the <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altay_Mountains" title="Altay Mountains">Altay Mountains</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_metallurgy_in_China#Iron" title="History of metallurgy in China">History of metallurgy in China § Iron</a></div>

<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><i>Dates are approximate, consult particular article for details</i></dd>
<dd><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#f2d97f; color:black;"> </span><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#ffd880; color:black;"> </span> Prehistoric (or Proto-historic) Iron Age <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#cccccc; color:black;"> </span> Historic Iron Age</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>In China, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_bronze_inscriptions" title="Chinese bronze inscriptions">Chinese bronze inscriptions</a> are found around 1200 BC, preceding the development of iron metallurgy, which was known by the 9th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Derevianko_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Derevianko-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>  Therefore, in China <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> had given way to history periodized by ruling dynasties by the start of iron use, so "Iron Age" is not typically used as to describe a period in Chinese history. Iron metallurgy reached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtse_River" title="Yangtse River">Yangtse Valley</a> toward the end of the 6th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham-42">[42]</a></sup> The few objects were found at <a href="/wiki/Changsha" title="Changsha">Changsha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>. The mortuary evidence suggests that the initial use of iron in <a href="/wiki/Lingnan" title="Lingnan">Lingnan</a> belongs to the mid-to-late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a> period (from about 350 BC). Important non-precious husi style metal finds include Iron tools found at the tomb at Guwei-cun of the 4th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>The techniques used in Lingnan are a combination of bivalve moulds of distinct southern tradition and the incorporation of piece mould technology from the <i>Zhongyuan</i>. The products of the combination of these two periods are bells, vessels, weapons and ornaments, and the sophisticated cast.
</p><p>An Iron Age culture of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> has tentatively been associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhang_Zhung_culture" title="Zhang Zhung culture">Zhang Zhung culture</a> described in early Tibetan writings.
</p><p><span class="anchor" id="Korea"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg/220px-Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg/330px-Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg/440px-Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Silla-Iron.armor-01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> chest and neck armour from the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Korea" title="National Museum of Korea">National Museum of Korea</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> (3rd century CE)</div></div></div>
<p>Iron objects were introduced to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a> through trade with chiefdoms and state-level societies in the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> area in the 4th century BC, just at the end of the Warring States Period but before the Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kim_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-kim-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-taylor-45">[45]</a></sup> Yoon proposes that iron was first introduced to chiefdoms located along North Korean river valleys that flow into the Yellow Sea such as the Cheongcheon and Taedong Rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Iron production quickly followed in the 2nd century BC, and iron implements came to be used by farmers by the 1st century in southern Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kim_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-kim-44">[44]</a></sup> The earliest known cast-iron axes in southern Korea are found in the <a href="/wiki/Geum_River" title="Geum River">Geum River</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_basin" title="River basin">basin</a>. The time that iron production begins is the same time that complex chiefdoms of <a href="/wiki/Samhan" title="Samhan">Proto-historic Korea</a> emerged. The complex chiefdoms were the precursors of early states such as <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gaya_confederacy" title="Gaya confederacy">Gaya</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-taylor-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Iron ingots were an important mortuary item and indicated the wealth or prestige of the deceased in this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="anchor" id="Japan"></span>
In Japan, iron items, such as tools, weapons, and decorative objects, are postulated to have entered Japan during the late <a href="/wiki/Yayoi_period" title="Yayoi period">Yayoi period</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 300 BC–AD 300)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keally_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-keally-49">[49]</a></sup> or the succeeding <a href="/wiki/Kofun_period" title="Kofun period">Kofun period</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> AD 250–538), most likely through contacts with the Korean Peninsula and China.
</p><p>Distinguishing characteristics of the Yayoi period include the appearance of new pottery styles and the start of intensive rice agriculture in paddy fields. Yayoi culture flourished in a geographic area from southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a> to northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>. The Kofun and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Asuka_period" title="Asuka period">Asuka periods</a> are sometimes referred to collectively as the <a href="/wiki/Yamato_period" title="Yamato period">Yamato period</a>; The word <i>kofun</i> is Japanese for the type of <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">burial mounds</a> dating from that era.
</p>

<dd><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#f2d97f; color:black;"> </span><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#ffd880; color:black;"> </span> Prehistoric (or Proto-historic) Iron Age <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#cccccc; color:black;"> </span> Historic Iron Age</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_in_India" title="Iron Age in India">Iron Age in India</a></div>
<p>Iron was being used in <a href="/wiki/Mundigak" title="Mundigak">Mundigak</a> to manufacture some items in the 3rd millennium BC such as a small copper/bronze bell with an iron clapper, a copper/bronze rod with two iron decorative buttons,. and a copper/bronze mirror handle with a decorative iron button.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Artefacts including small knives and blades have been discovered in the Indian state of <a href="/wiki/Telangana" title="Telangana">Telangana</a> which have been dated between 2,400 BC and 1800 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/History_of_metallurgy_in_the_Indian_subcontinent" title="History of metallurgy in the Indian subcontinent">history of metallurgy in the Indian subcontinent</a> began prior to the 3rd millennium BC. Archaeological sites in India, such as Malhar, Dadupur, Raja Nala Ka Tila, Lahuradewa, <a href="/wiki/Kosambi" title="Kosambi">Kosambi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jhusi" title="Jhusi">Jhusi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allahabad" title="Allahabad">Allahabad</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> show iron implements in the period 1800–1200 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tewari_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tewari-10">[10]</a></sup> As the evidence from the sites Raja Nala ka tila, Malhar suggest the use of Iron in c.1800/1700 BC. The extensive use of iron smelting is from <a href="/wiki/Sonbhadra_district" title="Sonbhadra district">Malhar</a> and its surrounding area. This site is assumed as the center for smelted bloomer iron to this area due to its location in the Karamnasa River and Ganga River. This site shows agricultural technology as iron implements sickles, nails, clamps, spearheads, etc, by at least c.1500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Archaeological excavations in Hyderabad show an Iron Age burial site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The beginning of the 1st millennium BC saw extensive developments in iron metallurgy in India. Technological advancement and mastery of iron metallurgy were achieved during this period of peaceful settlements. One ironworking centre in <a href="/wiki/East_India" title="East India">East India</a> has been dated to the first millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCP_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCP-55">[55]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_India" title="Southern India">Southern India</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Mysore" title="Mysore">Mysore</a>) iron appeared as early as 12th to 11th centuries BC; these developments were too early for any significant close contact with the northwest of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCP_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCP-55">[55]</a></sup> The Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upanishad" title="Upanishad">Upanishads</a> mention metallurgy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and the Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan" title="Mauryan">Mauryan</a> period saw advances in metallurgy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> As early as 300 BC, certainly by AD 200, high-quality steel was produced in southern India, by what would later be called the <a href="/wiki/Crucible_steel" title="Crucible steel">crucible technique</a>. In this system, high-purity wrought iron, charcoal, and glass were mixed in a crucible and heated until the iron melted and absorbed the carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>The protohistoric Early Iron Age in Sri Lanka lasted from 1000 BC to 600 BC. Radiocarbon evidence has been collected from <a href="/wiki/Anuradhapura" title="Anuradhapura">Anuradhapura</a> and Aligala shelter in <a href="/wiki/Sigiriya" title="Sigiriya">Sigiriya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The Anuradhapura settlement is recorded to extend 10 ha (25 acres) by 800 BC and grew to 50 ha (120 acres) by 700–600 BC to become a town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The skeletal remains of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Iron_Age" title="Early Iron Age">Early Iron Age</a> chief were excavated in Anaikoddai, <a href="/wiki/Jaffna" title="Jaffna">Jaffna</a>. The name 'Ko Veta' is engraved in Brahmi script on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(insignia)" title="Seal (insignia)">seal</a> buried with the skeleton and is assigned by the excavators to the 3rd century BC. Ko, meaning "King" in Tamil, is comparable to such names as Ko Atan and Ko Putivira occurring in contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmi" title="Brahmi">Brahmi</a> inscriptions in south India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> It is also speculated that Early Iron Age sites may exist in <a href="/wiki/Kandarodai" title="Kandarodai">Kandarodai</a>, Matota, <a href="/wiki/Pilapitiya" title="Pilapitiya">Pilapitiya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tissamaharama" title="Tissamaharama">Tissamaharama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia">Southeast Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Southeast Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lingling-o.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="496" data-file-width="496" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Lingling-o.JPG/200px-Lingling-o.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Lingling-o.JPG/300px-Lingling-o.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Lingling-o.JPG/400px-Lingling-o.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lingling-o.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lingling-o earrings from <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></div></div></div>
<div class="timeline-wrapper"><map name="timeline_9mcp9brzngtawwzi44q8425592oot43"><area alt="Tarumanagara" coords="621,44,735,65" href="/wiki/Tarumanagara" shape="rect" title="Tarumanagara"/><area alt="Buni culture" coords="505,35,608,56" href="/wiki/Buni_culture" shape="rect" title="Buni culture"/><area alt="Prehistory of Indonesia" coords="378,44,542,65" href="/wiki/Prehistory_of_Indonesia" shape="rect" title="Prehistory of Indonesia"/><area alt="History of the Philippines (900-1521)" coords="624,24,733,45" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(900%2D1521)" shape="rect" title="History of the Philippines (900-1521)"/><area alt="History of the Philippines" coords="489,14,625,35" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines" shape="rect" title="History of the Philippines"/><area alt="Igorot society" coords="403,24,517,45" href="/wiki/Igorot_society" shape="rect" title="Igorot society"/><area alt="Sa Huỳnh culture" coords="321,14,446,35" href="/wiki/Sa_Hu%E1%BB%B3nh_culture" shape="rect" title="Sa Huỳnh culture"/><area alt="Imperial Vietnam" coords="575,6,700,27" href="/wiki/Imperial_Vietnam" shape="rect" title="Imperial Vietnam"/><area alt="Óc Eo culture" coords="581,-5,695,16" href="/wiki/%C3%93c_Eo_culture" shape="rect" title="Óc Eo culture"/><area alt="Sa Huỳnh culture" coords="316,-6,452,15" href="/wiki/Sa_Hu%E1%BB%B3nh_culture" shape="rect" title="Sa Huỳnh culture"/></map><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/timeline/9mcp9brzngtawwzi44q8425592oot43.png" usemap="#timeline_9mcp9brzngtawwzi44q8425592oot43"/></div>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><i>Dates are approximate, consult particular article for details</i></dd>
<dd><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#ffd880; color:black;"> </span> Prehistoric (or <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">Proto-historic</a>) Iron Age <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="legend-color" style="background-color:#cccccc; color:black;"> </span> Historic Iron Age</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Archaeology in Thailand at sites Ban Don Ta Phet and Khao Sam Kaeo yielding metallic, stone, and glass artifacts stylistically associated with the Indian subcontinent suggest Indianization of Southeast Asia beginning in the 4th to 2nd centuries BC during the late Iron Age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sa_Huynh_culture" title="Sa Huynh culture">Sa Huynh culture</a> showed evidence of an extensive trade network. Sa Huynh beads were made from glass, carnelian, agate, olivine, zircon, gold and garnet; most of these materials were not local to the region and were most likely imported. Han-Dynasty-style bronze mirrors were also found in Sa Huynh sites. Conversely, Sa Huynh produced ear ornaments have been found in archaeological sites in Central Thailand, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Orchid_Island" title="Orchid Island">Orchid Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham2_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham2-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 211–217">: 211–217 </span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-Saharan_Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sub-Saharan Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iron_metallurgy_in_Africa" title="Iron metallurgy in Africa">Iron metallurgy in Africa</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nok_culture" title="Nok culture">Nok culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urewe" title="Urewe">Urewe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bantu_expansion" title="Bantu expansion">Bantu expansion</a></div>
<p>In Sub-Saharan Africa, where there was no continent-wide universal Bronze Age, the use of iron immediately succeeded the use of stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1910CH_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB1910CH-21">[21]</a></sup>
Metallurgy was characterized by the absence of a Bronze Age, and the transition from stone to iron in tool substances. Early evidence for iron technology in Sub-Saharan Africa can be found at sites such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KM2_and_KM3_sites" title="KM2 and KM3 sites">KM2 and KM3</a> in northwest <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> and parts of Nigeria and the Central African Republic. <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a> was one of the relatively few places in Africa to have a sustained Bronze Age along with <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and much of the rest of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="784" data-file-width="677" decoding="async" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png/200px-East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png/300px-East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png/400px-East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:East%26southern_africa_early_iron_age.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Iron Age finds in East and Southern Africa, corresponding to the early 1st millennium Bantu expansion</div></div></div>
<p>Very early copper and bronze working sites in <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> may date to as early as 1500 BC. There is also evidence of iron metallurgy in Termit, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> from around this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millermintz_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-millermintz-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a> was a major manufacturer and exporter of iron after the expulsion of the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-fifth Dynasty of Egypt">Nubian dynasty</a> from Egypt by the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Assyrians</a> in the 7th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though there is some uncertainty, some archaeologists believe that iron metallurgy was developed independently in sub-Saharan West Africa, separately from Eurasia and neighboring parts of North and Northeast Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eggert_2014_51–59_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eggert_2014_51–59-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl-2009_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-2009-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Archaeological sites containing iron smelting furnaces and slag have also been excavated at sites in the <a href="/wiki/Nsukka" title="Nsukka">Nsukka</a> region of southeast <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Igbo_people" title="Igbo people">Igboland</a>: dating to 2000 BC at the site of <a href="/wiki/Lejja" title="Lejja">Lejja</a> (Eze-Uzomaka 2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eze-Uzomaka_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eze-Uzomaka-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl-2009_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-2009-71">[71]</a></sup> and to 750 BC and at the site of <a href="/wiki/Opi_(archaeological_site)" title="Opi (archaeological site)">Opi</a> (Holl 2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl-2009_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-2009-71">[71]</a></sup> The site of Gbabiri (in the Central African Republic) has yielded evidence of iron metallurgy, from a reduction furnace and blacksmith workshop; with earliest dates of 896-773 BC and 907-796 BC respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eggert_2014_53–54_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eggert_2014_53–54-72">[72]</a></sup> Similarly, smelting in bloomery-type furnaces appear in the <a href="/wiki/Nok_culture" title="Nok culture">Nok culture</a> of central Nigeria by about 550 BC and possibly a few centuries earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eggert_2014_51–59_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eggert_2014_51–59-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eggert_2014_53–54_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eggert_2014_53–54-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Iron and copper working in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> spread south and east from Central Africa in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Bantu_expansion" title="Bantu expansion">Bantu expansion</a>, from the Cameroon region to the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">African Great Lakes</a> in the 3rd century BC, reaching the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape</a> around AD 400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millermintz_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-millermintz-11">[11]</a></sup>
However, iron working may have been practiced in Central Africa as early as the 3rd millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
Instances of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a> based on complex preheating principles were found to be in production around the 1st century AD in northwest <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
