<p><b>Medical imaging</b> is the technique and process of <a href="/wiki/Imaging" title="Imaging">imaging</a> the interior of a body for clinical analysis and medical intervention, as well as visual representation of the function of some organs or tissues (<a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>). Medical imaging seeks to reveal internal structures hidden by the skin and bones, as well as to diagnose and treat <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>. Medical imaging also establishes a database of normal <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> to make it possible to identify abnormalities. Although imaging of removed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> can be performed for medical reasons, such procedures are usually considered part of <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathology</a> instead of medical imaging.
</p><p>Measurement and recording techniques that are not primarily designed to produce <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalography</a> (EEG), <a href="/wiki/Magnetoencephalography" title="Magnetoencephalography">magnetoencephalography</a> (MEG), <a href="/wiki/Electrocardiography" title="Electrocardiography">electrocardiography</a> (ECG), and others, represent other technologies that produce data susceptible to representation as a parameter graph versus time or <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a> that contain data about the measurement locations. In a limited comparison, these technologies can be considered forms of medical imaging in another discipline.
</p><p>As of 2010, 5 billion medical imaging studies had been conducted worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Radiation exposure from medical imaging in 2006 made up about 50% of total ionizing radiation exposure in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science09_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science09-2">[2]</a></sup> Medical imaging equipment is manufactured using technology from the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_industry" title="Semiconductor industry">semiconductor industry</a>, including <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a> chips, <a href="/wiki/Power_semiconductor_device" title="Power semiconductor device">power semiconductor devices</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensors" title="Sensors">sensors</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_sensors" title="Image sensors">image sensors</a> (particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMOS_sensor" title="CMOS sensor">CMOS sensors</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosensors" title="Biosensors">biosensors</a>, and processors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microcontrollers" title="Microcontrollers">microcontrollers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microprocessors" title="Microprocessors">microprocessors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processors" title="Digital signal processors">digital signal processors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_processor" title="Media processor">media processors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System-on-chip" title="System-on-chip">system-on-chip</a> devices. As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, annual shipments of medical imaging chips amount to 46<span class="nowrap"> </span>million units and <span style="white-space: nowrap">$1.1 billion</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Medical imaging is often perceived to designate the set of techniques that noninvasively produce images of the internal aspect of the body. In this restricted sense, medical imaging can be seen as the solution of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> <a href="/wiki/Inverse_problem" title="Inverse problem">inverse problems</a>. This means that cause (the properties of living tissue) is inferred from effect (the observed signal). In the case of <a href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasound" title="Medical ultrasound">medical ultrasound</a>, the probe consists of ultrasonic pressure waves and echoes that go inside the tissue to show the internal structure. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Projectional_radiography" title="Projectional radiography">projectional radiography</a>, the probe uses <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">radiation</a>, which is absorbed at different rates by different tissue types such as bone, muscle, and fat.
</p><p>The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasiveness_of_surgical_procedures" title="Invasiveness of surgical procedures">noninvasive</a>" is used to denote a procedure where no instrument is introduced into a patient's body, which is the case for most imaging techniques used.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3117" data-file-width="1792" decoding="async" height="383" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg/220px-X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg/330px-X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg/440px-X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:X-ray_General_Illustration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plain x-ray of the wrist and hand</div></div></div>
<p>In the clinical context, "invisible light" medical imaging is generally equated to <a href="/wiki/Radiology" title="Radiology">radiology</a> or "clinical imaging". "Visible light" medical imaging involves digital video or still pictures that can be seen without special equipment. Dermatology and wound care are two modalities that use visible light imagery. Interpretation of medical images is generally undertaken by a physician specialising in radiology known as a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiologist" title="Radiologist">radiologist</a></i>; however, this may be undertaken by any healthcare professional who is trained and certified in radiological clinical evaluation. Increasingly interpretation is being undertaken by non-physicians, for example <a href="/wiki/Radiographer" title="Radiographer">radiographers</a> frequently train in interpretation as part of expanded practice. Diagnostic <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a> designates the technical aspects of medical imaging and in particular the acquisition of medical images. The radiographer (also known as a radiologic technologist) is usually responsible for acquiring medical images of diagnostic quality; although other professionals may train in this area, notably some radiological interventions performed by radiologists are done so without a radiographer.
</p><p>As a field of scientific investigation, medical imaging constitutes a sub-discipline of <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_physics" title="Medical physics">medical physics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> depending on the context: Research and development in the area of instrumentation, image acquisition (e.g., radiography), modeling and quantification are usually the preserve of biomedical engineering, medical physics, and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>; Research into the application and interpretation of medical images is usually the preserve of radiology and the medical sub-discipline relevant to medical condition or area of medical science (<a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiology" title="Cardiology">cardiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, etc.) under investigation. Many of the techniques developed for medical imaging also have <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industry_(manufacturing)" title="Industry (manufacturing)">industrial</a> applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiography">Radiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">Radiography</a></div>
<p>Two forms of radiographic images are in use in medical imaging. Projection radiography and fluoroscopy, with the latter being useful for catheter guidance. These 2D techniques are still in wide use despite the advance of 3D tomography due to the low cost, high resolution, and depending on the application, lower radiation dosages with 2D technique. This imaging modality utilizes a wide beam of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X_rays" title="X rays">x rays</a> for image acquisition and is the first imaging technique available in modern medicine.
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Fluoroscopy" title="Fluoroscopy">Fluoroscopy</a></i> produces real-time images of internal structures of the body in a similar fashion to <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a>, but employs a constant input of x-rays, at a lower dose rate. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contrast_media" title="Contrast media">Contrast media</a>, such as barium, iodine, and air are used to visualize internal organs as they work. Fluoroscopy is also used in image-guided procedures when constant feedback during a procedure is required. An image receptor is required to convert the radiation into an image after it has passed through the area of interest. Early on this was a fluorescing screen, which gave way to an Image Amplifier (IA) which was a large vacuum tube that had the receiving end coated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cesium_iodide" title="Cesium iodide">cesium iodide</a>, and a mirror at the opposite end. Eventually the mirror was replaced with a TV camera.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Projectional_radiography" title="Projectional radiography">Projectional radiographs</a></i>, more commonly known as x-rays, are often used to determine the type and extent of a fracture as well as for detecting pathological changes in the lungs. With the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio-opaque" title="Radio-opaque">radio-opaque</a> contrast media, such as <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, they can also be used to visualize the structure of the stomach and intestines – this can help diagnose ulcers or certain types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_cancer" title="Colon cancer">colon cancer</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_resonance_imaging">Magnetic resonance imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Magnetic resonance imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">Magnetic resonance imaging</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="895" data-file-width="907" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg/220px-MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg/330px-MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg/440px-MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MRI_Scan_General_Illustration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One frame of an MRI scan of the head showing the eyes and brain.</div></div></div>
<p>A magnetic resonance imaging instrument (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI_scanner" title="MRI scanner">MRI scanner</a>), or "nuclear magnetic resonance (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR" title="NMR">NMR</a>) imaging" scanner as it was originally known, uses powerful magnets to polarize and excite <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> nuclei (i.e., single <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>) of water molecules in human tissue, producing a detectable signal which is spatially encoded, resulting in images of the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownCheng2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrownCheng2014-5">[5]</a></sup> The MRI machine emits a radio frequency (RF) pulse at the resonant frequency of the hydrogen atoms on water molecules. Radio frequency antennas ("RF coils") send the pulse to the area of the body to be examined. The RF pulse is absorbed by protons, causing their direction with respect to the primary magnetic field to change. When the RF pulse is turned off, the protons "relax" back to alignment with the primary magnet and emit radio-waves in the process. This radio-frequency emission from the hydrogen-atoms on water is what is detected and reconstructed into an image. The resonant frequency of a spinning magnetic dipole (of which protons are one example) is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larmor_frequency" title="Larmor frequency">Larmor frequency</a> and is determined by the strength of the main magnetic field and the chemical environment of the nuclei of interest. MRI uses three <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic fields</a>: a very strong (typically 1.5 to 3 <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">teslas</a>) static magnetic field to polarize the hydrogen nuclei, called the primary field; gradient fields that can be modified to vary in space and time (on the order of 1 kHz) for spatial encoding, often simply called gradients; and a spatially homogeneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio-frequency" title="Radio-frequency">radio-frequency</a> (RF) field for manipulation of the hydrogen nuclei to produce measurable signals, collected through an <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">RF antenna</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">CT</a>, MRI traditionally creates a two-dimensional image of a thin "slice" of the body and is therefore considered a <a href="/wiki/Tomography" title="Tomography">tomographic</a> imaging technique. Modern MRI instruments are capable of producing images in the form of 3D blocks, which may be considered a generalization of the single-slice, tomographic, concept. Unlike CT, MRI does not involve the use of <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a> and is therefore not associated with the same health hazards. For example, because MRI has only been in use since the early 1980s, there are no known long-term effects of exposure to strong static fields (this is the subject of some debate; see 'Safety' in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">MRI</a>) and therefore there is no limit to the number of scans to which an individual can be subjected, in contrast with <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">CT</a>. However, there are well-identified health risks associated with tissue heating from exposure to the RF field and the presence of implanted devices in the body, such as pacemakers. These risks are strictly controlled as part of the design of the instrument and the scanning protocols used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Because CT and MRI are sensitive to different tissue properties, the appearances of the images obtained with the two techniques differ markedly. In CT, X-rays must be blocked by some form of dense tissue to create an image, so the image quality when looking at soft tissues will be poor. In MRI, while any nucleus with a net nuclear spin can be used, the proton of the hydrogen atom remains the most widely used, especially in the clinical setting, because it is so ubiquitous and returns a large signal. This nucleus, present in water molecules, allows the excellent soft-tissue contrast achievable with MRI.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A number of different pulse sequences can be used for specific MRI diagnostic imaging (multiparametric MRI or mpMRI). It is possible to differentiate tissue characteristics by combining two or more of the following imaging sequences, depending on the information being sought: T1-weighted (T1-MRI), T2-weighted (T2-MRI), diffusion weighted imaging (DWI-MRI), dynamic contrast enhancement (DCE-MRI), and spectroscopy (MRI-S). For example, imaging of prostate tumors is better accomplished using T2-MRI and DWI-MRI than T2-weighted imaging alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The number of applications of mpMRI for detecting disease in various organs continues to expand, including <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> studies, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breast_tumor" title="Breast tumor">breast tumors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_tumor" title="Pancreatic tumor">pancreatic tumors</a>, and assessing the effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascular" title="Vascular">vascular</a> disruption agents on cancer tumors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_medicine">Nuclear medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nuclear medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">Nuclear medicine</a></div>
<p>Nuclear medicine encompasses both diagnostic imaging and treatment of disease, and may also be referred to as molecular medicine or molecular imaging and therapeutics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Nuclear medicine uses certain properties of isotopes and the energetic particles emitted from radioactive material to diagnose or treat various pathology. Different from the typical concept of anatomic radiology, nuclear medicine enables assessment of physiology. This function-based approach to medical evaluation has useful applications in most subspecialties, notably oncology, neurology, and cardiology. <i><a href="/wiki/Gamma_camera" title="Gamma camera">Gamma cameras</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PET_scanner" title="PET scanner">PET scanners</a></i> are used in e.g. scintigraphy, SPECT and PET to detect regions of biologic activity that may be associated with a disease. Relatively short-lived <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m" title="Technetium-99m"><sup>99m</sup>Tc</a> is administered to the patient. Isotopes are often preferentially absorbed by biologically active tissue in the body, and can be used to identify tumors or <a href="/wiki/Fracture" title="Fracture">fracture</a> points in bone. Images are acquired after collimated photons are detected by a crystal that gives off a light signal, which is in turn amplified and converted into count data.
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Scintigraphy" title="Scintigraphy">Scintigraphy</a></i> ("scint") is a form of diagnostic test wherein <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> are taken internally, for example, intravenously or orally. Then, gamma cameras capture and form two-dimensional<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> images from the radiation emitted by the radiopharmaceuticals.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Single-photon_emission_computed_tomography" title="Single-photon emission computed tomography">SPECT</a></i> is a 3D tomographic technique that uses gamma camera data from many projections and can be reconstructed in different planes. A dual detector head gamma camera combined with a CT scanner, which provides localization of functional SPECT data, is termed a SPECT-CT camera, and has shown utility in advancing the field of molecular imaging. In most other medical imaging modalities, energy is passed through the body and the reaction or result is read by detectors. In SPECT imaging, the patient is injected with a radioisotope, most commonly Thallium 201TI, Technetium 99mTC, Iodine 123I, and Gallium 67Ga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dhawan_P_2003_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dhawan_P_2003-12">[12]</a></sup> The radioactive gamma rays are emitted through the body as the natural decaying process of these isotopes takes place. The emissions of the gamma rays are captured by detectors that surround the body. This essentially means that the human is now the source of the radioactivity, rather than the medical imaging devices such as  X-ray or CT.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">Positron emission tomography</a></i> (PET) uses coincidence detection to image functional processes. Short-lived positron emitting isotope, such as <a href="/wiki/Fluorine-18" title="Fluorine-18"><sup>18</sup>F</a>, is incorporated with an organic substance such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, creating F18-fluorodeoxyglucose, which can be used as a marker of metabolic utilization. Images of activity distribution throughout the body can show rapidly growing tissue, like tumor, metastasis, or infection. PET images can be viewed in comparison to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">computed tomography</a> scans to determine an anatomic correlate. Modern scanners may integrate PET, allowing <a href="/wiki/PET-CT" title="PET-CT">PET-CT</a>, or <a href="/wiki/PET-MRI" title="PET-MRI">PET-MRI</a> to optimize the image reconstruction involved with positron imaging. This is performed on the same equipment without physically moving the patient off of the gantry. The resultant hybrid of functional and anatomic imaging information is a useful tool in non-invasive diagnosis and patient management.</li></ul>
<p>Fiduciary markers are used in a wide range of medical imaging applications. Images of the same subject produced with two different imaging systems may be correlated (called image registration) by placing a fiduciary marker in the area imaged by both systems.  In this case, a marker which is visible in the images produced by both imaging modalities must be used.  By this method, functional information from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPECT" title="SPECT">SPECT</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> can be related to anatomical information provided by <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Similarly, fiducial points established during MRI can be correlated with brain images generated by <a href="/wiki/Magnetoencephalography#Magnetic_source_imaging" title="Magnetoencephalography">magnetoencephalography</a> to localize the source of brain activity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ultrasound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasound" title="Medical ultrasound">Medical ultrasound</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="970" data-file-width="1552" decoding="async" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg/220px-Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg/330px-Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg/440px-Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abdominal_Ultrasound_General_Illustration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ultrasound image showing the liver, gallbladder and common bile duct.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasound" title="Medical ultrasound">Medical ultrasound</a> uses high frequency <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> sound waves in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">megahertz</a> range that are reflected by tissue to varying degrees to produce (up to 3D) images. This is commonly associated with <a href="/wiki/Obstetric_ultrasonography" title="Obstetric ultrasonography">imaging the fetus</a> in pregnant women. Uses of ultrasound are much broader, however.  Other important uses include imaging the abdominal organs, heart, breast, muscles, tendons, arteries and veins.  While it may provide less anatomical detail than techniques such as CT or MRI, it has several advantages which make it ideal in numerous situations, in particular that it studies the function of moving structures in real-time, emits no <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a>, and contains <a href="/wiki/Speckle_tracking_echocardiography" title="Speckle tracking echocardiography">speckle</a> that can be used in <a href="/wiki/Elastography" title="Elastography">elastography</a>. Ultrasound is also used as a popular research tool for capturing raw data, that can be made available through an <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound_research_interface" title="Ultrasound research interface">ultrasound research interface</a>, for the purpose of tissue characterization and implementation of new image processing techniques. The concepts of ultrasound differ from other medical imaging modalities in the fact that it is operated by the transmission and receipt of sound waves. The high frequency sound waves are sent into the tissue and depending on the composition of the different tissues; the signal will be attenuated and returned at separate intervals.  A path of reflected sound waves in a multilayered structure can be defined by an input acoustic impedance (ultrasound sound wave) and the Reflection and transmission coefficients of the relative structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dhawan_P_2003_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dhawan_P_2003-12">[12]</a></sup> It is very safe to use and does not appear to cause any adverse effects. It is also relatively inexpensive and quick to perform. Ultrasound scanners can be taken to critically ill patients in intensive care units, avoiding the danger caused while moving the patient to the radiology department. The real-time moving image obtained can be used to guide drainage and biopsy procedures. Doppler capabilities on modern scanners allow the blood flow in arteries and veins to be assessed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elastography">Elastography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elastography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elastography" title="Elastography">Elastography</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Figure1TI.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="864" data-file-width="696" decoding="async" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Figure1TI.png/220px-Figure1TI.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Figure1TI.png/330px-Figure1TI.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Figure1TI.png/440px-Figure1TI.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Figure1TI.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3D tactile image (C) is composed from 2D pressure maps (B) recorded in the process of tissue phantom examination (A).</div></div></div>
<p>Elastography is a relatively new imaging modality that maps the elastic properties of soft tissue. This modality emerged in the last two decades. Elastography is useful in medical diagnoses, as elasticity can discern healthy from unhealthy tissue for specific organs/growths. For example, cancerous tumours will often be harder than the surrounding tissue, and diseased livers are stiffer than healthy ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarv_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarv-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> There are several elastographic techniques based on the use of ultrasound, magnetic resonance imaging and tactile imaging. The wide clinical use of ultrasound elastography is a result of the implementation of technology in clinical ultrasound machines. Main branches of ultrasound elastography include Quasistatic Elastography/Strain Imaging, Shear Wave Elasticity Imaging (SWEI), Acoustic Radiation Force Impulse imaging (ARFI), Supersonic Shear Imaging (SSI), and Transient Elastography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarv_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sarv-15">[15]</a></sup> In the last decade a steady increase of activities in the field of elastography is observed demonstrating successful application of the technology in various areas of medical diagnostics and treatment monitoring.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photoacoustic_imaging">Photoacoustic imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Photoacoustic imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoacoustic_imaging_in_biomedicine" title="Photoacoustic imaging in biomedicine">Photoacoustic imaging in biomedicine</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoacoustic_imaging_in_biomedicine" title="Photoacoustic imaging in biomedicine">Photoacoustic imaging</a> is a recently developed hybrid biomedical imaging modality based on the photoacoustic effect. It combines the advantages of optical absorption contrast with an ultrasonic spatial resolution for deep imaging in (optical) diffusive or quasi-diffusive regime. Recent studies have shown that photoacoustic imaging can be used in vivo for tumor angiogenesis monitoring, blood oxygenation mapping, functional brain imaging, and skin melanoma detection, etc.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomography">Tomography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tomography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1123" data-file-width="794" decoding="async" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png/220px-TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png/330px-TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png/440px-TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TomographyPrinciple_Illustration.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Basic principle of <a href="/wiki/Tomography" title="Tomography">tomography</a>: superposition free tomographic cross sections S<sub>1</sub> and S<sub>2</sub> compared with the (not tomographic) projected image P</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tomography" title="Tomography">Tomography</a> is the imaging by sections or sectioning. The main such methods in medical imaging are:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_computed_tomography" title="X-ray computed tomography">X-ray computed tomography</a> (CT), or Computed Axial Tomography (CAT) scan, is a helical tomography technique (latest generation), which traditionally produces a 2D image of the structures in a thin section of the body. In CT, a beam of X-rays spins around an object being examined and is picked up by sensitive radiation detectors after having penetrated the object from multiple angles. A computer then analyses the information received from the scanner's detectors and constructs a detailed image of the object and its contents using the mathematical principles laid out in the <a href="/wiki/Radon_transform" title="Radon transform">Radon transform</a>. It has a greater <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a> dose burden than projection radiography; repeated scans must be limited to avoid health effects. CT is based on the same principles as X-Ray projections but in this case, the patient is enclosed in a surrounding ring of detectors assigned with 500–1000 scintillation detectors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dhawan_P_2003_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dhawan_P_2003-12">[12]</a></sup> (fourth-generation X-Ray CT scanner geometry). Previously in older generation scanners, the X-Ray beam was paired by a translating source and detector. Computed tomography has almost completely replaced <a href="/wiki/Focal_plane_tomography" title="Focal plane tomography">focal plane tomography</a> in X-ray tomography imaging.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">Positron emission tomography</a> (PET) also used in conjunction with computed tomography, <a href="/wiki/PET-CT" title="PET-CT">PET-CT</a>, and magnetic resonance imaging <a href="/wiki/PET-MRI" title="PET-MRI">PET-MRI</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">Magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI) commonly produces tomographic images of cross-sections of the body. (See separate MRI section in this article.)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Echocardiography">Echocardiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Echocardiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Echocardiography" title="Echocardiography">Echocardiography</a></div>
<p>When ultrasound is used to image the heart it is referred to as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echocardiogram" title="Echocardiogram">echocardiogram</a>.  Echocardiography allows detailed structures of the heart, including chamber size, heart function, the valves of the heart, as well as the pericardium (the sac around the heart) to be seen.  Echocardiography uses 2D, 3D, and <a href="/wiki/Doppler_echocardiography" title="Doppler echocardiography">Doppler</a> imaging to create pictures of the heart and visualize the blood flowing through each of the four heart valves. Echocardiography is widely used in an array of patients ranging from those experiencing symptoms, such as shortness of breath or chest pain, to those undergoing cancer treatments. Transthoracic ultrasound has been proven to be safe for patients of all ages, from infants to the elderly, without risk of harmful side effects or radiation, differentiating it from other imaging modalities. Echocardiography is one of the most commonly used imaging modalities in the world due to its portability and use in a variety of applications. In emergency situations, echocardiography is quick, easily accessible, and able to be performed at the bedside, making it the modality of choice for many physicians.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_particle_imaging">Magnetic particle imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Magnetic particle imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_particle_imaging" title="Magnetic particle imaging">Magnetic particle imaging</a></div><p>Using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superparamagnetic_Iron_Oxide_Nanoparticles" title="Superparamagnetic Iron Oxide Nanoparticles">superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles</a>, magnetic particle imaging (<a href="/wiki/Magnetic_particle_imaging" title="Magnetic particle imaging">MPI</a>) is a developing diagnostic imaging technique used for tracking <a href="/wiki/Superparamagnetism" title="Superparamagnetism">superparamagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> nanoparticles. <p>The amount of data obtained in a single MR or CT scan is very extensive. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_three-dimensional_images">Creation of three-dimensional images</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Creation of three-dimensional images">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volume_rendering" title="Volume rendering">Volume rendering</a> techniques have been developed to enable CT, MRI and ultrasound scanning software to produce 3D images for the physician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref3-23">[23]</a></sup> Traditionally CT and MRI scans produced 2D static output on film. To produce 3D images, many scans are made and then <a href="/wiki/Medical_image_computing" title="Medical image computing">combined by computers</a> to produce a 3D model, which can then be manipulated by the physician. <a href="/wiki/3D_ultrasound" title="3D ultrasound">3D ultrasounds</a> are produced using a somewhat similar technique.
In diagnosing disease of the viscera of the abdomen, ultrasound is particularly sensitive on imaging of biliary tract, urinary tract and female reproductive organs (ovary, fallopian tubes).  As for example, diagnosis of gallstone by dilatation of common bile duct and stone in the common bile duct.
With the ability to visualize important structures in great detail, 3D visualization methods are a valuable resource for the diagnosis and surgical treatment of many pathologies. It was a key resource for the famous, but ultimately unsuccessful attempt by Singaporean surgeons to separate Iranian twins <a href="/wiki/Ladan_and_Laleh_Bijani" title="Ladan and Laleh Bijani">Ladan and Laleh Bijani</a> in 2003. The 3D equipment was used previously for similar operations with great success.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DICOM">DICOM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: DICOM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/DICOM" title="DICOM">Digital Imaging and Communication in Medicine (DICOM)</a> Standard is used globally to store, exchange, and transmit medical images. The DICOM Standard incorporates protocols for imaging techniques such as radiography, computed tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), ultrasound, and radiation therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compression_of_medical_images">Compression of medical images</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Compression of medical images">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Medical imaging techniques produce very large amounts of data, especially from CT, MRI and PET modalities.  As a result, storage and communications of electronic image data are prohibitive without the use of compression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/JPEG_2000" title="JPEG 2000">JPEG 2000</a> image compression is used by the <a href="/wiki/DICOM" title="DICOM">DICOM</a> standard for storage and transmission of medical images.  The cost and feasibility of accessing large image data sets over low or various bandwidths are further addressed by use of another DICOM standard, called <a href="/wiki/JPIP" title="JPIP">JPIP</a>, to enable efficient streaming of the <a href="/wiki/JPEG_2000" title="JPEG 2000">JPEG 2000</a> compressed image data.
</p>

Cloud computing offers the imaging department of the future the tools to manage data much more intelligently."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Applied_Radiology1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Applied_Radiology1-31">[31]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_pharmaceutical_clinical_trials">Use in pharmaceutical clinical trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Use in pharmaceutical clinical trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medical imaging has become a major tool in clinical trials since it enables rapid diagnosis with visualization and quantitative assessment.
</p><p>A typical <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trial</a> goes through multiple phases and can take up to eight years. <a href="/wiki/Clinical_endpoint" title="Clinical endpoint">Clinical endpoints</a> or outcomes are used to determine whether the therapy is safe and effective. Once a patient reaches the endpoint, he or she is generally excluded from further experimental interaction. Trials that rely solely on <a href="/wiki/Clinical_endpoint" title="Clinical endpoint">clinical endpoints</a> are very costly as they have long durations and tend to need large numbers of patients.
</p><p>In contrast to clinical endpoints, <a href="/wiki/Surrogate_endpoint" title="Surrogate endpoint">surrogate endpoints</a> have been shown to cut down the time required to confirm whether a drug has clinical benefits. Imaging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomarkers" title="Biomarkers">biomarkers</a> (a characteristic that is objectively measured by an imaging technique, which is used as an indicator of pharmacological response to a therapy) and surrogate endpoints have shown to facilitate the use of small group sizes, obtaining quick results with good statistical power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hajnal_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-hajnal-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Imaging is able to reveal subtle change that is indicative of the progression of therapy that may be missed out by more subjective, traditional approaches.  Statistical bias is reduced as the findings are evaluated without any direct patient contact.
</p><p>Imaging techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> (PET) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI) are routinely used in oncology and neuroscience areas,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> For example, measurement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumour" title="Tumour">tumour</a> shrinkage is a commonly used surrogate endpoint in solid tumour response evaluation.  This allows for faster and more objective assessment of the effects of anticancer drugs.  In <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">MRI</a> scans of the entire brain can accurately assess the rate of hippocampal atrophy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> while PET scans can measure the brain's metabolic activity by measuring regional glucose metabolism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hajnal_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-hajnal-32">[32]</a></sup> and beta-amyloid plaques using tracers such as <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_compound_B" title="Pittsburgh compound B">Pittsburgh compound B</a> (PiB).  Historically less use has been made of quantitative medical imaging in other areas of drug development although interest is growing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>An imaging-based trial will usually be made up of three components:
</p>
<ol><li>A realistic imaging protocol.  The protocol is an outline that standardizes (as far as practically possible) the way in which the images are acquired using the various modalities (<a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">PET</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPECT" title="SPECT">SPECT</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">CT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a>).  It covers the specifics in which images are to be stored, processed and evaluated.</li>
<li>An imaging centre that is responsible for collecting the images, perform quality control and provide tools for data storage, distribution and analysis.  It is important for images acquired at different time points are displayed in a standardised format to maintain the reliability of the evaluation.  Certain specialised imaging contract research organizations provide end to end medical imaging services, from protocol design and site management through to data quality assurance and image analysis.</li>
<li>Clinical sites that recruit patients to generate the images to send back to the imaging centre.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shielding">Shielding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Shielding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">Lead</a> is the main material used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shielding_(radiography)" title="Shielding (radiography)">radiographic shielding</a> against scattered X-rays.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a>, there is <a href="/wiki/MRI_RF_shielding" title="MRI RF shielding">MRI RF shielding</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_shielding" title="Magnetic shielding">magnetic shielding</a> to prevent external disturbance of image quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_protection">Privacy protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Privacy protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medical imaging are generally covered by laws of <a href="/wiki/Medical_privacy" title="Medical privacy">medical privacy</a>. For example, in the United States the <a href="/wiki/Health_Insurance_Portability_and_Accountability_Act" title="Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act">Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act</a> (HIPAA) sets restrictions for health care providers on utilizing <a href="/wiki/Protected_health_information" title="Protected health information">protected health information</a>, which is any individually identifiable information relating to the past, present, or future physical or mental health of any individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> While there has not been any definitive legal decision in the matter, at least one study has indicated that medical imaging may contain biometric information that can uniquely identify a person, and so may qualify as PHI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The UK General Medical Council's ethical guidelines indicate that the Council does not require consent prior to secondary uses of X-ray images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Organizations in the medical imaging industry include manufacturers of imaging equipment, freestanding radiology facilities, and hospitals.
</p><p>The global market for manufactured devices was estimated at $5 billion in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Notable manufacturers as of 2012 included <a href="/wiki/Fujifilm" title="Fujifilm">Fujifilm</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">GE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens_Healthineers" title="Siemens Healthineers">Siemens Healthineers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shimadzu_Corp." title="Shimadzu Corp.">Shimadzu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carestream_Health" title="Carestream Health">Carestream Health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hologic" title="Hologic">Hologic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Esaote" title="Esaote">Esaote</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In 2016, the manufacturing industry was characterized as oligopolistic and mature; new entrants included in <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neusoft" title="Neusoft">Neusoft Medical</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, as estimate as of 2015 places the US market for imaging scans at about $100b, with 60% occurring in hospitals and 40% occurring in freestanding clinics, such as the <a href="/wiki/RadNet" title="RadNet">RadNet</a> chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright">Copyright</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Copyright">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As per chapter 300 of the <i>Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices</i>, "the Office will not register works produced by a machine or mere mechanical process that operates randomly or automatically without any creative input or intervention from a human author" including "Medical imaging produced by x-rays, ultrasounds, magnetic resonance imaging, or other diagnostic equipment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> This position differs from the broad copyright protections afforded to photographs. While the Copyright Compendium is an agency statutory interpretation and not legally binding, courts are likely to give deference to it if they find it reasonable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Yet, there is no U.S. federal case law directly addressing the issue of the copyrightability of x-ray images.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivatives">Derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1994" data-file-width="2333" decoding="async" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg/280px-Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg/420px-Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg/560px-Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Derivative_of_medical_imaging.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In a derivative of a medical image created in the U.S., added annotations and explanations may be copyrightable, but the medical image itself remains public domain.</div></div></div>
<p>An extensive definition of the term <i>derivative work</i> is given by the United States Copyright Act in <a href="/wiki/Title_17_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 17 of the United States Code">17 U.S.C.</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/101" rel="nofollow">§ 101</a>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>A "derivative work" is a work based upon one or more preexisting works, such as a translation...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[note 1]</a></sup> art reproduction, abridgment, condensation, or any other form in which a work may be recast, transformed, or adapted. A work consisting of editorial revisions, annotations, elaborations, or other modifications which, as a whole, represent an original work of authorship, is a "derivative work".</p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Title_17_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 17 of the United States Code">17 U.S.C.</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/103(b)" rel="nofollow">§ 103(b)</a> provides:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The copyright in a compilation or derivative work extends only to the material contributed by the author of such work, as distinguished from the preexisting material employed in the work, and does not imply any exclusive right in the preexisting material. The copyright in such work is independent of, and does not affect or enlarge the scope, duration, ownership, or subsistence of, any copyright protection in the preexisting material.</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Germany, <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">X-ray images</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasound" title="Medical ultrasound">medical ultrasound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">PET</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scintigraphy" title="Scintigraphy">scintigraphy</a> images are protected by (copyright-like) <a href="/wiki/Related_rights" title="Related rights">related rights or neighbouring rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> This protection does not require creativity (as would be necessary for <i>regular</i> copyright protection) and lasts only for 50 years after image creation, if not published within 50 years, or for 50 years after the first legitimate publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> The letter of the law grants this right to the "Lichtbildner",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> i.e. the person who created the image. The literature seems to uniformly consider the medical doctor, dentist or veterinary physician as the rights holder, which may result from the circumstance that in Germany many x-rays are performed in ambulatory settings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Medical images created in the United Kingdom will normally be protected by copyright due to "the high level of skill, labour and judgement required to produce a good quality x-ray, particularly to show contrast between bones and various soft tissues".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> The Society of Radiographers believe this copyright is owned by employer (unless the radiographer is self-employed—though even then their contract might require them to transfer ownership to the hospital). This copyright owner can grant certain permissions to whoever they wish, without giving up their ownership of the copyright. So the hospital and its employees will be given permission to use such radiographic images for the various purposes that they require for medical care. Physicians employed at the hospital will, in their contracts, be given the right to publish patient information in journal papers or books they write (providing they are made anonymous). Patients may also be granted permission to "do what they like with" their own images.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_imaging&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Cyber Law in Sweden</i> states: "Pictures can be protected as photographic works or as photographic pictures. The former requires a higher level of originality; the latter <i>protects all types of photographs, also the ones taken</i> by amateurs, or <i>within medicine</i> or science. The protection requires some sort of photographic technique being used, which includes digital cameras as well as holograms created by laser technique. The difference between the two types of work is the term of protection, which amounts to seventy years after the death of the author of a photographic work as opposed to fifty years, from the year in which the photographic picture was taken."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Medical imaging may possibly be included in the scope of "photography", similarly to a U.S. statement that "MRI images, CT scans, and the like are analogous to photography."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
