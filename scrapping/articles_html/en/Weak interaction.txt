<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>, the <b>weak interaction</b>, which is also often called the <b>weak force</b> or <b>weak nuclear force</b>, is one of the four known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_interactions" title="Fundamental interactions">fundamental interactions</a>, with the others being <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>. It is the mechanism of interaction between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subatomic_particles" title="Subatomic particles">subatomic particles</a> that is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of atoms: The weak interaction participates in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>. The theory describing its behaviour and effects is sometimes called <b>quantum flavourdynamics</b> (<b>QFD</b>); however, the term QFD is rarely used, because the weak force is better understood by <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak theory</a> (EWT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griffiths_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-griffiths-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effective range of the weak force is limited to subatomic distances and is less than the diameter of a proton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> of <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a> provides a uniform framework for understanding the electromagnetic, weak, and strong interactions. An interaction occurs when two particles (typically, but not necessarily, <a href="/wiki/Half-integer" title="Half-integer">half-integer</a> <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>) exchange integer-spin, force-carrying <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a>. The fermions involved in such exchanges can be either elementary (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>) or composite (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>), although at the deepest levels, all weak interactions ultimately are between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elementary_particles" title="Elementary particles">elementary particles</a>.
</p><p>In the weak interaction, fermions can exchange three types of force carriers, namely <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons"><span class="texhtml">W</span><sup>+</sup>, <span class="texhtml">W</span><sup>−</sup>, and <span class="texhtml">Z</span> bosons</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">masses</a> of these bosons are far greater than the mass of a proton or neutron, which is consistent with the short range of the weak force. In fact, the force is termed <i>weak</i> because its <a href="/wiki/Field_strength" title="Field strength">field strength</a> over any set distance is typically several orders of magnitude less than that of the electromagnetic force, which itself is further orders of magnitude less than the strong nuclear force.
</p><p>The weak interaction is the only fundamental interaction that breaks <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">parity symmetry</a>, and similarly, but far more rarely, the only interaction to break <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CP-symmetry" title="CP-symmetry">charge–parity symmetry</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">Quarks</a>, which make up composite particles like neutrons and protons, come in six "flavours" –  up, down, strange, charm, top and bottom –  which give those composite particles their properties. The weak interaction is unique in that it allows quarks to swap their flavour for another. The swapping of those properties is mediated by the force carrier bosons. For example, during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-minus_decay" title="Beta-minus decay">beta-minus decay</a>, a down quark within a neutron is changed into an up quark, thus converting the neutron to a proton and resulting in the emission of an electron and an electron antineutrino. Another important example of a phenomenon involving the weak interaction is the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#Hydrogen_fusion" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">fusion of hydrogen into helium</a>, which powers the Sun's thermonuclear process. 
</p><p>Most fermions decay by a weak interaction over time. Such decay makes <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> possible, as <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a> decays through the weak interaction to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen-14" title="Nitrogen-14">nitrogen-14</a>. It can also create <a href="/wiki/Radioluminescence" title="Radioluminescence">radioluminescence</a>, commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Tritium_radioluminescence" title="Tritium radioluminescence">tritium luminescence</a>, and in the related field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betavoltaics" title="Betavoltaics">betavoltaics</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (but <i>not</i> similar <a href="/wiki/Radium_dial" title="Radium dial">radium luminescence</a>).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak force</a> separated into the electromagnetic and weak forces during the <a href="/wiki/Quark_epoch" title="Quark epoch">quark epoch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe#Early_universe" title="Chronology of the universe">early universe</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1933, <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> proposed the first theory of the weak interaction, known as <a href="/wiki/Fermi%27s_interaction" title="Fermi's interaction">Fermi's interaction</a>. He suggested that <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> could be explained by a four-<a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermion</a> interaction, involving a contact force with no range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fermi's_theory_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fermi's_theory-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fermi's_theory_translation_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fermi's_theory_translation-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, it is better described as a <a href="/wiki/Non-contact_force" title="Non-contact force">non-contact force</a> field having a finite range, albeit very short.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Sheldon_Glashow" title="Sheldon Glashow">Sheldon Glashow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdus_Salam" title="Abdus Salam">Abdus Salam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Weinberg" title="Steven Weinberg">Steven Weinberg</a> unified the electromagnetic force and the weak interaction by showing them to be two aspects of a single force, now termed the electroweak force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salam_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salam-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons#Discovery" title="W and Z bosons">existence of the <span class="texhtml">W</span> and <span class="texhtml">Z</span> bosons</a> was not directly confirmed until 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 8">(p8)</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weak_Decay_(flipped).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="692" data-file-width="659" decoding="async" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Weak_Decay_%28flipped%29.svg/280px-Weak_Decay_%28flipped%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Weak_Decay_%28flipped%29.svg/420px-Weak_Decay_%28flipped%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Weak_Decay_%28flipped%29.svg/560px-Weak_Decay_%28flipped%29.svg.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weak_Decay_(flipped).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A diagram depicting the decay routes due to the charged weak interaction and some indication of their likelihood. The intensity of the lines is given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CKM_parameters" title="CKM parameters">CKM parameters</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The electrically charged weak interaction is unique in a number of respects:
</p>
<ul><li>It is the only interaction that can change the <a href="/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics)" title="Flavour (particle physics)">flavour</a> of quarks and leptons (i.e., of changing one type of quark into another).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup></li>
<li>It is the only interaction that violates <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)"><b>P</b>, or parity symmetry</a>. It is also the only one that violates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CP-symmetry" title="CP-symmetry">charge–parity <b>CP</b> symmetry</a>.</li>
<li>Both the electrically charged and the electrically neutral interactions are mediated (propagated) by <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">force carrier particles</a> that have significant masses, an unusual feature which is explained in the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Due to their large mass (approximately 90 GeV/<i>c</i><sup>2</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDG_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDG-10">[9]</a></sup>) these carrier particles, called the <span class="texhtml">W</span> and <span class="texhtml">Z</span> bosons, are short-lived with a lifetime of under 10<sup>−24</sup> seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The weak interaction has a <a href="/wiki/Coupling_constant" title="Coupling constant">coupling constant</a> (an indicator of how frequently interactions occur) between 10<sup>−7</sup> and 10<sup>−6</sup>, compared to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_Structure_Constant" title="Fine Structure Constant">electromagnetic coupling constant</a> of about 10<sup>−2</sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a> coupling constant of <span class="texhtml">1</span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coupling_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-coupling-12">[11]</a></sup> consequently the weak interaction is "weak" in terms of intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physnet_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-physnet-13">[12]</a></sup> The weak interaction has a very short effective range (around 10<sup>−17</sup> to 10<sup>−16</sup> m (0.01 to 0.1 fm)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physnet_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-physnet-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coupling_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-coupling-12">[11]</a></sup> At distances around 10<sup>−18</sup> meters (0.001 fm), the weak interaction has an intensity of a similar magnitude to the electromagnetic force, but this starts to decrease <a href="/wiki/Yukawa_potential" title="Yukawa potential">exponentially</a> with increasing distance. Scaled up by just one and a half orders of magnitude, at distances of around 3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−17</sup> m, the weak interaction becomes 10,000 times weaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The weak interaction affects all the <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>; <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> interact only through gravity and the weak interaction. The weak interaction does not produce <a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bound states</a>, nor does it involve <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">binding energy</a> –  something that gravity does on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_scale" title="Astronomical scale">astronomical scale</a>, the electromagnetic force does at the molecular and atomic levels, and the strong nuclear force does only at the subatomic level, inside of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greiner_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-greiner-16">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Its most noticeable effect is due to its first unique feature: The charged weak interaction causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavour_changing_processes" title="Flavour changing processes">flavour change</a>. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> is heavier than a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> (its partner <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleon</a>) and can decay into a proton by changing the <a href="/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics)" title="Flavour (particle physics)">flavour</a> (type) of one of its two <i>down</i> quarks to an <i>up</i> quark. Neither the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a> permit flavour changing, so this can only proceed by <b>weak decay</b>; without weak decay, quark properties such as strangeness and charm (associated with the strange quark and charm quark, respectively) would also be conserved across all interactions.
</p><p>All <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a> are unstable because of weak decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 29">(p29)</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[c]</a></sup>
In the process known as <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>, a <i>down</i> quark in the <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> can change into an <i>up</i> quark by emitting a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">virtual</a> <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> boson, which then decays into an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> and an electron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antineutrino" title="Antineutrino">antineutrino</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 28">(p28)</span></sup> Another example is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> –  a common variant of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> –  wherein a proton and an electron within an atom interact and are changed to a neutron (an up quark is changed to a down quark), and an electron neutrino is emitted.
</p><p>Due to the large masses of the W bosons, particle transformations or decays (e.g., flavour change) that depend on the weak interaction typically occur much more slowly than transformations or decays that depend only on the strong or electromagnetic forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[d]</a></sup>
For example, a neutral <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pion</a> decays electromagnetically, and so has a life of only about 10<sup>−16</sup> seconds. In contrast, a charged pion can only decay through the weak interaction, and so lives about 10<sup>−8</sup> seconds, or a hundred million times longer than a neutral pion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 30">(p30)</span></sup> A particularly extreme example is the weak-force decay of a free neutron, which takes about 15 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cottingham-Greenwood-1986-2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 28">(p28)</span></sup>
</p>

<p>All particles have a property called <i><a href="/wiki/Weak_isospin" title="Weak isospin">weak isospin</a></i> (symbol <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span><sub>3</sub>), which serves as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Additive_quantum_number" title="Additive quantum number">additive quantum number</a> that restricts how the particle can interact with the <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">±</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> of the weak force. Weak isospin plays the same role in the weak interaction with <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">±</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> as <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> does in <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">color charge</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>; a different number with a similar name, <i><a href="/wiki/Weak_charge" title="Weak charge">weak charge</a></i>, <a href="#weak_charge_anchor">discussed below</a>, is used for interactions with the <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>. All left-handed <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a> have a weak isospin value of either <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac" role="math">+<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="tion"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">2</span></span></span> or <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac" role="math">−<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="tion"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">2</span></span></span>; all right-handed fermions have 0 isospin. For example, the up quark has <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span><sub>3</sub> = <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac" role="math">+<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="tion"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">2</span></span></span></span> and the down quark has <span class="nowrap"> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span><sub>3</sub> = <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac" role="math">−<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="tion"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">2</span></span></span>.</span> A quark never decays through the weak interaction into a quark of the same <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span><sub>3</sub>: Quarks with a <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span><sub>3</sub> of <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac" role="math">+<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="tion"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">2</span></span></span> only decay into quarks with a <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span><sub>3</sub> of <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1050945101" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="sfrac" role="math">−<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="tion"><span class="num">1</span><span class="sr-only">/</span><span class="den">2</span></span></span> and conversely.
</p>

isospin of +1 normally decays into a <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_types">Interaction types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interaction types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two types of weak interaction (called <i><a href="/wiki/Feynman_diagram" title="Feynman diagram">vertices</a></i>). The first type is called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charged-current_interaction" title="Charged-current interaction">charged-current interaction</a>" because the <i>weakly interacting</i> fermions form a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_the_Standard_Model#The_charged_and_neutral_current_couplings_and_Fermi_theory" title="Mathematical formulation of the Standard Model">current</a> with total <i>electric</i> charge that is nonzero.  The second type is called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutral-current_interaction" title="Neutral-current interaction">neutral-current interaction</a>" because the <i>weakly interacting</i> fermions form a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_the_Standard_Model#The_charged_and_neutral_current_couplings_and_Fermi_theory" title="Mathematical formulation of the Standard Model">current</a> with total <i>electric</i> charge of zero. It is responsible for the (rare) deflection of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>. The two types of interaction follow different <a href="/wiki/Selection_rule" title="Selection rule">selection rules</a>. This naming convention is often misunderstood to label the electric charge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W_and_Z_boson" title="W and Z boson"><span class="texhtml">W</span> and <span class="texhtml">Z</span> bosons</a>, however the naming convention predates the concept of the mediator bosons, and clearly (at least in name) labels the charge of the current (formed from the fermions), not necessarilly the bosons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[f]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electroweak_theory">Electroweak theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electroweak theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">Electroweak interaction</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> of particle physics describes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interaction" title="Electromagnetic interaction">electromagnetic interaction</a> and the weak interaction as two different aspects of a single electroweak interaction. This theory was developed around 1968 by <a href="/wiki/Sheldon_Glashow" title="Sheldon Glashow">Sheldon Glashow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdus_Salam" title="Abdus Salam">Abdus Salam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Weinberg" title="Steven Weinberg">Steven Weinberg</a>, and they were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics#1970s" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">1979 Nobel Prize in Physics</a> for their work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a> provides an explanation for the presence of <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons">three massive gauge bosons</a> (<span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>, <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>, <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>, the three carriers of the weak interaction), and the massless <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> (<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">γ</span>, the carrier of the electromagnetic interaction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDGHiggs_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDGHiggs-28">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the electroweak theory, at very high energies, the universe has four components of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higgs_field" title="Higgs field">Higgs field</a> whose interactions are carried by four massless gauge <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a> – each similar to the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> – forming a complex scalar Higgs field doublet. Likewise, there are four massless electroweak bosons. However, at low energies, this gauge symmetry is <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_symmetry_breaking" title="Spontaneous symmetry breaking">spontaneously broken</a> down to the <span class="texhtml">U(1)</span> symmetry of electromagnetism, since one of the Higgs fields acquires a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_expectation_value" title="Vacuum expectation value">vacuum expectation value</a>. Naïvely, the symmetry-breaking would be expected to produce three massless <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_boson" title="Goldstone boson">bosons</a>, but instead those "extra" three Higgs bosons become incorporated into the three weak bosons, which then acquire mass through the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a>. These three composite bosons are the <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>, <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>, and <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> bosons actually observed in the weak interaction. The fourth electroweak gauge boson is the photon (<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">γ</span>) of electromagnetism, which does not couple to any of the Higgs fields and so remains massless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDGHiggs_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-PDGHiggs-28">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>This theory has made a number of predictions, including a prediction of the masses of the <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> and <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> bosons before their discovery and detection in 1983.
</p><p>On 4 July 2012, the CMS and the ATLAS experimental teams at the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> independently announced that they had confirmed the formal discovery of a previously unknown boson of mass between 125 and 127 GeV/<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span>², whose behaviour so far was "consistent with" a Higgs boson, while adding a cautious note that further data and analysis were needed before positively identifying the new boson as being a Higgs boson of some type. By 14 March 2013, a Higgs boson was tentatively confirmed to exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a speculative case where the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">electroweak symmetry breaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_scale" title="Electroweak scale">scale</a> were lowered, the unbroken <span class="texhtml">SU(2)</span> interaction would eventually become <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">confining</a>. Alternative models where <span class="texhtml">SU(2)</span> becomes confining above that scale appear quantitatively similar to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> at lower energies, but dramatically different above symmetry breaking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Violation_of_symmetry">Violation of symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Violation of symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Right_left_helicity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="380" decoding="async" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Right_left_helicity.svg/280px-Right_left_helicity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Right_left_helicity.svg/420px-Right_left_helicity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Right_left_helicity.svg/560px-Right_left_helicity.svg.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Right_left_helicity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chirality_(physics)" title="Chirality (physics)">Left- and right-handed particles</a>: <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">p</span> is the particle's momentum and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">S</span> is its <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>. Note the lack of reflective symmetry between the states.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">laws of nature</a> were long thought to remain the same under mirror <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflection</a>. The results of an experiment viewed via a mirror were expected to be identical to the results of a separately constructed, mirror-reflected copy of the experimental apparatus watched through the mirror. This so-called law of <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">parity</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics)" title="Conservation law (physics)">conservation</a> was known to be respected by classical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>; it was assumed to be a universal law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[24]</a></sup> However, in the mid-1950s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chen-Ning_Yang" title="Chen-Ning Yang">Chen-Ning Yang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsung-Dao_Lee" title="Tsung-Dao Lee">Tsung-Dao Lee</a> suggested that the weak interaction might violate this law. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chien_Shiung_Wu" title="Chien Shiung Wu">Chien Shiung Wu</a> and collaborators in 1957 discovered that the weak interaction violates parity, earning Yang and Lee the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics#1950s" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">1957 Nobel Prize in Physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the weak interaction was once described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermi%27s_theory" title="Fermi's theory">Fermi's theory</a>, the discovery of parity violation and <a href="/wiki/Renormalization" title="Renormalization">renormalization</a> theory suggested that a new approach was needed. In 1957, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Marshak" title="Robert Marshak">Robert Marshak</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Sudarshan" title="George Sudarshan">George Sudarshan</a> and, somewhat later, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Murray_Gell-Mann" title="Murray Gell-Mann">Murray Gell-Mann</a> proposed a <b>V − A</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometry)" title="Vector (geometry)">vector</a> minus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_vector" title="Axial vector">axial vector</a> or left-handed) <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_(field_theory)" title="Lagrangian (field theory)">Lagrangian</a> for weak interactions. In this theory, the weak interaction acts only on left-handed particles (and right-handed antiparticles). Since the mirror reflection of a left-handed particle is right-handed, this explains the maximal violation of parity. The <i>V − A</i> theory was developed before the discovery of the Z boson, so it did not include the right-handed fields that enter in the neutral current interaction.
</p><p>However, this theory allowed a compound symmetry <b><a href="/wiki/CP_violation" title="CP violation">CP</a></b> to be conserved. <b>CP</b> combines parity <b>P</b> (switching left to right) with charge conjugation <b>C</b> (switching particles with antiparticles). Physicists were again surprised when in 1964, <a href="/wiki/James_Cronin" title="James Cronin">James Cronin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Val_Fitch" title="Val Fitch">Val Fitch</a> provided clear evidence in <a href="/wiki/Kaon" title="Kaon">kaon</a> decays that <i>CP</i> symmetry could be broken too, winning them the 1980 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[26]</a></sup> In 1973, <a href="/wiki/Makoto_Kobayashi_(physicist)" title="Makoto Kobayashi (physicist)">Makoto Kobayashi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toshihide_Maskawa" title="Toshihide Maskawa">Toshihide Maskawa</a> showed that <i>CP</i> violation in the weak interaction required more than two generations of particles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KM_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-KM-34">[27]</a></sup> effectively predicting the existence of a then unknown third generation. This discovery earned them half of the 2008 Nobel Prize in Physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike parity violation, <i>CP</i> violation occurs only in rare circumstances. Despite its limited occurrence under present conditions, it is widely believed to be the reason that there is much more matter than <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> in the universe, and thus forms one of <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov" title="Andrei Sakharov">Andrei Sakharov</a>'s three conditions for <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">baryogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[29]</a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Because of its unique ability to change particle <a href="/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics)" title="Flavour (particle physics)">flavour</a>, analysis of the weak interaction is occasionally called <i>quantum flavour dynamics</i>, in analogy to the name <i><a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a></i> sometimes used for the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong force</a>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-14"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-14">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Compare to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Femtometer" title="Femtometer">proton charge radius</a> of 8.3×10<sup>−16</sup> m ~ 0.83 fm.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">
The neutral pion (<span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>π<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>), however, decays electromagnetically, and several other <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a> (when their quantum numbers permit) mostly decay via a <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-18"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-18">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">
The prominent and possibly unique exception to this rule is the decay of the <a href="/wiki/Top_quark" title="Top quark">top quark</a>, whose mass exceeds the combined masses of the <a href="/wiki/Bottom_quark" title="Bottom quark">bottom quark</a> and <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> boson that it decays into, hence it has a no energy constraint slowing its transition. Its unique speed of decay by the weak force is much higher than the speed with which the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a> (or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_force" title="Color force">color force</a>") can bind it to other quarks.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-20"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-20">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Some hypothesised fermions, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Sterile_neutrino" title="Sterile neutrino">sterile neutrinos</a></i>, would have zero weak hypercharge –  in fact, no <a href="/wiki/Charge_(physics)" title="Charge (physics)">gauge charges</a> of any known kind. Whether any such particles actually exist is an active area of research.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The exchange of a virtual <span class="texhtml">W</span> boson can be equally well thought of as (say) the emission of a <span class="texhtml">W</span><sup>+</sup> or the absorption of a <span class="texhtml">W</span><sup>−</sup>; that is, for time on the vertical co‑ordinate axis, as a <span class="texhtml">W</span><sup>+</sup> from left to right, or equivalently as a <span class="texhtml">W</span><sup>−</sup> from right to left.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">
The only fermions which the <span class="texhtml"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> </span> does <i>not</i> interact with at all are the hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Sterile_neutrino" title="Sterile neutrino">"sterile" neutrinos</a>: Left-chiral anti-neutrinos and right-chiral neutrinos. They are called "sterile" because they would not interact with any Standard Model particle, but so far remain entirely a conjecture: As yet (October 2021), no such neutrinos are known to actually exist.
<dl><dd>“MicroBooNE has made a very comprehensive exploration through multiple types of interactions, and multiple analysis and reconstruction techniques,” says co-spokesperson Bonnie Fleming of Yale. “They all tell us the same thing, and that gives us very high confidence in our results that we are not seeing a hint of a sterile neutrino.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CERN-2021-10-28_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-CERN-2021-10-28-24">[18]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>... “eV-scale sterile neutrinos no longer appear to be experimentally motivated, and never solved any outstanding problems in the Standard Model,” says theorist Mikhail Shaposhnikov of EPFL. “But GeV-to-keV-scale sterile neutrinos – so-called Majorana fermions – are well motivated theoretically and do not contradict any existing experiment.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CERN-2021-10-28_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-CERN-2021-10-28-24">[18]</a></sup></dd></dl>
</span></li>
</ol></div>
