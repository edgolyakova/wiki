<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Knife</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Tool or weapon with a cutting edge or blade</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Knife_(disambiguation)" title="Knife (disambiguation)">Knife (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Damascus_Bowie.jpg"><img alt="Large knife with polished wooden handle, lying next to a leather sheath" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1221" data-file-width="1458" decoding="async" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Damascus_Bowie.jpg/330px-Damascus_Bowie.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Damascus_Bowie.jpg/495px-Damascus_Bowie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Damascus_Bowie.jpg/660px-Damascus_Bowie.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Damascus_Bowie.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Bowie_knife" title="Bowie knife">Bowie knife</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pattern_welding" title="Pattern welding">pattern-welded</a> steel</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>knife</b> (<a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural"><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">PL</span></span></a>: <b>knives</b>; from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <span title="Old Norse-language text"><i lang="non">knifr</i></span> 'knife, dirk'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a> or <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapon</a> with a cutting edge or <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">blade</a>, usually attached to a <a href="/wiki/Handle" title="Handle">handle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hilt" title="Hilt">hilt</a>. One of the earliest tools used by humanity, knives appeared at least <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">2.5 million years ago</a>, as evidenced by the <a href="/wiki/Oldowan" title="Oldowan">Oldowan</a> tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palomar.edu_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-palomar.edu-3">[3]</a></sup>  Originally made of wood, bone, and stone (such as <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a>), over the centuries, in step with improvements in both metallurgy and manufacturing, knife blades have been made from <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.  Most modern knives have either fixed or folding blades; blade patterns and styles vary by maker and country of origin.
</p><p>Knives can serve various purposes. Hunters use a <a href="/wiki/Hunting_knife" title="Hunting knife">hunting knife</a>, soldiers use the <a href="/wiki/Combat_knife" title="Combat knife">combat knife</a>, scouts, campers, and <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hikers</a> carry a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocket_knife" title="Pocket knife">pocket knife</a>; there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitchen_knives" title="Kitchen knives">kitchen knives</a> for preparing foods (the chef's knife, the paring knife, bread knife, cleaver), table knives (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butter_knives" title="Butter knives">butter knives</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steak_knives" title="Steak knives">steak knives</a>), weapons (<a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">daggers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Switchblade" title="Switchblade">switchblades</a>), knives for throwing or juggling, and knives for religious ceremony or display (the <a href="/wiki/Kirpan" title="Kirpan">kirpan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JK_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-JK-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts">Parts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Parts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Knife_making" title="Knife making">Knife making</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_knife#Nomenclature" title="Kitchen knife">Kitchen knife § Nomenclature</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Knife_parts.jpg"><img alt="Different parts of a knife indicated with numerals" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="209" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Knife_parts.jpg/220px-Knife_parts.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Knife_parts.jpg/330px-Knife_parts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Knife_parts.jpg/440px-Knife_parts.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Knife_parts.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Characteristic parts of a knife</div></div></div>
<p>A modern knife consists of:
</p>
<ol><li>the <i>blade</i></li>
<li>the <i>handle</i></li>
<li>the <i>point</i> – the end of the knife used for piercing</li>
<li>the <i>edge</i> – the cutting surface of the knife extending from the point to the heel</li>
<li>the <i><a href="/wiki/Grind" title="Grind">grind</a></i> – the <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">cross section</a> shape of the blade</li>
<li>the <i>spine</i> – the thickest section of the blade; on a single-edged knife, the side opposite the edge; on a two-edged knife, more toward the middle</li>
<li>the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuller_(weapon)" title="Fuller (weapon)">fuller</a></i> – a groove added to make the blade lighter</li>
<li>the <i><a href="/wiki/Ricasso" title="Ricasso">ricasso</a></i> – the flat section of the blade located at the junction of the blade and the knife's bolster or guard</li>
<li>the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guard_(weapon)" title="Guard (weapon)">guard</a></i> – the barrier between the blade and the handle which prevents the hand from slipping forward onto the blade and protects the hand from the external forces that are usually applied to the blade during use</li>
<li>the <i>hilt</i> or <i>butt</i> – the end of the handle utilized for blunt force</li>
<li>the <i><a href="/wiki/Lanyard" title="Lanyard">lanyard</a></i> – a strap used to secure the knife to the wrist</li></ol>
<p>The blade edge can be plain or <a href="/wiki/Serrated_blade" title="Serrated blade">serrated</a>, or a combination of both. Single-edged knives may have a <i>reverse edge</i> or <i>false edge</i> occupying a section of the spine.  These edges are usually serrated and are used to further enhance function.
</p><p>The handle, used to grip and manipulate the blade safely, may include a <i><a href="/wiki/Tang_(tools)" title="Tang (tools)">tang</a></i>, a portion of the blade that extends into the handle. Knives are made with partial tangs (extending part way into the handle, known as "stick tangs") or full tangs (extending the full length of the handle, often visible on top and bottom). There is also the <i>enterçado</i> construction method present in antique knives from Brazil, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sorocaban_Knife" title="Sorocaban Knife">Sorocaban Knife</a>, which consists in riveting a repurposed blade to the ricasso of a bladeless handle. The handle may include a bolster, a piece of heavy material (usually metal) situated at the front or rear of the handle. The bolster, as its name suggests, is used to mechanically strengthen the knife.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="607" data-file-width="987" decoding="async" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png/220px-Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png/330px-Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png/440px-Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fixed_Blade_Full_Tang_Survival_Knife.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Gerber_Legendary_Blades" title="Gerber Legendary Blades">Gerber</a>-made full-<a href="/wiki/Tang_(tools)" title="Tang (tools)">tang</a> survival knife.  The metal from the blade extends into the handle.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blade">Blade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Blade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blade" title="Blade">Blade</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg/220px-Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg/330px-Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg/440px-Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Morakniv_Basic_511_Carbon_Steel_5.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Morakniv carbon steel knife</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chinese_knife_factory.jpg"><img alt="Seated female workers making knives on machinery with large spinning drums" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2960" data-file-width="1928" decoding="async" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Chinese_knife_factory.jpg/220px-Chinese_knife_factory.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Chinese_knife_factory.jpg/330px-Chinese_knife_factory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Chinese_knife_factory.jpg/440px-Chinese_knife_factory.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chinese_knife_factory.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Knife blade mass production</div></div></div>
<p>Knife blades can be manufactured from a variety of materials, each of which has advantages and disadvantages. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">Carbon steel</a>, an alloy of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, can be very sharp. It holds its edge well, and remains easy to sharpen, but is vulnerable to rust and stains. Stainless steel is an alloy of iron, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, possibly <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>, with only a small amount of carbon. It is not able to take quite as sharp an edge as carbon steel, but is highly resistant to corrosion. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stainless_Steel#Types_of_stainless_steel" title="Stainless Steel">High carbon stainless steel</a> is stainless steel with a higher amount of carbon, intended to incorporate the better attributes of carbon steel and stainless steel. High carbon stainless steel blades do not discolor or stain, and maintain a sharp edge. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laminate" title="Laminate">Laminated</a> blades use multiple metals to create a layered sandwich, combining the attributes of both. For example, a harder, more brittle steel may be sandwiched between an outer layer of softer, tougher, stainless steel to reduce vulnerability to corrosion. In this case, however, the part most affected by corrosion, the edge, is still vulnerable. <a href="/wiki/Damascus_steel" title="Damascus steel">Damascus steel</a> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Pattern_welding" title="Pattern welding">pattern welding</a> with similarities to laminate construction. Layers of different steel types are welded together, but then the stock is manipulated to create patterns in the steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">Titanium</a> is a metal that has a better strength-to-weight ratio, is more wear resistant, and more flexible than steel. Although less hard and unable to take as sharp an edge, carbides in the titanium alloy allow them to be heat-treated to a sufficient hardness. <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">Ceramic</a> blades are hard, brittle, and lightweight: they may maintain a sharp edge for years with no maintenance at all, but are as fragile as glass and will break if dropped on a hard surface. They are immune to common corrosion, and can only be sharpened on silicon carbide sandpaper and some grinding wheels. <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">Plastic</a> blades are not especially sharp and typically <a href="/wiki/Serrated_blade" title="Serrated blade">serrated</a>. They are often disposable.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Microtome-knife-profile.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="350" decoding="async" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Microtome-knife-profile.svg/120px-Microtome-knife-profile.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Microtome-knife-profile.svg/180px-Microtome-knife-profile.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Microtome-knife-profile.svg/240px-Microtome-knife-profile.svg.png 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Microtome-knife-profile.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Knife blades have different profiles</div></div></div>
<p>Steel blades are commonly shaped by <a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">forging</a> or stock removal. Forged blades are made by heating a single piece of steel, then shaping the metal while hot using a hammer or press. Stock removal blades are shaped by grinding and removing metal. With both methods, after shaping, the steel must be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_treatment" title="Heat treatment">heat treated</a>. This involves heating the steel above its critical point, then <a href="/wiki/Quenching" title="Quenching">quenching</a> the blade to harden it. After hardening, the blade is <a href="/wiki/Tempering_(metallurgy)" title="Tempering (metallurgy)">tempered</a> to remove stresses and make the blade tougher. Mass manufactured kitchen cutlery uses both the forging and stock removal processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Forging tends to be reserved for manufacturers' more expensive product lines, and can often be distinguished from stock removal product lines by the presence of an integral bolster, though integral bolsters can be crafted through either shaping method.
</p><p>Knives are sharpened in various ways. Flat ground blades have a profile that tapers from the thick spine to the sharp edge in a straight or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convex" title="wikt:convex">convex</a> line. Seen in cross section, the blade would form a long, thin triangle, or where the taper does not extend to the back of the blade, a long thin rectangle with one peaked side. Hollow ground blades have <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concave" title="wikt:concave">concave</a>, beveled edges. The resulting blade has a thinner edge, so it may have better cutting ability for shallow cuts, but it is lighter and less durable than flat ground blades and will tend to bind in deep cuts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Serrated blade knives have a wavy, scalloped or saw-like blade. Serrated blades are more well suited for tasks that require aggressive 'sawing' motions, whereas plain edge blades are better suited for tasks that require push-through cuts (e.g., shaving, chopping, slicing).
</p><p>Many knives have holes in the blade for various uses. Holes are commonly drilled in blades to reduce friction while cutting, increase single-handed usability of pocket knives, and, for butchers' knives, allow hanging out of the way when not in use.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed-blade_features">Fixed-blade features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fixed-blade features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg"><img alt="Fixed-blade knife with wooden handle on a table, next to a molded plastic sheath" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg/220px-CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg/330px-CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg/440px-CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CKc_Pen_Knife.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>cKc pen knife</div></div></div>
<p>A fixed blade knife, sometimes called a <a href="/wiki/Sheath_knife" title="Sheath knife">sheath knife</a>, does not fold or slide, and is typically stronger due to the tang, the extension of the blade into the handle, and lack of moving parts.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Folding_blade_features">Folding blade features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Folding blade features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="Folding pocket knife with multiple exposed tools" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1105" data-file-width="1982" decoding="async" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Swiss_army_knife_open_20050612_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Army_knife" title="Swiss Army knife">Swiss Army knife</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocket_knife" title="Pocket knife">Pocket knife</a></div>
<p>A folding knife connects the blade to the handle through a <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">pivot</a>, allowing the blade to fold into the handle. To prevent injury to the knife user through the blade accidentally closing on the user's hand, folding knives typically have a locking mechanism. Different locking mechanisms are favored by various individuals for reasons such as perceived strength (lock safety), legality, and ease of use.
Popular locking mechanisms include:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Slip joint</i> – Found most commonly on traditional pocket knives, the opened blade does not lock, but is held in place by a spring device that allows the blade to fold if a certain amount of pressure is applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kertzman2013sj_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kertzman2013sj-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Lockback</i> – Also known as the <i>spine lock</i>, the lockback includes a pivoted latch affixed to a spring, and can be disengaged only by pressing the latch down to release the blade.</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walker_Linerlock" title="Walker Linerlock">Linerlock</a></i> – Invented by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Walker_(knifemaker)" title="Michael Walker (knifemaker)">Michael Walker</a>, a Linerlock is a folding knife with a side-spring lock that can be opened and closed with one hand without repositioning the knife in the hand. The lock is self-adjusting for wear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winter_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-winter-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Compression Lock</i> – A variant of the Liner Lock, it uses a small piece of metal at the tip of the lock to lock into a small corresponding impression in the blade.  This creates a lock that doesn't disengage when the blade is torqued, instead of becoming more tightly locked.  It is released by pressing the tab of metal to the side, to allow the blade to be placed into its groove set into the handle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winter_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-winter-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Frame Lock</i> – Also known as the <i>integral lock</i> or <i>monolock</i>, this locking mechanism was invented by a custom knifemaker <a href="/wiki/Chris_Reeve" title="Chris Reeve">Chris Reeve</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Sebenza" title="Sebenza">Sebenza</a> as an update to the liner lock. The frame lock works in a manner similar to the liner lock but uses a partial cutout of the actual knife handle, rather than a separate liner inside the handle to hold the blade in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKM93_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NKM93-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KI2000_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-KI2000-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Collar lock</i> – found on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opinel_knife" title="Opinel knife">Opinel knives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Media1996_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Media1996-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Button Lock</i> – Found mainly on automatic knives, this type of lock uses a small push-button to open and release the knife.<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BM-AxisLock.jpg"><img alt="Close-up of the pivot joint of a folding knife, showing locking barrel inserted through holes in the handle" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="577" decoding="async" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/BM-AxisLock.jpg/220px-BM-AxisLock.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/BM-AxisLock.jpg/330px-BM-AxisLock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/BM-AxisLock.jpg/440px-BM-AxisLock.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BM-AxisLock.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Benchmade Axis Lock mechanism</div></div></div></li>
<li><i>Axis Lock</i> – A locking mechanism exclusively licensed to the <a href="/wiki/Benchmade" title="Benchmade">Benchmade Knife Company</a>.  A cylindrical bearing is tensioned such that it will jump between the knife blade and some feature of the handle to lock the blade open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahern2010_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahern2010-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Arc Lock</i> – A locking mechanism exclusively licensed to <a href="/wiki/SOG_Specialty_Knives" title="SOG Specialty Knives">SOG Specialty Knives</a>.  It differs from an axis lock in that the cylindrical bearing is tensioned by a rotary spring rather than an axial spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShidelerSigler2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShidelerSigler2008-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Ball Bearing Lock</i> – A locking mechanism exclusively licensed to <a href="/wiki/Spyderco" title="Spyderco">Spyderco</a>.  This lock is conceptually similar to the axis and arc locks but the bearing is instead a <a href="/wiki/Ball_bearing" title="Ball bearing">ball bearing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suermondt2004_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suermondt2004-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Tri-Ad Lock</i> – A locking mechanism exclusively licensed to <a href="/wiki/Cold_Steel_(company)" title="Cold Steel (company)">Cold Steel</a>. It is a form of lockback which incorporates a thick steel stop pin between the front of the latch and the back of the tang to transfer force from the blade into the handle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kertzman2012-cs_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kertzman2012-cs-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>PickLock</i> – A round post on the back base of the blade locks into a hole in a spring tab in the handle. To close, manually lift (pick) the spring tab (lock) off the blade post with your fingers, or in "Italian Style Stilettos" swivel the bolster (hand guard) clockwise to lift the spring tab off the blade post.</li></ul>
<p>Another prominent feature of many folding knives is the opening mechanism. Traditional pocket knives and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Army_Knife" title="Swiss Army Knife">Swiss Army knives</a> commonly employ the nail nick, while modern folding knives more often use a stud, hole, disk, or <i>flipper</i> located on the blade, all of which have the benefit of allowing the user to open the knife with one hand.
</p><p>The "wave" feature is another prominent design, which uses a part of the blade that protrudes outward to catch on one's pocket as it is drawn, thus opening the blade; this was patented by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Emerson" title="Ernest Emerson">Ernest Emerson</a> and is not only used on many of the Emerson knives, but also on knives produced by several other manufacturers, notably <a href="/wiki/Spyderco" title="Spyderco">Spyderco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cold_Steel_(company)" title="Cold Steel (company)">Cold Steel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cutler_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cutler-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Automatic</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Switchblade" title="Switchblade">switchblade</a></i> knives open using the stored energy from a spring that is released when the user presses a button or lever or other <a href="/wiki/Actuator" title="Actuator">actuator</a> built into the handle of the knife. Automatic knives are severely restricted by law in the UK and most American states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inc.1994_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inc.1994-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increasingly common are <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assisted_opening_knife" title="Assisted opening knife">assisted opening</a></i> knives which use springs to propel the blade once the user has moved it past a certain angle. These differ from automatic or switchblade knives in that the blade is not released by means of a button or catch on the handle; rather, the blade itself is the actuator. Most assisted openers use flippers as their opening mechanism. Assisted opening knives can be as fast or faster than automatic knives to deploy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_locking_mechanisms">Common locking mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Common locking mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:291px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Folding_knife_locking_mechanisms.svg"><img alt="Folding knife locking mechanisms.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="521" data-file-width="289" decoding="async" height="521" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Folding_knife_locking_mechanisms.svg/289px-Folding_knife_locking_mechanisms.svg.png" width="289"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"></div></div></div>
<p>In the <b>lock back</b>, as in many folding knives, a stop pin acting on the top (or behind) the blade prevents it from rotating clockwise. A hook on the tang of the blade engages with a hook on the rocker bar which prevents the blade from rotating counter-clockwise. The rocker bar is held in position by a torsion bar. To release the knife the rocker bar is pushed downwards as indicated and pivots around the rocker pin, lifting the hook and freeing the blade.
</p><p>When negative pressure (pushing down on the spine) is applied to the blade all the stress is transferred from the hook on the blade's tang to the hook on the rocker bar and thence to the small rocker pin. Excessive stress can shear one or both of these hooks rendering the knife effectively useless. Knife company Cold Steel uses a variant of the lock back called the <b>Tri-Ad Lock</b> which introduces a pin in front of the rocker bar to relieve stress on the rocker pin, has an elongated hole around the rocker pin to allow the mechanism to wear over time without losing strength and angles the hooks so that the faces no longer meet vertically.
</p><p>The bolt in the <b>bolt lock</b> is a rectangle of metal that is constrained to slide only back and forward. When the knife is open a spring biases the bolt to the forward position where it rests above the tang of the blade preventing the blade from closing. Small knobs extend through the handle of the knife on both sides allowing the user to slide the bolt backward freeing the knife to close. The <b>Axis Lock</b> used by knife maker Benchmade is functionally identical to the bolt lock except that it uses a cylinder rather than a rectangle to trap the blade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahern2010_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ahern2010-12">[12]</a></sup> The <b>Arc Lock</b> by knife maker SOG is similar to the Axis Lock except the cylinder follows a curved path rather than a straight path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShidelerSigler2008_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ShidelerSigler2008-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <b>liner lock</b>, an "L"-shaped split in the liner allows part of the liner to move sideways from its resting position against the handle to the centre of the knife where it rests against the flat end of the tang. To disengage, this leaf spring is pushed so it again rests flush against the handle allowing the knife to rotate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winter_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-winter-8">[8]</a></sup> A <b>frame lock</b> is functionally identical but instead of using a thin liner inside the handle material uses a thicker piece of metal as the handle and the same split in it allows a section of the frame to press against the tang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winter_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-winter-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sliding_blade_features">Sliding blade features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sliding blade features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sliding_knife" title="Sliding knife">Sliding knife</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OTF_knife_GM08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="83" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/OTF_knife_GM08.jpg/220px-OTF_knife_GM08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/OTF_knife_GM08.jpg/330px-OTF_knife_GM08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/OTF_knife_GM08.jpg/440px-OTF_knife_GM08.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OTF_knife_GM08.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An OTF knife, showing the sliding blade being extended from the handle</div></div></div>
<p>A sliding knife is a knife that can be opened by sliding the knife blade out the front of the handle. One method of opening is where the blade exits out the front of the handle point-first and then is locked into place (an example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_knife" title="Gravity knife">gravity knife</a>). Another form is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OTF_knife" title="OTF knife">OTF</a> (out-the-front) switchblade, which only requires the push of a button or spring to cause the blade to slide out of the handle and lock into place. To retract the blade back into the handle, a release lever or button, usually the same control as to open, is pressed.  A very common form of sliding knife is the sliding <a href="/wiki/Utility_knife" title="Utility knife">utility knife</a> (commonly known as a stanley knife or boxcutter).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Handle">Handle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Handle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handle_(grip)" title="Handle (grip)">Handle (grip)</a></div>
<p>The handles of knives can be made from a number of different materials, each of which has advantages and disadvantages. Handles are produced in a wide variety of shapes and styles. Handles are often textured to enhance grip.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">Wood</a> handles provide good grip and are warm in the hand, but are more difficult to care for. They do not resist water well, and will crack or warp with prolonged exposure to water. Modern stabilized and laminated woods have largely overcome these problems. Many beautiful and exotic hardwoods are employed in the manufacture of custom and some production knives. In some countries it is now forbidden for commercial butchers' knives to have wood handles, for sanitary reasons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">Plastic</a> handles are more easily cared for than wooden handles, but can be slippery and become brittle over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JK_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-JK-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injection_mold" title="Injection mold">Injection molded</a> handles made from higher grade plastics are composed of <a href="/wiki/Polyphthalamide" title="Polyphthalamide">polyphthalamide</a>, and when marketed under trademarked names such as <i><a href="/wiki/Zytel" title="Zytel">Zytel</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grivory" title="Grivory">Grivory</a></i>, are reinforced with <i><a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a>. These are often used by major knife manufacturers.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">Rubber</a> handles such as <a href="/wiki/Kraton_(polymer)" title="Kraton (polymer)">Kraton</a> or Resiprene-C are generally preferred over plastic due to their durable and cushioning nature.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Micarta" title="Micarta">Micarta</a> is a popular handle material on <i>user</i> knives due to its toughness and stability. Micarta is nearly impervious to water, is <i>grippy</i> when wet, and is an excellent insulator. Micarta has come to refer to any fibrous material cast in resin. There are many varieties of micarta available. One very popular version is a fiberglass impregnated resin called G-10.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">Leather</a> handles are seen on some hunting and military knives, notably the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KA-BAR" title="KA-BAR">KA-BAR</a>. Leather handles are typically produced by stacking leather washers, or less commonly, as a sleeve surrounding another handle material. Russian manufacturers often use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birchbark" title="Birchbark">birchbark</a> in the same manner.</li>
<li>Skeleton handles refers to the practice of using the tang itself as the handle, usually with sections of material removed to reduce weight. Skeleton handled knives are often wrapped with <a href="/wiki/Parachute_cord" title="Parachute cord">parachute cord</a> or other wrapping materials to enhance grip.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">Stainless steel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> handles are durable and sanitary, but can be slippery. To counter this, premium knife makers make handles with ridges, bumps, or indentations to provide extra grip. Another problem with knives that have metal handles is that, since metal is an excellent heat-conductor, these knives can be very uncomfortable, and even painful or dangerous, when handled without gloves or other protective handwear in (very) cold climates.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="1088" decoding="async" height="84" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg/300px-Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg/450px-Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg/600px-Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Knife_handle_made_of_buffalo_horn.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A traditional knife handle made from buffalo horn (<a href="/wiki/Tasikmalaya" title="Tasikmalaya">Tasikmalaya</a>, Indonesia)</div></div></div>
<p>More exotic materials usually only seen on art or ceremonial knives include: Stone, bone, mammoth tooth, mammoth ivory, oosik (walrus penis bone), walrus tusk, antler (often called stag in a knife context), sheep horn, buffalo horn, teeth, and mop (mother of pearl or "pearl"). Many materials have been employed in knife handles.
</p><p>Handles may be adapted to accommodate the needs of people with disabilities. For example, knife handles may be made thicker or with more cushioning for people with <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a> in their hands. A non-slip handle accommodates people with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palmar_hyperhidrosis" title="Palmar hyperhidrosis">palmar hyperhidrosis</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Birchbark_handle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Birchbark_handle.jpg/250px-Birchbark_handle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Birchbark_handle.jpg/375px-Birchbark_handle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Birchbark_handle.jpg/500px-Birchbark_handle.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Birchbark_handle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Birchbark knife handle</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information on the various types of knives: <a href="/wiki/Blade#Patterns_of_knife_blades" title="Blade">Blade § Patterns of knife blades</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons">Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tuarknife.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Tuarknife.jpg/220px-Tuarknife.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Tuarknife.jpg/330px-Tuarknife.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Tuarknife.jpg/440px-Tuarknife.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tuarknife.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A large traditional <a href="/wiki/Tuareg_people" title="Tuareg people">Tuareg</a> knife.</div></div></div>
<p>As a weapon, the knife is universally adopted as an essential tool. It is the essential element of a <a href="/wiki/Knife_fight" title="Knife fight">knife fight</a>. For example:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ballistic_knife" title="Ballistic knife">Ballistic knife</a>: A specialized combat knife with a detachable gas- or spring-propelled blade that can be fired to a distance of several feet or meters by pressing a trigger or switch on the handle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">Bayonet</a>: A knife-shaped close-quarters combat weapon designed to attach to the muzzle of a rifle or similar weapon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Butterfly_knife" title="Butterfly knife">Butterfly knife</a>: A folding pocket knife also known as a "balisong" or "batangas" with two counter-rotating handles where the blade is concealed within grooves in the handles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Combat_knife" title="Combat knife">Combat knife</a>: Any knife intended to be used by soldiers in the field, as a general-use tool, but also for fighting.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">Dagger</a>: A single-edged or double-edged combat knife with a central spine and edge(s) sharpened their full length, used primarily for thrusting or <a href="/wiki/Stabbing" title="Stabbing">stabbing</a>. Variations include the <a href="/wiki/Stiletto" title="Stiletto">Stiletto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Push_dagger" title="Push dagger">Push dagger</a>. See <a href="/wiki/List_of_daggers" title="List of daggers">List of daggers</a> for a more detailed list.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fighting_knife" title="Fighting knife">Fighting knife</a>: A knife with a blade designed to inflict a lethal injury in a physical confrontation between two or more individuals at very short range (grappling distance). Well known examples include the <a href="/wiki/Bowie_knife" title="Bowie knife">Bowie knife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ka-Bar" title="Ka-Bar">Ka-Bar combat knife</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fairbairn%E2%80%93Sykes_fighting_knife" title="Fairbairn–Sykes fighting knife">Fairbairn–Sykes fighting knife</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Genoese_knife" title="Genoese knife">Genoese knife</a>: produced from the 12th century with a guardless handle</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karambit" title="Karambit">Karambit</a>: A knife with a curved blade resembling a tiger's claw, and a handle with one or two safety holes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rampuri" title="Rampuri">Rampuri</a>: An Indian gravity knife having a single-edged blade roughly 9 to 12 inches (23 to 30 cm) long.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shiv_(weapon)" title="Shiv (weapon)">Shiv</a>: A crudely made homemade knife out of everyday materials, especially prevalent in prisons among inmates. An alternate name in some prisons is <i>shank</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">Sword</a>: An evolution of the knife with a lengthened and strengthened blade used primarily for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%AAl%C3%A9e" title="Mêlée">mêlée</a> combat and hunting.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Throwing_knife" title="Throwing knife">Throwing knife</a>: A knife designed and weighted for throwing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trench_knife" title="Trench knife">Trench knife</a>: Purpose-made or improvised knives, intended for close-quarter fighting, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a>; some have a d-shaped integral hand guard.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_equipment">Sports equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sports equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Throwing_knife" title="Throwing knife">Throwing knife</a>: A knife designed and weighted for throwing.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Utensils">Utensils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Utensils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="2017" decoding="async" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG/220px-Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG/330px-Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG/440px-Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Old_Swiss_table_knives.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Table_knife" title="Table knife">Table knives</a></div></div></div>
<p>A primary aspect of the knife as a tool includes dining, used either in <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">food preparation</a> or as <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">cutlery</a>. Examples of this include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Kitchen_knife#Bread_knife" title="Kitchen knife">Bread knife</a>: A knife with a serrated blade for cutting <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boning_knife" title="Boning knife">Boning knife</a>: A knife used for removing the bones of <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Butcher_knife" title="Butcher knife">Butcher's Knife</a>: A knife designed and used primarily for the butchering and/or dressing of animals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kitchen_knife#Carving" title="Kitchen knife">Carving knife</a>: A knife for carving large cooked meats such as <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roasting" title="Roasting">roasts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hams" title="Hams">hams</a>, and other large cooked meats.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chef%27s_knife" title="Chef's knife">Chef's knife</a>: Also known as a French knife, a cutting tool used in preparing food</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cleaver" title="Cleaver">Cleaver</a>: A large knife that varies in its shape but usually resembles a rectangular-bladed hatchet. It is used mostly for hacking through bones as a kitchen knife or butcher knife, and can also be used for crushing via its broad side, typically garlic.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electric_knife" title="Electric knife">Electric knife</a>: An electrical device consisting of two serrated blades that are clipped together, providing a sawing action when powered on</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kitchen_knife" title="Kitchen knife">Kitchen knife</a>: Any knife, including the chef's knife, that is intended to be used in food preparation</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oyster_knife" title="Oyster knife">Oyster knife</a>: Has a short, thick blade for prying open oyster shells</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mezzaluna" title="Mezzaluna">Mezzaluna</a>: A two-handled arc-shaped knife used in a rocking motion as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herb_chopper" title="Herb chopper">herb chopper</a> or for cutting other foods</li>
<li>Paring or Coring Knife: A knife with a small but sharp blade used for cutting out the cores from fruit.</li>
<li>Rocker knife is a knife that cuts with a rocking motion, which is primarily used by people whose disabilities prevent them from using a fork and knife simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braddom2010_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braddom2010-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Table_knife" title="Table knife">Table knife</a> or Case knife: A piece of cutlery, either a <a href="/wiki/Butter_knife" title="Butter knife">butter knife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steak_knife" title="Steak knife">steak knife</a>, or both, that is part of a <a href="/wiki/Table_setting" title="Table setting">table setting</a>, accompanying the <a href="/wiki/Fork" title="Fork">fork</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spoon" title="Spoon">spoon</a></li></ul>

<p>As a utility tool the knife can take many forms, including:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bowie_knife" title="Bowie knife">Bowie knife</a>: Commonly, any large sheath knife, or a specific style of large knife popularized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Bowie" title="Jim Bowie">Jim Bowie</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1831" data-file-width="1985" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg/180px-Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg/270px-Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg/360px-Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arbelos_Shoemakers_Knife.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Head knife (Round knife).</div></div></div>
<ul><li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bushcraft_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bushcraft knife (page does not exist)">Bushcraft knife</a>: A sturdy, normally fixed blade knife used while camping in the wilderness.</li>
<li>Camping Knife: A camping knife is used for camping and survival purposes in a wilderness environment.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Head_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Head knife (page does not exist)">Head knife</a> or Round knife: A knife with a semicircular blade used since antiquity to cut leather.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crooked_knife" title="Crooked knife">Crooked knife</a>: Sometimes referred to as a "curved knife", "carving knife" or in the Algonquian language the "mocotaugan" is a utilitarian knife used for carving.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diving_Knives#Tools" title="Diving Knives">Diver's knife</a>: A knife adapted for use in diving and water sports and a necessary part of <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">standard diving dress</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Electrician%27s_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Electrician's knife (page does not exist)">Electrician's knife</a>: A short-bladed knife used to cut electrical insulation.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Folding_Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Folding Knife (page does not exist)">Folding Knife</a>: A folding knife is a knife with one or more blades that fit inside the handle that can still fit in a pocket. It is also known as a jackknife or jack-knife.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hunting_knife" title="Hunting knife">Hunting knife</a>: A knife used to dress large game.</li>
<li>Kiridashi: A small Japanese knife having a chisel grind and a sharp point, used as a general-purpose utility knife.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Linoleum_knife" title="Linoleum knife">Linoleum knife</a>: is a small knife that has a short, stiff blade with a curved point and a handle and is used to cut <a href="/wiki/Linoleum" title="Linoleum">linoleum</a> or other sheet materials.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Machete" title="Machete">Machete</a>: A large heavy knife used to cut through thick vegetation such as sugar cane or jungle undergrowth; it may be used as an offensive weapon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marking_knife" title="Marking knife">Marking knife</a>: A woodworking tool used for marking out workpieces.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Palette_knife" title="Palette knife">Palette knife</a>: A knife, or <a href="/wiki/Frosting_spatula" title="Frosting spatula">frosting spatula</a>, lacking a cutting edge, used by artists for tasks such as mixing and applying paint and in <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a> for spreading <a href="/wiki/Icing_(food)" title="Icing (food)">icing</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paper_knife" title="Paper knife">Paper knife</a>: Or a "letter opener" it is a knife made of metal or plastic, used for opening mail.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocket_knife" title="Pocket knife">Pocket knife</a>: a folding knife designed to be carried in a pants pocket. Subtypes include:
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockback_knife" title="Lockback knife">Lockback knife</a>: a folding knife with a mechanism that locks the blade into the open position, preventing accidental closure while in use</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Multi-tool" title="Multi-tool">Multi-tool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Army_knife" title="Swiss Army knife">Swiss Army knife</a>, which combine a folding knife blade with other tools and implements, such as pliers, scissors, or screwdrivers</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Produce_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Produce knife (page does not exist)">Produce knife</a>: A knife with a rectangular profile and a blunt front edge used by grocers to cut <a href="/wiki/Produce" title="Produce">produce</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rigging_knife" title="Rigging knife">Rigging knife</a>: A knife used to cut <a href="/wiki/Rigging" title="Rigging">rigging</a> in sailing vessels.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scalpel" title="Scalpel">Scalpel</a>: A medical knife, used to perform <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Straight_razor" title="Straight razor">Straight razor</a>: A reusable knife blade used for <a href="/wiki/Shaving" title="Shaving">shaving</a> hair.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Survival_knife" title="Survival knife">Survival knife</a>: A sturdy knife, sometimes with a hollow handle filled with survival equipment.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Switchblade" title="Switchblade">Switchblade</a>: A knife with a folding blade that springs out of the grip when a button or lever on the grip is pressed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Utility_knife" title="Utility knife">Utility knife</a>: A short knife with a replaceable (typically) triangular blade, used for cutting sheet materials including <a href="/wiki/Card_stock" title="Card stock">card stock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">paperboard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corrugated_fiberboard" title="Corrugated fiberboard">corrugated fiberboard</a>, also called a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boxcutter" title="Boxcutter">boxcutter</a> knife</i> or <i>boxcutter</i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_carving_knife" title="Wood carving knife">Wood carving knife</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whittling" title="Whittling">whittling</a> knives: Knives used to shape wood in the arts of <a href="/wiki/Wood_carving" title="Wood carving">wood carving</a> and whittling, often with short, thin replaceable blades for better control.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Craft_knife" title="Craft knife">Craft knife</a>: A scalpel-like form of non-retractable utility knife with a (typically) long handle and a replaceable pointed blade, used for precise, clean cutting in arts and crafts, often called an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-acto_knife" title="X-acto knife">X-acto knife</a></i> in the US and Canada after the popular brand name.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_and_religious_implements">Traditional and religious implements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Traditional and religious implements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Athame" title="Athame">Athame</a>: A typically black-handled and double-edged ritual knife used in <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a> and other derivative forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neopagan" title="Neopagan">Neopagan</a> <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>. (see also <a href="/wiki/Boline" title="Boline">Boline</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dirk" title="Dirk">Dirk</a>: A long bladed thrusting dagger worn by Scottish Highlanders for customary and ceremonial purposes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Katar_(dagger)" title="Katar (dagger)">Katar</a>: An <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Push_dagger" title="Push dagger">push dagger</a> sometimes used ceremonially.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilaya" title="Kilaya">Kilaya</a>: A <a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">dagger</a> used in Tibetan Buddhist rituals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kirpan" title="Kirpan">Kirpan</a>: A ceremonial knife that all baptised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikhs</a> must wear as one of the five visible symbols of the Sikh faith (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kakars" title="Kakars">Kakars</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kris" title="Kris">Kris</a>: A dagger used in Indo-Malay cultures, often by nobility and sometimes in religious rituals</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kukri" title="Kukri">Kukri</a>: A <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepalese</a> knife used as a tool and weapon</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maguro_b%C5%8Dch%C5%8D" title="Maguro bōchō">Maguro bōchō</a>: A traditional Japanese knife with a long specialized blade that is used to fillet large ocean fish.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Puukko" title="Puukko">Puukko</a>: A traditional <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Finland" title="Culture of Finland">Finnish</a> style woodcraft belt-knife used as a tool rather than a weapon</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seax" title="Seax">Seax</a>: A <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> single-edged knife, dagger or short sword used both as a tool and as a weapon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sgian-dubh" title="Sgian-dubh">Sgian-dubh</a>: A small knife traditionally worn with the Highland and Isle dress (<a href="/wiki/Kilt" title="Kilt">Kilt</a>) of Scotland.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ulu" title="Ulu">Ulu</a>: An <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> woman's all-purpose knife.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yakutian_knife" title="Yakutian knife">Yakutian knife</a>: A traditional <a href="/wiki/Yakuts" title="Yakuts">Yakuts</a> knife used as a tool for wood carving and meat or fish cutting. Can be used as a part of yakutian ethnic costume.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rituals_and_superstitions">Rituals and superstitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Rituals and superstitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg"><img alt="Painting of an elderly man holding a young boy's head down with one hand; a winged angel restrains the man's other hand, which grasps a knife. A ram looks on from the side; in the background is a Renaissance landscape with hills, trees, and a castle." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="1085" decoding="async" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg/250px-The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg/375px-The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg/500px-The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Sacrifice_of_Isaac_by_Caravaggio.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Binding_of_Isaac" title="Binding of Isaac">The Sacrifice of Isaac</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Caravaggio" title="Caravaggio">Caravaggio</a>, (1590–1610; Oil on canvas; <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi</a>). <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> is holding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrificial" title="Sacrificial">sacrificial</a> knife.</div></div></div>
<p>The knife plays a significant role in some cultures through <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superstition" title="Superstition">superstition</a>, as the knife was an essential tool for survival since early man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palomar.edu_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-palomar.edu-3">[3]</a></sup> Knife symbols can be found in various cultures to symbolize all stages of life; for example, a knife placed under the bed while giving birth is said to ease the pain, or, stuck into the headboard of a cradle, to protect the baby;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-askyewolfe.com_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-askyewolfe.com-21">[21]</a></sup> knives were included in some Anglo-Saxon burial rites, so the dead would not be defenseless in the next world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The knife plays an important role in some <a href="/wiki/Initiation" title="Initiation">initiation</a> rites, and many cultures perform rituals with a variety of knives, including the ceremonial sacrifices of animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">Samurai</a> warriors, as part of <a href="/wiki/Bushido" title="Bushido">bushido</a>, could perform ritual suicide, or <a href="/wiki/Seppuku" title="Seppuku">seppuku</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Tant%C5%8D" title="Tantō">tantō</a>, a common Japanese knife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Athame" title="Athame">athame</a>, a ceremonial knife, is used in <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a> and derived forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neopagan" title="Neopagan">neopagan</a> <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, a black-handled knife placed under the <a href="/wiki/Pillow" title="Pillow">pillow</a> is used to keep away nightmares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As early as 1646 reference is made to a superstition of laying a knife across another piece of cutlery being a sign of witchcraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A common belief is that if a knife is given as a gift, the relationship of the giver and recipient will be severed. Something such as a small coin, dove or a valuable item is exchanged for the gift, rendering "payment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldsuperstitions.com_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-oldsuperstitions.com-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Knife_legislation" title="Knife legislation">Knife legislation</a></div>
<p>Knives are typically restricted by law, because they are often used in crime, although restrictions vary greatly by country or state and type of knife. For example, some laws prohibit carrying knives in public while other laws prohibit private ownership of certain knives, such as <a href="/wiki/Switchblade" title="Switchblade">switchblades</a>.
</p>
