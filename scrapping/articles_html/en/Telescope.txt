<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Telescope</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Optical instrument that makes distant objects appear magnified</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Telescope_(disambiguation)" title="Telescope (disambiguation)">Telescope (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:100inchHooker.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="904" data-file-width="554" decoding="async" height="359" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/100inchHooker.jpg/220px-100inchHooker.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/100inchHooker.jpg/330px-100inchHooker.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/100inchHooker.jpg/440px-100inchHooker.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:100inchHooker.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 100-inch (2.54 m) Hooker <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a> at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">Mount Wilson Observatory</a> near Los Angeles, USA, used by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> to measure galaxy redshifts and discover the general expansion of the universe.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>telescope</b> is a device used to observe distant objects by their emission, <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflection</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Originally meaning only an <a href="/wiki/Optical_instrument" title="Optical instrument">optical instrument</a> using <a href="/wiki/Lens" title="Lens">lenses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curved_mirror" title="Curved mirror">curved mirrors</a>, or a combination of both to observe distant objects, the word <i>telescope</i> now refers to a wide range of instruments capable of detecting different regions of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>, and in some cases other types of detectors.
</p><p>The first known practical telescopes were <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescopes</a> with glass <a href="/wiki/Lens" title="Lens">lenses</a> and were invented in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> at the beginning of the 17th century. They were used for both terrestrial applications and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a>, which uses mirrors to collect and focus light, was invented within a few decades of the first refracting telescope.
</p><p>In the 20th century, many new types of telescopes were invented, including <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a> in the 1930s and <a href="/wiki/Infrared_telescope" title="Infrared telescope">infrared telescopes</a> in the 1960s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>telescope</i> was coined in 1611 by the Greek mathematician <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Demisiani" title="Giovanni Demisiani">Giovanni Demisiani</a> for one of <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>'s instruments presented at a banquet at the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei" title="Accademia dei Lincei">Accademia dei Lincei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starry_Messenger" title="Starry Messenger">Starry Messenger</a></i>, Galileo had used the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> term <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">perspicillum</i></span>. The root of the word is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> τῆλε, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanized" title="Romanized">romanized</a> <i>tele</i> 'far' and σκοπεῖν, <i>skopein</i> 'to look or see'; τηλεσκόπος, <i>teleskopos</i> 'far-seeing'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_telescope" title="History of the telescope">History of the telescope</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galileu_Galilei_1608-2008%3D400_anos_do_telesc%C3%B3pio_-_panoramio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="325" data-file-width="270" decoding="async" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Galileu_Galilei_1608-2008%3D400_anos_do_telesc%C3%B3pio_-_panoramio.jpg/220px-Galileu_Galilei_1608-2008%3D400_anos_do_telesc%C3%B3pio_-_panoramio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1a/Galileu_Galilei_1608-2008%3D400_anos_do_telesc%C3%B3pio_-_panoramio.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galileu_Galilei_1608-2008%3D400_anos_do_telesc%C3%B3pio_-_panoramio.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>17th century telescope</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest existing record of a telescope was a 1608 patent submitted to the government in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> by Middelburg spectacle maker <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lipperhey" title="Hans Lipperhey">Hans Lipperhey</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The actual inventor is unknown but word of it spread through Europe. <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo</a> heard about it and, in 1609, built his own version, and made his telescopic observations of celestial objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The idea that the <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)" title="Objective (optics)">objective</a>, or light-gathering element, could be a mirror instead of a lens was being investigated soon after the invention of the refracting telescope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The potential advantages of using <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic mirrors</a>—reduction of <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">spherical aberration</a> and no <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">chromatic aberration</a>—led to many proposed designs and several attempts to build <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescopes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1668, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> built the first practical reflecting telescope, of a design which now bears his name, the <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_telescope" title="Newtonian telescope">Newtonian reflector</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Achromatic_lens" title="Achromatic lens">achromatic lens</a> in 1733 partially corrected color aberrations present in the simple lens<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and enabled the construction of shorter, more functional refracting telescopes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Reflecting telescopes, though not limited by the color problems seen in refractors, were hampered by the use of fast tarnishing <a href="/wiki/Speculum_metal" title="Speculum metal">speculum metal</a> mirrors employed during the 18th and early 19th century—a problem alleviated by the introduction of silver coated glass mirrors in 1857, and aluminized mirrors in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The maximum physical size limit for refracting telescopes is about 1 meter (39 inches), dictating that the vast majority of large optical researching telescopes built since the turn of the 20th century have been reflectors. The largest reflecting telescopes currently have objectives larger than 10 meters (33 feet), and work is underway on several 30-40m designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 20th century also saw the development of telescopes that worked in a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> from <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_telescope" title="Gamma-ray telescope">gamma-rays</a>. The first purpose-built radio telescope went into operation in 1937. Since then, a large variety of complex astronomical instruments have been developed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_space">In space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">Space telescope</a></div>
<p>Since the atmosphere is opaque for most of the electromagnetic spectrum, only a few bands can be observed from the Earth's surface. These bands are visible – near-infrared and a portion of the radio-wave part of the spectrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For this reason there are no X-ray or far-infrared ground-based telescopes as these have to be observed from orbit. Even if a wavelength is observable from the ground, it might still be advantageous to place a telescope on a satellite due to issues such as clouds, <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">astronomical seeing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution#Effect_on_astronomy" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The disadvantages of launching a space telescope include cost, size, maintainability and upgradability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_electromagnetic_spectrum">By electromagnetic spectrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: By electromagnetic spectrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png"><img alt="Radio, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, x-ray and gamma ray" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1076" data-file-width="1364" decoding="async" height="316" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png/400px-Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png/600px-Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png/800px-Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crab_Nebula_in_Multiple_Wavelengths_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Six views of the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">Crab Nebula</a> at different wavelengths of light</div></div></div>
<p>The name "telescope" covers a wide range of instruments. Most detect <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, but there are major differences in how astronomers must go about collecting light (electromagnetic radiation) in different frequency bands.
</p><p>As wavelengths become longer, it becomes easier to use antenna technology to interact with electromagnetic radiation (although it is possible to make very tiny antenna). The near-infrared can be collected much like visible light, however in the far-infrared and submillimetre range, telescopes can operate more like a radio telescope. For example, the <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell_Telescope" title="James Clerk Maxwell Telescope">James Clerk Maxwell Telescope</a> observes from wavelengths from 3 μm (0.003 mm) to 2000 μm (2 mm), but uses a parabolic aluminum antenna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a>, observing from about 3 μm (0.003 mm) to 180 μm (0.18 mm) uses a mirror (reflecting optics). Also using reflecting optics, the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> with <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_Camera_3" title="Wide Field Camera 3">Wide Field Camera 3</a> can observe in the frequency range from about 0.2 μm (0.0002 mm) to 1.7 μm (0.0017 mm) (from ultra-violet to infrared light).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>With photons of the shorter wavelengths, with the higher frequencies, glancing-incident optics, rather than fully reflecting optics are used. Telescopes such as <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">TRACE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> use special mirrors to reflect <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">extreme ultraviolet</a>, producing higher resolution and brighter images than are otherwise possible. A larger aperture does not just mean that more light is collected, it also enables a finer angular resolution.
</p><p>Telescopes may also be classified by location: ground telescope, <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">space telescope</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_telescope" title="Flying telescope">flying telescope</a>. They may also be classified by whether they are operated by <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">professional astronomers</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomer" title="Amateur astronomer">amateur astronomers</a>. A vehicle or permanent campus containing one or more telescopes or other instruments is called an <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_and_submilimeter">Radio and submilimeter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radio and submilimeter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">Radio telescope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">Radio astronomy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Submillimetre_astronomy" title="Submillimetre astronomy">Submillimetre astronomy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1700" data-file-width="2600" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ALMA_Greater_than_the_Sum_of_its_Parts_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three radio telescopes belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">Atacama Large Millimeter Array</a></div></div></div>
<p>Radio telescopes are <a href="/wiki/Directional_antenna" title="Directional antenna">directional</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_antennas" title="Radio antennas">radio antennas</a> that typically employ a large dish to collect radio waves. The dishes are sometimes constructed of a conductive wire mesh whose openings are smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> being observed.
</p><p>Unlike an optical telescope, which produces a magnified image of the patch of sky being observed, a traditional radio telescope dish contains a single receiver and records a single time-varying signal characteristic of the observed region; this signal may be sampled at various frequencies. In some newer radio telescope designs, a single dish contains an array of several receivers; this is known as a <a href="/wiki/Focal-plane_array_(radio_astronomy)" title="Focal-plane array (radio astronomy)">focal-plane array</a>.
</p><p>By collecting and correlating signals simultaneously received by several dishes, high-resolution images can be computed. Such multi-dish arrays are known as <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_interferometer" title="Astronomical interferometer">astronomical interferometers</a> and the technique is called <a href="/wiki/Aperture_synthesis" title="Aperture synthesis">aperture synthesis</a>. The 'virtual' apertures of these arrays are similar in size to the distance between the telescopes. As of 2005, the record array size is many times the diameter of the Earth – using space-based <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Very-long-baseline-interferometry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Very-long-baseline-interferometry (page does not exist)">very-long-baseline-interferometry</a> (VLBI) telescopes such as the Japanese <a href="/wiki/HALCA" title="HALCA">HALCA</a> (Highly Advanced Laboratory for Communications and Astronomy) VSOP (VLBI Space Observatory Program) satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aperture synthesis is now also being applied to optical telescopes using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_interferometry#Astronomical_optical_interferometry" title="Optical interferometry">optical interferometers</a> (arrays of optical telescopes) and <a href="/wiki/Aperture_masking_interferometry" title="Aperture masking interferometry">aperture masking interferometry</a> at single reflecting telescopes.
</p><p>Radio telescopes are also used to collect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radiation" title="Microwave radiation">microwave radiation</a>, which has the advantage of being able to pass through the atmosphere and interstellar gas and dust clouds.
</p><p>Some radio telescopes such as the <a href="/wiki/Allen_Telescope_Array" title="Allen Telescope Array">Allen Telescope Array</a> are used by programs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_for_Extraterrestrial_Intelligence" title="Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence">SETI</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> to search for extraterrestrial life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared">Infrared</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infrared">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Infrared_telescope" title="Infrared telescope">Infrared telescope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">Infrared astronomy</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visible_light">Visible light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Visible light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">Optical telescope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visible-light_astronomy" title="Visible-light astronomy">Visible-light astronomy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png"><img alt="Dome-like telescope with extruding mirror mount" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1300" data-file-width="1300" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png/220px-Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png/330px-Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png/440px-Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auxilary_VLT_telescope.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of four auxiliary telescopes belong to the <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a> array</div></div></div>
<p>An optical telescope gathers and <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focuses</a> light mainly from the visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Optical telescopes increase the apparent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_size" title="Angular size">angular size</a> of distant objects as well as their apparent <a href="/wiki/Brightness" title="Brightness">brightness</a>. For the image to be observed, photographed, studied, and sent to a computer, telescopes work by employing one or more curved optical elements, usually made from glass <a href="/wiki/Lens" title="Lens">lenses</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>, to gather light and other electromagnetic radiation to bring that light or radiation to a focal point. Optical telescopes are used for <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and in many non-astronomical instruments, including: <i><a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">theodolites</a></i> (including <i>transits</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Spotting_scope" title="Spotting scope">spotting scopes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Monocular" title="Monocular">monoculars</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">camera lenses</a></i>, and <i>spyglasses</i>. There are three main optical types:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescope</a> which uses lenses to form an image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a> which uses an arrangement of mirrors to form an image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catadioptric#Catadioptric_telescopes" title="Catadioptric">catadioptric telescope</a> which uses mirrors combined with lenses to form an image.</li></ul>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_imager" title="Fresnel imager">Fresnel imager</a> is a proposed ultra-lightweight design for a space telescope that uses a <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a> to focus light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beyond these basic optical types there are many sub-types of varying optical design classified by the task they perform such as <a href="/wiki/Astrograph" title="Astrograph">astrographs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Comet_seeker" title="Comet seeker">comet seekers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_telescope" title="Solar telescope">solar telescopes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultraviolet">Ultraviolet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ultraviolet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_astronomy" title="Ultraviolet astronomy">Ultraviolet astronomy</a></div>
<p>Most ultraviolet light is absorbed by the Earth's atmosphere, so observations at these wavelengths must be performed from the upper atmosphere or from space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X-ray">X-ray</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: X-ray">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/X-ray_telescope" title="X-ray telescope">X-ray telescope</a> and <a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy" title="X-ray astronomy">X-ray astronomy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2667" data-file-width="3247" decoding="async" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg/220px-ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg/330px-ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg/440px-ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ASTRO-H_soft_X-ray_mirror.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hitomi_(satellite)" title="Hitomi (satellite)"><i>Hitomi</i> telescope</a>'s X-ray focusing mirror, consisting of over two hundred <a href="/wiki/Concentric_objects" title="Concentric objects">concentric</a> aluminium shells</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> are much harder to collect and focus than electromagnetic radiation of longer wavelengths. X-ray telescopes can use <a href="/wiki/X-ray_optics" title="X-ray optics">X-ray optics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">Wolter telescopes</a> composed of ring-shaped 'glancing' mirrors made of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> that are able to reflect the rays just a few <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a>. The mirrors are usually a section of a rotated <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabola</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbola" title="Hyperbola">hyperbola</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a>. In 1952, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Wolter" title="Hans Wolter">Hans Wolter</a> outlined 3 ways a telescope could be built using only this kind of mirror.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Examples of space observatories using this type of telescope are the <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Observatory" title="Einstein Observatory">Einstein Observatory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/ROSAT" title="ROSAT">ROSAT</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra X-ray Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 2012 the <a href="/wiki/NuSTAR" title="NuSTAR">NuSTAR</a> X-ray Telescope was launched which uses <a href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">Wolter telescope</a> design optics at the end of a long <a href="/wiki/Deployable_structure" title="Deployable structure">deployable</a> mast to enable photon energies of 79 keV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nustar1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nustar1-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma_ray">Gamma ray</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gamma ray">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_astronomy" title="Gamma-ray astronomy">Gamma-ray astronomy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_(S37-99-056).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4134" data-file-width="4134" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_%28S37-99-056%29.jpg/220px-Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_%28S37-99-056%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_%28S37-99-056%29.jpg/330px-Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_%28S37-99-056%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_%28S37-99-056%29.jpg/440px-Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_%28S37-99-056%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory_grappeled_by_Atlantis_(S37-99-056).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory" title="Compton Gamma Ray Observatory">Compton Gamma Ray Observatory</a> released into orbit by the Space Shuttle in 1991</div></div></div>
<p>Higher energy X-ray and gamma ray telescopes refrain from focusing completely and use <a href="/wiki/Coded_aperture" title="Coded aperture">coded aperture</a> masks: the patterns of the shadow the mask creates can be reconstructed to form an image.
</p><p>X-ray and Gamma-ray telescopes are usually installed on high-flying balloons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> or Earth-orbiting <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> is opaque to this part of the electromagnetic spectrum. An example of this type of telescope is the <a href="/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope" title="Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope</a> which was launched in June 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The detection of very high energy gamma rays, with shorter wavelength and higher frequency than regular gamma rays, requires further specialization. An example of this type of observatory is the ground based telescope <a href="/wiki/VERITAS" title="VERITAS">VERITAS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>A discovery in 2012 may allow focusing gamma-ray telescopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wogan_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-wogan-47">[47]</a></sup> At photon energies greater than 700 keV, the index of refraction starts to increase again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wogan_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-wogan-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
