<p><b>Measurement</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Quantification_(science)" title="Quantification (science)">quantification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Variable_and_attribute_(research)" title="Variable and attribute (research)">attributes</a> of an object or event, which can be used to compare with other objects or events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pedhazur_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pedhazur-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bipm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bipm-2">[2]</a></sup>
In other words, measurement is a process of determining how large or small a <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">physical quantity</a> is as compared to a basic reference quantity of the same kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_and_Freedman_University_Physics_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young_and_Freedman_University_Physics-3">[3]</a></sup>
The scope and application of measurement are dependent on the context and discipline. In <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural sciences</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, measurements do not apply to <a href="/wiki/Level_of_measurement#Nominal_level" title="Level of measurement">nominal properties</a> of objects or events, which is consistent with the guidelines of the <i>International vocabulary of metrology</i> published by the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bipm_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-bipm-2">[2]</a></sup> However, in other fields such as <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Sciences" title="Social Sciences">social</a> and <a href="/wiki/Behavioural_sciences" title="Behavioural sciences">behavioural sciences</a>, measurements can have <a href="/wiki/Level_of_measurement" title="Level of measurement">multiple levels</a>, which would include nominal, ordinal, interval and ratio scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pedhazur_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pedhazur-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koch_2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koch_2008-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Measurement is a cornerstone of <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_research" title="Quantitative research">quantitative research</a> in many disciplines. Historically, many <a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">measurement systems</a> existed for the varied fields of human existence to facilitate comparisons in these fields. Often these were achieved by local agreements between trading partners or collaborators. Since the 18th century, developments progressed towards unifying, widely accepted standards that resulted in the modern <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI). This system reduces all physical measurements to a mathematical combination of seven base units. The science of measurement is pursued in the field of <a href="/wiki/Metrology" title="Metrology">metrology</a>.
</p><p>Measurement is defined as the process of comparison of an unknown quantity with a known or standard quantity.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The measurement of a property may be categorized by the following criteria: <a href="/wiki/Level_of_measurement" title="Level of measurement">type</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)" title="Magnitude (mathematics)">magnitude</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">unit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They enable unambiguous comparisons between measurements.
</p>
<ul><li>The <i>level</i> of measurement is a taxonomy for the methodological character of a comparison. For example, two states of a property may be compared by ratio, difference, or ordinal preference. The type is commonly not explicitly expressed, but implicit in the definition of a measurement procedure.</li>
<li>The <i>magnitude</i> is the numerical value of the characterization, usually obtained with a suitably chosen <a href="/wiki/Measuring_instrument" title="Measuring instrument">measuring instrument</a>.</li>
<li>A <i>unit</i> assigns a mathematical weighting factor to the magnitude that is derived as a ratio to the property of an artifact used as standard or a natural physical quantity.</li>
<li>An <i>uncertainty</i> represents the <a href="/wiki/Observational_error" title="Observational error">random and systemic errors</a> of the measurement procedure; it indicates a confidence level in the measurement. Errors are evaluated by methodically repeating measurements and considering the <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">accuracy and precision</a> of the measuring instrument.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization_of_measurement_units">Standardization of measurement units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standardization of measurement units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Measurements most commonly use the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI) as a comparison framework. The system defines seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_base_units" title="SI base units">fundamental units</a>: <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candela" title="Candela">candela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">ampere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a>. All of these units are defined without reference to a particular physical object which serves as a standard. Artifact-free definitions fix measurements at an exact value related to a <a href="/wiki/Physical_constant" title="Physical constant">physical constant</a> or other invariable phenomena in nature, in contrast to standard artifacts which are subject to deterioration or destruction. Instead, the measurement unit can only ever change through increased accuracy in determining the value of the constant it is tied to.
</p>

<p>The first proposal to tie an SI base unit to an experimental standard independent of fiat was by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Charles Sanders Peirce</a> (1839–1914),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> who proposed to define the metre in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This directly influenced the <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson–Morley experiment">Michelson–Morley experiment</a>; Michelson and Morley cite Peirce, and improve on his method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards">Standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the exception of a few fundamental <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantum</a> constants, units of measurement are derived from historical agreements. Nothing inherent in nature dictates that an <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inch</a> has to be a certain length, nor that a <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mile</a> is a better measure of distance than a <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometre</a>. Over the course of human history, however, first for convenience and then for necessity, standards of measurement evolved so that communities would have certain common benchmarks. Laws regulating measurement were originally developed to prevent fraud in commerce.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">Units of measurement</a> are generally defined on a scientific basis, overseen by governmental or independent agencies, and established in international treaties, pre-eminent of which is the <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> (CGPM), established in 1875 by the <a href="/wiki/Metre_Convention" title="Metre Convention">Metre Convention</a>, overseeing the International System of Units (SI).  For example, the metre was redefined in 1983 by the CGPM in terms of the speed of light, the kilogram was redefined in 2019 in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">Planck constant</a> and the international yard was defined in 1960 by the governments of the United States, United Kingdom, Australia and South Africa as being <i>exactly</i> 0.9144 metres.
</p><p>In the United States, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIST" title="NIST">NIST</a>), a division of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">United States Department of Commerce</a>, regulates commercial measurements. In the United Kingdom, the role is performed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Physical_Laboratory_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Physical Laboratory (United Kingdom)">National Physical Laboratory</a> (NPL), in Australia by the <a href="/wiki/National_Measurement_Institute,_Australia" title="National Measurement Institute, Australia">National Measurement Institute</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in South Africa by the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research" title="Council for Scientific and Industrial Research">Council for Scientific and Industrial Research</a> and in India the <a href="/wiki/National_Physical_Laboratory_of_India" title="National Physical Laboratory of India">National Physical Laboratory of India</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units_and_systems">Units and systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Units and systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">Unit of measurement</a> and <a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">System of measurement</a></div><p>
unit is known or standard quantity in terms of which other physical quantities are measured.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1066" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg/170px-MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg/255px-MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg/340px-MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Baby_bottle" title="Baby bottle">baby bottle</a> that measures in three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">measurement systems</a>—<a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_unit" title="Imperial unit">imperial (UK)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">US customary</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_and_US_customary_systems">Imperial and US customary systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Imperial and US customary systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Imperial_and_US_customary_measurement_systems" title="Imperial and US customary measurement systems">Imperial and US customary measurement systems</a></div>
<p>Before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit">SI units</a> were widely adopted around the world, the British systems of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_unit" title="English unit">English units</a> and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_unit" title="Imperial unit">imperial units</a> were used in Britain, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> and the United States.  The system came to be known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._customary_units" title="U.S. customary units">U.S. customary units</a> in the United States and is still in use there and in a few <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> countries.  These various systems of measurement have at times been called <i>foot-pound-second</i> systems after the Imperial units for length, weight and time even though the tons, hundredweights, gallons, and nautical miles, for example, are different for the U.S. units.  Many Imperial units remain in use in Britain, which has officially switched to the SI system—with a few exceptions such as road signs, which are still in miles. Draught beer and cider must be sold by the imperial pint, and milk in returnable bottles can be sold by the imperial pint. Many people measure their height in feet and inches and their weight in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_(imperial_mass)" title="Stone (imperial mass)">stone</a> and pounds, to give just a few examples. Imperial units are used in many other places, for example, in many Commonwealth countries that are considered metricated, land area is measured in acres and floor space in square feet, particularly for commercial transactions (rather than government statistics). Similarly, gasoline is sold by the gallon in many countries that are considered metricated.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_system">Metric system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metric system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> is a decimal <a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">system of measurement</a> based on its units for length, the metre and for mass, the kilogram. It exists in several variations, with different choices of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement#Base_and_derived_units" title="Units of measurement">base units</a>, though these do not affect its day-to-day use. Since the 1960s, the International System of Units (SI) is the internationally recognised metric system. Metric units of mass, length, and electricity are widely used around the world for both everyday and scientific purposes.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_System_of_Units">International System of Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: International System of Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (abbreviated as SI from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a> name <i>Système International d'Unités</i>) is the modern revision of the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a>. It is the world's most widely used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_of_units" title="System of units">system of units</a>, both in everyday <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>. The SI was developed in 1960 from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre%E2%80%93kilogram%E2%80%93second" title="Metre–kilogram–second">metre–kilogram–second</a> (MKS) system, rather than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second" title="Centimetre–gram–second">centimetre–gram–second</a> (CGS) system, which, in turn, had many variants. <p>In the SI, base units are the simple measurements for time, length, mass, temperature, amount of substance, electric current and light intensity.  Derived units are constructed from the base units, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>, i.e. the unit for power, is defined from the base units as m<sup>2</sup>·kg·s<sup>−3</sup>. Other physical properties may be measured in compound units, such as material density, measured in kg/m<sup>3</sup>.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Converting_prefixes">Converting prefixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Converting prefixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The SI allows easy multiplication when switching among units having the same base but different prefixes. To convert from metres to centimetres it is only necessary to multiply the number of metres by 100, since there are 100 centimetres in a metre. Inversely, to switch from centimetres to metres one multiplies the number of centimetres by 0.01 or divides the number of centimetres by 100.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Length">Length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Length_measurement" title="Length measurement">Length measurement</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_length,_distance,_or_range_measuring_devices" title="List of length, distance, or range measuring devices">List of length, distance, or range measuring devices</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CarpentersRule.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="356" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/CarpentersRule.png/220px-CarpentersRule.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/CarpentersRule.png/330px-CarpentersRule.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/CarpentersRule.png/440px-CarpentersRule.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CarpentersRule.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 2-metre carpenter's <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a> or rule is a tool used in, for example, <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">technical drawing</a>, engineering, and carpentry, to measure lengths or distances or to draw straight lines.  Strictly speaking, the <i>ruler</i> is the instrument used to <b>rule</b> straight lines and the calibrated instrument used for determining length is called a <i>measure</i>, however common usage calls both instruments <i>rulers</i> and the special name <i>straightedge</i> is used for an unmarked rule.  The use of the word <i>measure</i>, in the sense of a measuring instrument, only survives in the phrase <i>tape measure</i>, an instrument that can be used to measure but cannot be used to draw straight lines.  As can be seen in the photographs on this page, a two-metre carpenter's rule can be folded down to a length of only 20 centimetres, to easily fit in a pocket, and a five-metre-long tape measure easily retracts to fit within a small housing.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_trades">Building trades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Building trades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Australian building trades adopted the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> in 1966 and the units used for measurement of length are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metres" title="Metres">metres</a> (m) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimetres" title="Millimetres">millimetres</a> (mm). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centimetres" title="Centimetres">Centimetres</a> (cm) are avoided as they cause confusion when reading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plans" title="Plans">plans</a>. For example, the length two and a half metres is usually recorded as 2500 mm or 2.5 m; it would be considered non-standard to record this length as 250 cm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Surveyor.27s_trade"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Surveyor's_trade">Surveyor's trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Surveyor's trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>American surveyors use a decimal-based system of measurement devised by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Gunter" title="Edmund Gunter">Edmund Gunter</a> in 1620. The base unit is <a href="/wiki/Gunter%27s_chain" title="Gunter's chain">Gunter's chain</a> of 66 feet (20 m) which is subdivided into 4 rods, each of 16.5 ft or 100 links of 0.66 feet. A link is abbreviated "lk",  and links "lks", in old deeds and land surveys done for the government.
</p><p>The <i>Standard Method of Measurement</i> (SMM) published by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Chartered_Surveyors" title="Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors">Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors</a> (RICS) consisted of classification tables and rules of measurement, allowing use of a uniform basis for measuring building works. It was first published in 1922, superseding a Scottish Standard Method of Measurement which had been published in 1915. Its seventh edition (SMM7) was first published in 1988 and revised in 1998. SMM7 was replaced by the <i>New Rules of Measurement</i>, volume 2 (NRM2), which were published in April 2012 by the RICS Quantity Surveying and Construction Professional Group and became operational on 1 January 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> NRM2 has been in general use since July 2013.
</p><p>SMM7 was accompanied by the Code of Procedure for the Measurement of Building Works (the SMM7 Measurement Code). Whilst SMM7 could have a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contractual</a> status within a project, for example in the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Contracts_Tribunal" title="Joint Contracts Tribunal">JCT</a> Standard form of Building Contract), the Measurement Code was not mandatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>NRM2 Is the second of three component parts within the NRM suite:
</p>
<ul><li>NRM1 - Order of cost estimating and cost planning for capital building works</li>
<li>NRM2 - Detailed measurement for building works</li>
<li>NRM3 - Order of cost estimating and cost planning for building maintenance works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time">Time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">Time</a></div>
<p>Time is an abstract measurement of elemental changes over a non spatial continuum.  It is denoted by numbers and/or named periods such as <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hours</a>, <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">days</a>, <a href="/wiki/Week" title="Week">weeks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">months</a> and <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">years</a>. It is an apparently irreversible series of occurrences within this non spatial continuum.  It is also used to denote an interval between two relative points on this continuum.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass">Mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale" title="Weighing scale">Weighing scale</a></div>
<p><i>Mass</i> refers to the intrinsic property of all material objects to resist changes in their momentum.  <i>Weight</i>, on the other hand, refers to the downward force produced when a mass is in a gravitational field.  In <a href="/wiki/Free_fall" title="Free fall">free fall</a>, (no net gravitational forces) objects lack weight but retain their mass.  The Imperial units of mass include the <a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">ounce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pound</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">ton</a>.  The metric units <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> and kilogram are units of mass.
</p><p>One device for measuring weight or mass is called a weighing scale or, often, simply a <i>scale</i>.  A spring scale measures force but not mass, a balance compares weight, both require a gravitational field to operate.  Some of the most accurate instruments for measuring weight or mass are based on load cells with a digital read-out, but require a gravitational field to function and would not work in free fall.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Measurement_in_economics" title="Measurement in economics">Measurement in economics</a></div>
<p>The measures used in economics are physical measures, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominal_price" title="Nominal price">nominal price</a> value measures and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_price" title="Real price">real price</a> measures. These measures differ from one another by the variables they measure and by the variables excluded from measurements.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Survey_research">Survey research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Survey research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Survey_methodology" title="Survey methodology">Survey methodology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EMMA_experiment.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3024" data-file-width="3952" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMMA_experiment.jpg/220px-EMMA_experiment.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMMA_experiment.jpg/330px-EMMA_experiment.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMMA_experiment.jpg/440px-EMMA_experiment.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EMMA_experiment.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Measurement station C of EMMA experiment situated at the depth of 75 meters in the <a href="/wiki/Pyh%C3%A4salmi_Mine" title="Pyhäsalmi Mine">Pyhäsalmi Mine</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In the field of survey research, measures are taken from individual attitudes, values, and behavior using <a href="/wiki/Questionnaire" title="Questionnaire">questionnaires</a> as a measurement instrument. As all other measurements, measurement in survey research is also vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Observational_error" title="Observational error">measurement error</a>, i.e. the departure from the true value of the measurement and the value provided using the measurement instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In substantive survey research, measurement error can lead to biased conclusions and wrongly estimated effects. In order to get accurate results, when measurement errors appear, the results need to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correction_for_attenuation" title="Correction for attenuation">corrected for measurement errors</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exactness_designation">Exactness designation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Exactness designation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following rules generally apply for displaying the exactness of measurements:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>All non-0 digits and any 0s appearing between them are significant for the exactness of any number. For example, the number 12000 has two significant digits, and has implied limits of 11500 and 12500.</li>
<li>Additional 0s may be added after a <a href="/wiki/Decimal_separator" title="Decimal separator">decimal separator</a> to denote a greater exactness, increasing the number of decimals. For example, 1 has implied limits of 0.5 and 1.5 whereas 1.0 has implied limits 0.95 and 1.05.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulties">Difficulties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Difficulties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since accurate measurement is essential in many fields, and since all measurements are necessarily approximations, a great deal of effort must be taken to make measurements as accurate as possible. For example, consider the <a href="/wiki/History_of_timekeeping_devices" title="History of timekeeping devices">problem of measuring the time</a> it takes an object to fall a distance of one metre (about 39 <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">in</a>).  Using physics, it can be shown that, in the gravitational field of the Earth, it should take any object about 0.45 second to fall one metre. However, the following are just some of the sources of <a href="/wiki/Measurement_uncertainty" title="Measurement uncertainty">error</a> that arise:
</p>
<ul><li>This computation used for the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">acceleration of gravity</a> 9.8 metres per second squared (32 ft/s<sup>2</sup>). But this measurement is not exact, but only precise to two significant digits.</li>
<li>The Earth's gravitational field varies slightly depending on height above sea level and other factors.</li>
<li>The computation of 0.45 seconds involved extracting a <a href="/wiki/Square_root" title="Square root">square root</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_operation" title="Mathematical operation">mathematical operation</a> that required rounding off to some number of significant digits, in this case two significant digits.</li></ul>
<p>Additionally, other sources of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental_error" title="Experimental error">experimental error</a> include:
</p>
<ul><li>carelessness,</li>
<li>determining of the exact time at which the object is released and the exact time it hits the ground,</li>
<li>measurement of the height and the measurement of the time both involve some error,</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_resistance" title="Air resistance">Air resistance</a>.</li>
<li>posture of human participants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Scientific experiments must be carried out with great care to eliminate as much error as possible, and to keep error estimates realistic.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_and_theories">Definitions and theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Definitions and theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_definition">Classical definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Classical definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the classical definition, which is standard throughout the physical sciences, <i>measurement</i> is the determination or estimation of ratios of quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michell,_J._1999_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michell,_J._1999-18">[18]</a></sup> Quantity and measurement are mutually defined: quantitative attributes are those possible to measure, at least in principle. The classical concept of quantity can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/John_Wallis" title="John Wallis">John Wallis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>, and was foreshadowed in <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid's Elements">Euclid's Elements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michell,_J._1999_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Michell,_J._1999-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Representational_theory">Representational theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Representational theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the representational theory, <i>measurement</i> is defined as "the correlation of numbers with entities that are not numbers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The most technically elaborated form of representational theory is also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Additive_conjoint_measurement" title="Additive conjoint measurement">additive conjoint measurement</a>. In this form of representational theory, numbers are assigned based on correspondences or similarities between the structure of number systems and the structure of qualitative systems. A property is quantitative if such structural similarities can be established. In weaker forms of representational theory, such as that implicit within the work of <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Smith_Stevens" title="Stanley Smith Stevens">Stanley Smith Stevens</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> numbers need only be assigned according to a rule.
</p><p>The concept of measurement is often misunderstood as merely the assignment of a value, but it is possible to assign a value in a way that is not a measurement in terms of the requirements of additive conjoint measurement.  One may assign a value to a person's height, but unless it can be established that there is a correlation between measurements of height and empirical relations, it is not a measurement according to additive conjoint measurement theory. Likewise, computing and assigning arbitrary values, like the "book value" of an asset in accounting, is not a measurement because it does not satisfy the necessary criteria.
</p><p>Three type of Representational theory
</p><p><b>1) Empirical relation</b>
</p><p>In science, an <b>empirical relationship</b> is a <b>relationship</b> or correlation based solely on <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a> rather than theory. An <b>empirical relationship</b> requires only confirmatory data irrespective of theoretical basis
</p><p><b>2) The rule of mapping</b>
</p><p>The real world is the Domain of mapping, and the mathematical world is the range. when we map the attribute to mathematical system, we have many choice for mapping and the range
</p><p><b>3) The representation condition of measurement</b>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_theory">Information theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Information theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">Information theory</a> recognises that all data are inexact and statistical in nature.  Thus the definition of measurement is: "A set of observations that reduce uncertainty where the result is expressed as a quantity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  This definition is implied in what scientists actually do when they measure something and report both the <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">mean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> of the measurements.  In practical terms, one begins with an initial guess as to the expected value of a quantity, and then, using various methods and instruments, reduces the uncertainty in the value.  Note that in this view, unlike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positivist" title="Positivist">positivist</a> representational theory, all measurements are uncertain, so instead of assigning one value, a range of values is assigned to a measurement.  This also implies that there is not a clear or neat distinction between <a href="/wiki/Approximation" title="Approximation">estimation</a> and measurement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_mechanics">Quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, a measurement is an action that determines a particular property (position, momentum, energy, etc.) of a quantum system. Before a measurement is made, a quantum system is simultaneously described by all values in a range of possible values, where the probability of measuring each value is determined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction" title="Wavefunction">wavefunction</a> of the system. When a measurement is performed, the wavefunction of the quantum system "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">collapses</a>" to a single, definite value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The unambiguous meaning of the <a href="/wiki/Measurement_problem" title="Measurement problem">measurement problem</a> is an unresolved fundamental problem in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In biology, there is generally no well established theory of measurement. However, the importance of the theoretical context is emphasized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Moreover, the theoretical context stemming from the theory of evolution leads to articulate the theory of measurement and historicity as a fundamental notion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
Among the most developed fields of measurement in biology are the measurement of genetic diversity and species diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
