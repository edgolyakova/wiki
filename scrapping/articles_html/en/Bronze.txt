<p><b>Bronze</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> consisting primarily of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, commonly with about 12–12.5% <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and often with the addition of other metals (including <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>) and sometimes non-metals, such as <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalloids" title="Metalloids">metalloids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. These additions produce a range of alloys that may be harder than copper alone, or have other useful properties, such as <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_tensile_strength" title="Ultimate tensile strength">strength</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Machinability" title="Machinability">machinability</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Three-age_system" title="Three-age system">archaeological period</a> in which bronze was the hardest metal in widespread use is known as the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. The beginning of the Bronze Age in western <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and India is conventionally dated to the mid-4th <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennium</a> BCE, and to the early 2nd millennium BCE in China;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> elsewhere it gradually spread across regions. The Bronze Age was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> starting from about 1300 BCE and reaching most of Eurasia by about 500 BCE, although bronze continued to be much more widely used than it is in modern times.
</p><p>Because historical artworks were often made of <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brasses</a> (copper and zinc) and bronzes with different compositions, modern museum and scholarly descriptions of older artworks increasingly use the generalized term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_alloy" title="Copper alloy">copper alloy</a>" instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5003" data-file-width="3752" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg/170px-HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg/255px-HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg/340px-HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HouMuWuDingFullView.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Houmuwu_ding" title="Houmuwu ding">Houmuwu</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Houmuwu_ding" title="Houmuwu ding">ding</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">后母戊鼎</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Hòumǔwù dǐng</span></i>), the heaviest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_ritual_bronze" title="Chinese ritual bronze">Chinese ritual bronze</a> ever found; 1300–1046 BCE; <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_China" title="National Museum of China">National Museum of China</a> (Beijing). This ding's name is based on the inscription in the bronze interior wall, which reads <span title="Chinese-language text"><i lang="zh-Latn">Hòumǔwù</i></span>, meaning 'Queen Mother Wu'</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>bronze</i> (1730–1740) is borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> <span title="Middle French (ca. 1400-1600)-language text"><i lang="frm">bronze</i></span> (1511), itself borrowed from Italian <span title="Italian-language text"><i lang="it">bronzo</i></span> 'bell metal, brass' (13th century, transcribed in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> as <span title="Medieval Latin-language text"><i lang="la">bronzium</i></span>) from either:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="undetermined-language text"><i lang="und-Latn">bróntion</i></span>, back-formation from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greek" title="Byzantine Greek">Byzantine Greek</a> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><i lang="grc-Latn">brontēsíon</i></span> (<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">βροντησίον</span></span>, 11th century), perhaps from <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><i lang="grc-Latn">Brentḗsion</i></span> (<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Βρεντήσιον</span></span>, '<a href="/wiki/Brindisi" title="Brindisi">Brindisi</a>', reputed for its bronze;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or originally:</li>
<li>in its earliest form from <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Old Persian</a> <span title="Persian-language text"><i lang="fa-Latn">birinj</i></span>, <span title="Persian-language text"><i lang="fa-Latn">biranj</i></span> (<span title="Persian-language text"><span dir="rtl" lang="fa">برنج</span></span>, 'brass', modern <span title="Persian-language text"><i lang="fa-Latn">berenj</i></span>) and <span title="Persian-language text"><i lang="fa-Latn">piring</i></span> (<span title="Persian-language text"><span dir="rtl" lang="fa">پرنگ</span></span>) 'copper',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> from which also came <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a> <span title="Georgian-language text"><i lang="ka-Latn">brinǯi</i></span> (<span title="Georgian-language text"><span lang="ka">ბრინჯი</span></span> ), <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> <i>pirinç,</i> and <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a> <span title="Armenian-language text"><i lang="hy-Latn">brinj</i></span> (<span title="Armenian-language text"><span lang="hy">բրինձ</span></span>), also meaning 'bronze'.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg/220px-ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg/330px-ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg/440px-ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ALB_-_Hortfund_Gro%C3%9F_Gaglow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hoard of bronze socketed axes from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> found in modern Germany. This was the top tool of the period, and also seems to have been used as a store of value.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1381" data-file-width="2397" decoding="async" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg/220px-Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg/330px-Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg/440px-Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magical_Roman_Nails.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman bronze nails with magical signs and inscriptions, 3rd-4th century AD.</div></div></div>
<p>The discovery of bronze enabled people to create metal objects that were harder and more durable than previously possible. Bronze <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armor" title="Armor">armor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building materials</a> such as decorative tiles were harder and more durable than their stone and copper ("<a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a>") predecessors. Initially, bronze was made out of copper and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenic_bronze" title="Arsenic bronze">arsenic bronze</a>, or from naturally or artificially mixed ores of copper and arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artifacts</a> so far known come from the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_plateau" title="Iranian plateau">Iranian plateau</a>, in the 5th millennium BCE, and are smelted from native arsenical copper and copper-arsenides, such as <a href="/wiki/Algodonite" title="Algodonite">algodonite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domeykite" title="Domeykite">domeykite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornton,_2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thornton,_2002-7">[7]</a></sup>  The earliest tin-copper-alloy artifact has been dated to c. 4650 BCE, in a <a href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_culture" title="Vinča culture">Vinča culture</a> site in <a href="/wiki/Plo%C4%8Dnik_(archaeological_site)" title="Pločnik (archaeological site)">Pločnik</a> (<a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>), and believed to have been smelted from a natural tin-copper ore, <a href="/wiki/Stannite" title="Stannite">stannite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-antiquity1312_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-antiquity1312-8">[8]</a></sup> Other early examples date to the late <a href="/wiki/History_of_Africa#Metallurgy" title="History of Africa">4th millennium BCE</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susa" title="Susa">Susa</a> (Iran) and some ancient sites in China, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luristan" title="Luristan">Luristan</a> (Iran),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornton,_2002_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thornton,_2002-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tepe_Sialk" title="Tepe Sialk">Tepe Sialk</a> (Iran),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornton,_2002_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thornton,_2002-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mundigak" title="Mundigak">Mundigak</a> (Afghanistan),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornton,_2002_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thornton,_2002-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (Iraq).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="according to what evidence?? (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Tin bronze was superior to arsenic bronze in that the alloying process could be more easily controlled, and the resulting alloy was stronger and easier to cast. Also, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">unlike arsenic</a>, metallic tin and fumes from tin refining are <a href="/wiki/Tin_poisoning" title="Tin poisoning">not toxic</a>. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">Tin</a> became the major non-copper ingredient of bronze in the late 3rd millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">Ores</a> of copper and the far rarer tin are not often found together (exceptions include <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in the United Kingdom, one ancient site in Thailand and one in Iran), so serious bronze work has always involved trade. <a href="/wiki/Tin_sources_and_trade_in_ancient_times" title="Tin sources and trade in ancient times">Tin sources and trade in ancient times</a> had a major influence on the development of cultures. In Europe, a major source of tin was the British deposits of ore in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, which were traded as far as <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicia</a> in the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>.
</p><p>In many parts of the world, large hoards of bronze artifacts are found, suggesting that bronze also represented a store of value and an indicator of social status. In Europe, large hoards of bronze tools, typically socketed axes (illustrated above), are found, which mostly show no signs of wear. With <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_ritual_bronze" title="Chinese ritual bronze">Chinese ritual bronzes</a>, which are documented in the inscriptions they carry and from other sources, the case is clear. These were made in enormous quantities for elite burials, and also used by the living for ritual offerings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_to_iron">Transition to iron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transition to iron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though bronze is generally harder than <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Vickers_hardness_test" title="Vickers hardness test">Vickers hardness</a> of 60–258 vs. 30–80,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> gave way to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> after a serious disruption of the tin trade: the <a href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Late Bronze Age collapse">population migrations of around 1200–1100 BCE</a> reduced the shipping of tin around the Mediterranean and from Britain, limiting supplies and raising prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As the art of working in iron improved, iron became cheaper and improved in quality. As cultures advanced from hand-wrought iron to machine-forged iron (typically made with <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammers</a> powered by water), blacksmiths learned how to make <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>. Steel is stronger than bronze and holds a sharper edge longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bronze was still used during the Iron Age, and has continued in use for many purposes to the modern day.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2520" data-file-width="3776" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg/220px-Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg/330px-Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg/440px-Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_bell_with_visible_material_structure.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bronze bell with a visible <a href="/wiki/Crystallite" title="Crystallite">crystallite</a> structure.</div></div></div>
<p>There are many different bronze alloys, but typically modern bronze is 88% <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and 12% tin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Alpha bronze</i> consists of the alpha <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> of tin in copper. Alpha bronze alloys of 4–5% tin are used to make coins, springs, <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbines</a> and blades. Historical "bronzes" are highly variable in composition, as most metalworkers probably used whatever scrap was on hand; the metal of the 12th-century English <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Candlestick" title="Gloucester Candlestick">Gloucester Candlestick</a> is bronze containing a mixture of copper, zinc, tin, lead, nickel, iron, antimony, arsenic and an unusually large amount of silver – between 22.5% in the base and 5.76% in the pan below the candle. The proportions of this mixture suggest that the candlestick was made from a hoard of old coins. The 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Benin_Bronzes" title="Benin Bronzes">Benin Bronzes</a> are in fact brass, and the 12th-century <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_art" title="Romanesque art">Romanesque</a> <a href="/wiki/Baptismal_font_at_St_Bartholomew%27s_Church,_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Baptismal font at St Bartholomew's Church, Liège">Baptismal font at St Bartholomew's Church, Liège</a> is described as both bronze and <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>.
</p><p>In the Bronze Age, two forms of bronze were commonly used: "classic bronze", about 10% tin, was used in casting; and "mild bronze", about 6% tin, was hammered from ingots to make sheets. Bladed weapons were mostly cast from classic bronze, while helmets and armor were hammered from mild bronze.
</p><p>Commercial bronze (90% copper and 10% zinc) and architectural bronze (57% copper, 3% <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, 40% zinc) are more properly regarded as brass alloys because they contain <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> as the main alloying ingredient. They are commonly used in architectural applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plastic bronze contains a significant quantity of lead, which makes for improved plasticity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> possibly used by the ancient Greeks in their ship construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1023754711">.mw-parser-output .vanchor>:target~.vanchor-text{background-color:#b1d2ff}</style><span class="vanchor"><span id="Silicon_bronze"></span><span class="vanchor-text">Silicon bronze</span></span> has a composition of Si: 2.80–3.80%, Mn: 0.50–1.30%, Fe: 0.80% max., Zn: 1.50% max., Pb: 0.05% max., Cu: balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other bronze alloys include <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_bronze" title="Aluminium bronze">aluminium bronze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphor_bronze" title="Phosphor bronze">phosphor bronze</a>, manganese bronze, <a href="/wiki/Bell_metal" title="Bell metal">bell metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenical_bronze" title="Arsenical bronze">arsenical bronze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speculum_metal" title="Speculum metal">speculum metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cymbal_alloys" title="Cymbal alloys">cymbal alloys</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bronzes are typically ductile alloys, considerably less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a> than cast iron. Typically bronze oxidizes only superficially; once a copper oxide (eventually becoming copper carbonate) layer is formed, the underlying metal is <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">protected from further corrosion</a>. This can be seen on statues from the Hellenistic period. However, if copper chlorides are formed, a corrosion-mode called "<a href="/wiki/Bronze_disease" title="Bronze disease">bronze disease</a>" will eventually completely destroy it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Copper-based <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> have lower <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a> than steel or iron and are more readily produced from their constituent metals. They are generally about 10 percent denser than steel, although alloys using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> may be slightly less dense. Bronze is a better conductor of heat and electricity than most steels. The cost of copper-base alloys is generally higher than that of steels but lower than that of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>-base alloys.
</p><p>Copper and its alloys have a huge variety of uses that reflect their versatile physical, mechanical, and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_property" title="Chemical property">chemical properties</a>. Some common examples are the high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> of pure copper, low-friction properties of bearing bronze (bronze that has a high lead content— 6–8%), resonant qualities of bell bronze (20% tin, 80% copper), and resistance to corrosion by <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> of several bronze alloys.
</p><p>The melting point of bronze varies depending on the ratio of the alloy components and is about 950 °C (1,742 °F). Bronze is usually nonmagnetic, but certain alloys containing iron or nickel may have magnetic properties.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg/220px-%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg/330px-%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg/440px-%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%E7%A7%A6%E9%93%9C%E8%AF%8F%E9%93%81%E6%9D%8304975.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bronze weight with an inscribed imperial order, <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a></div></div></div>

<p>Bronze, or bronze-like alloys and mixtures, were used for coins over a longer period. Bronze was especially suitable for use in boat and ship fittings prior to the wide employment of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> owing to its combination of toughness and resistance to salt water corrosion. Bronze is still commonly used in ship propellers and submerged bearings.
</p><p>In the 20th century, silicon was introduced as the primary alloying element, creating an alloy with wide application in industry and the major form used in contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statuary" title="Statuary">statuary</a>. Sculptors may prefer silicon bronze because of the ready availability of silicon bronze brazing rod, which allows color-matched repair of defects in castings. Aluminum is also used for the structural metal aluminum bronze.
</p><p>Bronze parts are <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">tough</a> and typically used for <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a>, clips, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_connector" title="Electrical connector">electrical connectors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">springs</a>.
</p><p>Bronze also has low <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> against dissimilar metals, making it important for <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannons</a> prior to modern <a href="/wiki/Engineering_tolerance" title="Engineering tolerance">tolerancing</a>, where iron cannonballs would otherwise stick in the barrel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlavudeenVenkateshwaran2006_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlavudeenVenkateshwaran2006-20">[20]</a></sup> It is still widely used today for springs, bearings, bushings, automobile transmission pilot bearings, and similar fittings, and is particularly common in the bearings of small <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a>. Phosphor bronze is particularly suited to precision-grade bearings and springs. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> strings.
</p><p>Unlike steel, bronze struck against a hard surface will not generate sparks, so it (along with <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_copper" title="Beryllium copper">beryllium copper</a>) is used to make <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mallet" title="Mallet">mallets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wrench" title="Wrench">wrenches</a> and other durable tools to be used in explosive atmospheres or in the presence of flammable vapors. Bronze is used to make <a href="/wiki/Bronze_wool" title="Bronze wool">bronze wool</a> for woodworking applications where <a href="/wiki/Steel_wool" title="Steel wool">steel wool</a> would discolor <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a>.
</p><p>Phosphor bronze is used for ships' propellers, musical instruments, and electrical contacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plain_bearing#Bronze" title="Plain bearing">Bearings</a> are often made of bronze for its friction properties. It can be impregnated with oil to make the proprietary <a href="/wiki/Oilite" title="Oilite">Oilite</a> and similar material for bearings. Aluminum bronze is hard and wear-resistant, and is used for bearings and machine tool ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculptures">Sculptures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sculptures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bronze_sculpture" title="Bronze sculpture">Bronze sculpture</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ormolu" title="Ormolu">Ormolu</a></div>
<p>Bronze is widely used for casting <a href="/wiki/Bronze_sculpture" title="Bronze sculpture">bronze sculptures</a>. Common bronze alloys have the unusual and desirable property of expanding slightly just before they set, thus filling the finest details of a mould. Then, as the bronze cools, it shrinks a little, making it easier to separate from the mould.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Sennacherib" title="Sennacherib">Sennacherib</a> (704–681 BCE) claims to have been the first to cast monumental bronze statues (of up to 30 tonnes) using two-part moulds instead of the <a href="/wiki/Lost-wax_casting" title="Lost-wax casting">lost-wax method</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bronze statues were regarded as the highest form of sculpture in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_art" title="Ancient Greek art">Ancient Greek art</a>, though survivals are few, as bronze was a valuable material in short supply in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Antique" title="Late Antique">Late Antique</a> and medieval periods. Many of the most famous Greek bronze sculptures are known through Roman copies in marble, which were more likely to survive.
</p><p>In India, bronze sculptures from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushana" title="Kushana">Kushana</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chausa_hoard" title="Chausa hoard">Chausa hoard</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Gupta" title="Gupta">Gupta</a> periods (<a href="/wiki/Brahma_from_Mirpur-Khas" title="Brahma from Mirpur-Khas">Brahma from Mirpur-Khas</a>, Akota Hoard, <a href="/wiki/Sultanganj_Buddha" title="Sultanganj Buddha">Sultanganj Buddha</a>) and later periods (<a href="/wiki/Hansi" title="Hansi">Hansi</a> Hoard) have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Indian Hindu artisans from the period of the <a href="/wiki/Chola_dynasty" title="Chola dynasty">Chola empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> used bronze to create intricate statues via the lost-wax casting method with ornate detailing depicting the deities of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>. The art form survives to this day, with many silpis, craftsmen, working in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Swamimalai" title="Swamimalai">Swamimalai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>.
</p><p>In antiquity other cultures also produced works of <a href="/wiki/High_culture" title="High culture">high art</a> using bronze. For example: in Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Benin_Bronzes" title="Benin Bronzes">bronze heads</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benin_Empire" title="Benin Empire">Kingdom of Benin</a>; in Europe, Grecian bronzes typically of figures from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>; in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">east Asia</a>, Chinese ritual bronzes of the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>—more often ceremonial vessels but including some figurine examples. Bronze sculptures, although known for their longevity, still undergo microbial degradation; such as from certain species of yeasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bronze continues into modern times as one of the materials of choice for monumental statuary.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mirrors">Mirrors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mirrors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bronze_mirror" title="Bronze mirror">Bronze mirror</a></div>
<p>Before it became possible to produce glass with acceptably flat surfaces, bronze was a standard material for mirrors. The reflecting surface was typically made slightly convex so that the whole face could be seen in a small mirror. Bronze was used for this purpose in many parts of the world, probably based on independent discoveries.
</p><p>Bronze mirrors survive from the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a> (2040–1750 BCE). In Europe, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscans" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a> were making bronze mirrors in the sixth century BCE, and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> mirrors followed the same pattern. Although other materials such as speculum metal had come into use, bronze mirrors were still being made in Japan in the eighteenth century AD.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_instruments">Musical instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Musical instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bianzhong.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2752" data-file-width="4768" decoding="async" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Bianzhong.jpg/220px-Bianzhong.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Bianzhong.jpg/330px-Bianzhong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Bianzhong.jpg/440px-Bianzhong.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bianzhong.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chinese bells:<a href="/wiki/Bianzhong_of_Marquis_Yi_of_Zeng" title="Bianzhong of Marquis Yi of Zeng">Bianzhong of Marquis Yi of Zeng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (476–221 BCE)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Picsingingbowls.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="166" data-file-width="250" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Picsingingbowls.jpg/220px-Picsingingbowls.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/Picsingingbowls.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Picsingingbowls.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Standing_bell" title="Standing bell">Singing bowls</a> from the 16th to 18th centuries. Annealed bronze continues to be made in the Himalayas</div></div></div>
<p>Bronze is the preferred metal for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_(instrument)" title="Bell (instrument)">bells</a> in the form of a high tin bronze alloy known colloquially as bell metal, which is about 23% tin.
</p><p>Nearly all professional <a href="/wiki/Cymbal" title="Cymbal">cymbals</a> are made from bronze, which gives a desirable balance of durability and <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>. Several types of bronze are used, commonly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B20_(bronze)" title="B20 (bronze)">B20 bronze</a>, which is roughly 20% tin, 80% copper, with traces of silver, or the tougher B8 bronze made from 8% tin and 92% copper. As the tin content in a bell or cymbal rises, the timbre drops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bronze is also used for the windings of steel and <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> strings of various <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">stringed instruments</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>, piano, <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>, and guitar. Bronze strings are commonly reserved on pianoforte for the lower pitch tones, as they possess a superior sustain quality to that of high-tensile steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bronzes of various metallurgical properties are widely used in struck <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiophones" title="Idiophones">idiophones</a> around the world, notably bells, singing bowls, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gongs" title="Gongs">gongs</a>, cymbals, and other idiophones from Asia. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibetan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singing_bowls" title="Singing bowls">singing bowls</a>, temple bells of many sizes and shapes, <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Gamelan" title="Gamelan">gamelan</a>, and other bronze <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instruments</a>. The earliest bronze archeological finds in Indonesia date from 1–2 BCE, including flat plates probably suspended and struck by a wooden or bone mallet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Ancient bronze drums from Thailand and Vietnam date back 2,000 years. Bronze bells from Thailand and Cambodia date back to 3,600 BCE.
</p><p>Some companies are now making <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophones</a> from phosphor bronze (3.5 to 10% tin and up to 1% phosphorus content).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Bell bronze/B20 is used to make the tone rings of many professional model <a href="/wiki/Banjo" title="Banjo">banjos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The tone ring is a heavy (usually 3 lb, 1.4 kg) folded or arched metal ring attached to a thick wood rim, over which a skin, or most often, a plastic membrane (or head) is stretched – it is the bell bronze that gives the banjo a crisp powerful lower register and clear bell-like treble register.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_references">Biblical references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Biblical references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are over 125 references to bronze (‘nehoshet’), which appears to be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> word used for copper and any of its alloys.  However, the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> era Hebrews are not thought to have had the capability to manufacture zinc (needed to make brass) and so it is likely that 'nehoshet’ refers to copper and its alloys with tin, now called bronze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>  In the King James Version, there is no use of the word 'bronze' and ‘nehoshet’ was translated as 'brass'.  Modern translations use 'bronze'.   Bronze (nehoshet) was used widely in the Tabernacle for items such as the bronze altar (Exodus Ch.27), bronze laver (Exodus Ch.30), utensils, and mirror (Exodus Ch.38). It was mentioned in the account of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> holding up a bronze snake on a pole in Numbers Ch.21. In First Kings, it is mentioned that <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Abiff" title="Hiram Abiff">Hiram</a> was very skilled in working with bronze, and he made many furnishings for <a href="/wiki/Solomon%27s_Temple" title="Solomon's Temple">Solomon's Temple</a> including pillars, capitals, stands, wheels, bowls, and plates, some of which were highly decorative (see I King 7:13-47). Bronze was also widely used as battle armor and helmet, as in the battle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_and_Goliath" title="David and Goliath">David and Goliath</a> in I Samuel 17:5-6;38 (also see II Chron. 12:10).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coins_and_medals">Coins and medals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Coins and medals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bronze_medal" title="Bronze medal">Bronze medal</a></div>
<p>Bronze has also been used in coins; most "copper" coins are actually bronze, with about 4 percent tin and 1 percent zinc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bronze_{{!}}_alloy_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-bronze_{{!}}_alloy-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>As with coins, bronze has been used in the manufacture of various types of <a href="/wiki/Medal" title="Medal">medals</a> for centuries, and are known in contemporary times for being awarded for third place in sporting competitions and other events. The later usage was in part attributed to the choices of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> and bronze to represent the first three <a href="/wiki/Ages_of_Man" title="Ages of Man">Ages of Man</a> in Greek mythology: the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age" title="Golden Age">Golden Age</a>, when men lived among the gods; the <a href="/wiki/Silver_age" title="Silver age">Silver age</a>, where youth lasted a hundred years; and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Heroic_Age" title="Greek Heroic Age">Bronze Age</a>, the era of heroes, and was first adopted at the <a href="/wiki/1904_Summer_Olympics" title="1904 Summer Olympics">1904 Summer Olympics</a>. At the 1896 event, silver was awarded to winners and bronze to runners-up, while at 1900 other prizes were given rather than medals.
</p>
