<p><b>Sanitation</b> refers to <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> conditions related to clean <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> and treatment and disposal of <a href="/wiki/Human_waste" title="Human waste">human excreta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">Preventing</a> human contact with <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> is part of sanitation, as is <a href="/wiki/Hand_washing" title="Hand washing">hand washing</a> with soap. Sanitation systems aim to protect human health by providing a clean environment that will stop the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmission_(medicine)" title="Transmission (medicine)">transmission of disease</a>, especially through the <a href="/wiki/Fecal%E2%80%93oral_route" title="Fecal–oral route">fecal–oral route</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, a main cause of <a href="/wiki/Undernutrition_in_children" title="Undernutrition in children">malnutrition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunted growth</a> in children, can be reduced through adequate sanitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There are many other diseases which are easily transmitted in communities that have low levels of sanitation, such as <a href="/wiki/Ascariasis" title="Ascariasis">ascariasis</a> (a type of intestinal worm infection or <a href="/wiki/Helminthiasis" title="Helminthiasis">helminthiasis</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">polio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trachoma" title="Trachoma">trachoma</a>, to name just a few. 
</p><p>A range of sanitation technologies and approaches exists. Some examples are <a href="/wiki/Community-led_total_sanitation" title="Community-led total sanitation">community-led total sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Container-based_sanitation" title="Container-based sanitation">container-based sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_sanitation" title="Ecological sanitation">ecological sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergency_sanitation" title="Emergency sanitation">emergency sanitation</a>, environmental sanitation, <a href="#Onsite_sanitation">onsite sanitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">sustainable sanitation</a>. A sanitation system includes the capture, storage, transport, treatment and disposal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse of human excreta and wastewater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Reuse activities within the sanitation system may focus on the nutrients, water, energy or organic matter contained in excreta and wastewater. This is referred to as the "sanitation value chain" or "sanitation economy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The people responsible for cleaning, maintaining, operating, or emptying a sanitation technology at any step of the sanitation chain are called "<a href="/wiki/Sanitation_worker" title="Sanitation worker">sanitation workers</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0.a_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0.a-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 2">: 2 </span></sup>
</p><p>Several sanitation "levels" are being used to compare sanitation service levels within countries or across countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanitation_{{!}}_JMP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanitation_{{!}}_JMP-8">[8]</a></sup> The sanitation ladder defined by the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme</a> in 2016 starts at <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> and moves upwards using the terms "unimproved", "limited", "basic", with the highest level being "<a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">safely managed</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanitation_{{!}}_JMP_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sanitation_{{!}}_JMP-8">[8]</a></sup> This is particularly applicable to <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Human_right_to_water_and_sanitation" title="Human right to water and sanitation">Human Right to Water and Sanitation</a> was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations (UN) General Assembly</a> in 2010. Sanitation is a <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">global development</a> priority and the subject of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup> The estimate in 2017 by <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">JMP</a> states that 4.5 billion people currently do not have <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">safely managed sanitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup> Lack of access to sanitation has an impact not only on <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> but also on human <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">dignity</a> and personal safety.
</p>

<p>There are some variations on the use of the term "sanitation" between countries and organizations. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> defines the term "sanitation" as follows:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Sanitation generally refers to the provision of facilities and services for the safe disposal of human urine and faeces. The word 'sanitation' also refers to the maintenance of hygienic conditions, through services such as garbage collection and wastewater disposal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Sanitation includes all four of these technical and non-technical systems: Excreta management systems, wastewater management systems (included here are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment_plants" title="Wastewater treatment plants">wastewater treatment plants</a>), <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">solid waste management</a> systems as well as drainage systems for rainwater, also called <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">stormwater drainage</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, many in the <a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a> sector only include excreta management in their definition of sanitation.
</p><p>Another example of what is included in sanitation is found in the handbook by <a href="/wiki/Sphere_(organization)" title="Sphere (organization)">Sphere</a> on "Humanitarian Charter and Minimum Standards in Humanitarian Response" which describes minimum standards in four "key response sectors" in <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_aid" title="Humanitarian aid">humanitarian response</a> situations. One of them is "Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene Promotion" (<a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a>) and it includes the following areas: <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">Hygiene</a> promotion, <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>, excreta management, <a href="/wiki/Vector_control" title="Vector control">vector control</a>, solid waste management and WASH in <a href="/wiki/Disease_outbreak" title="Disease outbreak">disease outbreaks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">healthcare</a> settings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sphere_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sphere-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 91">: 91 </span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">Hygiene</a> promotion is seen by many as an integral part of sanitation. The <a href="/wiki/Water_Supply_and_Sanitation_Collaborative_Council" title="Water Supply and Sanitation Collaborative Council">Water Supply and Sanitation Collaborative Council</a> defines sanitation as "The collection, transport, treatment and disposal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse of human excreta</a>, domestic wastewater and solid waste, and associated hygiene promotion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the fact that sanitation includes wastewater treatment, the two terms are often used side by side as "sanitation and wastewater management".
</p><p>Another definition is in the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_International_Development" title="Department for International Development">DFID</a> guidance manual on water supply and sanitation programmes from 1998:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"For the purposes of this manual, the word ‘sanitation’ alone is taken to mean the safe management of human excreta. It therefore includes both the ‘hardware’ (e.g. latrines and sewers) and the ‘software’ (regulation, hygiene promotion) needed to reduce faecal-oral disease transmission. It encompasses too the re-use and ultimate disposal of human excreta. The term environmental sanitation is used to cover the wider concept of controlling all the factors in the physical environment which may have deleterious impacts on human health and well-being. In developing countries, it normally includes drainage, solid waste management, and vector control, in addition to the activities covered by the definition of sanitation."</p></blockquote>
<p>Sanitation can include personal sanitation and public hygiene. Personal sanitation work can include handling <a href="/wiki/Feminine_hygiene" title="Feminine hygiene">menstrual waste</a>, cleaning household <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilets</a>, and managing household <a href="/wiki/Garbage" title="Garbage">garbage</a>. Public sanitation work can involve garbage collection, transfer and treatment (<a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste management</a>), cleaning drains, streets, schools, trains, <a href="/wiki/Public_space" title="Public space">public spaces</a>, community toilets and <a href="/wiki/Public_toilet" title="Public toilet">public toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewerage" title="Sewerage">sewers</a>, operating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment_plants" title="Sewage treatment plants">sewage treatment plants</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRIA_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRIA-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 4">: 4 </span></sup> Workers who provide these services for other people are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanitation_workers" title="Sanitation workers">sanitation workers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purposes">Purposes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purposes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_(26170609358).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2419" data-file-width="3027" decoding="async" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_%2826170609358%29.jpg/350px-2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_%2826170609358%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_%2826170609358%29.jpg/525px-2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_%2826170609358%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_%2826170609358%29.jpg/700px-2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_%2826170609358%29.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2016_EPI_Environmental_Health_Objective_-_Water_and_Sanitation_(26170609358).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Access to safe drinking water and sanitation (2016)</div></div></div>
<p>The overall purposes of sanitation are to provide a healthy living environment for everyone, to protect the natural resources (such as <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>), and to provide safety, security and <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">dignity</a> for people when they <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defecate" title="Defecate">defecate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urinate" title="Urinate">urinate</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Human_right_to_water_and_sanitation" title="Human right to water and sanitation">Human Right to Water and Sanitation</a> was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations (UN) General Assembly</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRWS2015_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRWS2015-18">[18]</a></sup> It has been recognized in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> through <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_law" title="International human rights law">human rights treaties</a>, declarations and other standards. It is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human right</a> to an adequate <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Effective sanitation systems provide barriers between excreta and humans in such a way as to break the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disease_transmission" title="Disease transmission">disease transmission</a> cycle (for example in the case of fecal-borne diseases).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This aspect is visualised with the F-diagram where all major routes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fecal-oral_route" title="Fecal-oral route">fecal-oral disease transmission</a> begin with the letter F: feces, fingers, flies, fields, fluids, food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sanitation infrastructure has to be adapted to several specific contexts including consumers' expectations and local resources available.
</p><p>Sanitation technologies may involve centralized <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> structures like <a href="/wiki/Sewerage" title="Sewerage">sewer systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> treatment and solid waste <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>. These structures are designed to treat <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a>. Sanitation technologies may also take the form of relatively simple onsite sanitation systems. This can in some cases consist of a simple <a href="/wiki/Pit_latrine" title="Pit latrine">pit latrine</a> or other type of <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">non-flush toilet</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_excreta" title="Human excreta">excreta</a> management part.
</p><p>Providing sanitation to people requires attention to the entire system, not just focusing on technical aspects such as the <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">fecal sludge management</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilley_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tilley-22">[22]</a></sup> The "sanitation chain" involves the experience of the user, excreta and wastewater collection methods, transporting and treatment of waste, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse</a> or disposal. All need to be thoroughly considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilley_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tilley-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impacts">Economic impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The benefits to society of managing human excreta are considerable, for public health as well as for the environment. As a rough estimate: For every US$1 spent on sanitation, the return to society is US$5.50.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 2">: 2 </span></sup>
</p><p>For developing countries, the economic costs of inadequate sanitation is a huge concern.  For example, according to a World Bank study, economic losses due to inadequate sanitation to The Indian economy are equivalent to 6.4% of its GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-24">[24]</a></sup> Most of these are due to premature mortality, time lost in accessing, loss of productivity, additional costs for healthcare among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-24">[24]</a></sup> Inadequate sanitation also leads to loss from potential tourism revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-24">[24]</a></sup> This study also found that impacts are disproportionately higher for the poor, women and children. Availability of toilet at home on the other hand, positively contributes to economic well-being of women as it leads to an increase in literacy and participation in labor force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The term sanitation is connected with various descriptors or adjectives to signify certain types of sanitation systems (which may deal only with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_excreta" title="Human excreta">human excreta</a> management or with the entire sanitation system, i.e. also greywater, stormwater and solid waste management) – in alphabetical order:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_sanitation">Basic sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basic sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">JMP</a> defined a new term: "basic sanitation service". This is defined as the use of <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">improved sanitation</a> facilities that are not shared with other households. A lower level of service is now called "limited sanitation service" which refers to use of improved sanitation facilities that are shared between two or more households.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Container-based_sanitation">Container-based sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Container-based sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1066933788">.mw-parser-output .excerpt-hat .mw-editsection-like{font-style:normal}</style><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Container-based_sanitation" title="Container-based sanitation">Container-based sanitation</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Container-based_sanitation&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Container-based_sanitation" title="Container-based sanitation">Container-based sanitation</a> (abbreviated as CBS) refers to a sanitation system where toilets collect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_excreta" title="Human excreta">human excreta</a> in sealable, removable containers (also called cartridges) that are transported to treatment facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Container-based_sanitation_Tilmans_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Container-based_sanitation_Tilmans-27">[27]</a></sup> This type of sanitation involves a commercial service which provides certain types of <a href="/wiki/Portable_toilet" title="Portable toilet">portable toilets</a>, and delivers empty containers when picking up full ones. The service transports and safely disposes of or reuses collected excreta. The cost of collection of excreta is usually borne by the users. With suitable development, support and functioning partnerships, CBS can be used to provide low-income urban populations with safe collection, transport and treatment of excrement at a lower cost than installing and maintaining <a href="/wiki/Sewerage" title="Sewerage">sewers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Container-based_sanitation_Shepardj_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Container-based_sanitation_Shepardj-28">[28]</a></sup> In most cases, CBS is based on the use of <a href="/wiki/Urine-diverting_dry_toilet" title="Urine-diverting dry toilet">urine-diverting dry toilets</a>.</div></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community-led_total_sanitation">Community-led total sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Community-led total sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Community-led_total_sanitation" title="Community-led total sanitation">Community-led total sanitation</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Community-led_total_sanitation&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Community-led_total_sanitation" title="Community-led total sanitation">Community-led total sanitation</a> (CLTS) is an approach used mainly in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> to improve sanitation and <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a> practices in a community. The approach tries to achieve <a href="/wiki/Behavior_change_(public_health)" title="Behavior change (public health)">behavior change</a> in mainly rural people by a process of "triggering", leading to spontaneous and long-term abandonment of <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> practices. It focuses on spontaneous and long-lasting behavior change of an entire community. The term "triggering" is central to the CLTS process: It refers to ways of igniting community interest in ending open defecation, usually by building simple <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilets</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pit_latrines" title="Pit latrines">pit latrines</a>. CLTS involves actions leading to increased self-respect and pride in one's community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Community-led_total_sanitation_:1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Community-led_total_sanitation_:1-29">[29]</a></sup> It also involves <a href="/wiki/Shame" title="Shame">shame</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disgust" title="Disgust">disgust</a> about one's own open defecation behaviors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Community-led_total_sanitation_:1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Community-led_total_sanitation_:1-29">[29]</a></sup> CLTS takes an approach to rural sanitation that works without hardware subsidies and that facilitates communities to recognize the problem of open defecation and take <a href="/wiki/Collective_action" title="Collective action">collective action</a> to clean up and become "open defecation free".</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_sanitation">Dry sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dry sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "dry sanitation" is not in widespread use and is not very well defined. It usually refers to a system that uses a type of <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">dry toilet</a> and no sewers to transport excreta. Often when people speak of "dry sanitation" they mean a sanitation system that uses <a href="/wiki/Urine-diverting_dry_toilet" title="Urine-diverting dry toilet">urine-diverting dry toilet</a> (UDDTs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flores_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flores-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_sanitation">Ecological sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecological sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Ecological_sanitation" title="Ecological sanitation">Ecological sanitation</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ecological_sanitation&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Ecological_sanitation" title="Ecological sanitation">Ecological sanitation</a>, commonly abbreviated as ecosan (also spelled eco-san or EcoSan), is an approach to sanitation provision which aims to safely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse excreta</a> in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ecological_sanitation_:2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ecological_sanitation_:2-33">[33]</a></sup> It is an approach, rather than a technology or a device which is characterized by a desire to "close the loop", mainly for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_nutrient" title="Plant nutrient">nutrients</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> between sanitation and agriculture in a safe manner. One of the aims is to minimise the use of <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable resources</a>. When properly designed and operated, ecosan systems provide a hygienically safe system to convert human excreta into nutrients to be returned to the soil, and water to be returned to the land. Ecosan is also called resource-oriented sanitation.</div></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_sanitation">Emergency sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Emergency sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Emergency_sanitation" title="Emergency sanitation">Emergency sanitation</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Emergency_sanitation&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Emergency_sanitation" title="Emergency sanitation">Emergency sanitation</a> is the management and technical processes required to provide sanitation in emergency situations. Emergency sanitation is required during <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_aid" title="Humanitarian aid">humanitarian relief</a> operations for <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a>, people affected by <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internally_displaced_person" title="Internally displaced person">internally displaced persons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emergency_sanitation_Harveybook_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emergency_sanitation_Harveybook-34">[34]</a></sup> There are three phases of emergency response: Immediate, short term and long term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emergency_sanitation_Harveybook_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emergency_sanitation_Harveybook-34">[34]</a></sup> In the immediate phase, the focus is on managing <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilet</a> technologies might include very basic <a href="/wiki/Latrine" title="Latrine">latrines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pit_latrines" title="Pit latrines">pit latrines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bucket_toilet" title="Bucket toilet">bucket toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Container-based_sanitation" title="Container-based sanitation">container-based toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_toilet" title="Chemical toilet">chemical toilets</a>. The short term phase might also involve technologies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urine-diverting_dry_toilets" title="Urine-diverting dry toilets">urine-diverting dry toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decentralized_wastewater_system" title="Decentralized wastewater system">decentralized wastewater systems</a>. Providing <a href="/wiki/Hand_washing" title="Hand washing">handwashing</a> facilities and <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">management of fecal sludge</a> are also part of emergency sanitation.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_sanitation">Environmental sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental sanitation encompasses the control of environmental factors that are connected to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disease_transmission" title="Disease transmission">disease transmission</a>. Subsets of this category are solid waste management, water and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> treatment, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a> treatment and <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> control.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fecal_sludge_management">Fecal sludge management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fecal sludge management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">Fecal sludge management</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fecal_sludge_management&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">Fecal sludge management</a> (FSM) (or faecal sludge management in British English) is the storage, collection, transport, treatment and safe end use or disposal of fecal sludge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fecal_sludge_management_:7_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fecal_sludge_management_:7-35">[35]</a></sup> Together, the collection, transport, treatment and end use of fecal sludge constitute the "value chain" or "service chain" of fecal sludge management. Fecal sludge is defined very broadly as what accumulates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onsite_sanitation" title="Onsite sanitation">onsite sanitation</a> systems (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Pit_latrine" title="Pit latrine">pit latrines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Container-based_sanitation" title="Container-based sanitation">container-based</a> solutions) and specifically is not transported through a <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewer</a>. It is composed of <a href="/wiki/Human_waste" title="Human waste">human excreta</a>, but also anything else that may go into an onsite containment technology, such as flushwater, cleansing materials (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Toilet_paper" title="Toilet paper">toilet paper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anal_hygiene" title="Anal hygiene">anal cleansing</a> materials), <a href="/wiki/Feminine_hygiene" title="Feminine hygiene">menstrual hygiene</a> products, grey water (i.e. bathing or kitchen water, including fats, oils and grease), and <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">solid waste</a>. Fecal sludge that is removed from septic tanks is called septage.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improved_and_unimproved_sanitation">Improved and unimproved sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Improved and unimproved sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">Improved sanitation</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Improved_sanitation&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">Improved sanitation</a> (related to but distinct from a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safely_managed_sanitation" title="Safely managed sanitation">safely managed sanitation</a> service") is a term used to categorize types of sanitation for monitoring purposes. It refers to the management of human feces at the household level. The term was coined by the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Program (JMP) for Water Supply and Sanitation</a> of <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a> in 2002 to help monitor the progress towards Goal Number 7 of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs). The opposite of "<a href="/wiki/Improved_water_source" title="Improved water source">improved sanitation</a>" has been termed "unimproved sanitation" in the JMP definitions. The same terms are used to monitor progress towards <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a> (Target 6.2, Indicator 6.2.1) from 2015 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Improved_sanitation_JMP2017_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Improved_sanitation_JMP2017-36">[36]</a></sup> Here, they are a component of the definition for "safely managed sanitation service".</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_sanitation">Lack of sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lack of sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lack of sanitation refers to the absence of sanitation. In practical terms it usually means lack of toilets or lack of hygienic toilets that anybody would want to use voluntarily. The result of lack of sanitation is usually <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> (and open urination but this is of less concern) with associated serious public health issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">[37]</a></sup> It is estimated that 2.4 billion people still lacked <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">improved sanitation</a> facilities including 660 million people who lack access to safe drinking water as of 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanitation_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-sanitation-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Onsite_sanitation">Onsite sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Onsite sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Onsite sanitation (or on-site sanitation) is defined as "a sanitation system in which excreta and wastewater are collected and stored or treated on the plot where they are generated".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilley_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tilley-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 173">: 173 </span></sup> The degree of treatment may be variable, from none to advanced. Examples are pit <a href="/wiki/Latrine" title="Latrine">latrines</a> (no treatment) and <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_treatment" title="Primary treatment">primary treatment</a> of wastewater). On-site sanitation systems are often connected to <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">fecal sludge management</a> (FSM) systems where the fecal sludge that is generated onsite is treated at an offsite location. Wastewater (<a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>) is only generated when piped <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> is available within the buildings or close to them.
</p><p>A related term is a <a href="/wiki/Decentralized_wastewater_system" title="Decentralized wastewater system">decentralized wastewater system</a> which refers in particular to the wastewater part of on-site sanitation. Similarly, an <a href="/wiki/Onsite_sewage_facility" title="Onsite sewage facility">onsite sewage facility</a> can treat the wastewater generated locally.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safely_managed_sanitation">Safely managed sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Safely managed sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities,_OWID.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="850" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities%2C_OWID.svg/220px-Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities%2C_OWID.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities%2C_OWID.svg/330px-Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities%2C_OWID.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities%2C_OWID.svg/440px-Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities%2C_OWID.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Share_of_population_using_safely_managed_sanitation_facilities,_OWID.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Share of population using safely managed sanitation facilities in 2015<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises,_OWID.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="850" decoding="async" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises%2C_OWID.svg/256px-Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises%2C_OWID.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises%2C_OWID.svg/384px-Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises%2C_OWID.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises%2C_OWID.svg/512px-Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises%2C_OWID.svg.png 2x" width="256"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Proportion_of_population_with_basic_handwashing_facilities_on_premises,_OWID.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Number of Handwashing Facilities in the world, 2017</div></div></div>
<p>Safely managed sanitation is the highest level of household sanitation envisioned by the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal Number 6</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> It is measured under the Sustainable Development Goal 6.2, Indicator 6.2.1, as the “Proportion of population using (a) safely managed sanitation services and (b) a hand-washing facility with soap and water”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unwater_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-unwater-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup> The current value in the 2017 baseline estimate by <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">JMP</a> is that 4.5 billion people currently do not have safely managed sanitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Safely managed sanitation is defined as an <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">improved sanitation</a> facility which is not shared with other households, and where the excreta produced is either treated and disposed in situ, stored temporarily and then emptied and transported to treatment off-site, or transported through a sewer with wastewater and then treated off-site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unwater_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-unwater-42">[42]</a></sup> In other words, safely managed sanitation is a basic sanitation service where in addition excreta are safely disposed of in situ or transported and treated offsite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sustainable sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_sanitation&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</a> is a sanitation system designed to meet certain criteria and to work well over the long-term. Sustainable sanitation systems consider the entire "sanitation value chain", from the experience of the user, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_excreta" title="Human excreta">excreta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> collection methods, transportation or conveyance of waste, treatment, and reuse or disposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sustainable_sanitation_Tilley_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_sanitation_Tilley-43">[43]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Sanitation_Alliance" title="Sustainable Sanitation Alliance">Sustainable Sanitation Alliance</a> (SuSanA) includes five features (or criteria) in its definition of "sustainable sanitation": Systems need to be economically and socially acceptable, technically and institutionally appropriate and protect the environment and natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sustainable_sanitation_:0_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_sanitation_:0-44">[44]</a></sup></div></div>
<h2><span id="Other_types.2C_concepts_and_systems"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types,_concepts_and_systems">Other types, concepts and systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other types, concepts and systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wastewater_management">Wastewater management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Wastewater management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">Wastewater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">Wastewater treatment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg/220px-Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg/330px-Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg/440px-Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wonga_wetlands_sewage_plant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">Sewage treatment</a> plant, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Wastewater management consists of collection, <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> (be it municipal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater" title="Industrial wastewater">industrial wastewater</a>), disposal or reuse of treated wastewater. The latter is also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_water" title="Reclaimed water">water reclamation</a>.
</p><p>Sanitation systems in urban areas of developed countries usually consist of the collection of <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> in gravity driven sewers, its treatment in <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">wastewater treatment plants</a> for <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">reuse</a> or disposal in rivers, lakes or the sea.
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> most wastewater is still discharged untreated into the environment. Alternatives to centralized sewer systems include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onsite_sanitation" title="Onsite sanitation">onsite sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decentralized_wastewater_system" title="Decentralized wastewater system">decentralized wastewater systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">dry toilets</a> connected to <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">fecal sludge management</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stormwater_drainage">Stormwater drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Stormwater drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">Storm drain</a></div>
<p>Sewers are either combined with <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drains</a> or separated from them as <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sanitary sewers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Combined_sewer" title="Combined sewer">Combined sewers</a> are usually found in the central, older parts or urban areas. Heavy <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">rainfall</a> and inadequate maintenance can lead to combined sewer overflows or <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">sanitary sewer overflows</a>, i.e., more or less diluted raw <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> being discharged into the environment. Industries often discharge wastewater into municipal sewers, which can complicate wastewater treatment unless industries pre-treat their discharges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_waste_disposal">Solid waste disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Solid waste disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">Waste management</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Israel_hiriya.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="229" data-file-width="322" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Israel_hiriya.jpg/220px-Israel_hiriya.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Israel_hiriya.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Israel_hiriya.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiriya" title="Hiriya">Hiriya Landfill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Disposal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_waste" title="Solid waste">solid waste</a> is most commonly conducted in <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>, but incineration, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">composting</a> and conversion to <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> are also avenues.  In the case of landfills, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_countries" title="Advanced countries">advanced countries</a> typically have rigid protocols for <a href="/wiki/Daily_cover" title="Daily cover">daily cover</a> with topsoil, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underdeveloped_countries" title="Underdeveloped countries">underdeveloped countries</a> customarily rely upon less stringent protocols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  The importance of daily cover lies in the reduction of vector contact and spreading of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. Daily cover also minimizes odor emissions and reduces windblown litter. Likewise, developed countries typically have requirements for perimeter sealing of the landfill with clay-type soils to minimize migration of <a href="/wiki/Leachate" title="Leachate">leachate</a> that could contaminate <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> (and hence jeopardize some <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> supplies).
</p><p>For incineration options, the release of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutant" title="Air pollutant">air pollutants</a>, including certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> components is an attendant adverse outcome.  Recycling and biofuel conversion are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> options that generally have superior lifecycle costs, particularly when total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> consequences are considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Composting value will ultimately be limited by the market demand for compost product.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_safety">Food safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Food safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canteen_kitchen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="260" decoding="async" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Canteen_kitchen.jpg/220px-Canteen_kitchen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Canteen_kitchen.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canteen_kitchen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> food preparation area.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">Food safety</a></div>
<p>Sanitation within the food industry means the adequate treatment of food-contact surfaces by a process that is effective in destroying vegetative cells of <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> significance, and in substantially reducing numbers of other undesirable microorganisms, but without adversely affecting the food or its safety for the consumer (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a>, 21CFR110, USA). <a href="/wiki/Sanitation_Standard_Operating_Procedures" title="Sanitation Standard Operating Procedures">Sanitation Standard Operating Procedures</a> are mandatory for food industries in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Similarly, in Japan, food hygiene has to be achieved through compliance with food sanitation law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fslj_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-fslj-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the food and <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">biopharmaceutical</a> industries, the term "sanitary equipment" means equipment that is fully cleanable using <a href="/wiki/Clean-in-place" title="Clean-in-place">clean-in-place</a> (CIP) and sterilization-in-place (SIP) procedures: that is fully drainable from cleaning solutions and other <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a>. The design should have a minimum amount of deadleg, or areas where the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> during cleaning is insufficient to remove product deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In general, to improve cleanability, this equipment is made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stainless_Steel" title="Stainless Steel">Stainless Steel</a> 316L, (an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> containing small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>). The surface is usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropolish" title="Electropolish">electropolished</a> to an effective surface roughness of less than 0.5 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometre</a> to reduce the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> adhesion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hygiene_promotion">Hygiene promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Hygiene promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">Hygiene</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hygiene_education.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="680" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Hygiene_education.jpg/220px-Hygiene_education.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Hygiene_education.jpg/330px-Hygiene_education.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Hygiene_education.jpg/440px-Hygiene_education.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hygiene_education.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hygiene education (on proper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handwashing" title="Handwashing">handwashing</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a></div></div></div>
<p>In many settings, provision of sanitation facilities alone does not guarantee good health of the population. Studies have suggested that the impact of hygiene practices have as great an impact on sanitation related diseases as the actual provision of sanitation facilities. Hygiene promotion is therefore an important part of sanitation and is usually key in maintaining good health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hygiene promotion is a planned approach of enabling people to act and change their behavior in an order to reduce and/or prevent incidences of <a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">water, sanitation and hygiene</a> (<a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> related diseases. It usually involves a participatory approach of engaging people to take responsibility of WASH services and infrastructure including its operation and maintenance. The three key elements of promoting hygiene are; mutual sharing of information and knowledge, the mobilization of affected communities and the provision of essential material and facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sphere_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sphere-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/WASH#WASH-attributable_burden_of_diseases_and_injuries" title="WASH">WASH § WASH-attributable burden of diseases and injuries</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=WASH&amp;action=edit#WASH-attributable_burden_of_diseases_and_injuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<p>Health impacts resulting from a lack of safe sanitation systems can be grouped into three categories: Direct impact (infections), <a href="/wiki/Sequela" title="Sequela">sequelae</a> (conditions caused by preceding infection) and broader well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WASH_WHOHealth2018_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-WASH_WHOHealth2018-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 2">: 2 </span></sup> The direct impacts include fecal-oral infections (through the <a href="/wiki/Fecal%E2%80%93oral_route" title="Fecal–oral route">fecal-oral route</a>), <a href="/wiki/Helminthiasis" title="Helminthiasis">helminth infections</a> and insect vector diseases (see also <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a>, which can contaminate drinking water). Conditions caused by preceding infection include <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunting</a> or growth faltering, consequences of stunting (<a href="/wiki/Obstructed_labour" title="Obstructed labour">obstructed labour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low_birth_weight" title="Low birth weight">low birthweight</a>), impaired <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive function</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> (related to repeated <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a> in undernourished children), <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a> (related to hookworm infections). The third category is "broader well-being" which includes: <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">Anxiety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_assault" title="Sexual assault">sexual assault</a> (and related consequences), adverse birth outcomes as well as long-term problems such as school absence, poverty, decreased <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">economic productivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_resistance" title="Antimicrobial resistance">antimicrobial resistance</a>.
</p>
Lack of clean water and proper sanitation can result in feces-contaminated <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> and cause life-threatening <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a> for infants. Most of the diseases resulting from lack of sanitation have a direct relation to poverty. For example, <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> – which is the most extreme form of "lack of sanitation" – is a major factor in causing various diseases, most notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diarrhea" title="Infectious diarrhea">diarrhea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helminthiasis" title="Helminthiasis">intestinal worm infections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WASH_UN2015_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-WASH_UN2015-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_aspects">Environmental aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Environmental aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indicator_organisms">Indicator organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Indicator organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When analyzing environmental samples, various types of <a href="/wiki/Indicator_organism" title="Indicator organism">indicator organisms</a> are used to check for fecal pollution of the sample. Commonly used indicators for <a href="/wiki/Bacteriological_water_analysis" title="Bacteriological water analysis">bacteriological water analysis</a> include the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> (abbreviated as <i>E. coli)</i> and non-specific fecal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coliforms" title="Coliforms">coliforms</a><i>.</i> With regards to samples of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_sludge" title="Sewage sludge">sewage sludge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biosolids" title="Biosolids">biosolids</a> or fecal matter from <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">dry toilets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helminth" title="Helminth">helminth</a> eggs are a commonly used indicator. With helminth egg analysis, eggs are extracted from the sample after which a viability test is done to distinguish between viable and non viable eggs. The viable fraction of the helminth eggs in the sample is then counted.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_Development_Goal_Number_6">Sustainable Development Goal Number 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Sustainable Development Goal Number 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png/220px-Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png/330px-Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png/440px-Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sustainable_Development_Goal_6.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>United Nations SDG 6 Logo</div></div></div><p> In the year 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> replaced the Millennium Development Goals. Sanitation is a <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">global development</a> priority and included <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a> (SDG 6).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-9">[9]</a></sup> The target is about "clean water and sanitation for all" by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDG6_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-SDG6-55">[55]</a></sup> It is estimated that 660 million people still lacked access to safe drinking water as of 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanitation_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-sanitation-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a> in 2020, the fight for clean water and sanitation is more important than ever. Handwashing is one of the most common prevention methods for Coronavirus, yet two out of five people do not have access to a hand-washing station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Millennium_Development_Goal_Number_7_until_2015">Millennium Development Goal Number 7 until 2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Millennium Development Goal Number 7 until 2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh,_Bangladesh.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="2304" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh%2C_Bangladesh.jpg/220px-Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh%2C_Bangladesh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh%2C_Bangladesh.jpg/330px-Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh%2C_Bangladesh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh%2C_Bangladesh.jpg/440px-Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh%2C_Bangladesh.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ring-slab_latrine_in_Kalibari_community_in_Mymensingh,_Bangladesh.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Example for lack of sanitation: Unhygienic pit latrine with ring slab in Kalibari community in Mymensingh, Bangladesh</div></div></div><p>
The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, during the Millennium Summit in New York in 2000 and the 2002 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Summit_on_Sustainable_Development" title="World Summit on Sustainable Development">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> in Johannesburg, developed the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs) aimed at poverty eradication and sustainable development. The specific sanitation goal for the year 2015 was to reduce by half the number of people who had no access to <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">potable water</a> and sanitation in the baseline year of 1990. As the JMP and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> (UNDP) Human Development Report in 2006 has shown, progress meeting the MDG sanitation target is slow, with a large gap between the target coverage and the current reality.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_(6765627649).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="438" data-file-width="718" decoding="async" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_%286765627649%29.jpg/220px-Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_%286765627649%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_%286765627649%29.jpg/330px-Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_%286765627649%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_%286765627649%29.jpg/440px-Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_%286765627649%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drive_to_2015_campaign_logo_(6765627649).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modified logo of <a href="/wiki/International_Year_of_Sanitation" title="International Year of Sanitation">International Year of Sanitation</a>, used in the UN Drive to 2015 campaign logo</div></div></div><p> In December 2006, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> declared 2008 "The <a href="/wiki/International_Year_of_Sanitation" title="International Year of Sanitation">International Year of Sanitation</a>", in recognition of the slow progress being made towards the MDGs sanitation target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurian_and_McCarney_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurian_and_McCarney-57">[57]</a></sup>  The year aimed to develop awareness and more actions to meet the target.
</p><p>There are numerous reasons for this gap. A major one is that sanitation is rarely given political attention received by other topics despite its key importance. Sanitation is not high on the international development agenda, and projects such as those relating to <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> projects are emphasised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WHO" title="WHO">WHO</a> and <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> (JMP) has been publishing reports of updated estimates every two years on the use of various types of drinking-water sources and sanitation facilities at the national, regional and global levels. The JMP report for 2015 stated that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanitation_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-sanitation-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Between 1990 and 2015, <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> rates have decreased from 38% to 25% globally. Just under one billion people (946 million) still practise open defecation worldwide in 2015.</li>
<li>82% of the global urban population, and 51% of the rural population is using improved sanitation facilities in 2015, as per the JMP definition of "<a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">improved sanitation</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiatives_to_promote_sanitation">Initiatives to promote sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Initiatives to promote sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011 the <a href="/wiki/Bill_%26_Melinda_Gates_Foundation" title="Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation">Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation</a> launched the "Reinvent the Toilet Challenge" to promote safer, more effective ways to treat human waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The program is aimed at developing technologies that might help bridge the global sanitation gap (for example the <a href="/wiki/Omni_Processor" title="Omni Processor">Omni Processor</a>, or technology for <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">fecal sludge management</a>). In 2015, the Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation published their "Water, sanitation, and hygiene strategy portfolio update and overview" called "Building demand for sanitation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>The latest innovations in the field of public health sanitation, currently in the testing phase, comprise - use of ‘locally produced alcohol-based hand rub’; ‘novel latrine improvement’; and ‘container-based sanitation’. <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)</a>, the national public health agency of the United States has recognized the stated three initiatives.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacity_development">Capacity development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Capacity development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">Capacity development</a> is regarded as an important mechanism to achieve progress in the sanitation sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> For example, in India the Sanitation Capacity Building platform (SCBP) was designed to "support and build the capacity of town/cities to plan and implement decentralized sanitation solutions" with funding by the Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation from 2015 to 2022.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-:13-64">[64]</a></sup> Results from this project showed that capacity development best happens on the job and in a learning organization culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-:42-65">[65]</a></sup> In a government capacity development initiative, it is critical to have an enabling policy and program funding to translate capacity development input into program and infrastructure outputs. Capacity development aims to empower staff and institutions, develop a learning strategy, learning content and training modules, as well as strengthened partnerships and institutions of learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-:42-65">[65]</a></sup> The Capacity Development Effectiveness Ladder Framework (CDEL) identifies five critical steps for capacity development interventions: Developing original learning content, partnerships for learning and outreach, learning strategy, visioning change and designing solutions, contribution to capacity development discourse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-:42-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs">Costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study was carried out in 2018 to compare the lifecycle costs of full sanitation chain systems in developing cities of Africa and Asia. It found that conventional sewer systems are in most cases the most expensive sanitation options, followed, in order of cost, by sanitation systems comprising <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a>, ventilated improved <a href="/wiki/Pit_latrine" title="Pit latrine">pit latrines</a> (VIP), <a href="/wiki/Urine-diverting_dry_toilet" title="Urine-diverting dry toilet">urine diversion dry toilets</a> and pour-flush pit latrines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-67">[67]</a></sup> The main determinants of urban sanitation financial costs include: Type of technology, labour, material and utility cost, density, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, level of service provided by the sanitation system, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> condition, energy cost and others (distance to <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> facility, climate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">end-use of treatment products</a>, business models, <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> height).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some grassroots organizations have trialled community-managed toilet blocks whose construction and maintenance costs can be covered by households. One study of Mumbai <a href="/wiki/Shanty_town" title="Shanty town">informal settlements</a> found that US$1.58 per adult would be sufficient for construction, and less than US$1/household/month would be sufficient for maintenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_water_supply_and_sanitation" title="History of water supply and sanitation">History of water supply and sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toilet#History" title="Toilet">Toilet § History</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_waste_management" title="History of waste management">History of waste management</a></div>
<p>Major human settlements could initially develop only where fresh surface water was plentiful, such as near rivers or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_springs" title="Natural springs">natural springs</a>. Throughout history people have devised systems to get water into their communities and households, and to dispose (and later also treat) wastewater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The focus of sewage treatment at that time was on conveying raw sewage to a natural body of water, e.g. a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, where it would be diluted and dissipated.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanitation_in_the_Indus_Valley_Civilization" title="Sanitation in the Indus Valley Civilization">Sanitation in the Indus Valley Civilization</a> in Asia is an example of public water supply and sanitation during the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> (3300–1300 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BCE</a>). <a href="/wiki/Sanitation_in_ancient_Rome" title="Sanitation in ancient Rome">Sanitation in ancient Rome</a> was quite extensive. These systems consisted of stone and wooden drains to collect and remove <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> from populated areas—see for instance the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca_Maxima" title="Cloaca Maxima">Cloaca Maxima</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Tiber" title="River Tiber">River Tiber</a> in Rome. The first sewers of ancient Rome were built between 800 and 735 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
