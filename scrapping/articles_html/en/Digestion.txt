<p><b>Digestion</b> is the breakdown of large insoluble <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> molecules into small water-soluble food molecules so that they can be absorbed into the watery <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. In certain organisms, these smaller substances are absorbed through the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_stream" title="Blood stream">blood stream</a>. Digestion is a form of <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a> that is often divided into two processes based on how food is broken down: mechanical and chemical digestion. The term <b>mechanical digestion</b> refers to the physical breakdown of large pieces of food into smaller pieces which can subsequently be accessed by <a href="/wiki/Digestive_enzyme" title="Digestive enzyme">digestive enzymes</a>. Mechanical digestion takes place in mouth through <a href="/wiki/Chewing" title="Chewing">mastication</a> and in small intestine through <a href="/wiki/Segmentation_contractions" title="Segmentation contractions">segmentation contractions</a>. In <b>chemical digestion</b>, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> break down food into the small molecules the body can use.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Human_digestive_system" title="Human digestive system">human digestive system</a>, food enters the mouth and mechanical digestion of the food starts by the action of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">mastication</a> (chewing), a form of mechanical digestion, and the wetting contact of <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a>. Saliva, a liquid secreted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salivary_glands" title="Salivary glands">salivary glands</a>, contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salivary_amylase" title="Salivary amylase">salivary amylase</a>, an enzyme which starts the digestion of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> in the food; the saliva also contains <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a>, which lubricates the food, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogencarbonate" title="Hydrogencarbonate">hydrogen carbonate</a>, which provides the ideal conditions of pH (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a>) for amylase to work, and <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> (Na<sup>+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, Cl<sup>−</sup>, HCO<sup>−</sup><sub>3</sub>). About 30% of starch is <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzed</a> into <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> in oral cavity(mouth). After undergoing mastication and starch digestion, the food will be in the form of a small, round slurry mass called a <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(digestion)" title="Bolus (digestion)">bolus</a>. It will then travel down the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> by the action of <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_juice" title="Gastric juice">Gastric juice</a> in the stomach starts <a href="/wiki/Proteolysis" title="Proteolysis">protein digestion</a>. Gastric juice mainly contains <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infants" title="Infants">infants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toddlers" title="Toddlers">toddlers</a> gastric juice also contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rennin" title="Rennin">rennin</a> to digest milk proteins. As the first two chemicals may damage the stomach wall, <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonates</a> are secreted by the stomach, providing a slimy layer that acts as a shield against the damaging effects of the chemicals like concentrated hydrochloric acid and mucus also helps in lubrication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Hydrochloric acid provides acidic pH for pepsin. At the same time protein digestion is occurring, mechanical mixing occurs by <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>, which is waves of muscular contractions that move along the stomach wall. This allows the mass of food to further mix with the digestive enzymes. Pepsin breaks down proteins into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptides" title="Peptides">peptides</a> or <a href="/wiki/Proteose" title="Proteose">proteoses</a>, which is further broken down into dipeptides and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> by enzymes in small intestine. Studies suggest that increasing the number of chews per bite increases relevant gut hormones and may decrease self-reported hunger and food intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>After some time (typically 1–2 hours in humans, 4–6 hours in dogs, 3–4 hours in house cats),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the resulting thick liquid is called <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a>.  When the <a href="/wiki/Pylorus" title="Pylorus">pyloric sphincter valve</a> opens, chyme enters the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> where it mixes with digestive enzymes from the <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a> and bile juice from the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and then passes through the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a>, in which digestion continues. When the chyme is fully digested, it is absorbed into the blood. 95% of nutrient absorption occurs in the small intestine. Water and minerals are reabsorbed back into the blood in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colon</a> (large intestine) where the pH is slightly acidic about 5.6 ~ 6.9.  Some vitamins, such as <a href="/wiki/Biotin" title="Biotin">biotin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a> (K<sub>2</sub>MK7) produced by bacteria in the colon are also absorbed into the blood in the colon. Absorption of water, simple sugar and alcohol also takes place in stomach. Waste material is eliminated from the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> during <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maton_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maton-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digestive_system">Digestive system</span></h2>
<p>Digestive systems take many forms. There is a fundamental distinction between internal and external digestion. External digestion developed earlier in evolutionary history, and most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> still rely on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In this process, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Secretion" title="Secretion">secreted</a> into the environment surrounding the organism, where they break down an organic material, and some of the products <a href="/wiki/Molecular_diffusion" title="Molecular diffusion">diffuse</a> back to the organism. <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animals</a> have a tube (<a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>) in which internal digestion occurs, which is more efficient because more of the broken down products can be captured, and the internal chemical environment can be more efficiently controlled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some organisms, including nearly all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiders" title="Spiders">spiders</a>, simply secrete biotoxins and digestive chemicals (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>) into the extracellular environment prior to ingestion of the consequent "soup". In others, once potential nutrients or food is inside the <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a>, digestion can be conducted to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesicle_(biology)" title="Vesicle (biology)">vesicle</a> or a sac-like structure, through a tube, or through several specialized organs aimed at making the absorption of nutrients more efficient.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Conjugation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="755" data-file-width="833" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Conjugation.svg/250px-Conjugation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Conjugation.svg/375px-Conjugation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Conjugation.svg/500px-Conjugation.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Conjugation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic drawing of bacterial conjugation. <b>1-</b> Donor cell produces <a href="/wiki/Pilus" title="Pilus">pilus</a>. <b>2-</b> Pilus attaches to recipient cell, bringing the two cells together. <b>3-</b> The mobile plasmid is nicked and a single strand of DNA is transferred to the recipient cell. <b>4-</b> Both cells recircularize their plasmids, synthesize second strands, and reproduce pili; both cells are now viable donors.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretion_systems">Secretion systems</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Secretion#Secretion_in_Gram_negative_bacteria" title="Secretion">Secretion § Secretion in Gram negative bacteria</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">Bacteria</a> use several systems to obtain nutrients from other organisms in the environments.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Channel_transport_system">Channel transport system</span></h4>
<p>In a channel transport system, several proteins form a contiguous channel traversing the inner and outer membranes of the bacteria. It is a simple system, which consists of only three protein subunits: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATP-binding_cassette_family" title="ATP-binding cassette family">ABC protein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Membrane_fusion_protein" title="Membrane fusion protein">membrane fusion protein</a> (MFP), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_membrane_protein" title="Outer membrane protein">outer membrane protein</a> (OMP)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (May 2011)">specify</span></a></i>]</sup>. This secretion system transports various molecules, from ions, drugs, to proteins of various sizes (20–900 kDa). The molecules secreted vary in size from the small <i>Escherichia coli</i> peptide colicin V, (10 kDa) to the <i>Pseudomonas fluorescens</i> cell adhesion protein LapA of 900 kDa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wooldridge_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wooldridge-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_syringe">Molecular syringe</span></h4>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_III_secretion_system" title="Type III secretion system">type III secretion system</a> means that a molecular syringe is used through which a bacterium (e.g. certain types of <i>Salmonella</i>, <i>Shigella</i>, <i>Yersinia</i>) can inject nutrients into protist cells. One such mechanism was first discovered in <i>Y. pestis</i> and showed that toxins could be injected directly from the bacterial cytoplasm into the cytoplasm of its host's cells rather than simply be secreted into the extracellular medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salyers_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salyers-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation_machinery">Conjugation machinery</span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_conjugation" title="Bacterial conjugation">conjugation</a> machinery of some bacteria (and archaeal flagella) is capable of transporting both DNA and proteins. It was discovered in <i>Agrobacterium tumefaciens</i>, which uses this system to introduce the Ti plasmid and proteins into the host, which develops the crown gall (tumor).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cascales_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cascales-8">[8]</a></sup> The VirB complex of <i>Agrobacterium tumefaciens</i> is the prototypic system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christie_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christie-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Diazotroph" title="Diazotroph">nitrogen fixing</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rhizobia" title="Rhizobia">Rhizobia</a></i> are an interesting case, wherein conjugative elements naturally engage in inter-<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdom</a> conjugation. Such elements as the <i><a href="/wiki/Agrobacterium" title="Agrobacterium">Agrobacterium</a></i> Ti or Ri plasmids contain elements that can transfer to plant cells. Transferred genes enter the plant cell nucleus and effectively transform the plant cells into factories for the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opines" title="Opines">opines</a>, which the bacteria use as carbon and energy sources. Infected plant cells form <a href="/wiki/Agrobacterium_tumefaciens" title="Agrobacterium tumefaciens">crown gall</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrobacterium_rhizogenes" title="Agrobacterium rhizogenes">root tumors</a>. The Ti and Ri plasmids are thus <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiont" title="Endosymbiont">endosymbionts</a> of the bacteria, which are in turn endosymbionts (or parasites) of the infected plant.
</p><p>The Ti and Ri plasmids are themselves conjugative. Ti and Ri transfer between bacteria uses an independent system (the <i>tra</i>, or transfer, operon) from that for inter-kingdom transfer (the <i>vir</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a>, operon). Such transfer creates virulent strains from previously avirulent <i>Agrobacteria</i>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_of_outer_membrane_vesicles">Release of outer membrane vesicles</span></h4>
<p>In addition to the use of the multiprotein complexes listed above, Gram-negative bacteria possess another method for release of material: the formation of outer membrane vesicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chatterjee_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chatterjee-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Portions of the outer membrane pinch off, forming spherical structures made of a lipid bilayer enclosing periplasmic materials.  Vesicles from a number of bacterial species have been found to contain virulence factors, some have immunomodulatory effects, and some can directly adhere to and intoxicate host cells.  While release of vesicles has been demonstrated as a general response to stress conditions, the process of loading cargo proteins seems to be selective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBrrom_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBrrom-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1670" data-file-width="1527" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg/125px-Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg/188px-Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg/250px-Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg 2x" width="125"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Venus_Flytrap_showing_trigger_hairs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Venus Flytrap (<i>Dionaea muscipula</i>) leaf</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastrovascular_cavity">Gastrovascular cavity</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gastrovascular_cavity" title="Gastrovascular cavity">gastrovascular cavity</a> functions as a stomach in both digestion and the distribution of nutrients to all parts of the body.  Extracellular digestion takes place within this central cavity, which is lined with the gastrodermis, the internal layer of <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelium</a>. This cavity has only one opening to the outside that functions as both a mouth and an anus: waste and undigested matter is excreted through the mouth/anus, which can be described as an incomplete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gut_(anatomy)" title="Gut (anatomy)">gut</a>.
</p><p>In a plant such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venus_Flytrap" title="Venus Flytrap">Venus Flytrap</a> that can make its own food through photosynthesis, it does not eat and digest its prey for the traditional objectives of harvesting energy and carbon, but mines prey primarily for essential nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus in particular) that are in short supply in its boggy, acidic habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leege_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leege-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phagosome">Phagosome</span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Phagosome" title="Phagosome">phagosome</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Vacuole" title="Vacuole">vacuole</a> formed around a particle absorbed by <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a>.  The vacuole is formed by the fusion of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a> around the particle. A phagosome is a <a href="/wiki/Cellular_compartment" title="Cellular compartment">cellular compartment</a> in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogenic" title="Pathogenic">pathogenic</a> microorganisms can be killed and digested. Phagosomes fuse with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lysosomes" title="Lysosomes">lysosomes</a> in their maturation process, forming <a href="/wiki/Phagolysosome" title="Phagolysosome">phagolysosomes</a>. In humans, <i><a href="/wiki/Entamoeba_histolytica" title="Entamoeba histolytica">Entamoeba histolytica</a></i> can phagocytose <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boettner_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boettner-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialised_organs_and_behaviours">Specialised organs and behaviours</span></h3>
<p>To aid in the digestion of their food, animals evolved organs such as beaks, <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radula" title="Radula">radulae</a>, teeth, crops, gizzards, and others.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Beaks">Beaks</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">Birds</a> have bony <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beaks</a> that are specialised according to the bird's <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Macaw" title="Macaw">macaws</a> primarily eat seeds, nuts, and fruit, using their beaks to open even the toughest seed. First they scratch a thin line with the sharp point of the beak, then they shear the seed open with the sides of the beak.
</p><p>The mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a> is equipped with a sharp horny beak mainly made of cross-linked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a>. It is used to kill and tear prey into manageable pieces. The beak is very robust, but does not contain any minerals, unlike the teeth and jaws of many other organisms, including marine species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miserez_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miserez-15">[15]</a></sup> The beak is the only indigestible part of the squid.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tongue">Tongue</span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">Tongue</a></div>
<p>The <b>tongue</b> is skeletal muscle on the floor of the <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a> of most vertebrates, that manipulates <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> for chewing (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">mastication</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Swallowing" title="Swallowing">swallowing</a> (deglutition). It is sensitive and kept moist by <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a>. The underside of the tongue is covered with a smooth <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membrane</a>. The tongue also has a touch sense for locating and positioning food particles that require further chewing. The tongue is utilized to roll food particles into a <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(digestion)" title="Bolus (digestion)">bolus</a> before being transported down the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> through <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublingual" title="Sublingual">sublingual</a> region underneath the front of the tongue is a location where the <a href="/wiki/Oral_mucosa" title="Oral mucosa">oral mucosa</a> is very thin, and underlain by a plexus of veins. This is an ideal location for introducing certain medications to the body. The sublingual route takes advantage of the highly <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">vascular</a> quality of the oral cavity, and allows for the speedy application of medication into the cardiovascular system, bypassing the gastrointestinal tract.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Teeth">Teeth</span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">Teeth</a></div>
<p>Teeth (singular tooth) are small whitish structures found in the jaws (or mouths) of many vertebrates that are used to tear, scrape, milk and chew food. Teeth are not made of bone, but rather of tissues of varying density and hardness, such as enamel, dentine and cementum. Human teeth have a blood and nerve supply which enables proprioception. This is the ability of sensation when chewing, for example if we were to bite into something too hard for our teeth, such as a chipped plate mixed in food, our teeth send a message to our brain and we realise that it cannot be chewed, so we stop trying.
</p><p>The shapes, sizes and numbers of types of animals' teeth are related to their diets. For example, herbivores have a number of molars which are used to grind plant matter, which is difficult to digest. <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">Carnivores</a> have <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">canine teeth</a> which are used to kill and tear meat.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Crop">Crop</span></h4>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a>, or croup, is a thin-walled expanded portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alimentary_tract" title="Alimentary tract">alimentary tract</a> used for the storage of food prior to digestion. In some birds it is an expanded, muscular pouch near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gullet" title="Gullet">gullet</a> or throat. In adult doves and pigeons, the crop can produce <a href="/wiki/Crop_milk" title="Crop milk">crop milk</a> to feed newly hatched birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain insects may have a <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a> or enlarged <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abomasum-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3200" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Abomasum-en.svg/150px-Abomasum-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Abomasum-en.svg/225px-Abomasum-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Abomasum-en.svg/300px-Abomasum-en.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abomasum-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rough illustration of a ruminant digestive system</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abomasum">Abomasum</span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system_of_ruminants" title="Digestive system of ruminants">Digestive system of ruminants</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">Herbivores</a> have evolved <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecums</a> (or an <a href="/wiki/Abomasum" title="Abomasum">abomasum</a> in the case of <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>). Ruminants have a fore-stomach with four chambers. These are the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reticulum_(anatomy)" title="Reticulum (anatomy)">reticulum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omasum" title="Omasum">omasum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abomasum" title="Abomasum">abomasum</a>. In the first two chambers, the rumen and the reticulum, the food is mixed with saliva and separates into layers of solid and liquid material.  Solids clump together to form the cud (or <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(digestion)" title="Bolus (digestion)">bolus</a>). The cud is then regurgitated, chewed slowly to completely mix it with saliva and to break down the particle size.
</p><p>Fibre, especially <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemi-cellulose" title="Hemi-cellulose">hemi-cellulose</a>, is primarily broken down into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_fatty_acids" title="Volatile fatty acids">volatile fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a> in these chambers (the reticulo-rumen) by microbes: (<a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>). In the omasum, water and many of the inorganic mineral elements are absorbed into the blood stream.
</p><p>The abomasum is the fourth and final stomach compartment in ruminants. It is a close equivalent of a monogastric stomach (e.g., those in humans or pigs), and digesta is processed here in much the same way. It serves primarily as a site for acid hydrolysis of microbial and dietary protein, preparing these protein sources for further digestion and absorption in the small intestine.  Digesta is finally moved into the small intestine, where the digestion and absorption of nutrients occurs. Microbes produced in the reticulo-rumen are also digested in the small intestine.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg/220px-Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg/330px-Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg/440px-Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flesh_fly_concentrating_food.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A flesh fly "blowing a bubble", possibly to concentrate its food by evaporating water</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialised_behaviours">Specialised behaviours</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">Regurgitation</a> has been mentioned above under abomasum and crop, referring to crop milk, a secretion from the lining of the crop of <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">pigeons and doves</a> with which the parents feed their young by regurgitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levi_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_characteristics_of_sharks" title="Physical characteristics of sharks">sharks</a> have the ability to turn their stomachs inside out and evert it out of their mouths in order to get rid of unwanted contents (perhaps developed as a way to reduce exposure to toxins).
</p><p>Other animals, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabbits" title="Rabbits">rabbits</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodents" title="Rodents">rodents</a>, practise <a href="/wiki/Coprophagia" title="Coprophagia">coprophagia</a> behaviours – eating specialised faeces in order to re-digest food, especially in the case of roughage. Capybara, rabbits, hamsters and other related species do not have a complex digestive system as do, for example, ruminants. Instead they extract more <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a> from grass by giving their food a second pass through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gut_(anatomy)" title="Gut (anatomy)">gut</a>. Soft faecal pellets of partially digested food are excreted and generally consumed immediately. They also produce normal droppings, which are not eaten.
</p><p>Young elephants, pandas, koalas, and hippos eat the faeces of their mother, probably to obtain the bacteria required to properly digest vegetation. When they are born, their intestines do not contain these bacteria (they are completely sterile). Without them, they would be unable to get any nutritional value from many plant components.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_earthworms">In earthworms</span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworm</a>'s digestive system consists of a <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gizzard" title="Gizzard">gizzard</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine">intestine</a>. The mouth is surrounded by strong lips, which act like a hand to grab pieces of dead grass, leaves, and weeds, with bits of soil to help chew. The lips break the food down into smaller pieces. In the pharynx, the food is lubricated by mucus secretions for easier passage. The esophagus adds calcium carbonate to neutralize the acids formed by food matter decay. Temporary storage occurs in the crop where food and calcium carbonate are mixed. The powerful muscles of the gizzard churn and mix the mass of food and dirt. When the churning is complete, the glands in the walls of the gizzard add enzymes to the thick paste, which helps chemically breakdown the organic matter. By <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>, the mixture is sent to the intestine where friendly bacteria continue chemical breakdown. This releases carbohydrates, protein, fat, and various vitamins and minerals for absorption into the body.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_vertebrate_digestion">Overview of vertebrate digestion</span></h2>
<p>In most <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, digestion is a multistage process in the digestive system, starting from ingestion of raw materials, most often other organisms. Ingestion usually involves some type of mechanical and chemical processing. Digestion is separated into four steps:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">Ingestion</a>: placing food into the mouth (entry of food in the digestive system),</li>
<li>Mechanical and chemical breakdown:  mastication and the mixing of the resulting <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(digestion)" title="Bolus (digestion)">bolus</a> with water, <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> in the stomach and intestine to break down complex molecules into simple structures,</li>
<li>Absorption: of nutrients from the digestive system to the circulatory and lymphatic capillaries through <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Active_transport" title="Active transport">active transport</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a>, and</li>
<li>Egestion (Excretion): Removal of undigested materials from the digestive tract through <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>.</li></ol>
<p>Underlying the process is muscle movement throughout the system through swallowing and <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>. Each step in digestion requires energy, and thus imposes an "overhead charge" on the energy made available from absorbed substances. Differences in that overhead cost are important influences on lifestyle, behavior, and even physical structures. Examples may be seen in humans, who differ considerably from other hominids (lack of hair, smaller jaws and musculature, different dentition, length of intestines, cooking, etc.).
</p><p>The major part of digestion takes place in the small intestine. The large intestine primarily serves as a site for fermentation of indigestible matter by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gut_(anatomy)" title="Gut (anatomy)">gut</a> bacteria and for resorption of water from digests before excretion.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, preparation for digestion begins with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cephalic_phase" title="Cephalic phase">cephalic phase</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> is produced in the <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digestive_enzyme" title="Digestive enzyme">digestive enzymes</a> are produced in the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>.  Mechanical and chemical digestion begin in the mouth where food is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">chewed</a>, and mixed with <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> to begin enzymatic processing of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starches</a>.  The stomach continues to break food down mechanically and chemically through churning and mixing with both acids and enzymes. <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine#Absorptions" title="Small intestine">Absorption</a> occurs in the stomach and <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>, and the process finishes with <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maton_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maton-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">human gastrointestinal tract</a> is around 9 meters long. <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">Food</a> digestion physiology varies between individuals and upon other factors such as the characteristics of the food and size of the meal, and the process of digestion normally takes between 24 and 72 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KongSingh2008_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-KongSingh2008-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Digestion begins in the <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">mouth</a> with the secretion of saliva and its digestive enzymes. Food is formed into a <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(digestion)" title="Bolus (digestion)">bolus</a> by the mechanical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">mastication</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swallowed" title="Swallowed">swallowed</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> from where it enters the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> through the action of <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_juice" title="Gastric juice">Gastric juice</a> contains <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a> which would damage the walls of the stomach and <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> and bicarbonates are secreted for protection. In the stomach further release of enzymes break down the food further and this is combined with the churning action of the stomach. Mainly proteins are digested in stomach. The partially digested food enters the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> as a thick semi-liquid <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a>. In the small intestine, the larger part of digestion takes place and this is helped by the secretions of <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_juice" title="Pancreatic juice">pancreatic juice</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestinal_juice" title="Intestinal juice">intestinal juice</a>.  The intestinal walls are lined with <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_villus" title="Intestinal villus">villi</a>, and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epithelial_cells" title="Epithelial cells">epithelial cells</a> is covered with numerous <a href="/wiki/Microvillus" title="Microvillus">microvilli</a> to improve the absorption of nutrients by increasing the <a href="/wiki/Surface_area" title="Surface area">surface area</a> of the intestine. Bile helps in emulsification of fats and also activates lipases.
</p><p>In the large intestine the passage of food is slower to enable fermentation by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a> to take place. Here water is absorbed and waste material stored as <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> to be removed by defecation via the <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neural_and_biochemical_control_mechanisms">Neural and biochemical control mechanisms</span></h3>
<p>Different <a href="/wiki/Phases_of_digestion" title="Phases of digestion">phases of digestion</a> take place including: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cephalic_phase" title="Cephalic phase">cephalic phase</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_phase" title="Gastric phase">gastric phase</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestinal_phase" title="Intestinal phase">intestinal phase</a>.
</p><p>The cephalic phase occurs at the sight, thought and smell of food, which stimulate the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>. Taste and smell stimuli are sent to the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla oblongata</a>. After this it is routed through the <a href="/wiki/Vagus_nerve" title="Vagus nerve">vagus nerve</a> and release of acetylcholine. Gastric secretion at this phase rises to 40% of maximum rate. Acidity in the stomach is not buffered by food at this point and thus acts to inhibit parietal (secretes acid) and <a href="/wiki/G_cell" title="G cell">G cell</a> (secretes gastrin) activity via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D_cell_(biology)" title="D cell (biology)">D cell</a> secretion of <a href="/wiki/Somatostatin" title="Somatostatin">somatostatin</a>.
</p><p>The gastric phase takes 3 to 4 hours. It is stimulated by <a href="/wiki/Gastric_distension" title="Gastric distension">distension</a> of the stomach, presence of food in stomach and decrease in <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. Distention activates long and myenteric reflexes. This activates the release of <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a>, which stimulates the release of more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_juice" title="Gastric juice">gastric juices</a>. As protein enters the stomach, it binds to <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> ions, which raises the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>. Inhibition of gastrin and <a href="/wiki/Gastric_acid" title="Gastric acid">gastric acid</a> secretion is lifted. This triggers <a href="/wiki/G_cell" title="G cell">G cells</a> to release <a href="/wiki/Gastrin" title="Gastrin">gastrin</a>, which in turn stimulates <a href="/wiki/Parietal_cell" title="Parietal cell">parietal cells</a> to secrete gastric acid.  Gastric acid is about 0.5% <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> (HCl), which lowers the pH to the desired pH of 1–3.  Acid release is also triggered by <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a>.
</p><p>The intestinal phase has two parts, the excitatory and the inhibitory. Partially digested food fills the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a>. This triggers intestinal gastrin to be released. Enterogastric reflex inhibits vagal nuclei, activating <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system" title="Sympathetic nervous system">sympathetic fibers</a> causing the <a href="/wiki/Pylorus" title="Pylorus">pyloric sphincter</a> to tighten to prevent more food from entering, and inhibits local reflexes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakdown_into_nutrients">Breakdown into nutrients</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" decoding="async" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b> with: digestion of other substances. You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Digestion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2011</span>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protein_digestion">Protein digestion</span></h3>
<p>Protein digestion occurs in the stomach and <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> in which 3 main enzymes, <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a> secreted by the stomach and <a href="/wiki/Trypsin" title="Trypsin">trypsin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chymotrypsin" title="Chymotrypsin">chymotrypsin</a> secreted by the pancreas, break down food proteins into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptides" title="Polypeptides">polypeptides</a> that are then broken down by various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exopeptidases" title="Exopeptidases">exopeptidases</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipeptidases" title="Dipeptidases">dipeptidases</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a>.  The digestive enzymes however are mostly secreted as their inactive precursors, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zymogens" title="Zymogens">zymogens</a>.  For example, trypsin is secreted by pancreas in the form of <a href="/wiki/Trypsinogen" title="Trypsinogen">trypsinogen</a>, which is activated in the duodenum by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enterokinase" title="Enterokinase">enterokinase</a> to form trypsin.  Trypsin then cleaves <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> to smaller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptide" title="Polypeptide">polypeptides</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fat_digestion">Fat digestion</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid_metabolism#Dietary_sources_of_fatty_acids,_their_digestion,_absorption,_transport_in_the_blood_and_storage" title="Fatty acid metabolism">Fatty acid metabolism § Dietary sources of fatty acids, their digestion, absorption, transport in the blood and storage</a></div>
<p>Digestion of some fats can begin in the mouth where <a href="/wiki/Lingual_lipase" title="Lingual lipase">lingual lipase</a> breaks down some short chain lipids into <a href="/wiki/Diglyceride" title="Diglyceride">diglycerides</a>. However fats are mainly digested in the small intestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mehta_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-mehta-19">[19]</a></sup>  The presence of fat in the small intestine produces hormones that stimulate the release of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pancreatic_lipase" title="Pancreatic lipase">pancreatic lipase</a> from the pancreas and <a href="/wiki/Bile_acid" title="Bile acid">bile</a> from the liver which helps in the emulsification of fats for absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mehta_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-mehta-19">[19]</a></sup>  Complete digestion of one molecule of fat (a <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglyceride</a>) results a mixture of fatty acids, mono- and di-glycerides, as well as some undigested triglycerides, but no free <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a> molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mehta_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-mehta-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbohydrate_digestion">Carbohydrate digestion</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate_metabolism" title="Carbohydrate metabolism">Carbohydrate metabolism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate_catabolism" title="Carbohydrate catabolism">Carbohydrate catabolism</a></div>
<p>In humans, dietary starches are composed of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> units arranged in long chains called amylose, a <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a>. During digestion, bonds between glucose molecules are broken by salivary and pancreatic <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, resulting in progressively smaller chains of glucose.  This results in simple sugars glucose and <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a> (2 glucose molecules) that can be absorbed by the small intestine.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lactase" title="Lactase">Lactase</a> is an enzyme that breaks down the <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> to its component parts, glucose and <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a>.  Glucose and galactose can be absorbed by the small intestine.  Approximately 65 percent of the adult population produce only small amounts of lactase and are unable to eat <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">unfermented</a> milk-based foods.  This is commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Lactose_intolerance" title="Lactose intolerance">lactose intolerance</a>.  Lactose intolerance varies widely by genetic heritage;  more than 90 percent of peoples of east Asian descent are lactose intolerant, in contrast to about 5 percent of people of northern European descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sucrase" title="Sucrase">Sucrase</a> is an enzyme that breaks down the disaccharide <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>, commonly known as table sugar, cane sugar, or beet sugar. Sucrose digestion yields the sugars <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> and glucose which are readily absorbed by the small intestine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_and_RNA_digestion">DNA and RNA digestion</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_metabolism" title="Nucleic acid metabolism">nucleic acid metabolism</a></div>
<p>DNA and RNA are broken down into <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">mononucleotides</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Nuclease" title="Nuclease">nucleases</a> <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribonuclease" title="Deoxyribonuclease">deoxyribonuclease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ribonuclease" title="Ribonuclease">ribonuclease</a> (DNase and RNase) from the pancreas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-destructive_digestion">Non-destructive digestion</span></h2>
<p>Some nutrients are complex molecules (for example <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a>) which would be destroyed if they were broken down into their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functional_groups" title="Functional groups">functional groups</a>.  To digest vitamin B<sub>12</sub> non-destructively, <a href="/wiki/Haptocorrin" title="Haptocorrin">haptocorrin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> strongly binds and protects the B<sub>12</sub> molecules from stomach acid as they enter the stomach and are cleaved from their protein complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21593496_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21593496-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the B<sub>12</sub>-haptocorrin complexes pass from the stomach via the pylorus to the duodenum, pancreatic proteases cleave haptocorrin from the B<sub>12</sub> molecules which rebind to <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_factor" title="Intrinsic factor">intrinsic factor</a> (IF).  These B<sub>12</sub>-IF complexes travel to the ileum portion of the small intestine where <a href="/wiki/Cubilin" title="Cubilin">cubilin</a> receptors enable <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(biology)" title="Assimilation (biology)">assimilation</a> and circulation of B<sub>12</sub>-IF complexes in the blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19627091_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19627091-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digestive_hormones">Digestive hormones</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Digestive_hormones.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="499" data-file-width="716" decoding="async" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Digestive_hormones.jpg/350px-Digestive_hormones.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Digestive_hormones.jpg/525px-Digestive_hormones.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Digestive_hormones.jpg/700px-Digestive_hormones.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Digestive_hormones.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Action of the major digestive hormones</div></div></div>
<p>There are at least five hormones that aid and regulate the digestive system in mammals. There are variations across the vertebrates, as for instance in birds. Arrangements are complex and additional details are regularly discovered. For instance, more connections to metabolic control (largely the glucose-insulin system) have been uncovered in recent years.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Gastrin" title="Gastrin">Gastrin</a> – is in the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> and stimulates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_gland" title="Gastric gland">gastric glands</a> to secrete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepsinogen" title="Pepsinogen">pepsinogen</a> (an inactive form of the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>. Secretion of gastrin is stimulated by food arriving in stomach. The secretion is inhibited by low <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Secretin" title="Secretin">Secretin</a> – is in the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> and signals the secretion of sodium bicarbonate in the <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a> and it stimulates the <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a> secretion in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>.  This hormone responds to the acidity of the chyme.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cholecystokinin" title="Cholecystokinin">Cholecystokinin</a> (CCK) – is in the duodenum and stimulates the release of digestive enzymes in the pancreas and stimulates the emptying of bile in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall_bladder" title="Gall bladder">gall bladder</a>. This hormone is secreted in response to fat in chyme.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_inhibitory_peptide" title="Gastric inhibitory peptide">Gastric inhibitory peptide</a> (GIP) – is in the duodenum and decreases the stomach churning in turn slowing the emptying in the stomach. Another function is to induce <a href="/wiki/Insulin#Release" title="Insulin">insulin secretion.</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Motilin" title="Motilin">Motilin</a> – is in the duodenum and increases the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migrating_myoelectric_complex" title="Migrating myoelectric complex">migrating myoelectric complex</a> component of gastrointestinal motility and stimulates the production of <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_of_pH">Significance of pH</span></h2>
<p>Digestion is a complex process controlled by several factors. <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> plays a crucial role in a normally functioning digestive tract. In the mouth, pharynx and esophagus, pH is typically about 6.8, very weakly acidic. <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">Saliva</a> controls pH in this region of the digestive tract. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salivary_amylase" title="Salivary amylase">Salivary amylase</a> is contained in saliva and starts the breakdown of carbohydrates into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monosaccharides" title="Monosaccharides">monosaccharides</a>. Most digestive enzymes are sensitive to pH and will denature in a high or low pH environment.
</p><p>The stomach's high acidity inhibits the breakdown of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a> within it. This acidity confers two benefits: it <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denatures</a> proteins for further digestion in the small intestines, and provides <a href="/wiki/Innate_immune_system" title="Innate immune system">non-specific immunity</a>, damaging or eliminating various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the small intestines, the duodenum provides critical pH balancing to activate digestive enzymes. The liver secretes bile into the duodenum to neutralize the acidic conditions from the stomach, and the <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_duct" title="Pancreatic duct">pancreatic duct</a> empties into the duodenum, adding <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> to neutralize the acidic <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a>, thus creating a neutral environment. The mucosal tissue of the small intestines is alkaline with a pH of about 8.5.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
