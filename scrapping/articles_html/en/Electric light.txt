<p>An <b>electric light</b>, <b>lamp</b>, or <a href="/wiki/Colloquialism" title="Colloquialism">colloquially</a> called <b>light bulb</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_device" title="Electrical device">electrical device</a> that produces <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. It is the most common form of artificial <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">lighting</a>. Lamps usually have a base made of <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>, metal, glass, or plastic, which secures the lamp in the socket of a <a href="/wiki/Light_fixture" title="Light fixture">light fixture</a>. The electrical connection to the socket may be made with a screw-thread base, two metal pins, two metal caps or a <a href="/wiki/Bayonet_mount" title="Bayonet mount">bayonet cap</a>.
</p><p>The three main categories of electric lights are incandescent lamps, which produce light by a <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb#Filament" title="Incandescent light bulb">filament</a> heated white-hot by <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas-discharge_lamp" title="Gas-discharge lamp">gas-discharge lamps</a>, which produce light by means of an <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a> through a gas, such as <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED lamps</a>, which produce light by a flow of electrons across a <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>.
</p><p>Before electric lighting became common in the early 20th century, people used <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_lamp" title="Oil lamp">oil lamps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fires</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freebert_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freebert-1">[1]</a></sup> English chemist <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> developed the first <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light</a> in 1802, followed by the first practical electric <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc light</a> in 1806. By the 1870s, Davy's arc lamp had been successfully commercialized, and was used to light many public spaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guarnieri_7-1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-guarnieri_7-1-2">[2]</a></sup> Efforts by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Swan" title="Joseph Swan">Joseph Swan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>
led to commercial incandescent light bulbs becoming widely available in the 1880s, and by the early twentieth century these had completely replaced <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc lamps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reisert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reisert-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freebert_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freebert-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The energy efficiency of electric lighting has increased radically since the first demonstration of arc lamps and the incandescent light bulb of the 19th century. Modern electric <a href="/wiki/Light#Light_sources" title="Light">light sources</a> come in a profusion of types and sizes adapted to many applications. Most modern electric lighting is powered by centrally generated electric power, but lighting may also be powered by mobile or standby electric generators or battery systems. <a href="/wiki/Electric_battery" title="Electric battery">Battery</a>-powered light is often reserved for when and where stationary lights fail, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Flashlight" title="Flashlight">flashlights</a> or electric <a href="/wiki/Lantern" title="Lantern">lanterns</a>, as well as in vehicles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incandescent">Incandescent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Incandescent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">Incandescent light bulb</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2722" data-file-width="3570" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg/220px-This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg/330px-This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg/440px-This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:This_room_is_equipped_with_Edison_electric_light.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sign with instructions on the use of light bulbs</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hagley,_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior,_Mason_memorial.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="486" data-file-width="888" decoding="async" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hagley%2C_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior%2C_Mason_memorial.jpg/220px-Hagley%2C_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior%2C_Mason_memorial.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hagley%2C_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior%2C_Mason_memorial.jpg/330px-Hagley%2C_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior%2C_Mason_memorial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hagley%2C_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior%2C_Mason_memorial.jpg/440px-Hagley%2C_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior%2C_Mason_memorial.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hagley,_St_John_the_Baptist_-_interior,_Mason_memorial.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A tablet at <a href="/wiki/Hagley#Churches" title="Hagley">St John the Baptist Church, Hagley</a> commemorates the installation of electric light in 1934.</div></div></div>
<p>While the ability of wires to illuminate when supplied with current was first discovered during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>, it took more than a century of continuous and incremental improvement, including numerous designs, patents, and resulting intellectual property disputes, until incandescent light bulbs became commercially available in the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOEHistory_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOEHistory-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In its modern form, the incandescent light bulb consists of a coiled filament of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> sealed in a globular glass chamber, either a vacuum or full of an <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">inert gas</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>. When an electric current is connected, the tungsten is heated to 2,000 to 3,300 K (1,730 to 3,030 °C; 3,140 to 5,480 °F) and glows, emitting light that approximates a <a href="/wiki/Continuous_spectrum" title="Continuous spectrum">continuous spectrum</a>. 
</p><p>Incandescent bulbs are highly inefficient, in that just 2-5% of the energy consumed is emitted as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light_spectrum" title="Visible light spectrum">visible, usable light</a>. The remaining 95% is lost as <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In warmer climates, the emitted heat must then be removed, putting additional pressure on <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In colder weather, the heat byproduct has some value, and has been successfully harnessed for warming in devices such as <a href="/wiki/Infrared_lamp" title="Infrared lamp">heat lamps</a>. Incandescent bulbs are nonetheless being <a href="/wiki/Phase-out_of_incandescent_light_bulbs" title="Phase-out of incandescent light bulbs">phased out</a> in favor of technologies like <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lamp" title="Compact fluorescent lamp">CFLs</a> and <a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED bulbs</a> in many countries due to their low energy efficiency. The <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> estimated in 2012 that a complete ban on incandescent bulbs would contribute 5 to 10 billion euros to the economy and save 15 billion metric tonnes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="Carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogen">Halogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Halogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Halogen_lamp" title="Halogen lamp">Halogen lamp</a></div>
<p>Halogen lamps are usually much smaller than standard incandescent lamps, because for successful operation a bulb temperature over 200 °C is generally necessary. For this reason, most have a bulb of fused silica (quartz) or aluminosilicate glass. This is often sealed inside an additional layer of glass. The outer glass is a safety precaution, to reduce ultraviolet emission and to contain hot glass shards should the inner envelope explode during operation. Oily residue from <a href="/wiki/Fingerprint" title="Fingerprint">fingerprints</a> may cause a hot quartz envelope to shatter due to excessive heat buildup at the contamination site. The risk of burns or fire is also greater with bare bulbs, leading to their prohibition in some places, unless enclosed by the luminaire.
</p><p>Those designed for 12- or 24-volt operation have compact filaments, useful for good optical control. Also, they have higher efficacies (lumens per watt) and better lives than non-halogen types. The light output remains almost constant throughout their life.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluorescent">Fluorescent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fluorescent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">Fluorescent lamp</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg/220px-Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg/330px-Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg/440px-Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leuchtstofflampen-chtaube050409.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Top, two compact fluorescent lamps. Bottom, two fluorescent tube lamps. A matchstick, left, is shown for scale.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">Fluorescent lamps</a> consist of a glass tube that contains mercury vapour or argon under low pressure. Electricity flowing through the tube causes the gases to give off ultraviolet energy. The inside of the tubes are coated with <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a> that give off visible light when struck by ultraviolet <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They have much higher efficiency than incandescent lamps. For the same amount of light generated, they typically use around one-quarter to one-third the power of an incandescent. The typical <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">luminous efficacy</a> of fluorescent lighting systems is 50–100 lumens per watt, several times the efficacy of incandescent bulbs with comparable light output. Fluorescent lamp fixtures are more costly than incandescent lamps, because they require a <a href="/wiki/Electrical_ballast" title="Electrical ballast">ballast</a> to regulate the <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">current</a> through the lamp, but the lower energy cost typically offsets the higher initial cost. <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lamp" title="Compact fluorescent lamp">Compact fluorescent lamps</a> are available in the same popular sizes as incandescent lamps and are used as an <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy-saving</a> alternative in homes. Because they contain mercury, many fluorescent lamps are classified as <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> recommends that fluorescent lamps be segregated from general waste for <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> or safe disposal, and some jurisdictions require recycling of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LED">LED</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: LED">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED lamp</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Led-lampa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Led-lampa.jpg/220px-Led-lampa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Led-lampa.jpg/330px-Led-lampa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Led-lampa.jpg/440px-Led-lampa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Led-lampa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED lamp</a> with E27 <a href="/wiki/Edison_screw" title="Edison screw">Edison screw</a> base</div></div></div>
<p>The solid-state <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diode</a> (LED) has been popular as an indicator light in <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> and professional audio gear since the 1970s. In the 2000s, efficacy and output have risen to the point where LEDs are now being used in lighting applications such as car headlights and brake lights, in flashlights and bicycle lights, as well as in decorative applications, such as holiday lighting. Indicator LEDs are known for their extremely long life, up to 100,000 hours, but lighting LEDs are operated much less conservatively, and consequently have shorter lives. LED technology is useful for lighting designers, because of its low power consumption, low heat generation, instantaneous on/off control, and in the case of single color LEDs, continuity of color throughout the life of the diode and relatively low cost of manufacture. LED lifetime depends strongly on the temperature of the diode. Operating an LED lamp in conditions that increase the internal temperature can greatly shorten the lamp's life.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_arc">Carbon arc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon arc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">Arc lamp</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg/220px-Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg/330px-Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg/440px-Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Xenon_short_arc_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 15 kW <a href="/wiki/Xenon_arc_lamp" title="Xenon arc lamp">xenon short-arc lamp</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a> projection system.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg/220px-Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg/330px-Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg/440px-Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Microscope_MercuryArcBulb_Detail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> arc lamp from a <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_microscope" title="Fluorescence microscope">fluorescence microscope</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Carbon arc lamps consist of two carbon rod <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> in open air, supplied by a current-limiting <a href="/wiki/Electrical_ballast" title="Electrical ballast">ballast</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a> is struck by touching the rod tips then separating them. The ensuing arc produces a white-hot <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> between the rod tips. These lamps have higher efficacy than filament lamps, but the carbon rods are short-lived and require constant adjustment in use, as the intense heat of the arc erodes them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edison_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-edison-11">[11]</a></sup> The lamps produce significant <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> output, they require ventilation when used indoors, and due to their intensity they need protection from direct sight.
</p><p>Invented by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> around 1805, the carbon arc was the first practical electric light. It was used commercially beginning in the 1870s for large building and street lighting until it was superseded in the early 20th century by the incandescent light. Carbon arc lamps operate at high power and produce high intensity white light. They also are a point source of light. They remained in use in limited applications that required these properties, such as <a href="/wiki/Movie_projector" title="Movie projector">movie projectors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stage_lighting" title="Stage lighting">stage lighting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Searchlight" title="Searchlight">searchlights</a>, until after World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edison_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-edison-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discharge">Discharge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Discharge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A discharge lamp has a glass or silica envelope containing two metal <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> separated by a gas. Gases used include, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal_halides" title="Metal halides">metal halide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>. The core operating principle is much the same as the carbon arc lamp, but the term "arc lamp" normally refers to carbon arc lamps, with more modern types of gas discharge lamp normally called discharge lamps. With some discharge lamps, very high voltage is used to strike the arc. This requires an electrical circuit called an igniter, which is part of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_ballast" title="Electrical ballast">electrical ballast</a> circuitry. After the arc is struck, the internal resistance of the lamp drops to a low level, and the ballast limits the current to the operating current. Without a ballast, excess current would flow, causing rapid destruction of the lamp.
</p><p>Some lamp types contain a small amount of neon, which permits striking at normal running voltage with no external ignition circuitry. <a href="/wiki/Sodium-vapor_lamp" title="Sodium-vapor lamp">Low-pressure sodium lamps</a> operate this way. The simplest ballasts are just an inductor, and are chosen where cost is the deciding factor, such as street lighting. More advanced electronic ballasts may be designed to maintain constant light output over the life of the lamp, may drive the lamp with a square wave to maintain completely flicker-free output, and shut down in the event of certain faults.
</p><p>The most efficient source of electric light is the low-pressure sodium lamp. It produces, for all practical purposes, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic_light" title="Monochromatic light">monochromatic</a> orange-yellow light, which gives a similarly monochromatic perception of any illuminated scene. For this reason, it is generally reserved for outdoor public lighting applications. Low-pressure sodium lights are favoured for public lighting by astronomers, since the <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> that they generate can be easily filtered, contrary to broadband or continuous spectra.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Form_factor">Form factor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Form factor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb#Bulb_shapes" title="Incandescent light bulb">Incandescent light bulb § Bulb shapes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lightbulb_socket" title="Lightbulb socket">Lightbulb socket</a></div>
<p>Many lamp units, or light bulbs, are specified in standardized shape codes and socket names. Incandescent bulbs and their retrofit replacements are often specified as "<a href="/wiki/A-series_light_bulb" title="A-series light bulb">A19</a>/A60 <a href="/wiki/Edison_screw" title="Edison screw">E26</a>/E27", a common size for these kind of light bulbs. In this example, the "A" parameters describe the bulb size and shape within the <a href="/wiki/A-series_light_bulb" title="A-series light bulb">A-series light bulb</a> while the "E" parameters describe the <a href="/wiki/Edison_screw" title="Edison screw">Edison screw</a> base size and thread characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_expectancy">Life expectancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Life expectancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Life expectancy for many types of lamp is defined as the number of hours of operation at which 50% of them fail, that is the <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> life of the lamps. Production tolerances as low as 1% can create a variance of 25% in lamp life, so in general some lamps will fail well before the rated life expectancy, and some will last much longer. For LEDs, lamp life is defined as the operation time at which 50% of lamps have experienced a 70% decrease in light output. In the 1900s the <a href="/wiki/Phoebus_cartel" title="Phoebus cartel">Phoebus cartel</a> formed in an attempt to reduce the life of electric light bulbs, an example of <a href="/wiki/Planned_obsolescence" title="Planned obsolescence">planned obsolescence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some types of lamp are also sensitive to switching cycles. Rooms with frequent switching, such as bathrooms, can expect much shorter lamp life than what is printed on the box. Compact fluorescent lamps are particularly sensitive to switching cycles.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1276" data-file-width="768" decoding="async" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg/170px-Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg/255px-Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg/340px-Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gluehlampe_01_KMJ.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A clear glass 60 W light bulb</div></div></div>
<p>The total amount of artificial light (especially from <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street light</a>) is sufficient for cities to be easily visible at night from the air, and from space. External lighting grew at a rate of 3-6 percent for the later half of the 20th century and is the major source of <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-15">[15]</a></sup> that burdens <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and others with 80% of the world's population living in areas with night time light pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Light pollution has been shown to have a negative effect on some wildlife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Electric lamps can be used as heat sources, for example in <a href="/wiki/Incubator_(egg)" title="Incubator (egg)">incubators</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Infrared_lamp" title="Infrared lamp">infrared lamps</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a> restaurants and toys such as the Kenner <a href="/wiki/Easy-Bake_Oven" title="Easy-Bake Oven">Easy-Bake Oven</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lamps can also be used for <a href="/wiki/Light_therapy" title="Light therapy">light therapy</a> to deal with such issues as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D_deficiency" title="Vitamin D deficiency">vitamin D deficiency</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> skin conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Acne" title="Acne">acne</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_affective_disorder" title="Seasonal affective disorder">seasonal affective disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Lamps which emit a specific frequency of blue light are also used to treat <a href="/wiki/Neonatal_jaundice" title="Neonatal jaundice">neonatal jaundice</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> with the treatment which was initially undertaken in hospitals being able to be conducted at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Electric lamps can also be used as a <a href="/wiki/Grow_light" title="Grow light">grow light</a> to aid in plant growth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> especially in indoor <a href="/wiki/Hydroponics" title="Hydroponics">hydroponics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_plants" title="Aquatic plants">aquatic plants</a> with recent research into the most effective types of light for plant growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to their nonlinear resistance characteristics, tungsten filament lamps have long been used as fast-acting <a href="/wiki/Thermistor" title="Thermistor">thermistors</a> in electronic circuits. Popular uses have included:
</p>
<ul><li>Stabilization of sine wave oscillators</li>
<li>Protection of <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">tweeters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker_enclosure" title="Loudspeaker enclosure">loudspeaker enclosures</a>; excess current that is too high for the tweeter illuminates the light rather than destroying the tweeter.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Automatic_gain_control" title="Automatic gain control">Automatic volume control</a> in telephones</li></ul>
<p>A stylized depiction of a light bulb features as the logo of the Turkish <a href="/wiki/Justice_and_Development_Party_(Turkey)" title="Justice and Development Party (Turkey)">AK Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_symbolism">Cultural symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cultural symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Western culture, a lightbulb — in particular, the appearance of an illuminated lightbulb above a person's head — signifies sudden inspiration.
