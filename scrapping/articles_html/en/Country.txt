<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Distinct territorial body or political entity</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">County</a>.</div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the rural area, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countryside" title="Countryside">Countryside</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Country_(disambiguation)" title="Country (disambiguation)">Country (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Map_(political).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="977" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/World_Map_%28political%29.svg/440px-World_Map_%28political%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/World_Map_%28political%29.svg/660px-World_Map_%28political%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/World_Map_%28political%29.svg/880px-World_Map_%28political%29.svg.png 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Map_(political).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The marked territories on this global map are mostly of countries that are <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign states</a> with full international recognition (brackets denote the country of a marked territory that is not a sovereign state). Some territories are countries in their own right but are <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">not recognized as such</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>), and some few marked territories are <a href="/wiki/List_of_territorial_disputes" title="List of territorial disputes">disputed</a> about which country they belong to (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a>) or if they are countries in their own right (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> (territory) or <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">the state known by the same name</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>country</b> is a distinct part of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">world</a>, such as a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">political entity</a>. It may be a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> or make up one part of a larger state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, the country of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> is an independent, sovereign state, while the country of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> is a component of a multi-part sovereign state, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. A country may be a non-<a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereign</a> or formerly sovereign <a href="/wiki/Administrative_division" title="Administrative division">political division</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>), a physical territory with a <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>), or a geographic region associated with certain distinct political, ethnic, or cultural characteristics (such as the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Country</a>).
</p><p>The definition and usage of the word "country" is flexible and has changed over time. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> wrote in 2010 that "any attempt to find a clear definition of a country soon runs into a thicket of exceptions and anomalies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most sovereign states, but not all countries, are members of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.
</p><p>The largest country by area is <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, while the smallest is the <a href="/wiki/Microstate" title="Microstate">microstate</a> <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>. The most populous is <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn Islands</a> are the least populous.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>country</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">contrée</i></span>, which derives from <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> (<span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">terra</i></span>) <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">contrata</i></span> ("(land) lying opposite"; "(land) spread before"), derived from <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">contra</i></span> ("against, opposite"). It most likely entered the English language after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_England" title="Norman invasion of England">Franco-Norman invasion</a> during the 11th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_a_country">Definition of a country</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_English">In English</span></h3>
<p>In English the word has increasingly become associated with political divisions, so that one sense, associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indefinite_article" title="Indefinite article">indefinite article</a> – "a country" – is now frequently used as a synonym for a state or a former sovereign state. It may also be used as a synonym for "nation". Taking as examples <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, cultural anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Clifford_Geertz" title="Clifford Geertz">Clifford Geertz</a> wrote in 1997 that "it is clear that the relationships between 'country' and 'nation' are so different from one [place] to the next as to be impossible to fold into a dichotomous opposition as they are into a promiscuous fusion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Areas much smaller than a political state may be referred to as countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> in England, "big sky country" (used in various contexts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_West" title="American West">American West</a>), "coal country" (used of parts of the US and elsewhere) and many other terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-5">[5]</a></sup> The word "country" is also used for the sense of sovereign territory or "district, native land".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-5">[5]</a></sup> An example of this in North America is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navajo_Country" title="Navajo Country">Navajo Country</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-oed-5">[5]</a></sup> The term "country" in English may also be used to describe <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural areas</a>, or used in the form "countryside." <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Williams" title="Raymond Williams">Raymond Williams</a>, a Welsh scholar, wrote in 1975:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>'Country' and 'city' are very powerful words, and this is not surprising when we remember how much they seem to stand for in the experience of human communities. In English, 'country' is both a nation and a part of a 'land'; 'the country' can be the whole society or its rural area. In the long history of human settlements, this connection between the land from which directly or indirectly we all get our living and the achievements of human society has been deeply known.</p></blockquote>
<p>The unclear definition of "country" in modern English was further commented upon by philosopher Simon Keller:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Often, a country is presumed to be identical to a collection of citizens. Sometimes, people say that a country is a project, an idea, or an ideal. Occasionally, philosophers entertain more metaphysically ambitious pictures, suggesting that a country is an organic entity with its own independent life and character, or that a country is an autonomous agent, just like you or me. Such claims are rarely explained or defended, however, and it is not clear how they should be assessed. We attribute so many different kinds of properties to countries, speaking as though a country can feature wheat fields waving or be girt by sea, can have a founding date and be democratic and free, can be English speaking, culturally diverse, war-torn, or Islamic.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><i>New Waves In Political Philosophy</i>, "Making Nonsense of Loyalty to Country", page 96</cite></div></blockquote><p><a href="/wiki/Melissa_Lucashenko" title="Melissa Lucashenko">Melissa Lucashenko</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">Aboriginal Australian</a> writer, expressed the difficulty of defining "country" in a 2005 essay, "Unsettlement":<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...What is this thing country? What does country
mean? ... I spoke with others who said country meant Home, but who added the caveat that Home resided in people rather than places—a kind of portable Country... I tried to tease out some ways in which non-Indigenous people have understood country. I made categories: Country as Economy. Country as Geography. Country as Society. Country as Myth. Country as History. For all that I walked, slept, breathed and dreamed Country, the language still would not come.</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_languages">In other languages</span></h3>
<p>The equivalent terms in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance languages</a> (e.g. the French <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr"><a href="/wiki/Pays_(France)" title="Pays (France)">pays</a></i></span>) have not carried the process of being identified with sovereign political states as far as the English <i>country</i>. These terms are derived from the Roman term <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Pagus" title="Pagus">pagus</a></i></span>, which continued to be used in the Middle Ages for small geographical areas similar to the size of English counties. In many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_countries" title="European countries">European countries</a>, the words are used for sub-divisions of the national territory, as in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German <span title="German-language text"><i lang="de">Bundesländer</i></span></a>, as well as a less formal term for a sovereign state. France has very many "<span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">pays</i></span>" that are officially recognized at some level and are either <a href="/wiki/Natural_region" title="Natural region">natural regions</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_Bray" title="Pays de Bray">Pays de Bray</a>, or reflect old political or economic entities, like the <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_la_Loire" title="Pays de la Loire">Pays de la Loire</a>.
</p><p>A version of "country" can be found in modern French as <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">contrée</i></span>, derived from the Old French word <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">cuntrée</i></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-oed-5">[5]</a></sup> that is used similarly to the word <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">pays</i></span> to define non-state regions, but can also be used to describe a political state in some particular cases. The modern Italian <span title="Italian-language text"><i lang="it">contrada</i></span> is a word with its meaning varying locally, but usually meaning a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ward_(country_subdivision)" title="Ward (country subdivision)">ward</a> or similar small division of a town, or a village or hamlet in the countryside.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_country-name_etymologies" title="List of country-name etymologies">List of country-name etymologies</a></div>
<p>Most countries have a long name and a short name. The long name is typically used in formal contexts and often describes the country's form of government. The short name is the country's common name by which it is typically identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The names of most countries are derived from a feature of the land, the name of a historical tribe or person, or a directional description.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> maintains a <a href="/wiki/List_of_ISO_3166_country_codes" title="List of ISO 3166 country codes">list of country codes</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/ISO_3166" title="ISO 3166">ISO 3166</a> to designate each country with a two-letter <a href="/wiki/Country_code" title="Country code">country code</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-14">[14]</a></sup> The name of a country can hold cultural and diplomatic significance. <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Upper_Volta" title="Republic of Upper Volta">Upper Volta</a> changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a> to reflect the end of French colonization, and the name of <a href="/wiki/North_Macedonia" title="North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a> was <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_naming_dispute" title="Macedonia naming dispute">disputed</a> for years due to a conflict with the similarly named <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(Greece)" title="Macedonia (Greece)">Macedonia</a> region in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols">Symbols</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/National_symbol" title="National symbol">National symbol</a></div><p>Symbols of a country may incorporate <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> or <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> symbols of any nation that the country includes. Many categories of symbols can be seen in flags, coats of arms, or seals. 
</p><h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flags">Flags</span></h4>
<p>Originally, flags representing a country would generally be the personal flag of its rulers; however, over time, the practice of using personal banners as flags of places was abandoned in favor of flags that had some significance to the nation, often its patron saint. Early examples of these were the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_republics" title="Maritime republics">maritime republics</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoa</a> which could be said to have a national flag as early as the 12th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarraclough19717–8_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarraclough19717–8-16">[16]</a></sup> However, these were still mostly used in the context of marine identification.
</p><p>Although some flags date back earlier, widespread use of flags outside of military or naval context begins only with the rise of the idea of the <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a> at the end of the 18th century and particularly are a product of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_revolution" title="Age of revolution">Age of Revolution</a>. Revolutions such as those in <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">America</a> called for people to begin thinking of themselves as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens" title="Citizens">citizens</a> as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Commoner" title="Commoner">subjects</a> under a king, and thus necessitated flags that represented the collective citizenry, not just the power and right of a ruling family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENadler2016_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENadler2016-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInglefieldMould197948_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInglefieldMould197948-18">[18]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a> becoming common across Europe in the 19th century, national flags came to represent most of the states of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENadler2016_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENadler2016-17">[17]</a></sup> Flags also began fostering a sense of unity between different peoples, such as the <a href="/wiki/Union_Jack" title="Union Jack">Union Jack</a> representing a union between <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, or began to represent unity between nations in a perceived shared struggle, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Pan-Slavic_colors" title="Pan-Slavic colors">Pan-Slavic colors</a> or later <a href="/wiki/Pan-Arab_colors" title="Pan-Arab colors">Pan-Arab colors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBartlett201131_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBartlett201131-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>As Europeans <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonized" title="Colonized">colonized</a> significant portions of the world, they exported ideas of nationhood and national symbols, including flags, with the adoption of a flag becoming seen as integral to the <a href="/wiki/Nation-building" title="Nation-building">nation-building</a> process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVirmani1999169_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVirmani1999169-20">[20]</a></sup> Political change, social reform, and revolutions combined with a growing sense of nationhood among ordinary people in the 19th and 20th centuries led to the birth of new nations and flags around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInglefieldMould197950_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInglefieldMould197950-21">[21]</a></sup> With so many flags being created, interest in these designs began to develop and the study of flags, <a href="/wiki/Vexillology" title="Vexillology">vexillology</a>, at both professional and amateur levels, emerged. After World War II, Western vexillology went through a phase of rapid development, with many research facilities and publications being established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXing20132_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXing20132-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_anthems">National anthems</span></h4>
<p>A national anthem is a <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">musical composition</a> symbolizing and evoking eulogies of the history and traditions of a country or nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Though the custom of an officially adopted national anthem became popular only in the 19th century, some national anthems predate this period, often existing as patriotic songs long before designation as national anthem. Several countries remain without an official national anthem. In these cases, there are established <i>de facto</i> anthems played at sporting events or diplomatic receptions. These include the United Kingdom ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen" title="God Save the Queen">God Save the Queen</a>") and Sweden (<span title="Swedish-language text"><i lang="sv"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Du_gamla,_Du_fria" title="Du gamla, Du fria">Du gamla, Du fria</a></i></span>). Some sovereign states that are made up of multiple countries or constituencies have associated musical compositions for each of them (such as with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>). These are sometimes referred to as national anthems even though they are not sovereign states (for example, "<a href="/wiki/Hen_Wlad_Fy_Nhadau" title="Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau">Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau</a>" is used for Wales, part of the United Kingdom). 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sovereignty_and_recognition">Sovereignty and recognition</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states" title="List of sovereign states">List of sovereign states</a></div>
<p>There is no universal agreement on the number of "countries" in the world since several states have disputed sovereignty status. By one application of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declarative_theory_of_statehood" title="Declarative theory of statehood">declarative theory of statehood</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutive_theory_of_statehood" title="Constitutive theory of statehood">constitutive theory of statehood</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> there are <a href="/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states" title="List of sovereign states">206 sovereign states</a>; of which 193 are members of the UN, two have observer status at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> (UNGA) (the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a>), and 11 others are neither a member nor observer at the UNGA.
</p><p>When referring to a specific polity, the term "country" may refer to a sovereign state, a <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Constituent_country" title="Constituent country">constituent country</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Dependent_territory" title="Dependent territory">dependent territory</a>. A sovereign state is a country <span class="clarify-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">that operates under a single government.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Clarify" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This passage needs to be better explained. (August 2022)">clarify</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> consists of 193 sovereign states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and it recognizes the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> and the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">State of Palestine</a> as <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_observers" title="United Nations General Assembly observers">observer states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Some countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Sahrawi</a>, have disputed sovereignty status. Some sovereign states are unions of separate polities, each of which may also be considered a country in its own right, called constituent countries. The <a href="/wiki/Danish_Realm" title="Danish Realm">Danish Realm</a> consists of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark proper</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="Kingdom of the Netherlands">Kingdom of the Netherlands</a> consists of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands proper</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sint_Maarten" title="Sint Maarten">Sint Maarten</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[note 1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> consists of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.
</p><p>Dependent territories are the territories of a sovereign state that are outside of its proper territory. These include the <a href="/wiki/Realm_of_New_Zealand" title="Realm of New Zealand">Realm of New Zealand</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dependencies_of_Norway" title="Dependencies of Norway">dependencies of Norway</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">territories of the United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">territories of Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Special_administrative_regions_of_China" title="Special administrative regions of China">special administrative regions of China</a>, the Danish Realm, <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land" title="Åland">Åland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overseas_France" title="Overseas France">Overseas France</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Netherlands" title="Caribbean Netherlands">Caribbean Netherlands</a>. Most dependent territories have ISO country codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-14">[14]</a></sup> Some dependent territories are treated as a separate "<a href="/wiki/Country_of_origin" title="Country of origin">country of origin</a>" in international trade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patriotism">Patriotism</span></h2>
<p>A positive emotional connection to a country a person belongs to is called <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotism</a>. Patriotism is a sense of love for, devotion to, and sense of attachment to one's country. This attachment can be a combination of many different feelings, and language relating to one's homeland, including ethnic, cultural, political, or historical aspects. It encompasses a set of concepts closely related to <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Civic_nationalism" title="Civic nationalism">civic nationalism</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Cultural_nationalism" title="Cultural nationalism">cultural nationalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span></h2>
<p>Several organizations seek to identify trends to produce economy country classifications. Countries are often distinguished as <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> or <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries.</a>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Department_of_Economic_and_Social_Affairs" title="United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs">United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs</a> annually produces the <i>World Economic Situation and Prospects Report</i> classifies states as developed countries, economies in transition, or developing countries. The report classifies country development based on per capita <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_income" title="Gross national income">gross national income</a> (GNI). The UN identifies subgroups within broad categories based on geographical location or ad hoc criteria. The UN outlines the geographical regions for developing economies like Africa, East Asia, South Asia, Western Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean. The 2019 report recognizes only developed countries in North America, Europe, Asia, and the Pacific. The majority of economies in transition and developing countries are found in Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean.
</p><p>The UN additionally recognizes multiple trends that impact the developmental status of countries in the <i>World Economic Situation and Prospects</i>. The report highlights fuel-exporting and fuel-importing countries, small island developing states, and landlocked developing countries. It also identifies heavily indebted developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> also classifies countries based on GNI per capita. The <i>World Bank Atlas method</i> classifies countries as low-income economies, lower-middle-income economies, upper-middle-income economies, or high-income economies. For the 2020 fiscal year, the World Bank defines low-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita of $1,025 or less in 2018; lower-middle-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita between $1,026 and $3,995; upper-middle-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita between $3,996 and $12,375; high-income economies as countries with a GNI per capita of $12,376 or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>It also identifies regional trends. The World Bank defines its regions as East Asia and Pacific, Europe and Central Asia, Latin America and the Caribbean, Middle East and North Africa, North America, South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa. Lastly, the World Bank distinguishes countries based on its operational policies. The three categories include <a href="/wiki/International_Development_Association" title="International Development Association">International Development Association</a> (IDA) countries, <a href="/wiki/International_Bank_for_Reconstruction_and_Development" title="International Bank for Reconstruction and Development">International Bank for Reconstruction and Development</a> (IBRD) countries, and Blend countries.
</p>
