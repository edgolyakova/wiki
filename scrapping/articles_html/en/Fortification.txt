<p>A <b>fortification</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> designed for the defense of territories in <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">warfare</a>, and is also used to establish rule in a region during peacetime. The term is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>fortis</i> ("strong") and <i>facere</i> ("to make").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJackson1911679_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJackson1911679-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>From very early history to modern times, <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">defensive walls</a> have often been necessary for cities to survive in an ever-changing world of invasion and conquest. Some settlements in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" title="Indus Valley civilization">Indus Valley civilization</a> were the first small cities to be fortified. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>, large stone walls had been built in <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean Greece</a>, such as the ancient site of <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a> (famous for the huge stone blocks of its '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclopean" title="Cyclopean">cyclopean</a>' walls). A Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Towns_of_ancient_Greece#Military_settlements" title="Towns of ancient Greece">phrourion</a></i> was a fortified collection of buildings used as a military <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison</a>, and is the equivalent of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman" title="Ancient Roman">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Castellum" title="Castellum">castellum</a> or <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> fortress. These constructions mainly served the purpose of a watch tower, to guard certain roads, passes, and borders. Though smaller than a real fortress, they acted as a border guard rather than a real strongpoint to watch and maintain the border.
</p><p>The art of setting out a military camp or constructing a fortification traditionally has been called "castrametation" since the time of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legions</a>. Fortification is usually divided into two branches: permanent fortification and field fortification. There is also an intermediate branch known as semi-permanent fortification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJackson1911680_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJackson1911680-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">Castles</a> are fortifications which are regarded as being distinct from the generic fort or fortress in that they are a residence of a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a> and command a specific defensive territory.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">Roman forts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_fort" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> were the main antecedents of castles in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, which emerged in the 9th century in the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> saw the creation of some towns built around castles.
</p><p>Medieval-style fortifications were largely made obsolete by the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannons</a> in the 14th century. Fortifications in the age of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">black powder</a> evolved into much lower structures with greater use of <a href="/wiki/Ditch_(fortification)" title="Ditch (fortification)">ditches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Rampart_(fortification)" title="Rampart (fortification)">ramparts</a> that would absorb and disperse the energy of cannon fire. Walls exposed to direct cannon fire were very vulnerable, so the walls were sunk into ditches fronted by earth slopes to improve protection.
</p><p>The arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_shell" title="Explosive shell">explosive shells</a> in the 19th century led to yet another stage in the evolution of fortification. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_fort" title="Star fort">Star forts</a> did not fare well against the effects of high explosive, and the intricate arrangements of bastions, flanking batteries and the carefully constructed lines of fire for the defending cannon could be rapidly disrupted by explosive shells. <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">Steel</a>-and-<a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> fortifications were common during the 19th and early 20th centuries. The advances in modern warfare since <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> have made large-scale fortifications <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obsolete" title="Obsolete">obsolete</a> in most situations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1911" data-file-width="1384" decoding="async" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg/220px-Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg/330px-Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg/440px-Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Imperial_Encyclopaedia_-_Military_Administration_-_pic522.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Small Chinese fort</div></div></div>

<p>Many United States Army installations are known as <i>forts</i>, although they are not always fortified. Indeed, during the pioneering era of North America, many outposts on the frontiers, even non-military outposts, were referred to generically as <i>forts</i>. Larger military installations may be called <i>fortresses</i>; smaller ones were once known as <i>fortalices</i>. The word <i>fortification</i> can also refer to the practice of improving an area's defense with defensive works. City <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">walls</a> are fortifications but are not necessarily called <i>fortresses</i>.
</p><p>The art of setting out a military camp or constructing a fortification traditionally has been called <i><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">castrametation</a></i> since the time of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legions</a>. The art/science of laying <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> to a fortification and of destroying it is commonly called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siegecraft" title="Siegecraft">siegecraft</a></i> or <i>siege warfare</i> and is formally known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poliorcetics" title="Poliorcetics">poliorcetics</a></i>. In some texts this latter term also applies to the art of building a fortification.
</p><p>Fortification is usually divided into two branches: permanent fortification and field fortification. Permanent fortifications are erected at leisure, with all the resources that a state can supply of constructive and <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical</a> skill, and are built of enduring materials. Field fortifications‚Äîfor example <a href="/wiki/Breastwork_(fortification)" title="Breastwork (fortification)">breastworks</a>‚Äîand often known as <i>fieldworks</i> or <i>earthworks</i>, are extemporized by troops in the field, perhaps assisted by such local labour and tools as may be procurable and with materials that do not require much preparation, such as <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">earth</a>, brushwood and light <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJackson1911680_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJackson1911680-2">[2]</a></sup> or sandbags (see <a href="/wiki/Sangar_(fortification)" title="Sangar (fortification)">sangar</a>). An example of field fortification<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> was the construction of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Necessity_National_Battlefield" title="Fort Necessity National Battlefield">Fort Necessity</a> by George Washington in 1754.
</p><p>There is also an intermediate branch known as <i>semi-permanent fortification</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This is employed when in the course of a campaign it becomes desirable to protect some locality with the best imitation of permanent defences that can be made in a short time, ample resources and skilled civilian labour being available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJackson1911680_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJackson1911680-2">[2]</a></sup> An example of this is the construction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_forts" title="Roman forts">Roman forts in England</a> and in other Roman territories where camps were set up with the intention of staying for some time, but not permanently.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">Castles</a> are fortifications which are regarded as being distinct from the generic fort or fortress in that it describes a residence of a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a> and commands a specific defensive territory. An example of this is the massive medieval castle of <a href="/wiki/Carcassonne" title="Carcassonne">Carcassonne</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic_Europe">Neolithic Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neolithic Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From very early history to modern times, <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">walls</a> have been a necessity for many cities. In Bulgaria, near the town of <a href="/wiki/Provadia" title="Provadia">Provadia</a> a walled fortified settlement today called <a href="/wiki/Solnitsata" title="Solnitsata">Solnitsata</a> starting from 4700 BC had a diameter of about 300 feet (91¬†m), was home to 350 people living in two-storey houses, and was encircled by a fortified wall. The huge walls around the settlement, which were built very tall and with stone blocks which are 6 feet (1.8¬†m) high and 4.5 feet (1.4¬†m) thick, make it one of the earliest walled settlements in Europe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but it is younger than the walled town of <a href="/wiki/Sesklo" title="Sesklo">Sesklo</a> in Greece from 6800 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Uruk" title="Uruk">Uruk</a> in ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>) is one of the world's oldest known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walled_cities" title="Walled cities">walled cities</a>. The Ancient Egyptians also built fortresses on the frontiers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" title="Nile Valley">Nile Valley</a> to protect against invaders from neighbouring territories, as well as circle-shaped mud brick walls around their cities. Many of the fortifications of the ancient world were built with mud brick, often leaving them no more than mounds of dirt for today's archaeologists.
</p><p>A massive prehistoric stone wall surrounded the ancient temple of <a href="/wiki/Ness_of_Brodgar" title="Ness of Brodgar">Ness of Brodgar</a> 3200 BC in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. Named the "Great Wall of Brodgar" it was 4 metres (13¬†ft) thick and 4 metres tall. The wall had some symbolic or ritualistic function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Assyrians" title="Ancient Assyrians">Assyrians</a> deployed large labour forces to build new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaces" title="Palaces">palaces</a>, temples and defensive walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic_Indus_Valley">Neolithic Indus Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neolithic Indus Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some settlements in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" title="Indus Valley civilization">Indus Valley civilization</a> were also fortified. By about 3500 BC, hundreds of small farming villages dotted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a> floodplain. Many of these settlements had fortifications and planned streets. The stone and mud brick houses of <a href="/wiki/Kot_Diji" title="Kot Diji">Kot Diji</a> were clustered behind massive stone flood dykes and defensive walls, for neighbouring communities bickered constantly about the control of prime agricultural land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mundigak" title="Mundigak">Mundigak</a> (c. 2500 BC) in present-day south-east <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> has defensive walls and square <a href="/wiki/Bastion" title="Bastion">bastions</a> of sun dried bricks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The entire city of <a href="/wiki/Kerma" title="Kerma">Kerma</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a> was encompassed by fortified walls surrounded by a ditch. Archaeology has revealed various Bronze Age bastions and foundations constructed of stone together with either baked or unfired brick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg/220px-Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg/330px-Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg/440px-Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Borg_in-Nadur_ruins.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Remains of a fortified village, <a href="/wiki/Bor%C4%A1_in-Nadur" title="Borƒ° in-Nadur">Borƒ° in-Nadur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>. Borƒ° in-Nadur is a notable example of <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> fortifications.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age_Europe">Bronze Age Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bronze Age Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, some settlements also began to be fortified. The most notable surviving example is <a href="/wiki/Bor%C4%A1_in-Nadur" title="Borƒ° in-Nadur">Borƒ° in-Nadur</a>, where a bastion built in around 1500 BC was found. <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a> was one of the most famous cities of the ancient world, especially as a result of the building program of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nebuchadnezzar" title="Nebuchadnezzar">Nebuchadnezzar</a>, who expanded the walls and built the <a href="/wiki/Ishtar_Gate" title="Ishtar Gate">Ishtar Gate</a>. Exceptions were few‚Äînotably, ancient <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a> and ancient <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> did not have walls for a long time, choosing to rely on their militaries for defence instead. Initially, these fortifications were simple constructions of wood and earth, which were later replaced by mixed constructions of stones piled on top of each other without <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>, large stone walls had been built in <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean Greece</a>, such as the ancient site of <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a> (famous for the huge stone blocks of its '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclopean" title="Cyclopean">cyclopean</a>' walls). In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_era" title="Classical era">classical era</a> <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, the city of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> built two parallel stone walls, called the <a href="/wiki/Long_Walls" title="Long Walls">Long Walls</a>, that reached their fortified seaport at <a href="/wiki/Piraeus" title="Piraeus">Piraeus</a> a few miles away.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> built large fortified settlements known as <a href="/wiki/Oppidum" title="Oppidum">oppida</a>, whose walls seem partially influenced by those built in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. The fortifications were continuously being expanded and improved. Around 600 BC, in <a href="/wiki/Heuneburg" title="Heuneburg">Heuneburg</a>, Germany, forts were constructed with a limestone foundation supported by a <a href="/wiki/Mudbrick" title="Mudbrick">mudbrick</a> wall approximately 4 metres tall, probably topped by a roofed walkway, thus reaching a total height of 6 metres. The wall was clad with lime plaster, regularly renewed. Towers protruded outwards from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg/220px-Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg/330px-Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg/440px-Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bibracte_Porte_Rebout.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstructed walls of <a href="/wiki/Bibracte" title="Bibracte">Bibracte</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">Gaulish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Oppidum" title="Oppidum">oppidum</a></i>, showing the construction technique known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murus_Gallicus" title="Murus Gallicus">murus gallicus</a></i>. <i>Oppida</i> were large fortified settlements used during the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oppidum_of_Manching" title="Oppidum of Manching">Oppidum of Manching</a> (German: Oppidum von Manching) was a large Celtic proto-urban or city-like settlement at modern-day Manching (near Ingolstadt), Bavaria (Germany). The settlement was founded in the 3rd century BC and existed until c. 50‚Äì30 BC. It reached its largest extent during the late La T√®ne period (late 2nd century BC), when it had a size of 380 hectares. At that time, 5,000 to 10,000 people lived within its 7.2¬†km long walls. The oppidum of <a href="/wiki/Bibracte" title="Bibracte">Bibracte</a> is another example of a Gaulish fortified settlement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_and_Iron_Age_Near_East">Bronze and Iron Age Near East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bronze and Iron Age Near East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casemate_wall" title="Casemate wall">Casemate wall</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Rome">Ancient Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ancient Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mura_aureliane" title="Mura aureliane">Mura aureliane</a> are a line of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_wall" title="City wall">city walls</a> built between 271 AD and 275 AD in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, during the reign of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperors</a> <a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius_Probus" title="Marcus Aurelius Probus">Probus</a>. The walls enclosed all the <a href="/wiki/Seven_hills_of_Rome" title="Seven hills of Rome">seven hills of Rome</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/Campus_Martius" title="Campus Martius">Campus Martius</a> and, on the right bank of the <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Trastevere" title="Trastevere">Trastevere</a> district. The river banks within the city limits appear to have been left unfortified, although they were fortified along the Campus Martius. The full circuit ran for 19 kilometres (12¬†mi) surrounding an area of 13.7 square kilometres (5.3¬†sq¬†mi). The walls were constructed in brick-faced concrete, 3.5 metres (11¬†ft) thick and 8 metres (26¬†ft) high, with a square tower every 100 Roman feet (29.6 metres (97¬†ft)). In the 5th century, remodelling doubled the height of the walls to 16 metres (52¬†ft). By 500 AD, the circuit possessed 383 towers, 7,020 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crenellation" title="Crenellation">crenellations</a>, 18 main gates, 5 <a href="/wiki/Postern" title="Postern">postern gates</a>, 116 <a href="/wiki/Latrine" title="Latrine">latrines</a>, and 2,066 large external windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-claridge_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-claridge-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> fortified their cities with massive, mortar-bound stone walls. The most famous of these are the largely extant <a href="/wiki/Aurelian_Walls" title="Aurelian Walls">Aurelian Walls</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodosian_Walls" title="Theodosian Walls">Theodosian Walls</a> of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, together with partial remains elsewhere. These are mostly city gates, like the <a href="/wiki/Porta_Nigra" title="Porta Nigra">Porta Nigra</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> or <a href="/wiki/Newport_Arch" title="Newport Arch">Newport Arch</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Lincolnshire" title="Lincoln, Lincolnshire">Lincoln</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian's Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a> was built by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> across the width of what is now <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">northern England</a> following a visit by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_Emperors" title="List of Roman Emperors">Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a> (AD¬†76‚Äì138) in AD¬†122.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_forts_in_India" title="List of forts in India">List of forts in India</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dholavira_gujarat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2292" data-file-width="3056" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dholavira_gujarat.jpg/220px-Dholavira_gujarat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dholavira_gujarat.jpg/330px-Dholavira_gujarat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dholavira_gujarat.jpg/440px-Dholavira_gujarat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dholavira_gujarat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Defensive wall of the ancient city of <a href="/wiki/Dholavira" title="Dholavira">Dholavira</a>, Gujarat 2600 BCE</div></div></div>
<p>A number of forts dating from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Later_Stone_Age" title="Later Stone Age">Later Stone Age</a> to the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a> may be found in India. "Fort" is the word used in India for all old fortifications. Numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilisation" title="Indus Valley civilisation">Indus Valley Civilization</a> sites exhibit evidences of fortifications. While <a href="/wiki/Dholavira" title="Dholavira">Dholavira</a> has stone-built fortification walls, <a href="/wiki/Harappa" title="Harappa">Harrapa</a> is fortified using baked bricks; sites such as <a href="/wiki/Kalibangan" title="Kalibangan">Kalibangan</a> exhibit <a href="/wiki/Mudbrick" title="Mudbrick">mudbrick</a> fortifications with bastions and <a href="/wiki/Lothal" title="Lothal">Lothal</a> has a quadrangular fortified layout. Evidence also suggested of fortifications in <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a>. Even a small town ‚Äì for instance, Kotada Bhadli, exhibiting sophisticated fortification-like bastions ‚Äì shows that nearly all major and minor towns of the Indus Valley Civilization were fortified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Forts also appeared in urban cities of the Gangetic valley during the second urbanisation period between 600 and 200 BC, and as many as 15 fortification sites have been identified by archaeologists throughout the Gangetic valley, such as <a href="/wiki/Kosambi" title="Kosambi">Kaushambi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahasthangarh" title="Mahasthangarh">Mahasthangarh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pataliputra" title="Pataliputra">Pataliputra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathura" title="Mathura">Mathura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahichchhatra" title="Ahichchhatra">Ahichchhatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajgir" title="Rajgir">Rajgir</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lauria_Nandangarh" title="Lauria Nandangarh">Lauria Nandangarh</a>. The earliest vedic brick fortification occurs in one of the stupa mounds of Lauria Nandangarh, which is 1.6¬†km in perimeter and oval in plan and encloses a habitation area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> India currently has over 180 forts, with the state of <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a> alone having over 70 forts, which are also known as <i>durg</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nossov-8_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nossov-8-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> many of them built by <a href="/wiki/Shivaji" title="Shivaji">Shivaji</a>, founder of the <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Maratha state</a>. A large majority of forts in India are in North India. The most notable forts are the <a href="/wiki/Red_Fort" title="Red Fort">Red Fort</a> at <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Agra_Fort" title="Agra Fort">Red Fort</a> at <a href="/wiki/Agra" title="Agra">Agra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chittor_Fort" title="Chittor Fort">Chittor Fort</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mehrangarh_Fort" title="Mehrangarh Fort">Mehrangarh Fort</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranthambhor_Fort" title="Ranthambhor Fort">Ranthambhor Fort</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amer_Fort" title="Amer Fort">Amer Fort</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jaisalmer_Fort" title="Jaisalmer Fort">Jaisalmer Fort</a> also in Rajasthan and <a href="/wiki/Gwalior_Fort" title="Gwalior Fort">Gwalior Fort</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nossov-8_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nossov-8-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg/220px-20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg/330px-20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg/440px-20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20090529_Great_Wall_8185.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> near <a href="/wiki/Jinshanling" title="Jinshanling">Jinshanling</a>. The Great Wall was a series of fortifications built across the historical northern borders of China.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_city_wall" title="Chinese city wall">Chinese city wall</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_castles_in_China" title="List of castles in China">List of castles in China</a></div>
<p>Large tempered earth (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a>) walls were built in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">ancient China</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> (c. 1600‚Äì1050 BC); the capital at ancient Ao had enormous walls built in this fashion (see <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> for more info). Although stone walls were built in China during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States" title="Warring States">Warring States</a> (481‚Äì221 BC), mass conversion to stone architecture did not begin in earnest until the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> (618‚Äì907 AD). The <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> had been built since the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> (221‚Äì207 BC), although its present form was mostly an engineering feat and remodelling of the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (1368‚Äì1644 AD).
</p><p>In addition to the Great Wall, a number of Chinese cities also employed the use of <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">defensive walls</a> to defend their cities. Notable <a href="/wiki/Chinese_city_wall" title="Chinese city wall">Chinese city walls</a> include the city walls of <a href="/wiki/Hangzhou_City_Walls" title="Hangzhou City Walls">Hangzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_Wall_of_Nanjing" title="City Wall of Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_City_(Shanghai)" title="Old City (Shanghai)">Old City of Shanghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_Wall_of_Suzhou" title="City Wall of Suzhou">Suzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fortifications_of_Xi%27an" title="Fortifications of Xi'an">Xi'an</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Walled_villages_of_Hong_Kong" title="Walled villages of Hong Kong">walled villages of Hong Kong</a>. The famous walls of the <a href="/wiki/Forbidden_City" title="Forbidden City">Forbidden City</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> were established in the early 15th century by the <a href="/wiki/Yongle_Emperor" title="Yongle Emperor">Yongle Emperor</a>. The Forbidden City made up the inner portion of the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_city_fortifications" title="Beijing city fortifications">Beijing city fortifications</a>.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_colonial_fortifications">Spanish colonial fortifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Spanish colonial fortifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish Era</a> several forts and outposts were built throughout the archipelago. Most notable is <a href="/wiki/Intramuros" title="Intramuros">Intramuros</a>, the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defensive_walls" title="Defensive walls">walled</a> city of Manila located along the southern bank of the <a href="/wiki/Pasig_River" title="Pasig River">Pasig River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The historic city was home to centuries-old churches, schools, convents, government buildings and residences, the best collection of Spanish colonial architecture before much of it was destroyed by the bombs of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Of all the buildings within the 67-acre city, only one building, the San Agustin Church, survived the war.

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_fortifications">Local fortifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Local fortifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ivatan_people" title="Ivatan people">Ivatan people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_the_Philippines" title="Architecture of the Philippines">Architecture of the Philippines</a></div>
<p>The Ivatan people of the northern islands of Batanes built their so-called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idjang" title="Idjang">idjang</a></i> on hills and elevated areas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> to protect themselves during times of war. These fortifications were likened to European castles because of their purpose. Usually, the only entrance to the castles would be via a rope ladder that would only be lowered for the villagers and could be kept away when invaders arrived.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3840" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg/220px-American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg/330px-American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg/440px-American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:American_flag_raised_over_Fort_Santiago_8-13-1898.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An American flag raised at the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Santiago" title="Fort Santiago">Fort Santiago</a>, 1898. Fort Santiago was a <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">citadel</a> that was a part of the <a href="/wiki/Intramuros" title="Intramuros">Intramuros</a>, a walled city within Manilla.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Igorot_people" title="Igorot people">Igorots</a> built forts made of stone walls that averaged several meters in width and about two to three times the width in height around 2000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irwin_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-irwin-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Muslim Filipinos of the south built strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortresses" title="Fortresses">fortresses</a> called <i>kota</i> or <i>moong</i> to protect their communities. Usually, many of the occupants of these kotas are entire families rather than just warriors. Lords often had their own kotas to assert their right to rule, it served not only as a military installation but as a palace for the local Lord. It is said that at the height of the <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Maguindanao" title="Sultanate of Maguindanao">Maguindanao Sultanate's</a> power, they blanketed the areas around Western <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a> with Kotas and other fortifications to block the Spanish advance into the region. These kotas were usually made of stone and bamboo or other light materials and surrounded by trench networks. As a result, some of these kotas were burned easily of destroyed. With further Spanish campaigns in the region, the Sultanate was subdued and majority of Kotas dismantled or destroyed. Kotas were not only used by the Muslims as defense against Spaniards and other foreigners, renegades and rebels also built fortifications in defiance of other chiefs in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> During the American occupation, rebels built strongholds and the Datus, Rajahs or Sultans often built and reinforced their kotas in a desperate bid to maintain rule over their subjects and their land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Many of these forts were also destroyed by American expeditions, as a result, very very few kotas still stand to this day.
</p><p>Notable Kotas:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Maynila" title="Kingdom of Maynila">Kota Selurong</a>: an outpost of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruneian_Empire" title="Bruneian Empire">Bruneian Empire</a> in Luzon, later became the City of <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cotabato_City" title="Cotabato City">Kuta Wato/Kota Bato</a>: Literally translates to "stone fort" the first known stone fortification in the country, its ruins exist as the "Kutawato Cave Complex"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jolo,_Sulu" title="Jolo, Sulu">Kota Sug/Jolo</a>: The capital and seat of the <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Sulu" title="Sultanate of Sulu">Sultanate of Sulu</a>. When it was occupied by the Spaniards in the 1870s they converted the kota into the world's smallest walled city.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Islamic_Arabia">Pre-Islamic Arabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pre-Islamic Arabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="During_Muhammad.27s_lifetime"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="During_Muhammad's_lifetime">During Muhammad's lifetime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: During Muhammad's lifetime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_expeditions_of_Muhammad" title="List of expeditions of Muhammad">List of expeditions of Muhammad</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="753" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg/220px-The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg/330px-The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg/440px-The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Battle_of_the_Trench.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the defences available during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Trench" title="Battle of the Trench">Battle of the Trench</a>, 627. Muslim defenders repelled the Confederates using Medina's natural fortifications and makeshift trenches.</div></div></div>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>'s era in Arabia, many tribes made use of fortifications. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Trench" title="Battle of the Trench">Battle of the Trench</a>, the largely outnumbered defenders of Medina, mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophets_of_Islam" title="Prophets of Islam">Islamic prophet</a> Muhammad, dug a trench, which together with Medina's natural fortifications, rendered the confederate <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> (consisting of horses and <a href="/wiki/Camel_cavalry" title="Camel cavalry">camels</a>) useless, locking the two sides in a stalemate. Hoping to make several attacks at once, the confederates persuaded the Medina-allied <a href="/wiki/Banu_Qurayza" title="Banu Qurayza">Banu Qurayza</a> to attack the city from the south. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muhammad_as_a_diplomat" title="Muhammad as a diplomat">Muhammad's diplomacy</a> derailed the negotiations, and broke up the confederacy against him. The well-organized defenders, the sinking of confederate morale, and poor weather conditions caused the siege to end in a fiasco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statesman_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-statesman-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ta%27if" title="Siege of Ta'if">Siege of Ta'if</a> in January 630,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mubarakpuri_2005_481_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mubarakpuri_2005_481-30">[30]</a></sup> Muhammad ordered his followers to attack enemies who fled from the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hunayn" title="Battle of Hunayn">Battle of Hunayn</a> and sought refuge in the fortress of Taif.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_Muir_p._142_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-William_Muir_p._142-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_world">Islamic world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Islamic world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Burj_(disambiguation)" title="Burj (disambiguation)">Burj (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caravanserai" title="Caravanserai">caravanserai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kasbah" title="Kasbah">kasbah</a>, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rabat_(disambiguation)" title="Rabat (disambiguation)">rabat (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabad" title="Rabad">rabad</a>, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Robat_(disambiguation)" title="Robat (disambiguation)">robat (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ribat" title="Ribat">ribat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Qala_(disambiguation)" title="Qala (disambiguation)">Qala (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qalat_(fortress)" title="Qalat (fortress)">Qalat (fortress)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qalat_(fortress)" title="Qalat (fortress)">qalat (fortress)</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Qasr_(disambiguation)" title="Qasr (disambiguation)">Qasr (disambiguation)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Walls_of_Benin" title="Walls of Benin">walls of Benin</a> are described as the world's second longest man-made structure, as well as the most extensive earthwork in the world, by the <i>Guinness Book of Records, 1974</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> 
The walls may have been constructed between the thirteenth and mid-fifteenth century CE<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogundiran_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ogundiran-34">[34]</a></sup> or, during the first millennium CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogundiran_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ogundiran-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacEachern_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacEachern-35">[35]</a></sup>
Strong citadels were also built other in areas of Africa. <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Yoruba_people" title="History of the Yoruba people">Yorubaland</a> for example had several sites surrounded by the full range of earthworks and ramparts seen elsewhere, and sited on ground. This improved defensive potential- such as hills and ridges. Yoruba fortifications were often protected with a double wall of trenches and ramparts, and in the Congo forests concealed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ditches" title="Ditches">ditches</a> and paths, along with the main works, often bristled with rows of sharpened stakes. Inner defenses were laid out to blunt an enemy penetration with a maze of defensive walls allowing for entrapment and <a href="/wiki/Crossfire" title="Crossfire">crossfire</a> on opposing forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_July_p._11-39_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_July_p._11-39-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>A military tactic of the <a href="/wiki/Ashanti_Empire" title="Ashanti Empire">Ashanti</a> was to create powerful log <a href="/wiki/Stockade" title="Stockade">stockades</a> at key points. This was employed in later wars against the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> to block British advances. Some of these fortifications were over a hundred yard long, with heavy parallel tree trunks. They were impervious to destruction by artillery fire. Behind these stockades numerous Ashanti soldiers were mobilized to check enemy movement. While formidable in construction, many of these strongpoints failed because Ashanti guns, gunpowder and bullets were poor, and provided little sustained killing power in defense. Time and time again British troops overcame or bypassed the stockades by mounting old-fashioned bayonet charges, after laying down some covering fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Defensive works were of importance in the tropical African Kingdoms. In the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kongo" title="Kingdom of Kongo">Kingdom of Kongo</a> field fortifications were characterized by trenches and low earthen embankments. Such strongpoints ironically, sometimes held up much better against European cannon than taller, more imposing structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thornton,_op._cit_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thornton,_op._cit-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2217" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg/220px-Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg/330px-Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg/440px-Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mury_obronne_Szprotawa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Medieval defensive walls and towers in <a href="/wiki/Szprotawa" title="Szprotawa">Szprotawa</a>, Poland, made of field stone and <a href="/wiki/Bog_iron" title="Bog iron">bog iron</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Europe">Medieval Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Medieval Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medieval_fortification" title="Medieval fortification">Medieval fortification</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Austrian_walled_towns" title="Austrian walled towns">Austrian walled towns</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">Roman forts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_fort" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> were the main antecedents of <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, which emerged in the 9th century in the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> saw the creation of some towns built around castles. These cities were only rarely protected by simple stone walls and more usually by a combination of both walls and <a href="/wiki/Ditch_(fortification)" title="Ditch (fortification)">ditches</a>. From the 12th century hundreds of settlements of all sizes were founded all across Europe, which very often obtained the right of fortification soon afterwards.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1073" data-file-width="1370" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg/220px-John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg/330px-John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg/440px-John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Smith_1624_map_of_Bermuda_with_Forts_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(explorer)" title="John Smith (explorer)">John Smith's</a> 1624 map of the <a href="/wiki/Castle_Islands_Fortifications,_Bermuda" title="Castle Islands Fortifications, Bermuda">fortifications of the Castle Harbour Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_Harbour,_Bermuda" title="St. George's Harbour, Bermuda">St. George's Harbour</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. Construction beginning in 1612, these were the first stone fortifications, with the first coastal artillery batteries, built by <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The founding of urban centres was an important means of territorial expansion and many cities, especially in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">eastern Europe</a>, were founded precisely for this purpose during the period of <a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">Eastern Colonisation</a>. These cities are easy to recognise due to their regular layout and large market spaces. The fortifications of these settlements were continuously improved to reflect the current level of military development.
During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance_era" title="Renaissance era">Renaissance era</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian Republic</a> raised great walls around cities, and the finest examples, among others, are in <a href="/wiki/Nicosia" title="Nicosia">Nicosia</a> (Cyprus), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rocca_di_Manerba_del_Garda_(Lombardy)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rocca di Manerba del Garda (Lombardy) (page does not exist)">Rocca di Manerba del Garda (Lombardy)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palmanova" title="Palmanova">Palmanova</a> (Italy), or <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a> (Croatia), which proved to be futile against attacks but still stand to this day. Unlike Venetians the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_architecture" title="Ottoman architecture">Ottomans</a> used to built smaller fortifications but in greater numbers, and only rarely fortified entire settlements such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po%C4%8Ditelj" title="Poƒçitelj">Poƒçitelj</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walled_city_of_Vratnik" title="Walled city of Vratnik">Vratnik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walled_city_of_Jajce" title="Walled city of Jajce">Jajce</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Bosnia" title="Ottoman Bosnia">Bosnia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_after_introduction_of_firearms">Development after introduction of firearms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Development after introduction of firearms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetian_Works_of_Defence_between_the_16th_and_17th_centuries:_Stato_da_Terra_%E2%80%93_western_Stato_da_Mar" title="Venetian Works of Defence between the 16th and 17th centuries: Stato da Terra ‚Äì western Stato da Mar">Venetian Works of Defence between the 16th and 17th centuries: Stato da Terra ‚Äì western Stato da Mar</a></div>
<p>Medieval-style fortifications were largely made obsolete by the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannons</a> on the 14th century <a href="/wiki/Battle" title="Battle">battlefield</a>. Fortifications in the age of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">black powder</a> evolved into much lower structures with greater use of <a href="/wiki/Ditch_(fortification)" title="Ditch (fortification)">ditches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Rampart_(fortification)" title="Rampart (fortification)">ramparts</a> that would absorb and disperse the energy of cannon fire. Walls exposed to direct cannon fire were very vulnerable, so were sunk into ditches fronted by earth slopes.
</p><p>This placed a heavy emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> of the fortification to allow defensive cannonry interlocking fields of fire to cover all approaches to the lower and thus more vulnerable walls.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Table_of_Fortification,_Cyclopaedia,_Volume_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2811" data-file-width="2065" decoding="async" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Table_of_Fortification%2C_Cyclopaedia%2C_Volume_1.jpg/170px-Table_of_Fortification%2C_Cyclopaedia%2C_Volume_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Table_of_Fortification%2C_Cyclopaedia%2C_Volume_1.jpg/255px-Table_of_Fortification%2C_Cyclopaedia%2C_Volume_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Table_of_Fortification%2C_Cyclopaedia%2C_Volume_1.jpg/340px-Table_of_Fortification%2C_Cyclopaedia%2C_Volume_1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Table_of_Fortification,_Cyclopaedia,_Volume_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Table of a typical <a href="/wiki/Bastion_fort" title="Bastion fort">bastion fort</a>, 1728. The development of bastion forts resulted from the increased use of cannons and firearms in the 14th century.</div></div></div>
<p>The evolution of this new style of fortification can be seen in transitional forts such as Sarzanello<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> in North West Italy which was built between 1492 and 1502. Sarzanello consists of both crenellated walls with towers typical of the medieval period but also has a <a href="/wiki/Ravelin" title="Ravelin">ravelin</a> like angular gun platform screening one of the curtain walls which is protected from flanking fire from the towers of the main part of the fort. Another example are the <a href="/wiki/Fortifications_of_Rhodes" title="Fortifications of Rhodes">fortifications of Rhodes</a> which were <i>frozen</i> at 1522 so that Rhodes is the only European walled town that still shows the transition between the classical medieval fortification and the modern ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-restoration_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-restoration-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fortifications also extended in depth, with protected batteries for defensive cannonry, to allow them to engage attacking cannon to keep them at a distance and prevent them bearing directly on the vulnerable walls.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Suomenlinna.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="1810" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Suomenlinna.jpg/220px-Suomenlinna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Suomenlinna.jpg/330px-Suomenlinna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Suomenlinna.jpg/440px-Suomenlinna.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Suomenlinna.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Suomenlinna" title="Suomenlinna">Suomenlinna</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_fortress" title="Sea fortress">sea fortress</a> from 18th century in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The result was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_fort" title="Star fort">star shaped fortifications</a> with tier upon tier of hornworks and <a href="/wiki/Bastion" title="Bastion">bastions</a>, of which <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bourtange" title="Fort Bourtange">Fort Bourtange</a> is an excellent example. There are also extensive fortifications from this era in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Nordic</a> states and in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, the fortifications of <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a> and the harbour <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> of <a href="/wiki/Suomenlinna" title="Suomenlinna">Suomenlinna</a> at <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a> being fine examples.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_shell" title="Explosive shell">explosive shells</a> in the 19th century led to yet another stage in the evolution of fortification. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_fort" title="Star fort">Star forts</a> did not fare well against the effects of high explosive and the intricate arrangements of bastions, flanking batteries and the carefully constructed lines of fire for the defending cannon could be rapidly disrupted by explosive shells.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malta_Delimara_one.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Malta_Delimara_one.jpg/220px-Malta_Delimara_one.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Malta_Delimara_one.jpg/330px-Malta_Delimara_one.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Malta_Delimara_one.jpg/440px-Malta_Delimara_one.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malta_Delimara_one.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ditch and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_scarp" title="Counter scarp">counter scarp</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Delimara" title="Fort Delimara">Fort Delimara</a>. Built in 1878, Delimara was built as a typical <a href="/wiki/Polygonal_fort" title="Polygonal fort">polygonal fort</a> ditches and counter scarps made to be very deep, vertically sided, and cut directly into the rocks.</div></div></div>
<p>Worse, the large open ditches surrounding forts of this type were an integral part of the defensive scheme, as was the covered way at the edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_scarp" title="Counter scarp">counter scarp</a>. The ditch was extremely vulnerable to bombardment with explosive shells.
</p><p>In response, military engineers evolved the <a href="/wiki/Polygonal_fort" title="Polygonal fort">polygonal</a> style of fortification. The ditch became deep and vertically sided, cut directly into the native rock or soil, laid out as a series of straight lines creating the central fortified area that gives this style of fortification its name.
</p><p>Wide enough to be an impassable barrier for attacking troops, but narrow enough to be a difficult target for enemy shellfire, the ditch was swept by fire from defensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blockhouses" title="Blockhouses">blockhouses</a> set in the ditch as well as firing positions cut into the outer face of the ditch itself.
</p><p>The profile of the fort became very low indeed, surrounded outside the ditch covered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caponiers" title="Caponiers">caponiers</a> by a gently sloping open area so as to eliminate possible cover for enemy forces, while the fort itself provided a minimal target for enemy fire. The entrypoint became a sunken gatehouse in the inner face of the ditch, reached by a curving ramp that gave access to the gate via a rolling bridge that could be withdrawn into the gatehouse.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2847" data-file-width="4288" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg/220px-14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg/330px-14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg/440px-14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:14-46-35-f-mutzig.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The tunnels of <a href="/wiki/Fort_de_Mutzig" title="Fort de Mutzig">Fort de Mutzig</a>, German fortifications built in 1893. By the 19th century, tunnels were used to connect <a href="/wiki/Blockhouse" title="Blockhouse">blockhouses</a> and firing points in the ditch to the fort.</div></div></div>
<p>Much of the fort moved underground. Deep passages and <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a> now connected the <a href="/wiki/Blockhouse" title="Blockhouse">blockhouses</a> and firing points in the ditch to the fort proper, with <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(artillery)" title="Magazine (artillery)">magazines</a> and machine rooms deep under the surface. The guns, however, were often mounted in open emplacements and protected only by a <a href="/wiki/Parapet" title="Parapet">parapet</a>; both in order to keep a lower profile and also because experience with guns in closed <a href="/wiki/Casemate" title="Casemate">casemates</a> had seen them put out of action by rubble as their own casemates were collapsed around them.
</p><p>Gone were citadels surrounding towns: forts were to be moved to the outside of the cities some 12¬†km to keep the enemy at a distance so their artillery could not bombard the city center. From now on a ring of forts were to be built at a spacing that would allow them to effectively cover the intervals between them.
</p><p>The new forts abandoned the principle of the bastion, which had also been made obsolete by advances in arms. The outline was a much simplified polygon, surrounded by a ditch. These forts, built in masonry and shaped stone, were designed to shelter their garrison against bombardment. One organizing feature of the new system involved the construction of two defensive curtains: an outer line of forts, backed by an inner ring or line at critical points of terrain or junctions (see, for example, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9r%C3%A9_de_Rivi%C3%A8res_system" title="S√©r√© de Rivi√®res system">S√©r√© de Rivi√®res system</a> in France).
</p><p>Traditional fortification however continued to be applied by European armies engaged in warfare in colonies established in Africa against lightly armed attackers from amongst the indigenous population. A relatively small number of defenders in a fort impervious to primitive weaponry could hold out against high odds, the only constraint being the supply of ammunition.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_and_21st_centuries">20th and 21st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 20th and 21st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fort_Campbell_09.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="553" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Fort_Campbell_09.jpg/220px-Fort_Campbell_09.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Fort_Campbell_09.jpg/330px-Fort_Campbell_09.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Fort_Campbell_09.jpg/440px-Fort_Campbell_09.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fort_Campbell_09.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gun emplacement in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Campbell_(Malta)" title="Fort Campbell (Malta)">Fort Campbell</a>, built in the 1930s. Due to the threat of <a href="/wiki/Aerial_warfare" title="Aerial warfare">aerial warfare</a>, the buildings were placed at a distance from each other, making it difficult to find from the air.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">Steel</a>-and-<a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> fortifications were common during the 19th and early 20th centuries. However the advances in modern warfare since <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> have made large-scale fortifications <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obsolete" title="Obsolete">obsolete</a> in most situations. In the 1930s and 1940s, some fortifications were built with designs taking into consideration the new threat of <a href="/wiki/Aerial_warfare" title="Aerial warfare">aerial warfare</a>, for example <a href="/wiki/Fort_Campbell_(Malta)" title="Fort Campbell (Malta)">Fort Campbell</a> in Malta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Despite this, only underground <a href="/wiki/Bunker" title="Bunker">bunkers</a> are still able to provide some protection in modern wars. Many historical fortifications were demolished during the modern age, but a considerable number survive as popular tourist destinations and prominent local <a href="/wiki/Landmark" title="Landmark">landmarks</a> today.
</p><p>The downfall of permanent fortifications had two causes:
</p>
<ul><li>The ever-escalating power, speed, and reach of artillery and air power meant that almost any target that could be located could be destroyed, if sufficient force were massed against it. As such, the more resources a defender devoted to reinforcing a fortification, the more combat power that fortification justified being devoted to destroying it, if the fortification's destruction was demanded by an attacker's strategy. From <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bunker_buster" title="Bunker buster">bunker busters</a> were used against fortifications. By 1950, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> were capable of destroying entire cities, and produced dangerous <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>. This led to the creation of civilian nuclear <a href="/wiki/Air_raid_shelter" title="Air raid shelter">air raid shelters</a>.</li>
<li>The second weakness of permanent fortification was its very permanency. Because of this it was often easier to go around a fortification and, with the rise of mobile warfare in the beginning of World War II, this became a viable offensive choice. When a defensive line was too extensive to be entirely bypassed, massive offensive might could be massed against one part of the line allowing a breakthrough, after which the rest of the line could be bypassed. Such was the fate of the many defensive lines built before and during World War II, such as the <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Line" title="Siegfried Line">Siegfried Line</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Stalin_Line" title="Stalin Line">Stalin Line</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Wall" title="Atlantic Wall">Atlantic Wall</a>. This was not the case with the <a href="/wiki/Maginot_Line" title="Maginot Line">Maginot Line</a>; it was designed to force the Germans to invade other countries (Belgium or Switzerland) to go around it, and was successful in that sense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="943" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="401" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg/170px-Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg/255px-Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg/340px-Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Image-GBU-24_Missile_testmontage-gi_BLU-109_bomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/GBU-24_Paveway_III" title="GBU-24 Paveway III">GBU-24</a> 2,000¬†lb (910¬†kg) bomb hits the ground. The development of <a href="/wiki/Bunker_buster" title="Bunker buster">bunker busters</a>, bombs designed to penetrate hardened targets buried underground, led to a decline in the use of fortifications.</div></div></div>
<p>Instead field fortification rose to dominate defensive action. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a> which dominated World War I, these defences were more temporary in nature. This was an advantage because since it was less extensive it formed a less obvious target for enemy force to be directed against.
</p><p>If sufficient power were massed against one point to penetrate it, the forces based there could be withdrawn and the line could be re-established relatively quickly. Instead of a supposedly impenetrable defensive line, such fortifications emphasized <a href="/wiki/Defence_in_depth" title="Defence in depth">defence in depth</a>, so that as defenders were forced to pull back or were overrun, the lines of defenders behind them could take over the defence.
</p><p>Because the mobile offensives practised by both sides usually focused on avoiding the strongest points of a defensive line, these defences were usually relatively thin and spread along the length of a line. The defence was usually not equally strong throughout however.
</p><p>The strength of the defensive line in an area varied according to how rapidly an attacking force could progress in the terrain that was being defended‚Äîboth the terrain the defensive line was built on and the ground behind it that an attacker might hope to break out into. This was both for reasons of the strategic value of the ground, and its defensive value.
</p><p>This was possible because while offensive tactics were focused on mobility, so were defensive tactics. The dug in defences consisted primarily of infantry and antitank guns. Defending tanks and tank destroyers would be concentrated in mobile <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigades</a> behind the defensive line. If a major offensive was launched against a point in the line, mobile reinforcements would be sent to reinforce that part of the line that was in danger of failing.
</p><p>Thus the defensive line could be relatively thin because the bulk of the fighting power of the defenders was not concentrated in the line itself but rather in the mobile reserves. A notable exception to this rule was seen in the defensive lines at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kursk" title="Battle of Kursk">Battle of Kursk</a> during World War II, where German forces deliberately attacked into the strongest part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> defences seeking to crush them utterly.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> that was being defended was of primary importance because <a href="/wiki/Open_terrain" title="Open terrain">open terrain</a> that tanks could move over quickly made possible rapid advances into the defenders' rear areas that were very dangerous to the defenders. Thus such terrain had to be defended at all cost.
</p><p>In addition, since in theory the defensive line only had to hold out long enough for mobile reserves to reinforce it, terrain that did not permit rapid advance could be held more weakly because the enemy's advance into it would be slower, giving the defenders more time to reinforce that point in the line. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Hurtgen_Forest" title="Battle of the Hurtgen Forest">Battle of the Hurtgen Forest</a> in Germany during the closing stages of World War II is an excellent example of how difficult terrain could be used to the defenders' advantage.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NORADNorth-Portal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2357" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/NORADNorth-Portal.jpg/220px-NORADNorth-Portal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/NORADNorth-Portal.jpg/330px-NORADNorth-Portal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/NORADNorth-Portal.jpg/440px-NORADNorth-Portal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NORADNorth-Portal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cheyenne_Mountain_Complex" title="Cheyenne Mountain Complex">Cheyenne Mountain Complex</a> is an underground <a href="/wiki/Bunker" title="Bunker">bunker</a> used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Aerospace_Defense_Command" title="North American Aerospace Defense Command">North American Aerospace Defense Command</a>. Cheyenne Mountain is an example of a mid-20th century fortification built deep in a mountain.</div></div></div>
<p>After World War II, <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missiles</a> capable of reaching much of the way around the world were developed, and so speed became an essential characteristic of the strongest militaries and defenses. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_silo" title="Missile silo">Missile silos</a> were developed, so missiles could be fired from the middle of a country and hit cities and targets in another country, and airplanes (and air carriers) became major defenses and offensive weapons (leading to an expansion of the use of airports and airstrips as fortifications). Mobile defenses could be had underwater, too, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_submarine" title="Nuclear submarine">nuclear submarines</a> capable of firing missiles. Some bunkers in the mid to late 20th century came to be buried deep inside mountains and prominent rocks, such as <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne_Mountain_Complex" title="Cheyenne Mountain Complex">Cheyenne Mountain Complex</a>. On the ground itself, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minefield" title="Minefield">minefields</a> have been used as hidden defences in modern warfare, often remaining long after the wars that produced them have ended.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Demilitarized_zone" title="Demilitarized zone">Demilitarized zones</a> along borders are arguably another type of fortification, although a passive kind, providing a buffer between potentially hostile militaries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_airfields">Military airfields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Military airfields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Military airfields offer a fixed "target rich" environment for even relatively small enemy forces, using <a href="/wiki/Hit-and-run_tactics" title="Hit-and-run tactics">hit-and-run tactics</a> by ground forces, stand-off attacks (mortars and rockets), air attacks, or ballistic missiles. Key targets ‚Äì aircraft, munitions, fuel, and vital technical personnel ‚Äì can be protected by fortifications.
</p><p>Aircraft can be protected by <a href="/wiki/Revetment" title="Revetment">revetments</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesco_barrier" title="Hesco barrier">Hesco barriers</a>, or hardened aircraft shelters which will protect from many types of attack. Larger aircraft types tend to be based outside the operational theatre.
</p><p>Munition storage follows safety rules which use fortifications (bunkers and bunds) to provide protection against accident and chain reactions (sympathetic detonations). Weapons for rearming aircraft can be stored in small fortified <i>expense</i> stores closer to the aircraft. At Bien Hoa South Vietnam on the morning of 16 May 1965, as aircraft were being re-fuelled and armed, a chain reaction explosion destroyed 13 aircraft, killed 34 personnel, and injured over 100; this, along with damage and losses of aircraft to enemy attack (by both <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_tactics" title="Infiltration tactics">infiltration</a> and stand off attacks), led to the construction of revetments and shelters to protect aircraft throughout South Vietnam.
</p><p>Aircrew and ground personnel will need protection during enemy attacks and fortifications range from culvert section "duck and cover" shelters to permanent air-raid shelters. Soft locations with high personnel densities such as accommodation and messing facilities can have limited protection by placing prefabricated concrete walls or barriers around them, examples of barriers are Jersey Barriers, T Barriers or Splinter Protection Units (SPUs). Older fortification may prove useful such as the old 'Yugo' pyramid shelters built in the 1980s which were used by US personnel on 8 Jan 2020 when Iran fired 11 ballistic missiles at Ayn al-Asad Airbase in Iraq.
</p><p>Fuel is volatile and has to comply with rules for storage which provide protection against accident. Fuel in underground bulk fuel installations is well protected though valves and controls are vulnerable to enemy action. Above ground tanks can be susceptible to attack.
</p><p>Ground support equipment will need to be protected by fortifications to be usable after an enemy attack.
</p><p>Permanent (concrete) guard fortifications are safer, stronger, last longer and are more cost effective than sandbag fortifications. Prefabricated positions can be made from concrete culvert sections. The British Yarnold Bunker is made from sections of a concrete pipe.
</p><p>Guard Towers provide increased field of view but a lower level of protection.
</p><p>Dispersal and camouflage of assets can supplement fortifications against some forms of airfield attack.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-insurgency">Counter-insurgency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Counter-insurgency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Just as in colonial periods, comparatively obsolete fortifications are still used for low-intensity conflicts. Such fortifications range in size from small patrol bases or <a href="/wiki/Forward_operating_base" title="Forward operating base">forward operating bases</a> up to huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbase" title="Airbase">airbases</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Bastion" title="Camp Bastion">Camp Bastion</a>/<a href="/wiki/Camp_Leatherneck" title="Camp Leatherneck">Leatherneck</a> in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>. Much like in the 18th and 19th century, because the enemy is not a powerful military force with the heavy weaponry required to destroy fortifications, walls of <a href="/wiki/Gabion" title="Gabion">gabion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandbag" title="Sandbag">sandbag</a> or even simple mud can provide protection against small arms and anti-tank weapons ‚Äì although such fortifications are still vulnerable to mortar and artillery fire.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forts">Forts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Forts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2200" data-file-width="2750" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg/220px-Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg/330px-Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg/440px-Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fortaleza_Ozama_RD_11_2017_6495.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Fortaleza_Ozama" title="Fortaleza Ozama">Ozama Fortress</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> is recognized by UNESCO for being the oldest military construction of European origin in the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Forts in modern American usage often refer to space set aside by governments for a permanent military facility; these often do not have any actual fortifications, and can have specializations (military barracks, administration, medical facilities, or intelligence).
</p><p>However, there are some modern fortifications that are referred to as forts. These are typically small semi permanent fortifications. In urban combat they are built by upgrading existing structures such as houses or public buildings. In field warfare they are often log, sandbag or <a href="/wiki/Gabion" title="Gabion">gabion</a> type construction.
</p><p>Such forts are typically only used in low level conflict, such as counterinsurgency conflicts or very low level conventional conflicts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Indonesia%E2%80%93Malaysia_confrontation" title="Indonesia‚ÄìMalaysia confrontation">Indonesia‚ÄìMalaysia confrontation</a>, which saw the use of log forts for use by forward <a href="/wiki/Platoon" title="Platoon">platoons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">companies</a>. The reason for this is that static above ground forts can not survive modern direct or indirect fire weapons larger than mortars, RPGs and small arms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prisons_and_others">Prisons and others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Prisons and others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fortifications designed to keep the inhabitants of a facility in rather than attacker out can also be found, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisons" title="Prisons">prisons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_camps" title="Concentration camps">concentration camps</a>, and other such facilities, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermax" title="Supermax">supermaxes</a> having some of the strongest of those. Those are covered in other articles, as most prisons and concentration camps are not primarily military forts (although forts, camps, and garrison towns have been used as prisons and/or concentration camps; such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theresienstadt" title="Theresienstadt">Theresienstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_detention_camp" title="Guantanamo Bay detention camp">Guantanamo Bay detention camp</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a> for example).
</p>
