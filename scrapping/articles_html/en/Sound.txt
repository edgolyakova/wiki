<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>sound</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a> that propagates as an <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_wave" title="Acoustic wave">acoustic wave</a>, through a <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">transmission medium</a> such as a gas, liquid or solid.
In human <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, sound is the <i>reception</i> of such waves and their <i>perception</i> by the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Only acoustic waves that have  <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a> lying between about 20 Hz and 20 kHz, the <a href="/wiki/Audio_frequency" title="Audio frequency">audio frequency</a> range, elicit an auditory percept in humans. In air at atmospheric pressure, these represent sound waves with <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of 17 meters (56 ft) to 1.7 centimeters (0.67 in).   Sound waves above 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">kHz</a> are known as <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasound</a> and are not audible to humans. Sound waves below 20 Hz are known as <a href="/wiki/Infrasound" title="Infrasound">infrasound</a>. Different animal species have varying <a href="/wiki/Hearing_range" title="Hearing range">hearing ranges</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics">Acoustics</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">Acoustics</a></div>
<p>Acoustics is the interdisciplinary science that deals with the study of <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_wave" title="Mechanical wave">mechanical waves</a> in gasses, liquids, and solids including <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a>, sound, ultrasound, and infrasound. A scientist who works in the field of <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustics</a> is an <i>acoustician</i>, while someone working in the field of <a href="/wiki/Acoustical_engineering" title="Acoustical engineering">acoustical engineering</a> may be called an <i>acoustical engineer</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Audio_engineer" title="Audio engineer">audio engineer</a>, on the other hand, is concerned with the recording, manipulation, mixing, and reproduction of sound.
</p><p>Applications of acoustics are found in almost all aspects of modern society, subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Aeroacoustics" title="Aeroacoustics">aeroacoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal_processing" title="Audio signal processing">audio signal processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architectural_acoustics" title="Architectural acoustics">architectural acoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioacoustics" title="Bioacoustics">bioacoustics</a>, electro-acoustics, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_noise" title="Environmental noise">environmental noise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musical_acoustics" title="Musical acoustics">musical acoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noise_control" title="Noise control">noise control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">psychoacoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">speech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasound</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Underwater_acoustics" title="Underwater acoustics">underwater acoustics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<p>Sound is defined as "(a) <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">Oscillation</a>  in pressure, stress, particle displacement, particle velocity, etc., propagated in a medium with internal forces (e.g., elastic or viscous), or the superposition of such propagated oscillation. (b) Auditory sensation evoked by the oscillation described in (a)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Sound can be viewed as a wave motion in  air or other elastic media. In this case, sound is a stimulus. Sound can also be viewed as an excitation of the hearing mechanism that results in the perception of sound. In this case, sound is a <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sensation</a>.
</p>

<p>Sound can propagate through a medium such as air, water and solids as <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_wave" title="Longitudinal wave">longitudinal waves</a> and also as a <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse wave</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solids" title="Solids">solids</a>. The sound waves are generated by a sound source, such as the vibrating <a href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(acoustics)" title="Diaphragm (acoustics)">diaphragm</a> of a stereo speaker. The sound source creates vibrations in the surrounding medium. As the source continues to vibrate the medium, the vibrations propagate away from the source at the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>, thus forming the sound wave. At a fixed distance from the source, the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, and displacement of the medium vary in time. At an instant in time, the pressure, velocity, and displacement vary in space. Note that the particles of the medium do not travel with the sound wave. This is intuitively obvious for a solid, and the same is true for liquids and gases (that is, the vibrations of particles in the gas or liquid transport the vibrations, while the <i>average</i> position of the particles over time does not change).  During propagation, waves can be <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflected</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracted</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuated</a> by the medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHU_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-JHU-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The behavior of sound propagation is generally affected by three things:
</p>
<ul><li>A complex relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and pressure of the medium. This relationship, affected by temperature, determines the speed of sound within the medium.</li>
<li>Motion of the medium itself. If the medium is moving, this movement may increase or decrease the absolute speed of the sound wave depending on the direction of the movement. For example, sound moving through wind will have its speed of propagation increased by the speed of the wind if the sound and wind are moving in the same direction. If the sound and wind are moving in opposite directions, the speed of the sound wave will be decreased by the speed of the wind.</li>
<li>The viscosity of the medium. Medium <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> determines the rate at which sound is attenuated. For many media, such as air or water, attenuation due to viscosity is negligible.</li></ul>
<p>When sound is moving through a medium that does not have constant physical properties, it may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refracted" title="Refracted">refracted</a> (either dispersed or focused).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHU_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-JHU-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The mechanical vibrations that can be interpreted as sound can travel through all <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">forms of matter</a>: gases, liquids, solids, and <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasmas</a>. The matter that supports the sound is called the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">medium</a>. Sound cannot travel through a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waves">Waves</span></h3>
<p>Sound is transmitted through gases, plasma, and liquids as <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_wave" title="Longitudinal wave">longitudinal waves</a>, also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compression</a> waves. It requires a medium to propagate. Through solids, however, it can be transmitted as both longitudinal waves and <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse waves</a>. Longitudinal sound waves are waves of alternating <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> deviations from the <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> pressure, causing local regions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rarefaction" title="Rarefaction">rarefaction</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse waves</a> (in solids) are waves of alternating <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shear stress</a> at right angle to the direction of propagation.
</p><p>Sound waves may be viewed using parabolic mirrors and objects that produce sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The energy carried by an oscillating sound wave converts back and forth between the potential energy of the extra <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compression</a> (in case of longitudinal waves) or lateral displacement <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strain_(materials_science)" title="Strain (materials science)">strain</a> (in case of transverse waves) of the matter, and the kinetic energy of the displacement velocity of particles of the medium.
</p>

<p>Although there are many complexities relating to the transmission of sounds, at the point of reception (i.e. the ears), sound is readily dividable into two simple elements: pressure and time. These fundamental elements form the basis of all sound waves. They can be used to describe, in absolute terms, every sound we hear.
</p><p>In order to understand the sound more fully, a complex wave such as the one shown in a blue background on the right of this text, is usually separated into its component parts, which are a combination of various sound wave frequencies (and noise).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handel,_S._1995_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handel,_S._1995-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall,_R._A._1986_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall,_R._A._1986-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matthews,_M._1999_pp._79-88_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews,_M._1999_pp._79-88-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sound <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> are often simplified to a description in terms of <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sinusoidal</a> <a href="/wiki/Plane_wave" title="Plane wave">plane waves</a>, which are characterized by these generic properties:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">Frequency</a>, or its inverse, <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">Amplitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure" title="Sound pressure">sound pressure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sound_intensity" title="Sound intensity">Intensity</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">Speed of sound</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direction_(geometry,_geography)" title="Direction (geometry, geography)">Direction</a></li></ul>
<p>Sound that is perceptible by humans has frequencies from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. In air at <a href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, the corresponding wavelengths of sound waves range from 17 m (56 ft) to 17 mm (0.67 in).  Sometimes speed and direction are combined as a <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometric)" title="Vector (geometric)">vector</a>; wave number and direction are combined as a <a href="/wiki/Wave_vector" title="Wave vector">wave vector</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">Transverse waves</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shear</a> waves, have the additional property, <i><a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a></i>, and are not a characteristic of sound waves.
</p>

In 20 °C (68 °F) air at sea level, the speed of sound is approximately 343 m/s (1,230 km/h; 767 mph) using the formula <span class="nowrap"><i>v</i> [m/s] = 331 + 0.6 <i>T</i> [°C]</span>.  The speed of sound is also slightly sensitive, being subject to a second-order <a href="/wiki/Anharmonicity" title="Anharmonicity">anharmonic</a> effect, to the sound amplitude, which means there are non-linear propagation effects, such as the production of harmonics and mixed tones not present in the original sound (see <a href="/wiki/Parametric_array" title="Parametric array">parametric array</a>). If <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">relativistic</a> effects are important, the speed of sound is calculated from the <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_Euler_equations" title="Relativistic Euler equations">relativistic Euler equations</a>.
</p><p>In fresh water the speed of sound is approximately 1,482 m/s (5,335 km/h; 3,315 mph). In steel, the speed of sound is about 5,960 m/s (21,460 km/h; 13,330 mph). Sound moves the fastest in solid atomic hydrogen at about 36,000 m/s (129,600 km/h; 80,530 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perception">Perception</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">Psychoacoustics</a></div>
<p>A distinct use of the term <i>sound</i> from its use in physics is that in physiology and psychology, where the term refers to the subject of <i>perception</i> by the brain. The field of <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">psychoacoustics</a> is dedicated to such studies. Webster's 1936 dictionary defined sound as: "1. The sensation of hearing, that which is heard; specif.: a. Psychophysics. Sensation due to stimulation of the auditory nerves and auditory centers of the brain, usually by vibrations transmitted in a material medium, commonly air, affecting the organ of hearing. b. Physics. Vibrational energy which occasions such a sensation. Sound is propagated by progressive longitudinal vibratory disturbances (sound waves)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This means that the correct response to the question: "<a href="/wiki/If_a_tree_falls_in_a_forest" title="If a tree falls in a forest">if a tree falls in the forest with no one to hear it fall, does it make a sound?</a>" is "yes", and "no", dependent on whether being answered using the physical, or the psychophysical definition, respectively.
</p><p>The physical reception of sound in any hearing organism is limited to a range of frequencies. Humans normally hear sound frequencies between approximately 20 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a> and 20,000 Hz (20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">kHz</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olson1967_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olson1967-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 382">: 382 </span></sup> The upper limit decreases with age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olson1967_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Olson1967-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 249">: 249 </span></sup> Sometimes <i>sound</i> refers to only those vibrations with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">frequencies</a> that are within the <a href="/wiki/Hearing_range" title="Hearing range">hearing range</a> for humans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> or sometimes it relates to a particular animal. Other species have different ranges of hearing. For example, dogs can perceive vibrations higher than 20 kHz.
</p><p>As a signal perceived by one of the major <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a>, sound is used by many species for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defence_mechanism_(biology)" title="Defence mechanism (biology)">detecting danger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a>, and communication. Earth's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">water</a>, and virtually any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_phenomenon" title="Physical phenomenon">physical phenomenon</a>, such as fire, rain, wind, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">surf</a>, or earthquake, produces (and is characterized by) its unique sounds. Many species, such as frogs, birds, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_mammals" title="Marine mammals">marine</a> and terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, have also developed special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> to produce sound. In some species, these produce <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_communication" title="Speech communication">speech</a>. Furthermore, humans have developed culture and technology (such as music, telephone and radio) that allows them to generate, record, transmit, and broadcast sound.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Noise" title="Noise">Noise</a> is a term often used to refer to an unwanted sound. In science and engineering, noise is an undesirable component that obscures a wanted signal. However, in sound perception it can often be used to identify the source of a sound and is an important component of timbre perception (see above).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Soundscape" title="Soundscape">Soundscape</a> is the component of the acoustic environment that can be perceived by humans. The acoustic environment is the combination of all sounds (whether audible to humans or not) within a given area as modified by the environment and understood by people, in context of the surrounding environment.
</p><p>There are, historically, six experimentally separable ways in which sound waves are analysed. They are:  <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duration_(music)" title="Duration (music)">duration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">loudness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texture_(music)" title="Texture (music)">sonic texture</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Sound_localization" title="Sound localization">spatial location</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Some of these terms have a standardised definition (for instance in the ANSI Acoustical Terminology <a href="/wiki/ANSI/ASA_S1.1-2013" title="ANSI/ASA S1.1-2013">ANSI/ASA S1.1-2013</a>). More recent approaches have also considered <a href="/wiki/Temporal_envelope_and_fine_structure" title="Temporal envelope and fine structure">temporal envelope and temporal fine structure</a> as perceptually relevant analyses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitch">Pitch</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pitch_perception.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="351" data-file-width="616" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Pitch_perception.png/220px-Pitch_perception.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Pitch_perception.png/330px-Pitch_perception.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Pitch_perception.png/440px-Pitch_perception.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pitch_perception.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 1. Pitch perception</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">Pitch</a> is perceived as how "low" or "high" a sound is and represents the cyclic, repetitive nature of the vibrations that make up sound. For simple sounds, pitch relates to the frequency of the slowest vibration in the sound (called the fundamental harmonic). In the case of complex sounds, pitch perception can vary. Sometimes individuals identify different pitches for the same sound, based on their personal experience of particular sound patterns. Selection of a particular pitch is determined by pre-conscious examination of vibrations, including their frequencies and the balance between them. Specific attention is given to recognising potential harmonics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Every sound is placed on a pitch continuum from low to high. For example: <a href="/wiki/White_noise" title="White noise">white noise</a> (random noise spread evenly across all frequencies) sounds higher in pitch than <a href="/wiki/Pink_noise" title="Pink noise">pink noise</a> (random noise spread evenly across octaves) as white noise has more high frequency content.  Figure 1 shows an example of pitch recognition. During the listening process, each sound is analysed for a repeating pattern (See Figure 1: orange arrows) and the results forwarded to the auditory cortex as a single pitch of a certain height (octave) and chroma (note name).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duration">Duration</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duration_perception.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="757" decoding="async" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Duration_perception.png/220px-Duration_perception.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Duration_perception.png/330px-Duration_perception.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Duration_perception.png/440px-Duration_perception.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duration_perception.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 2. Duration perception</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Duration_(music)" title="Duration (music)">Duration</a> is perceived as how "long" or "short" a sound is and relates to onset and offset signals created by nerve responses to sounds. The duration of a sound usually lasts from the time the sound is first noticed until the sound is identified as having changed or ceased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Sometimes this is not directly related to the physical duration of a sound. For example; in a noisy environment, gapped sounds (sounds that stop and start) can sound as if they are continuous because the offset messages are missed owing to disruptions from noises in the same general bandwidth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This can be of great benefit in understanding distorted messages such as radio signals that suffer from interference, as (owing to this effect) the message is heard as if it was continuous. Figure 2 gives an example of duration identification. When a new sound is noticed (see Figure 2, Green arrows), a sound onset message is sent to the auditory cortex. When the repeating pattern is missed, a sound offset messages is sent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loudness">Loudness</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loudness_perception_v5.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Loudness_perception_v5.gif/220px-Loudness_perception_v5.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Loudness_perception_v5.gif/330px-Loudness_perception_v5.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Loudness_perception_v5.gif/440px-Loudness_perception_v5.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Loudness_perception_v5.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 3. Loudness perception</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">Loudness</a> is perceived as how "loud" or "soft" a sound is and relates to the totalled number of auditory nerve stimulations over short cyclic time periods, most likely over the duration of theta wave cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This means that at short durations, a very short sound can sound softer than a longer sound even though they are presented at the same intensity level. Past around 200 ms this is no longer the case and the duration of the sound no longer affects the apparent loudness of the sound. Figure 3 gives an impression of how loudness information is summed over a period of about 200 ms before being sent to the auditory cortex. Louder signals create a greater 'push' on the Basilar membrane and thus stimulate more nerves, creating a stronger loudness signal. A more complex signal also creates more nerve firings and so sounds louder (for the same wave amplitude) than a simpler sound, such as a sine wave.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timbre">Timbre</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Timbre_perception.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="570" data-file-width="734" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Timbre_perception.png/220px-Timbre_perception.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Timbre_perception.png/330px-Timbre_perception.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Timbre_perception.png/440px-Timbre_perception.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Timbre_perception.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 4. Timbre perception</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">Timbre</a> is perceived as the quality of different sounds (e.g. the thud of a fallen rock, the whir of a drill, the tone of a musical instrument or the quality of a voice) and represents the pre-conscious allocation of a sonic identity to a sound (e.g. “it's an oboe!"). This identity is based on information gained from frequency transients, noisiness, unsteadiness, perceived pitch and the spread and intensity of overtones in the sound over an extended time frame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handel,_S._1995_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Handel,_S._1995-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall,_R._A._1986_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall,_R._A._1986-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matthews,_M._1999_pp._79-88_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews,_M._1999_pp._79-88-11">[11]</a></sup> The way a sound changes over time (see figure 4) provides most of the information for timbre identification. Even though a small section of the wave form from each instrument looks very similar (see the expanded sections indicated by the orange arrows in figure 4), differences in changes over time between the clarinet and the piano are evident in both loudness and harmonic content. Less noticeable are the different noises heard, such as air hisses for the clarinet and hammer strikes for the piano.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Texture">Texture</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Texture_(music)" title="Texture (music)">Sonic texture</a> relates to the number of sound sources and the interaction between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The word <i>texture</i>, in this context, relates to the cognitive separation of auditory objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cariani,_P._2012_pp._351-390_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cariani,_P._2012_pp._351-390-31">[31]</a></sup> In music, texture is often referred to as the difference between <a href="/wiki/Unison" title="Unison">unison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homophony" title="Homophony">homophony</a>, but it can also relate (for example) to a busy cafe; a sound which might be referred to as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cacophony" title="Cacophony">cacophony</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spatial_location">Spatial location</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sound_localization" title="Sound localization">Sound localization</a></div>
<p>Spatial location represents the cognitive placement of a sound in an environmental context; including the placement of a sound on both the horizontal and vertical plane, the distance from the sound source and the characteristics of the sonic environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cariani,_P._2012_pp._351-390_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cariani,_P._2012_pp._351-390-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In a thick texture, it is possible to identify multiple sound sources using a combination of spatial location and timbre identification.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency">Frequency</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Audio_frequency" title="Audio frequency">Audio frequency</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="114" data-file-width="481" decoding="async" height="69" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg/290px-Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg/435px-Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg/580px-Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ultrasound_range_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Approximate frequency ranges corresponding to ultrasound, with rough guide of some applications</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</a> is sound waves with frequencies higher than 20,000 Hz. Ultrasound is not different from audible sound in its physical properties it just cannot be heard by humans. Ultrasound devices operate with frequencies from 20 kHz up to several gigahertz.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasound" title="Medical ultrasound">Medical ultrasound</a> is commonly used for diagnostics and treatment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrasound">Infrasound</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Perception_of_infrasound" title="Perception of infrasound">Perception of infrasound</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Infrasound" title="Infrasound">Infrasound</a> is sound waves with frequencies lower than 20 Hz. Although sounds of such low frequency are too low for humans to hear, whales, elephants and other animals can detect infrasound and use it to communicate. It can be used to detect volcanic eruptions and is used in some types of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
