<p><b>Nutrition</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiological</a> process by which an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> to support its <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>. It provides organisms with <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>, which can be <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolized</a> to create energy and chemical structures. Failure to obtain sufficient nutrients causes <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nutritional_science" title="Nutritional science">Nutritional science</a> is the study of nutrition, though it typically emphasizes <a href="/wiki/Human_nutrition" title="Human nutrition">human nutrition</a>.
</p><p>The type of organism determines what nutrients it needs and how it obtains them. Organisms obtain nutrients by consuming <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>, consuming inorganic matter, absorbing light, or some combination of these. Some can produce nutrients internally by consuming basic elements, while some must consume other organisms to obtain preexisting nutrients. All forms of life require <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_thermodynamics" title="Biological thermodynamics">energy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> as well as various other molecules. Animals require complex nutrients such as <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, obtaining them by consuming other organisms. Humans have developed <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a> to replace <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">foraging</a> and advance human nutrition. Plants acquire nutrients through <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and the atmosphere. Fungi absorb nutrients around them by breaking them down and absorbing them through the <a href="/wiki/Mycelium" title="Mycelium">mycelium</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Study">Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nutritional_science" title="Nutritional science">Nutritional science</a></div>
<p>Scientific analysis of food and nutrients began during the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_revolution" title="Chemical revolution">chemical revolution</a> in the late-18th century. Chemists in the 18th and 19th centuries experimented with different elements and food sources to develop theories of nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Modern nutrition science began in the 1910s as individual micronutrients began to be identified. The first vitamin to be chemically identified was <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">thiamine</a> in 1926, and the role of vitamins in nutrition was studied in the following decades. The first recommended dietary allowances for humans were developed during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to its importance in human health, the study of nutrition has heavily emphasized human nutrition and agriculture, while <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> is a secondary concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer20122_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer20122-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Nutrients are substances that provide energy and physical components to the organism, allowing it to survive, grow, and reproduce. Nutrients can be basic elements or complex <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a>. Approximately 30 elements are found in <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> being the most important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201770–72_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201770–72-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macronutrient" title="Macronutrient">Macronutrients</a> are the primary substances required by an organism, and <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrients</a> are substances required by an organism in trace amounts. Organic micronutrients are classified as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, and inorganic micronutrients are classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_(nutrition)" title="Mineral (nutrition)">minerals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWu20172–4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu20172–4-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nutrients are absorbed by the cells and used in metabolic biochemical reactions. These include fueling reactions that create precursor <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolites</a> and energy, biosynthetic reactions that convert precursor metabolites into building block molecules, <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerizations</a> that combine these molecules into macromolecule <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, and assembly reactions that use these polymers to construct cellular structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201770–72_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201770–72-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_groups">Nutritional groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nutritional groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Primary_nutritional_groups" title="Primary nutritional groups">Primary nutritional groups</a></div>
<p>Organisms can be classified by how they obtain carbon and energy. <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">Heterotrophs</a> are organisms that obtain nutrients by consuming the carbon of other organisms, while <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> are organisms that produce their own nutrients from the carbon of inorganic substances like <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mixotroph" title="Mixotroph">Mixotrophs</a> are organisms that can be heterotrophs and autotrophs, including some <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous_plant" title="Carnivorous plant">carnivorous plants</a>. <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">Phototrophs</a> obtain energy from light, while <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemotrophs</a> obtain energy by consuming chemical energy from matter. <a href="/wiki/Organotroph" title="Organotroph">Organotrophs</a> consume other organisms to obtain electrons, while <a href="/wiki/Lithotroph" title="Lithotroph">lithotrophs</a> obtain electrons from inorganic substances, such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydrogen" title="Dihydrogen">dihydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)" title="Iron(II)">iron(II)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201772–79_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201772–79-6">[6]</a></sup> Prototrophs can create essential nutrients from other compounds, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auxotroph" title="Auxotroph">auxotrophs</a> must consume preexisting nutrients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201793_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201793-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In nutrition, the <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a> of an organism is the sum of foods it eats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_cycle">Nutrient cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nutrient cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycle" title="Nutrient cycle">Nutrient cycle</a></div><p>A <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycle" title="Nutrient cycle">nutrient cycle</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycle</a> involving the movement of inorganic matter through a combination of soil, organisms, air or water, where they are exchanged in organic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> Energy flow is a unidirectional and noncyclic pathway, whereas the movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_nutrients" title="Mineral nutrients">mineral nutrients</a> is cyclic. Mineral cycles include the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_cycle" title="Phosphorus cycle">phosphorus cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_cycle" title="Oxygen cycle">oxygen cycle</a>, among others that continually recycle along with other mineral nutrients into <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(ecology)" title="Productivity (ecology)">productive</a> ecological nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foraging">Foraging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Foraging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">Foraging</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BonoboFishing04.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1952" data-file-width="2608" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/BonoboFishing04.jpeg/220px-BonoboFishing04.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/BonoboFishing04.jpeg/330px-BonoboFishing04.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/BonoboFishing04.jpeg/440px-BonoboFishing04.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BonoboFishing04.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Bonobo" title="Bonobo">bonobo</a> fishing for <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> with a prepared stick</div></div></div>
<p>Foraging is the process of seeking out nutrients in the environment. It may also be defined to include the subsequent use of the resources. Some organisms, such as animals and bacteria, can navigate to find nutrients, while others, such as plants and fungi, extend outward to find nutrients. Foraging may be random, in which the organism seeks nutrients without method, or it may be systematic, in which the organism can go directly to a food source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201783–85_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201783–85-10">[10]</a></sup> Organisms are able to detect nutrients through <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a> or other forms of <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_sensing" title="Nutrient sensing">nutrient sensing</a>, allowing them to regulate nutrient intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer201236_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer201236-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Optimal_foraging_theory" title="Optimal foraging theory">Optimal foraging theory</a> is a model that explains foraging behavior as a <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93benefit_analysis" title="Cost–benefit analysis">cost–benefit analysis</a> in which an animal must maximize the gain of nutrients while minimizing the amount of time and energy spent foraging. It was created to analyze the foraging habits of animals, but it can also be extended to other organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201716_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201716-12">[12]</a></sup> Some organisms are specialists that are adapted to forage for a single food source, while others are generalists that can consume a variety of food sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrews201798_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrews201798-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_deficiency">Nutrient deficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nutrient deficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</a></div>
<p>Nutrient deficiencies, known as <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>, occur when an organism does not have the nutrients that it needs. This may be caused by absorbing insufficient nutrients or by suddenly losing nutrients. When this occurs, an organism will adapt by reducing energy consumption and expenditure to prolong the use of stored nutrients. It will use stored energy reserves until they are depleted, and it will then break down its own body mass for additional energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_organisms">In organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal">Animal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Animal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Animal_nutrition" title="Animal nutrition">Animal nutrition</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2513" data-file-width="3769" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg/220px-Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg/330px-Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg/440px-Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kingfisher_eating_a_tadpole.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher" title="Kingfisher">kingfisher</a> eating a <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpole</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Ari%C3%A8ge_(river)" title="Ariège (river)">Ariège river</a>, France</div></div></div>
<p>Animals are heterotrophs that consume other organisms to obtain nutrients. <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">Herbivores</a> are animals that eat plants, <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a> are animals that eat other animals, and <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a> are animals that eat both plants and other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWu20171_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu20171-15">[15]</a></sup> Many herbivores rely on bacterial fermentation to create digestible nutrients from indigestible plant cellulose, while obligate carnivores must eat animal meats to obtain certain vitamins or nutrients their bodies cannot otherwise synthesize. Animals generally have a higher requirement of energy in comparison to plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The macronutrients essential to animal life are carbohydrates, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWu20172–4_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu20172–4-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbohydrates are molecules that store significant amounts of energy. Animals digest and metabolize carbohydrates to obtain this energy. Carbohydrates are typically synthesized by plants during metabolism, and animals have to obtain most carbohydrates from nature, as they have only a limited ability to generate them. They include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oligosaccharide" title="Oligosaccharide">oligosaccharides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a>. <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">Glucose</a> is the simplest form of carbohydrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201221–26_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201221–26-17">[17]</a></sup> Carbohydrates are broken down to produce glucose and <a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acid" title="Short-chain fatty acid">short-chain fatty acids</a>, and they are the most abundant nutrients for herbivorous land animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWu2017193–194_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu2017193–194-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lipids provide animals with fats and oils. They are not soluble in water, and they can store energy for an extended period of time. They can be obtained from many different plant and animal sources. Most dietary lipids are <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a> and fatty acids. <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">Phospholipids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sterol" title="Sterol">sterols</a> are found in smaller amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201249–55_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201249–55-19">[19]</a></sup> An animal's body will reduce the amount of fatty acids it produces as dietary fat intake increases, while it increases the amount of fatty acids it produces as carbohydrate intake increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWu2017271_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu2017271-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protein consumed by animals is broken down to amino acids, which would be later used to synthesize new proteins. Protein is used to form cellular structures, fluids,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201270–73_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201270–73-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> (biological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>). Enzymes are essential to most <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> processes, as well as <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">DNA replication</a>, <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair" title="DNA repair">repair</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EXPASY2000_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-EXPASY2000-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Much of <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">animal behavior</a> is governed by nutrition. <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration" title="Animal migration">Migration patterns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_breeder" title="Seasonal breeder">seasonal breeding</a> take place in conjunction with food availability, and <a href="/wiki/Courtship_display" title="Courtship display">courtship displays</a> are used to display an animal's health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer20123–4_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer20123–4-23">[23]</a></sup> Animals develop positive and negative associations with foods that affect their health, and they can instinctively avoid foods that have caused <a href="/wiki/Toxic_injury" title="Toxic injury">toxic injury</a> or nutritional imbalances through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conditioned_food_aversion" title="Conditioned food aversion">conditioned food aversion</a>. Some animals, such as rats, do not seek out new types of foods unless they have a nutrient deficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer201239–41_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimpsonRaubenheimer201239–41-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Human">Human</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Human">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Human_nutrition" title="Human nutrition">Human nutrition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">Cooking</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brazilian_Gourmet,_Fitzrovia,_London_(5990236962).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2520" data-file-width="3776" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Brazilian_Gourmet%2C_Fitzrovia%2C_London_%285990236962%29.jpg/260px-Brazilian_Gourmet%2C_Fitzrovia%2C_London_%285990236962%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Brazilian_Gourmet%2C_Fitzrovia%2C_London_%285990236962%29.jpg/390px-Brazilian_Gourmet%2C_Fitzrovia%2C_London_%285990236962%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Brazilian_Gourmet%2C_Fitzrovia%2C_London_%285990236962%29.jpg/520px-Brazilian_Gourmet%2C_Fitzrovia%2C_London_%285990236962%29.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brazilian_Gourmet,_Fitzrovia,_London_(5990236962).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">Bread</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ham" title="Ham">ham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_preserves" title="Fruit preserves">fruit preserve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> prepared for human consumption</div></div></div>
<p>Early human nutrition consisted of foraging for nutrients similar to that of other animals, but it diverged at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a>, in which humans developed agriculture to produce food. The Chemical Revolution in the 18th century allowed humans to study the nutrients in foods and develop more advanced methods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_preparation" title="Food preparation">food preparation</a>. Major advances in economics and technology during the 20th century allowed <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_fortification" title="Food fortification">food fortification</a> to better meet the nutritional needs of humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Human_behavior" title="Human behavior">Human behavior</a> is closely related to human nutrition, making it a subject of <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> in addition to biology. Nutrition in humans is balanced with eating for pleasure, and optimal diet may vary depending on the demographics and health concerns of each person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell20121_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell20121-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans are omnivores that eat a variety of foods. Cultivation of <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a> and production of <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> has made up a key component of human nutrition since the beginning of agriculture. Early humans hunted animals for meat, and modern humans domesticate animals to consume their meat and eggs. The development of <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a> has also allowed humans in some cultures to consume the <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> of other animals and produce it into foods such as <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>. Other foods eaten by humans include nuts, seeds, fruits, and vegetables. Access to domesticated animals as well as <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oils</a> has caused a significant increase in human intake of fats and oils. Humans have developed advanced methods of <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">food processing</a> that prevents contamination of pathogenic microorganisms and simplify the production of food. These include drying, freezing, heating, milling, pressing, packaging, refrigeration, and irradiation. Most cultures add <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> to foods before eating to add flavor, though most do not significantly affect nutrition. Other additives are also used to improve the safety, quality, flavor, and nutritional content of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell2012409–437_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell2012409–437-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans obtain most carbohydrates as starch from cereals, though sugar has grown in importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201221–26_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201221–26-17">[17]</a></sup> Lipids can be found in <a href="/wiki/Animal_fat" title="Animal fat">animal fat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butterfat" title="Butterfat">butterfat</a>, vegetable oil, and <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">leaf vegetables</a>, and they are also used to increase flavor in foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201249–55_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201249–55-19">[19]</a></sup> Protein can be found in virtually all foods, as it makes up cellular material, though certain methods of food processing may reduce the amount of protein in a food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201286_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell201286-28">[28]</a></sup> Humans can also obtain energy from <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, which is both a food and a drug, but it provides relatively few essential nutrients and is associated with nutritional deficiencies and other health risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell2012109–120_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMannTruswell2012109–120-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In humans, poor nutrition can cause deficiency-related diseases such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">preterm birth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stillbirth" title="Stillbirth">stillbirth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cretinism" title="Cretinism">cretinism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitney-Rolfes-lead_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitney-Rolfes-lead-30">[30]</a></sup> or nutrient excess health-threatening conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_syndrome" title="Metabolic syndrome">metabolic syndrome</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and such common chronic systemic diseases as <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Osteoporosis" title="Osteoporosis">osteoporosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Undernutrition can lead to <a href="/wiki/Wasting" title="Wasting">wasting</a> in acute cases, and <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunting</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marasmus" title="Marasmus">marasmus</a> in chronic cases of malnutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitney-Rolfes-lead_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitney-Rolfes-lead-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Domesticated_animal">Domesticated animal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Domesticated animal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_animal" title="Domesticated animal">domesticated animals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">working animals</a>, as well as other <a href="/wiki/Captivity_(animal)" title="Captivity (animal)">animals in captivity</a>, nutrition is managed by humans through <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">animal feed</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">Fodder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> are provided to livestock. Specialized <a href="/wiki/Pet_food" title="Pet food">pet food</a> has been manufactured since 1860, and subsequent research and development have addressed the nutritional needs of pets. <a href="/wiki/Dog_food" title="Dog food">Dog food</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cat_food" title="Cat food">cat food</a> in particular are heavily studied and typically include all essential nutrients for these animals. Cats are sensitive to some common nutrients, such as <a href="/wiki/Taurine" title="Taurine">taurine</a>, and require additional nutrients derived from meat. Large-breed puppies are susceptible to overnutrition, as small-breed dog food is more energy dense than they can absorb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant">Plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plant_nutrition" title="Plant nutrition">Plant nutrition</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Photosynthesis_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="354" data-file-width="354" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Photosynthesis_en.svg/220px-Photosynthesis_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Photosynthesis_en.svg/330px-Photosynthesis_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Photosynthesis_en.svg/440px-Photosynthesis_en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Photosynthesis_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic of photosynthesis in plants. The carbohydrates produced are stored in or used by the plant.</div></div></div>
<p>Most plants obtain nutrients through inorganic substances <a href="/wiki/Plant_nutrients_in_soil" title="Plant nutrients in soil">absorbed from the soil</a> or the atmosphere. Carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and sulfur are essential nutrients that make up organic material in a plant and allow enzymic processes. These are absorbed ions in the soil, such as <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a>, or they are absorbed as gases, such as carbon dioxide, water, oxygen gas, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>. Phosphorus, boron, and silicon are used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esterification" title="Esterification">esterification</a>. They are obtained through the soil as <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silicic_acid" title="Silicic acid">silicic acid</a>, respectively. Other nutrients used by plants are potassium, sodium, calcium, magnesium, manganese, chlorine, iron, copper, zinc, and molybdenum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMengelKirkbyKosegartenAppel20011–3_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMengelKirkbyKosegartenAppel20011–3-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plants uptake essential elements from the soil through their <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> and from the air (consisting of mainly nitrogen and oxygen) through their <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a>. Nutrient uptake in the soil is achieved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation_exchange" title="Cation exchange">cation exchange</a>, wherein <a href="/wiki/Root_hair" title="Root hair">root hairs</a> pump <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">hydrogen ions</a> (H<sup>+</sup>) into the soil through <a href="/wiki/Proton_pump" title="Proton pump">proton pumps</a>. These hydrogen ions displace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> attached to negatively charged soil particles so that the cations are available for uptake by the root. In the leaves, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomata" title="Stomata">stomata</a> open to take in carbon dioxide and expel <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMengelKirkbyKosegartenAppel2001111–135_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMengelKirkbyKosegartenAppel2001111–135-40">[40]</a></sup> Although <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> is plentiful in the Earth's atmosphere, very few plants can use this directly. Most plants, therefore, require nitrogen compounds to be present in the soil in which they grow. This is made possible by the fact that largely inert atmospheric nitrogen is changed in a <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a> process to biologically usable forms in the soil by bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>As these nutrients do not provide the plant with energy, they must obtain energy by other means. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_plant" title="Green plant">Green plants</a> absorb energy from <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chloroplasts" title="Chloroplasts">chloroplasts</a> and convert it to usable energy through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMengelKirkbyKosegartenAppel2001136–137_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMengelKirkbyKosegartenAppel2001136–137-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungus">Fungus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fungus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fungiculture" title="Fungiculture">Fungiculture</a></div>
<p>Fungi are chemoheterotrophs that consume external matter for energy. Most fungi absorb matter through the root-like mycelium, which grows through the organism's source of nutrients and can extend indefinitely. The fungus excretes <a href="/wiki/Fungal_extracellular_enzyme_activity" title="Fungal extracellular enzyme activity">extracellular enzymes</a> to break down surrounding matter and then absorbs the nutrients through the cell wall. Fungi can be parasitic, saprophytic, or symbiotic. Parasitic fungi attach and feed on living hosts, such as animals, plants, or other fungi. Saprophytic fungi feed on dead and decomposing organisms. Symbiotic fungi grow around other organisms and exchange nutrients with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protist">Protist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Protist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">Protists</a> include all <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a> that are not animals, plants, or fungi, resulting in great diversity between them. <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">Algae</a> are photosynthetic protists that can produce energy from light. Several types of protists use mycelium similar to those of fungi. <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">Protozoa</a> are heterotrophic protists, and different protozoa seek nutrients in different ways. <a href="/wiki/Flagellate" title="Flagellate">Flagellate</a> protozoa use a <a href="/wiki/Flagellum" title="Flagellum">flagellum</a> to assist in hunting for food, and some protozoa travel via infectious spores to act as parasites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Many protists are mixotrophic, having both phototrophic and heterotrophic characteristics. Mixotrophic protists will typically depend on one source of nutrients while using the other as a supplemental source or a temporary alternative when its primary source is unavailable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prokaryote">Prokaryote</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Prokaryote">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Microbial_metabolism" title="Microbial metabolism">Microbial metabolism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metabolism.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="598" data-file-width="720" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Metabolism.png/220px-Metabolism.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Metabolism.png/330px-Metabolism.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Metabolism.png/440px-Metabolism.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metabolism.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Simplified view of <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">cellular metabolism</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">Prokaryotes</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, vary greatly in how they obtain nutrients across nutritional groups. Prokaryotes can only transport soluble compounds across their cell envelopes, but they can break down chemical components around them. Some lithotrophic prokaryotes are <a href="/wiki/Extremophile" title="Extremophile">extremophiles</a> that can survive in nutrient-deprived environments by breaking down inorganic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Phototrophic prokaryotes, such as <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloroflexia" title="Chloroflexia">Chloroflexia</a>, can engage in photosynthesis to obtain energy from sunlight. This is common among bacteria that form in mats atop geothermal springs. Phototrophic prokaryotes typically obtain carbon from assimilating carbon dioxide through the <a href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some prokaryotes, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bdellovibrio" title="Bdellovibrio">Bdellovibrio</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Ensifer_(bacterium)" title="Ensifer (bacterium)"><i>Ensifer</i></a>, are predatory and feed on other single-celled organisms. Predatory prokaryotes seek out other organisms through <a href="/wiki/Chemotaxis" title="Chemotaxis">chemotaxis</a> or random collision, merge with the organism, degrade it, and absorb the released nutrients. Predatory strategies of prokaryotes include attaching to the outer surface of the organism and degrading it externally, entering the cytoplasm of the organism, or by entering the <a href="/wiki/Periplasm" title="Periplasm">periplasmic space</a> of the organism. Groups of predatory prokaryotes may forgo attachment by collectively producing <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolytic</a> enzymes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
