<p>A <b>festival</b> is an event ordinarily celebrated by a <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> and centering on some characteristic aspect of that community and its <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a>. It is often marked as a local or national <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">holiday</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mel%C4%81" title="Melā">mela</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_holidays" title="Muslim holidays">eid</a>. A festival constitutes typical cases of <a href="/wiki/Glocalization" title="Glocalization">glocalization</a>, as well as the high culture-low culture interrelationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Next to religion and <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>, a significant origin is <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a>. Food is such a vital resource that many festivals are associated with <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a> time. Religious commemoration and thanksgiving for good harvests are blended in events that take place in <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> in the northern hemisphere and <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> in the southern.
</p><p>Festivals often serve to fulfill specific communal purposes, especially in regard to commemoration or thanking to the gods, goddesses or saints: they are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patronal_festivals" title="Patronal festivals">patronal festivals</a>. They may also provide <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a>, which was particularly important to local communities before the advent of mass-produced entertainment. Festivals that focus on cultural or ethnic topics also seek to inform community members of their traditions; the involvement of <a href="/wiki/Old_age" title="Old age">elders</a> sharing stories and experience provides a means for unity among <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">families</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Attendants of festivals are often motivated by a desire for escapism, socialization and camaraderie; the practice has been seen as a means of creating geographical connection, belonging and adaptability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="772" decoding="async" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg/240px-Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg/360px-Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg/480px-Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_van_Bredael_-_A_Festival_in_Antwerp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Festival at <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, Belgium, 17th century</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="702" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg/240px-L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg/360px-L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg/480px-L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:L%C3%A4ndliches_Fest_in_Schwaben.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Country Festival in <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabia</a></div></div></div>
<p>The word "festival" was originally used as an adjective from the late fourteenth century, deriving from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> via <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed1-4">[4]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, a "festival dai" was a religious holiday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its first recorded used as a noun was in 1589 (as "Festifall").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed1-4">[4]</a></sup> Feast first came into usage as a noun circa 1200,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and its first recorded use as a verb was circa 1300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <b>gala</b> comes from <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> word <i>khil'a</i>, meaning robe of honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The word gala was initially used to describe "festive dress", but came to be a synonym of festival starting in the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Festivals have long been significant in human culture and is found in virtually all cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECudny201613_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECudny201613-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The importance of festivals, to the present, is found in private and public; secular and religious life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> societies relied heavily upon festivals, both communal and administrative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandt_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandt-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saturnalia" title="Saturnalia">Saturnalia</a> was likely influential to Christmas and <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup> Celebration of social occasions, religion and nature were common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup> Specific festivals have century-long histories and festivals in general have developed over the last few centuries – some traditional festivals in <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, for example, predate European <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonisation</a> of the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Festivals prospered following the Second World War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup> Both established in 1947, <a href="/wiki/Festival_d%27Avignon" title="Festival d'Avignon">Avignon Festival</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Festival_Fringe" title="Edinburgh Festival Fringe">Edinburgh Festival Fringe</a> have been notable in shaping the modern model of festivals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Art festivals became more prominent by the turn of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-12">[12]</a></sup> In modern times, festivals are commodified as a global tourist prospect although they are commonly <a href="/wiki/State_ownership" title="State ownership">public</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Not-for-Profit" title="Not-for-Profit">not-for-profit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many festivals have religious origins and entwine cultural and religious significance in traditional activities. The most important religious festivals such as <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Hashanah" title="Rosh Hashanah">Rosh Hashanah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr" title="Eid al-Fitr">Eid al-Fitr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Adha" title="Eid al-Adha">Eid al-Adha</a> serve to mark out the year. Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Harvest_festival" title="Harvest festival">harvest festivals</a>, celebrate seasonal change. Events of historical significance, such as important <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> victories or other nation-building events also provide the impetus for a festival. An early example is the festival established by <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptian</a> Pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Ramesses_III" title="Ramesses III">Ramesses III</a> celebrating his victory over the Libyans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berrett_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berrett-19">[19]</a></sup> In many countries, royal holidays commemorate dynastic events just as agricultural holidays are about harvests. Festivals are often commemorated annually.
</p><p>There are numerous <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_festivals#Types_of_festivals" title="Outline of festivals">types of festivals</a> in the world and most countries celebrate important events or traditions with traditional cultural events and activities. Most culminate in the consumption of specially prepared food (showing the connection to "feasting") and they bring people together. Festivals are also strongly associated with national holidays. Lists of national festivals are published to make participation easier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_festivals">Types of festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types of festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scale of festivals varies; in location and attendance, they may range from a local to national level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECudny201615_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECudny201615-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup> Music festivals, for example, often bring together disparate groups of people, such that they are both localised and global.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The "vast majority" of festivals are, however, local, modest and populist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-23">[23]</a></sup> The abundance of festivals significantly hinders quantifying the total there of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-12">[12]</a></sup> There exists significant variation among festivals, beyond binary dichotomies of sacred and secular, rural and urban, people and establishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_festivals">Religious festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religious festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religious_festival" title="Religious festival">Religious festival</a></div>
<p>Among many <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religions</a>, a feast is a set of celebrations in honour of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bleeker_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bleeker-24">[24]</a></sup> A feast and a festival are historically interchangeable. Most religions have festivals that recur annually and some, such as <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>, Easter and Eid al-Adha are <a href="/wiki/Moveable_feast" title="Moveable feast">moveable feasts</a> – that is, those that are determined either by lunar or agricultural cycles or the calendar in use at the time. The <a href="/wiki/Sed_festival" title="Sed festival">Sed festival</a>, for example, celebrated the thirtieth year of an Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a>'s rule and then every three (or four in one case) years after that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashanti_people" title="Ashanti people">Ashantis</a>, most of their traditional festivals are linked to gazette sites which are believed to be sacred with several rich biological resources in their pristine forms. Thus, the annual commemoration of the festivals helps in maintaining the buoyancy of the conserved natural site, assisting in biodiversity conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_calendar" title="Liturgical calendar">liturgical calendar</a>, there are two principal feasts, properly known as the Feast of the Nativity of our Lord (Christmas) and the Feast of the Resurrection (Easter), but minor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patronal_festivals" title="Patronal festivals">festivals in honour of local patron saints</a> are celebrated in almost all countries influenced by Christianity. In the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> liturgical calendars there are a great number of lesser feasts throughout the year commemorating saints, sacred events or doctrines. In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, each day of the year has at least one specific religious festival, either from Catholic, Islamic, or indigenous origins.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> religious festivals, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esala_Perahera" title="Esala Perahera">Esala Perahera</a> are held in Sri Lanka and Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerson_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerson-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Sindhi_festivals" title="List of Sindhi festivals">Hindu festivals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Holi" title="Holi">Holi</a> are very ancient. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> community celebrates the <a href="/wiki/Vaisakhi" title="Vaisakhi">Vaisakhi</a> festival marking the new year and birth of the <a href="/wiki/Khalsa" title="Khalsa">Khalsa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roy2005_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roy2005-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_festivals">Arts festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arts festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arts_festival" title="Arts festival">Arts festival</a></div>
<p>Among the many offspring of general arts festivals are also more specific types of festivals, including ones that showcase intellectual or creative achievement such as <a href="/wiki/Science_festival" title="Science festival">science festivals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literary_festival" title="Literary festival">literary festivals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_festival" title="Music festival">music festivals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  Sub-categories include <a href="/wiki/Comedy_festival" title="Comedy festival">comedy festivals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_festival" title="Rock festival">rock festivals</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_jazz_festivals" title="List of jazz festivals">jazz festivals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buskers_festival" title="Buskers festival">buskers festivals</a>; <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> festivals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theatre_festival" title="Theatre festival">theatre festivals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Storytelling_festival" title="Storytelling festival">storytelling festivals</a>; and re-enactment festivals such as <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_fair" title="Renaissance fair">Renaissance fairs</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, aside from numerous art festivals scattered throughout the year, February is known as national arts month, the culmination of all art festivals in the entire archipelago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The modern model of music festivals began in the 1960s-70s and have become a lucrative global industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Predecessors extend back to the 11th century and some, such as the Three Choirs Festival, remain to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Film_festival" title="Film festival">Film festivals</a> involve the screenings of several different films, and are usually held annually. Some of the most significant film festivals include the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin International Film Festival</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">Venice Film Festival</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>. With the increasing importance of digitalization, one of the online film festival is Best Istanbul Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_festival" title="Food festival">Food festival</a></div>
<p>A food festival is an event celebrating food or drink. These often highlight the output of producers from a certain region. Some food festivals are focused on a particular item of food, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Peanut_Festival" title="National Peanut Festival">National Peanut Festival</a> in the United States, or the <a href="/wiki/Galway_International_Oyster_Festival" title="Galway International Oyster Festival">Galway International Oyster Festival</a> in Ireland. There are also specific beverage festivals, such as the famous <a href="/wiki/Oktoberfest" title="Oktoberfest">Oktoberfest</a> in Germany for <a href="/wiki/Beer_festival" title="Beer festival">beer</a>. Many countries hold festivals to celebrate <a href="/wiki/Wine_festival" title="Wine festival">wine</a>. One example is the global celebration of the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_nouveau" title="Beaujolais nouveau">Beaujolais nouveau</a>, which involves shipping the new wine around the world for its release date on the third Thursday of November each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haine2006_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haine2006-35">[35]</a></sup> Both Beaujolais nouveau and the Japanese rice wine <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a> are associated with harvest time. In the Philippines, there are at least two hundred festivals dedicated to food and drinks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_and_harvest_festivals">Seasonal and harvest festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Seasonal and harvest festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Seasonal festivals, such as <a href="/wiki/Beltane" title="Beltane">Beltane</a>, are determined by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">solar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendars</a> and by the cycle of the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>, especially because of its effect on food supply, as a result of which there is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_harvest_festivals" title="List of harvest festivals">wide range</a> of ancient and modern <a href="/wiki/Harvest_festival" title="Harvest festival">harvest festivals</a>. Ancient Egyptians relied upon the seasonal <a href="/wiki/Inundation" title="Inundation">inundation</a> caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, which provided fertile land for crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunson_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunson-36">[36]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, in autumn the return of the cattle from the mountain <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastures</a> to the stables in the valley is celebrated as <i><a href="/wiki/Almabtrieb" title="Almabtrieb">Almabtrieb</a></i>. A recognized winter festival, the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a>, is set by the lunar calendar, and celebrated from the day of the second new moon after the <a href="/wiki/Dongzhi_(solar_term)" title="Dongzhi (solar term)">winter solstice</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dree_Festival" title="Dree Festival">Dree Festival</a> of the Apatanis living in Lower Subansiri District of Arunachal Pradesh is celebrated every year from July 4 to 7 by praying for a bumper crop harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoAP_PR_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-GoAP_PR-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">Midsummer</a> or St John's Day, is an example of a seasonal festival, related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a> of a Christian saint as well as a celebration of the time of the <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">summer solstice</a> in the northern hemisphere, where it is particularly important in Sweden. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_carnival" title="Winter carnival">Winter carnivals</a> also provide the opportunity to utilise to celebrate creative or sporting activities requiring snow and ice. In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, each day of the year has at least one festival dedicated to harvesting of crops, fishes, crustaceans, milk, and other local goods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholarly literature notes that festivals functionally disseminate political values and meaning, such as ownership of place, which undergoes transformation in accordance with the festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-23">[23]</a></sup> Furthermore, a festival may act as an artefact which allows citizens to achieve "certain ideals", including those of identity and ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Festivals may be used to rehabilitate or elevate the image of a city; the <a href="/wiki/Ephemerality" title="Ephemerality">ephemerality</a> of festivals means that their impact is often incorporeal, of name, memory and perception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-:4-23">[23]</a></sup> In deviating from routine, festivals may reinforce the convention, be it social, cultural or economic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-:4-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Study_of_festivals">Study of festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Study of festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Festive_ecology" title="Festive ecology">Festive ecology</a> – explores the relationships between the <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> associated with cultural events such as festivals, processions and special occasions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heortology" title="Heortology">Heortology</a> – the study of <a href="/wiki/Religious_festival" title="Religious festival">religious festivals</a>.  It was originally only used in respect of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> festivals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> but it now covers all <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religions</a>, in particular those of Ancient <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/List_of_foods_with_religious_symbolism" title="List of foods with religious symbolism">list of foods with religious symbolism</a> for some topical overlap.</li></ul>
