<p><b>Electromagnetism</b> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> involving the study of the <b>electromagnetic force</b>, a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_interaction" title="Physical interaction">physical interaction</a> that occurs between <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electrically charged</a> particles. The electromagnetic force is carried by <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic fields</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric fields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>, and it is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>.  It is one of the four <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interactions</a> (commonly called forces) in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>, together with the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> At high energy, the weak force and electromagnetic force are unified as a single <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak force</a>.
</p><p>Electromagnetic phenomena are defined in terms of the electromagnetic force, sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force</a>, which includes both <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a> as different manifestations of the same phenomenon. The electromagnetic force plays a major role in determining the internal properties of most objects encountered in daily life.  The electromagnetic attraction between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_nuclei" title="Atomic nuclei">atomic nuclei</a> and their orbital <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> holds <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> together.  Electromagnetic forces are responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a> between atoms which create <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a>.   The electromagnetic force governs all chemical processes, which arise from interactions between the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">electrons</a> of neighboring atoms.  Electromagnetism is very widely used in modern technology, and electromagnetic theory is the basis of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electric power engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> including digital technology.
</p><p>There are numerous <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_descriptions_of_the_electromagnetic_field" title="Mathematical descriptions of the electromagnetic field">mathematical descriptions of the electromagnetic field</a>. Most prominently, <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">Maxwell's equations</a> describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated and altered by each other and by charges and currents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The theoretical implications of electromagnetism, particularly the establishment of the speed of light based on properties of the "medium" of propagation (<a href="/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism)" title="Permeability (electromagnetism)">permeability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permittivity" title="Permittivity">permittivity</a>), led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> in 1905.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_theory">History of the theory</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_electromagnetic_theory" title="History of electromagnetic theory">History of electromagnetic theory</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1123" data-file-width="794" decoding="async" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg/220px-A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg/330px-A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg/440px-A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism_Volume_2_003.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cover of <i>A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism</i></div></div></div>
<p>Originally, electricity and magnetism were considered to be two separate forces. This view changed with the publication of <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>'s 1873 <i><a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism" title="A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism">A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in which the interactions of positive and negative charges were shown to be mediated by one force. There are four main effects resulting from these interactions, all of which have been clearly demonstrated by experiments:
</p>
<ol><li>Electric charges <i><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1023754711">.mw-parser-output .vanchor>:target~.vanchor-text{background-color:#b1d2ff}</style><span class="vanchor"><span id="attract"></span><span class="vanchor-text">attract</span></span></i> or <i><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1023754711" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="vanchor"><span id="repel"></span><span class="vanchor-text">repel</span></span></i> one another with a force <a href="/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)#Inverse_proportionality" title="Proportionality (mathematics)">inversely proportional</a> to the square of the distance between them: unlike charges attract, like ones repel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Magnetic poles (or states of polarization at individual points) attract or repel one another in a manner similar to positive and negative charges and always exist as pairs: every north pole is yoked to a south pole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>An electric current inside a wire creates a corresponding circumferential magnetic field outside the wire. Its direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise) depends on the direction of the current in the wire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lscience_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-lscience-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>A current is induced in a loop of wire when it is moved toward or away from a magnetic field, or a magnet is moved towards or away from it; the direction of current depends on that of the movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lscience_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-lscience-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>In April 1820, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_%C3%98rsted" title="Hans Christian Ørsted">Hans Christian Ørsted</a> observed that an electrical current in a wire caused a nearby compass needle to move. At the time of discovery, Ørsted did not suggest any satisfactory explanation of the phenomenon, nor did he try to represent the phenomenon in a mathematical framework. However, three months later he began more intensive investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Soon thereafter he published his findings, proving that an electric current produces a magnetic field as it flows through a wire. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGS" title="CGS">CGS</a> unit of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">magnetic induction</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oersted" title="Oersted">oersted</a>) is named in honor of his contributions to the field of electromagnetism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>His findings resulted in intensive research throughout the scientific community in electrodynamics. They influenced French physicist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re" title="André-Marie Ampère">André-Marie Ampère</a>'s developments of a single mathematical form to represent the magnetic forces between current-carrying conductors. Ørsted's discovery also represented a major step toward a unified concept of energy.
</p><p>This unification, which was observed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>, extended by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>, and partially reformulated by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside" title="Oliver Heaviside">Oliver Heaviside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz" title="Heinrich Hertz">Heinrich Hertz</a>, is one of the key accomplishments of 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_physics" title="Mathematical physics">mathematical physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It has had far-reaching consequences, one of which was the understanding of the nature of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. Unlike what was proposed by the electromagnetic theory of that time, light and other <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic waves</a> are at present seen as taking the form of <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantized</a>, self-propagating <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillatory</a> electromagnetic field disturbances called <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>. Different <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a> of oscillation give rise to the different forms of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> at the lowest frequencies, to visible light at intermediate frequencies, to <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> at the highest frequencies.
</p><p>Ørsted was not the only person to examine the relationship between electricity and magnetism. In 1802, <a href="/wiki/Gian_Domenico_Romagnosi" title="Gian Domenico Romagnosi">Gian Domenico Romagnosi</a>, an Italian legal scholar, deflected a magnetic needle using a Voltaic pile. The factual setup of the experiment is not completely clear, nor if current flowed across the needle or not. An account of the discovery was published in 1802 in an Italian newspaper, but it was largely overlooked by the contemporary scientific community, because Romagnosi seemingly did not belong to this community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>
An earlier (1735), and often neglected, connection between electricity and magnetism was reported by a Dr. Cookson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The account stated:</p><blockquote><p>A tradesman at Wakefield in Yorkshire, having put up a great number of knives and forks in a large box ...  and having placed the box in the corner of a large room, there happened a sudden storm of thunder, lightning, &amp;c.  ... The owner emptying the box on a counter where some nails lay, the persons who took up the knives, that lay on the nails, observed that the knives took up the nails. On this the whole number was tried, and found to do the same, and that, to such a degree as to take up large nails, packing needles, and other iron things of considerable weight ...</p></blockquote> <p><a href="/wiki/E._T._Whittaker" title="E. T. Whittaker">E. T. Whittaker</a> suggested in 1910 that this particular event was responsible for lightning to be "credited with the power of magnetizing steel; and it was doubtless this which led Franklin in 1751 to attempt to magnetize a sewing-needle by means of the discharge of Leyden jars."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_forces">Fundamental forces</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="305" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif/220px-Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/81/Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Representation of the electric field vector of a wave of circularly polarized electromagnetic radiation.</div></div></div>
<p>The electromagnetic force is one of the four known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_force" title="Fundamental force">fundamental forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-space-14">[14]</a></sup> The other fundamental forces are:
</p>
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong nuclear force</a>, which binds <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleons</a>, and binds nucleons to form <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-space-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak nuclear force</a>, which binds to all known particles in the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>, and causes certain forms of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a>. (In <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a> though, the <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak interaction</a> is the unified description of two of the four known fundamental interactions of nature: electromagnetism and the weak interaction);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-space-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-space-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>All other forces (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, contact forces) are derived from these four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_forces" title="Fundamental forces">fundamental forces</a> and they are known as <a href="/wiki/Force#Non-fundamental_forces" title="Force">non-fundamental forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Roughly speaking, all the forces involved in interactions between <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> can be explained by the electromagnetic force acting between the electrically charged <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nuclei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> of the atoms. Electromagnetic forces also explain how these particles carry momentum by their movement. This includes the forces we experience in "pushing" or "pulling" ordinary material objects, which result from the <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> that act between the individual <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in our bodies and those in the objects. The electromagnetic force is also involved in all forms of <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical phenomena</a>.
</p><p>A necessary part of understanding the intra-atomic and intermolecular forces is the effective force generated by the momentum of the electrons' movement, such that as electrons move between interacting atoms they carry momentum with them. As a collection of electrons becomes more confined, their minimum momentum necessarily increases due to the <a href="/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle" title="Pauli exclusion principle">Pauli exclusion principle</a>. The behaviour of matter at the molecular scale including its density is determined by the balance between the electromagnetic force and the force generated by the exchange of momentum carried by the electrons themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_electrodynamics">Classical electrodynamics</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_electrodynamics" title="Classical electrodynamics">Classical electrodynamics</a></div>
<p>In 1600, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">William Gilbert</a> proposed, in his <i><a href="/wiki/De_Magnete" title="De Magnete">De Magnete</a></i>, that electricity and magnetism, while both capable of causing attraction and repulsion of objects, were distinct effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Mariners had noticed that lightning strikes had the ability to disturb a compass needle. The link between lightning and electricity was not confirmed until <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>'s proposed experiments in 1752 were conducted on 10<span class="nowrap"> </span>May 1752 by <a href="/wiki/Thomas-Fran%C3%A7ois_Dalibard" title="Thomas-François Dalibard">Thomas-François Dalibard</a> of France using a 40-foot-tall (12 m) iron rod instead of a kite and he successfully extracted electrical sparks from a cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the first to discover and publish a link between man-made electric current and magnetism was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romagnosi" title="Romagnosi">Gian Romagnosi</a>, who in 1802 noticed that connecting a wire across a <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">voltaic pile</a> deflected a nearby <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> needle. However, the effect did not become widely known until 1820, when Ørsted performed a similar experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oersted_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oersted-20">[20]</a></sup> Ørsted's work influenced Ampère to produce a theory of electromagnetism that set the subject on a mathematical foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>A theory of electromagnetism, known as <a href="/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism" title="Classical electromagnetism">classical electromagnetism</a>, was developed by various physicists during the period between 1820 and 1873 when it culminated in the publication of a <a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism" title="A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism">treatise</a> by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>, which unified the preceding developments into a single theory and discovered the electromagnetic nature of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In classical electromagnetism, the behavior of the electromagnetic field is described by a set of equations known as <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">Maxwell's equations</a>, and the electromagnetic force is given by the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the peculiarities of classical electromagnetism is that it is difficult to reconcile with <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, but it is compatible with special relativity. According to Maxwell's equations, the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in a vacuum is a universal constant that is dependent only on the <a href="/wiki/Permittivity" title="Permittivity">electrical permittivity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_permeability" title="Magnetic permeability">magnetic permeability</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space" title="Free space">free space</a>. This violates <a href="/wiki/Galilean_invariance" title="Galilean invariance">Galilean invariance</a>, a long-standing cornerstone of classical mechanics. One way to reconcile the two theories (electromagnetism and classical mechanics) is to assume the existence of a <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">luminiferous aether</a> through which the light propagates. However, subsequent experimental efforts failed to detect the presence of the aether. After important contributions of <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Lorentz">Hendrik Lorentz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincaré">Henri Poincaré</a>, in 1905, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> solved the problem with the introduction of special relativity, which replaced classical kinematics with a new theory of kinematics compatible with classical electromagnetism. (For more information, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_special_relativity" title="History of special relativity">History of special relativity</a>.)
</p><p>In addition, relativity theory implies that in moving frames of reference, a magnetic field transforms to a field with a nonzero electric component and conversely, a moving electric field transforms to a nonzero magnetic component, thus firmly showing that the phenomena are two sides of the same coin. Hence the term "electromagnetism". (For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism_and_special_relativity" title="Classical electromagnetism and special relativity">Classical electromagnetism and special relativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Covariant_formulation_of_classical_electromagnetism" title="Covariant formulation of classical electromagnetism">Covariant formulation of classical electromagnetism</a>.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extension_to_nonlinear_phenomena">Extension to nonlinear phenomena</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_in_X-Ray.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="690" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Sun_in_X-Ray.png/300px-Sun_in_X-Ray.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Sun_in_X-Ray.png/450px-Sun_in_X-Ray.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Sun_in_X-Ray.png/600px-Sun_in_X-Ray.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun_in_X-Ray.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">Magnetic reconnection</a> in the solar <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> gives rise to <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>, a complex magnetohydrodynamical phenomenon.</div></div></div>
<p>The Maxwell equations are <i>linear,</i> in that a change in the sources (the charges and currents) results in a proportional change of the fields. <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">Nonlinear dynamics</a> can occur when electromagnetic fields couple to matter that follows nonlinear dynamical laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This is studied, for example, in the subject of <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">magnetohydrodynamics</a>, which combines Maxwell theory with the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Formulas for physical laws of electromagnetism (such as <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">Maxwell's equations</a>) need to be adjusted depending on what system of units one uses. This is because there is no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One-to-one_correspondence" title="One-to-one correspondence">one-to-one correspondence</a> between electromagnetic units in SI and those in CGS, as is the case for mechanical units. Furthermore, within CGS, there are several plausible choices of electromagnetic units, leading to different unit "sub-systems", including <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_units" title="Gaussian units">Gaussian</a>, "ESU", "EMU", and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heaviside%E2%80%93Lorentz" title="Heaviside–Lorentz">Heaviside–Lorentz</a>. Among these choices, Gaussian units are the most common today, and in fact the phrase "CGS units" is often used to refer specifically to <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_units" title="Gaussian units">CGS-Gaussian units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
