<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Refrigeration</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Process of moving heat from one location to another in controlled conditions</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kuehlregal_USA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1881" data-file-width="2821" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Kuehlregal_USA.jpg/220px-Kuehlregal_USA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Kuehlregal_USA.jpg/330px-Kuehlregal_USA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Kuehlregal_USA.jpg/440px-Kuehlregal_USA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kuehlregal_USA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Commercial refrigeration</div></div></div>
<p>The term <b>refrigeration</b> refers to the process of removing heat from an enclosed space or substance for the purpose of lowering the temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary.iifiir.org_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary.iifiir.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ashrae.org_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ashrae.org-2">[2]</a></sup> Refrigeration can be considered an artificial, or human-made, <a href="/wiki/Cooling" title="Cooling">cooling</a> method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary.iifiir.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary.iifiir.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ashrae.org_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ashrae.org-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Refrigeration refers to the process by which energy, in the form of heat, is removed from a low-temperature medium and transferred to a high-temperature medium. This work of energy transfer is traditionally driven by <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">mechanical</a> means, but can also be driven by heat, <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser_cooling" title="Laser cooling">laser</a>, or other means. Refrigeration has many applications, including household <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerators</a>, industrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezer" title="Freezer">freezers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>. <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">Heat pumps</a> may use the heat output of the refrigeration process, and also may be designed to be reversible, but are otherwise similar to air conditioning units.
</p><p>Refrigeration has had a large impact on industry, lifestyle, agriculture, and settlement patterns. The idea of preserving food dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">ancient Roman empire</a>. Mechanical refrigeration has rapidly evolved in the last century, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_harvesting" title="Ice harvesting">ice harvesting</a> to <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator_car" title="Refrigerator car">temperature-controlled rail cars</a>. The introduction of refrigerated rail cars contributed to the westward expansion of the United States, allowing settlement in areas that were not on main transport channels such as rivers, harbors, or valley trails. Settlements were also developing in infertile parts of the country, filled with newly discovered natural resources.
</p><p>These new settlement patterns sparked the building of large cities which are able to thrive in areas that were otherwise thought to be inhospitable, such as <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas, and <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>, Nevada. In most developed countries, cities are heavily dependent upon refrigeration in <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">supermarkets</a> in order to obtain their food for daily consumption. The increase in food sources has led to a larger concentration of agricultural sales coming from a smaller percentage of farms. Farms today have a much larger output per person in comparison to the late 1800s. This has resulted in new food sources available to entire populations, which has had a large impact on the nutrition of society.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For a chronological guide, see <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_low-temperature_technology" title="Timeline of low-temperature technology">Timeline of low-temperature technology</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_forms_of_cooling">Earliest forms of cooling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earliest forms of cooling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The seasonal harvesting of snow and ice is an ancient practice estimated to have begun earlier than 1000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> A Chinese collection of lyrics from this time period known as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shijing" title="Shijing">Shijing</a></i>, describes religious ceremonies for filling and emptying ice cellars. However, little is known about the construction of these ice cellars or the purpose of the ice. The next ancient society to record the harvesting of ice may have been the Jews in the book of Proverbs, which reads, “As the cold of snow in the time of harvest, so is a faithful messenger to them who sent him.” Historians have interpreted this to mean that the Jews used ice to cool beverages rather than to preserve food. Other ancient cultures such as the Greeks and the Romans dug large snow pits insulated with grass, chaff, or branches of trees as cold storage. Like the Jews, the Greeks and Romans did not use ice and snow to preserve food, but primarily as a means to cool beverages. Egyptians cooled water by evaporation in shallow earthen jars on the roofs of their houses at night. The ancient people of India used this same concept to produce ice. The Persians stored ice in a pit called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakhchal" title="Yakhchal">Yakhchal</a> and may have been the first group of people to use cold storage to preserve food. In the Australian outback before a reliable electricity supply was available many farmers used a <a href="/wiki/Coolgardie_safe" title="Coolgardie safe">Coolgardie safe</a>, consisting of a room with <a href="/wiki/Hessian_fabric" title="Hessian fabric">hessian</a> (burlap) curtains hanging from the ceiling soaked in water. The water would evaporate and thereby cool the room, allowing many perishables such as fruit, butter, and cured meats to be kept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_harvesting">Ice harvesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ice harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ice_cutting" title="Ice cutting">Ice cutting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_trade" title="Ice trade">Ice trade</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ice_Harvesting,_Massachusetts,_early_1850s.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="752" data-file-width="1310" decoding="async" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ice_Harvesting%2C_Massachusetts%2C_early_1850s.jpg/220px-Ice_Harvesting%2C_Massachusetts%2C_early_1850s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ice_Harvesting%2C_Massachusetts%2C_early_1850s.jpg/330px-Ice_Harvesting%2C_Massachusetts%2C_early_1850s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ice_Harvesting%2C_Massachusetts%2C_early_1850s.jpg/440px-Ice_Harvesting%2C_Massachusetts%2C_early_1850s.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ice_Harvesting,_Massachusetts,_early_1850s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ice harvesting in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, 1852, showing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroad</a> line in the background, used to transport the ice.</div></div></div>
<p>Before 1830, few Americans used ice to refrigerate foods due to a lack of ice-storehouses and iceboxes. As these two things became more widely available, individuals used axes and saws to <a href="/wiki/Ice_cutting" title="Ice cutting">harvest ice</a> for their storehouses. This method proved to be difficult, dangerous, and certainly did not resemble anything that could be duplicated on a commercial scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the difficulties of harvesting ice, Frederic Tudor thought that he could capitalize on this new commodity by harvesting ice in New England and shipping it to the Caribbean islands as well as the southern states. In the beginning, Tudor lost thousands of dollars, but eventually turned a profit as he constructed icehouses in Charleston, Virginia and in the Cuban port town of Havana. These icehouses as well as better insulated ships helped reduce ice wastage from 66% to 8%. This efficiency gain influenced Tudor to expand his ice market to other towns with icehouses such as New Orleans and Savannah. This ice market further expanded as harvesting ice became faster and cheaper after one of Tudor's suppliers, Nathaniel Wyeth, invented a horse-drawn ice cutter in 1825. This invention as well as Tudor's success inspired others to get involved in the <a href="/wiki/Ice_trade" title="Ice trade">ice trade</a> and the ice industry grew.
</p><p>Ice became a mass-market commodity by the early 1830s with the price of ice dropping from six cents per pound to a half of a cent per pound. In New York City, ice consumption increased from 12,000 tons in 1843 to 100,000 tons in 1856. Boston's consumption leapt from 6,000 tons to 85,000 tons during that same period. Ice harvesting created a “cooling culture” as majority of people used ice and iceboxes to store their dairy products, fish, meat, and even fruits and vegetables. These early cold storage practices paved the way for many Americans to accept the refrigeration technology that would soon take over the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration_research">Refrigeration research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Refrigeration research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_Cullen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="815" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/William_Cullen.jpg/170px-William_Cullen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/William_Cullen.jpg/255px-William_Cullen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/William_Cullen.jpg/340px-William_Cullen.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:William_Cullen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Cullen" title="William Cullen">William Cullen</a>, the first to conduct experiments into artificial refrigeration.</div></div></div>
<p>The history of artificial refrigeration began when Scottish professor <a href="/wiki/William_Cullen" title="William Cullen">William Cullen</a> designed a small refrigerating machine in 1755. Cullen used a pump to create a partial <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> over a container of <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>, which then <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiled</a>, absorbing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat</a> from the surrounding air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The experiment even created a small amount of ice, but had no practical application at that time.
</p><p>In 1758, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hadley_(chemist)" title="John Hadley (chemist)">John Hadley</a>, professor of chemistry, collaborated on a project investigating the principle of evaporation as a means to rapidly cool an object at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. They confirmed that the evaporation of highly volatile liquids, such as alcohol and ether, could be used to drive down the temperature of an object past the freezing point of water. They conducted their experiment with the bulb of a mercury thermometer as their object and with a bellows used to quicken the evaporation; they lowered the temperature of the thermometer bulb down to −14 °C (7 °F), while the ambient temperature was 18 °C (65 °F). They noted that soon after they passed the freezing point of water 0 °C (32 °F), a thin film of ice formed on the surface of the thermometer's bulb and that the ice mass was about a <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020198016">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style>6.4 millimetres (<span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">1</span>⁄<span class="den">4</span></span> in) thick when they stopped the experiment upon reaching −14 °C (7 °F). Franklin wrote, "From this experiment, one may see the possibility of freezing a man to death on a warm summer's day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1805, American inventor <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Evans" title="Oliver Evans">Oliver Evans</a> described a closed <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">vapor-compression refrigeration</a> cycle for the production of ice by ether under vacuum.
</p><p>In 1820, the English scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> liquefied <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and other gases by using high pressures and low temperatures, and in 1834, an American expatriate to Great Britain, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Perkins" title="Jacob Perkins">Jacob Perkins</a>, built the first working vapor-compression refrigeration system in the world. It was a closed-cycle that could operate continuously, as he described in his patent:
</p>
<dl><dd>I am enabled to use volatile fluids for the purpose of producing the cooling or freezing of fluids, and yet at the same time constantly condensing such volatile fluids, and bringing them again into operation without waste.</dd></dl>
<p>His prototype system worked although it did not succeed commercially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burstall_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-burstall-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1842, a similar attempt was made by American physician, <a href="/wiki/John_Gorrie" title="John Gorrie">John Gorrie</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> who built a working prototype, but it was a commercial failure. Like many of the medical experts during this time, Gorrie thought too much exposure to tropical heat led to mental and physical degeneration, as well as the spread of diseases such as malaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He conceived the idea of using his refrigeration system to cool the air for comfort in homes and hospitals to prevent disease. American engineer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Twining" title="Alexander Twining">Alexander Twining</a> took out a British patent in 1850 for a vapour compression system that used ether.
</p><p>The first practical vapour-compression refrigeration system was built by <a href="/wiki/James_Harrison_(engineer)" title="James Harrison (engineer)">James Harrison</a>, a British journalist who had emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. His 1856 patent was for a vapour-compression system using ether, alcohol, or ammonia. He built a mechanical ice-making machine in 1851 on the banks of the Barwon River at Rocky Point in <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, and his first commercial ice-making machine followed in 1854. Harrison also introduced commercial vapour-compression refrigeration to breweries and meat-packing houses, and by 1861, a dozen of his systems were in operation. He later entered the debate of how to compete against the American advantage of unrefrigerated <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a> sales to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. In 1873 he prepared the sailing ship <i>Norfolk</i> for an experimental beef shipment to the United Kingdom, which used a cold room system instead of a refrigeration system. The venture was a failure as the ice was consumed faster than expected.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="1096" decoding="async" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg/220px-AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg/330px-AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg/440px-AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AppareilCarr%C3%A9.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Carr%C3%A9" title="Ferdinand Carré">Ferdinand Carré</a>'s ice-making device</div></div></div>
<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_refrigeration" title="Absorption refrigeration">gas absorption</a> refrigeration system using gaseous ammonia dissolved in water (referred to as "aqua ammonia") was developed by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Carr%C3%A9" title="Ferdinand Carré">Ferdinand Carré</a> of France in 1859 and patented in 1860. <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Linde" title="Carl von Linde">Carl von Linde</a>, an engineer specializing in <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a> and professor of engineering at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_M%C3%BCnchen" title="Technische Universität München">Technological University of Munich</a> in Germany, began researching refrigeration in the 1860s and 1870s in response to demand from brewers for a technology that would allow year-round, large-scale production of <a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">lager</a>; he patented an improved method of liquefying gases in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> His new process made possible using gases such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" title="Methyl chloride">methyl chloride</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>Cl) as refrigerants and they were widely used for that purpose until the late 1920s.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thaddeus_Lowe" title="Thaddeus Lowe">Thaddeus Lowe</a>, an American balloonist, held several patents on ice-making machines. His "Compression Ice Machine" would revolutionize the cold-storage industry. In 1869, other investors and he purchased an old steamship onto which they loaded one of Lowe's refrigeration units and began shipping fresh fruit from New York to the Gulf Coast area, and fresh meat from Galveston, Texas back to New York, but because of Lowe's lack of knowledge about shipping, the business was a costly failure.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">Refrigerator</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="503" decoding="async" height="82" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg/220px-Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg/330px-Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg/440px-Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Early_refrigerator_car_design_circa_1870.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An 1870 refrigerator car design. Hatches in the roof provided access to the tanks for the storage of harvested ice at each end.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AndrewMuhlPatent02.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="897" data-file-width="606" decoding="async" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/AndrewMuhlPatent02.png/220px-AndrewMuhlPatent02.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/AndrewMuhlPatent02.png/330px-AndrewMuhlPatent02.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/AndrewMuhlPatent02.png/440px-AndrewMuhlPatent02.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AndrewMuhlPatent02.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Icemaker Patent by Andrew Muhl, dated December 12, 1871.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1842, <a href="/wiki/John_Gorrie" title="John Gorrie">John Gorrie</a> created a system capable of refrigerating water to produce ice. Although it was a commercial failure, it inspired scientists and inventors around the world. France's Ferdinand Carre was one of the inspired and he created an ice producing system that was simpler and smaller than that of Gorrie. During the Civil War, cities such as New Orleans could no longer get ice from New England via the coastal ice trade. Carre's refrigeration system became the solution to New Orleans ice problems and, by 1865, the city had three of Carre's machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1867, in San Antonio, Texas, a French immigrant named Andrew Muhl built an ice-making machine to help service the expanding beef industry before moving it to Waco in 1871. In 1873, the patent for this machine was contracted by the Columbus Iron Works, a company acquired by the W.C. Bradley Co., which went on to produce the first commercial ice-makers in the US.
</p><p>By the 1870s, breweries had become the largest users of harvested ice. Though the ice-harvesting industry had grown immensely by the turn of the 20th century, pollution and sewage had begun to creep into natural ice, making it a problem in the metropolitan suburbs. Eventually, breweries began to complain of tainted ice. Public concern for the purity of water, from which ice was formed, began to increase in the early 1900s with the rise of germ theory. Numerous media outlets published articles connecting diseases such as typhoid fever with natural ice consumption. This caused ice harvesting to become illegal in certain areas of the country. All of these scenarios increased the demands for modern refrigeration and manufactured ice. Ice producing machines like that of Carre's and Muhl's were looked to as means of producing ice to meet the needs of grocers, farmers, and food shippers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Refrigerated railroad cars were introduced in the US in the 1840s for short-run transport of dairy products, but these used harvested ice to maintain a cool temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1089" data-file-width="1650" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG/220px-SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG/330px-SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG/440px-SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS_Dunedin_by_Frederick_Tudgay.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dunedin_(ship)" title="Dunedin (ship)"><i>Dunedin</i></a>, the first commercially successful refrigerated ship.</div></div></div>
<p>The new refrigerating technology first met with widespread industrial use as a means to freeze meat supplies for transport by sea in <a href="/wiki/Reefer_ship" title="Reefer ship">reefer ships</a> from the British <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> and other countries to the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>. Although not actually the first to achieve successful transportation of frozen goods overseas (the <i>Strathleven</i> had arrived at the London docks on 2 February 1880 with a cargo of frozen beef, mutton and butter from Sydney and Melbourne <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>), the breakthrough is often attributed to <a href="/wiki/William_Soltau_Davidson" title="William Soltau Davidson">William Soltau Davidson</a>, an entrepreneur who had emigrated to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Davidson thought that Britain's rising population and meat demand could mitigate the slump in world <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> markets that was heavily affecting New Zealand. After extensive research, he commissioned the <a href="/wiki/Dunedin_(ship)" title="Dunedin (ship)"><i>Dunedin</i></a> to be refitted with a compression refrigeration unit for meat shipment in 1881. On February 15, 1882, the <i>Dunedin</i> sailed for London with what was to be the first commercially successful refrigerated shipping voyage, and the foundation of the refrigerated <a href="/wiki/Meat_industry" title="Meat industry">meat industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> commented "Today we have to record such a triumph over physical difficulties, as would have been incredible, even unimaginable, a very few days ago...". The <i><a href="/wiki/Marlborough_(1876_ship)" title="Marlborough (1876 ship)">Marlborough</a></i>—sister ship to the <i>Dunedin</i> – was immediately converted and joined the trade the following year, along with the rival <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Shipping_Company" title="New Zealand Shipping Company">New Zealand Shipping Company</a> vessel <i>Mataurua</i>, while the German Steamer <i>Marsala</i> began carrying frozen New Zealand lamb in December 1882. Within five years, 172 shipments of frozen meat were sent from New Zealand to the United Kingdom, of which only 9 had significant amounts of meat condemned. Refrigerated shipping also led to a broader meat and dairy boom in <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and South America. <a href="/wiki/J_%26_E_Hall" title="J &amp; E Hall">J &amp; E Hall</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a>, England outfitted the <i>SS Selembria</i> with a vapor compression system to bring 30,000 carcasses of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutton" title="Mutton">mutton</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> in 1886.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In the years ahead, the industry rapidly expanded to Australia, Argentina and the United States.
</p><p>By the 1890s, refrigeration played a vital role in the distribution of food. The meat-packing industry relied heavily on natural ice in the 1880s and continued to rely on manufactured ice as those technologies became available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By 1900, the meat-packing houses of Chicago had adopted ammonia-cycle commercial refrigeration. By 1914, almost every location used artificial refrigeration. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meat_packing_industry#Meatpackers" title="Meat packing industry">major meat packers</a>, Armour, Swift, and Wilson, had purchased the most expensive units which they installed on train cars and in branch houses and storage facilities in the more remote distribution areas.
</p><p>By the middle of the 20th century, refrigeration units were designed for installation on trucks or lorries. Refrigerated vehicles are used to transport perishable goods, such as frozen foods, fruit and vegetables, and temperature-sensitive chemicals. Most modern refrigerators keep the temperature between –40 and –20 °C, and have a maximum payload of around 24,000 kg gross weight (in Europe).
</p><p>Although commercial refrigeration quickly progressed, it had limitations that prevented it from moving into the household. First, most refrigerators were far too large. Some of the commercial units being used in 1910 weighed between five and two hundred tons. Second, commercial refrigerators were expensive to produce, purchase, and maintain. Lastly, these refrigerators were unsafe. It was not uncommon for commercial refrigerators to catch fire, explode, or leak toxic gases. Refrigeration did not become a household technology until these three challenges were overcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_and_consumer_use">Home and consumer use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Home and consumer use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="577" data-file-width="813" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png/220px-Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png/330px-Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png/440px-Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Early_household_refrigerator_in_Popular_Science_1919.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An early example of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumerization" title="Consumerization">consumerization</a> of mechanical refrigeration that began in the early 20th century. The <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a> was <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg/220px-Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg/330px-Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg/440px-Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Food_into_a_refrigerator_-_20111002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A modern home refrigerator</div></div></div>
<p>During the early 1800s, consumers preserved their food by storing food and ice purchased from ice harvesters in iceboxes. In 1803, Thomas Moore patented a metal-lined butter-storage tub which became the prototype for most iceboxes. These iceboxes were used until nearly 1910 and the technology did not progress. In fact, consumers that used the icebox in 1910 faced the same challenge of a moldy and stinky icebox that consumers had in the early 1800s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>General Electric (GE) was one of the first companies to overcome these challenges. In 1911, GE released a household refrigeration unit that was powered by gas. The use of gas eliminated the need for an electric compressor motor and decreased the size of the refrigerator. However, electric companies that were customers of GE did not benefit from a gas-powered unit. Thus, GE invested in developing an electric model. In 1927, GE released the Monitor Top, the first refrigerator to run on electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1930, Frigidaire, one of GE's main competitors, synthesized <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> With the invention of synthetic refrigerants based mostly on a chlorofluorocarbon (CFC) chemical, safer refrigerators were possible for home and consumer use. Freon led to the development of smaller, lighter, and cheaper refrigerators. The average price of a refrigerator dropped from $275 to $154 with the synthesis of Freon. This lower price allowed ownership of refrigerators in American households to exceed 50% by 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Freon is a trademark of the DuPont Corporation and refers to these CFCs, and later hydro chlorofluorocarbon (HCFC) and hydro fluorocarbon (HFC), refrigerants developed in the late 1920s. These refrigerants were considered — at the time — to be less harmful than the commonly-used refrigerants of the time, including methyl formate, ammonia, methyl chloride, and sulfur dioxide. The intent was to provide refrigeration equipment for home use without danger. These CFC refrigerants answered that need. In the 1970s, though, the compounds were found to be reacting with atmospheric ozone, an important protection against solar ultraviolet radiation, and their use as a refrigerant worldwide was curtailed in the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> of 1987.
</p>

<p>In the last century, refrigeration allowed new settlement patterns to emerge. This new technology has allowed for new areas to be settled that are not on a natural channel of transport such as a river, valley trail or harbor that may have otherwise not been settled. Refrigeration has given opportunities to early settlers to expand westward and into rural areas that were unpopulated. These new settlers with rich and untapped soil saw opportunity to profit by sending raw goods to the eastern cities and states. In the 20th century, refrigeration has made “Galactic Cities” such as Dallas, Phoenix and Los Angeles possible.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigerated_rail_cars">Refrigerated rail cars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Refrigerated rail cars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The refrigerated rail car (<a href="/wiki/Refrigerated_van" title="Refrigerated van">refrigerated van</a> or <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator_car" title="Refrigerator car">refrigerator car</a>), along with the dense railroad network, became an exceedingly important link between the marketplace and the farm allowing for a national opportunity rather than a just a regional one. Before the invention of the refrigerated rail car, it was impossible to ship perishable food products long distances. The beef packing industry made the first demand push for refrigeration cars. The railroad companies were slow to adopt this new invention because of their heavy investments in cattle cars, stockyards, and feedlots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Refrigeration cars were also complex and costly compared to other rail cars, which also slowed the adoption of the refrigerated rail car. After the slow adoption of the refrigerated car, the beef packing industry dominated the refrigerated rail car business with their ability to control ice plants and the setting of icing fees. The United States Department of Agriculture estimated that, in 1916, over sixty-nine percent of the cattle killed in the country was done in plants involved in interstate trade. The same companies that were also involved in the meat trade later implemented refrigerated transport to include vegetables and fruit. The meat packing companies had much of the expensive machinery, such as refrigerated cars, and cold storage facilities that allowed for them to effectively distribute all types of perishable goods. During World War I, a national refrigerator car pool was established by the United States Administration to deal with problem of idle cars and was later continued after the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The idle car problem was the problem of refrigeration cars sitting pointlessly in between seasonal harvests. This meant that very expensive cars sat in rail yards for a good portion of the year while making no revenue for the car's owner. The car pool was a system where cars were distributed to areas as crops matured ensuring maximum use of the cars. Refrigerated rail cars moved eastward from vineyards, orchards, fields, and gardens in western states to satisfy Americas consuming market in the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The refrigerated car made it possible to transport perishable crops hundreds and even thousands of kilometres or miles. The most noticeable effect the car gave was a regional specialization of vegetables and fruits. The refrigeration rail car was widely used for the transportation of perishable goods up until the 1950s. By the 1960s, the nation's interstate highway system was adequately complete allowing for trucks to carry the majority of the perishable food loads and to push out the old system of the refrigerated rail cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_west_and_into_rural_areas">Expansion west and into rural areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Expansion west and into rural areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The widespread use of refrigeration allowed for a vast amount of new agricultural opportunities to open up in the United States. New markets emerged throughout the United States in areas that were previously uninhabited and far-removed from heavily populated areas. New agricultural opportunity presented itself in areas that were considered rural, such as states in the south and in the west. Shipments on a large scale from the south and California were both made around the same time, although natural ice was used from the Sierras in California rather than manufactured ice in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Refrigeration allowed for many areas to specialize in the growing of specific fruits. California specialized in several fruits, grapes, peaches, pears, plums, and apples, while Georgia became famous for specifically its peaches. In California, the acceptance of the refrigerated rail cars led to an increase of car loads from 4,500 carloads in 1895 to between 8,000 and 10,000 carloads in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The Gulf States, Arkansas, Missouri and Tennessee entered into strawberry production on a large-scale while Mississippi became the center of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomato_industry" title="Tomato industry">tomato industry</a>. New Mexico, Colorado, Arizona, and Nevada grew cantaloupes. Without refrigeration, this would have not been possible. By 1917, well-established fruit and vegetable areas that were close to eastern markets felt the pressure of competition from these distant specialized centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Refrigeration was not limited to meat, fruit and vegetables but it also encompassed dairy product and dairy farms. In the early twentieth century, large cities got their dairy supply from farms as far as 640 kilometres (400 mi). Dairy products were not as easily transported over great distances like fruits and vegetables due to greater perishability. Refrigeration made production possible in the west far from eastern markets, so much in fact that dairy farmers could pay transportation cost and still undersell their eastern competitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Refrigeration and the refrigerated rail gave opportunity to areas with rich soil far from natural channel of transport such as a river, valley trail or harbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schimd_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schimd-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_the_galactic_city">Rise of the galactic city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rise of the galactic city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Edge city" was a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Joel_Garreau" title="Joel Garreau">Joel Garreau</a>, whereas the term "galactic city" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">Lewis Mumford</a>. These terms refer to a concentration of business, shopping, and entertainment outside a traditional downtown or central business district in what had previously been a residential or rural area. There were several factors contributing to the growth of these cities such as Los Angeles, Las Vegas, Houston, and Phoenix. The factors that contributed to these large cities include reliable automobiles, highway systems, refrigeration, and agricultural production increases. Large cities such as the ones mentioned above have not been uncommon in history, but what separates these cities from the rest are that these cities are not along some natural channel of transport, or at some crossroad of two or more channels such as a trail, harbor, mountain, river, or valley. These large cities have been developed in areas that only a few hundred years ago would have been uninhabitable. Without a cost efficient way of cooling air and transporting water and food from great distances, these large cities would have never developed. The rapid growth of these cities was influenced by refrigeration and an agricultural productivity increase, allowing more distant farms to effectively feed the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schimd_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schimd-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_agriculture_and_food_production">Impact on agriculture and food production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impact on agriculture and food production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Agriculture's role in developed countries has drastically changed in the last century due to many factors, including refrigeration. Statistics from the 2007 census gives information on the large concentration of agricultural sales coming from a small portion of the existing farms in the United States today. This is a partial result of the market created for the frozen meat trade by the first successful shipment of frozen sheep carcasses coming from New Zealand in the 1880s. As the market continued to grow, regulations on food processing and quality began to be enforced. Eventually, electricity was introduced into rural homes in the United States, which allowed refrigeration technology to continue to expand on the farm, increasing output per person. Today, refrigeration's use on the farm reduces humidity levels, avoids spoiling due to bacterial growth, and assists in preservation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The introduction of refrigeration and evolution of additional technologies drastically changed agriculture in the United States. During the beginning of the 20th century, farming was a common occupation and lifestyle for United States citizens, as most farmers actually lived on their farm. In 1935, there were 6.8 million farms in the United States and a population of 127 million. Yet, while the United States population has continued to climb, citizens pursuing agriculture continue to decline. Based on the 2007 US Census, less than one percent of a population of 310 million people claim farming as an occupation today. However, the increasing population has led to an increasing demand for agricultural products, which is met through a greater variety of crops, fertilizers, pesticides, and improved technology. Improved technology has decreased the risk and time involved for agricultural management and allows larger farms to increase their output per person to meet society's demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meat_packing_and_trade">Meat packing and trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Meat packing and trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to 1882, the <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a> of New Zealand had been experimenting with sowing grass and crossbreeding sheep, which immediately gave their farmers economic potential in the exportation of meat. In 1882, the first successful shipment of sheep carcasses was sent from <a href="/wiki/Port_Chalmers" title="Port Chalmers">Port Chalmers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a>, New Zealand, to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. By the 1890s, the frozen meat trade became increasingly more profitable in New Zealand, especially in <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Region" title="Canterbury Region">Canterbury</a>, where 50% of exported sheep carcasses came from in 1900. It wasn't long before Canterbury meat was known for the highest quality, creating a demand for New Zealand meat around the world. In order to meet this new demand, the farmers improved their feed so sheep could be ready for the slaughter in only seven months. This new method of shipping led to an economic boom in New Zealand by the mid 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, the Meat Inspection Act of 1891 was put in place in the United States because local butchers felt the refrigerated railcar system was unwholesome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> When meat packing began to take off, consumers became nervous about the quality of the meat for consumption. <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">Upton Sinclair</a>'s 1906 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">The Jungle</a></i> brought negative attention to the meat packing industry, by drawing to light unsanitary working conditions and processing of diseased animals. The book caught the attention of President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Federal_Meat_Inspection_Act" title="Federal Meat Inspection Act">the 1906 Meat Inspection Act</a> was put into place as an amendment to the Meat Inspection Act of 1891. This new act focused on the quality of the meat and environment it is processed in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_in_rural_areas">Electricity in rural areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Electricity in rural areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1930s, 90 percent of the urban population of the United States <a href="/wiki/Electrification" title="Electrification">had electric power</a>, in comparison to only 10 percent of rural homes. At the time, power companies did not feel that extending power to rural areas (<a href="/wiki/Rural_electrification" title="Rural electrification">rural electrification</a>) would produce enough profit to make it worth their while. However, in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> realized that rural areas would continue to lag behind urban areas in both poverty and production if they were not electrically wired. On May 11, 1935, the president signed an executive order called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_Electrification_Administration" title="Rural Electrification Administration">Rural Electrification Administration</a>, also known as REA. The agency provided loans to fund electric infrastructure in the rural areas. In just a few years, 300,000 people in rural areas of the United States had received power in their homes.
</p><p>While electricity dramatically improved working conditions on farms, it also had a large impact on the safety of food production. Refrigeration systems were introduced to the farming and <a href="/wiki/Food_distribution" title="Food distribution">food distribution</a> processes, which helped in <a href="/wiki/Food_preservation" title="Food preservation">food preservation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">kept food supplies safe</a>. Refrigeration also allowed for shipment of perishable commodities throughout the United States. As a result, United States farmers quickly became the most productive in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and entire new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_systems" title="Food systems">food systems</a> arose.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Farm_use">Farm use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Farm use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In order to reduce humidity levels and spoiling due to bacterial growth, refrigeration is used for meat, produce, and dairy processing in farming today. Refrigeration systems are used the heaviest in the warmer months for farming produce, which must be cooled as soon as possible in order to meet quality standards and increase the shelf life. Meanwhile, dairy farms refrigerate milk year round to avoid spoiling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_lifestyle_and_diet">Effects on lifestyle and diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effects on lifestyle and diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late 19th Century and into the very early 20th Century, except for staple foods (sugar, rice, and beans) that needed no refrigeration, the available foods were affected heavily by the seasons and what could be grown locally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Refrigeration has removed these limitations. Refrigeration played a large part in the feasibility and then popularity of the modern supermarket. Fruits and vegetables out of season, or grown in distant locations, are now available at relatively low prices. Refrigerators have led to a huge increase in meat and dairy products as a portion of overall supermarket sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> As well as changing the goods purchased at the market, the ability to store these foods for extended periods of time has led to an increase in leisure time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Prior to the advent of the household refrigerator, people would have to shop on a daily basis for the supplies needed for their meals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_nutrition">Impact on nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Impact on nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The introduction of refrigeration allowed for the hygienic handling and storage of perishables, and as such, promoted output growth, consumption, and the availability of nutrition. The change in our method of food preservation moved us away from salts to a more manageable sodium level. The ability to move and store perishables such as meat and dairy led to a 1.7% increase in dairy consumption and overall protein intake by 1.25% annually in the US after the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>People were not only consuming these perishables because it became easier for they themselves to store them, but because the innovations in refrigerated transportation and storage led to less spoilage and waste, thereby driving the prices of these products down. Refrigeration accounts for at least 5.1% of the increase in adult stature (in the US) through improved nutrition, and when the indirect effects associated with improvements in the quality of nutrients and the reduction in illness is additionally factored in, the overall impact becomes considerably larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-45">[45]</a></sup> Recent studies have also shown a negative relationship between the number of refrigerators in a household and the rate of gastric cancer mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_applications_of_refrigeration">Current applications of refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Current applications of refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Probably the most widely used current applications of refrigeration are for <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> of private homes and public buildings, and refrigerating foodstuffs in homes, restaurants and large storage warehouses. The use of <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerators</a> and walk-in coolers and freezers in kitchens, factories and warehouses <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> for storing and processing fruits and vegetables has allowed adding fresh salads to the modern diet year round, and storing fish and meats safely for long periods.
The optimum temperature range for perishable food storage is 3 to 5 °C (37 to 41 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>In commerce and manufacturing, there are many uses for refrigeration. Refrigeration is used to liquefy gases – <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, for example. In compressed air purification, it is used to <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condense</a> water vapor from compressed air to reduce its moisture content. In <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">chemical plants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> plants, refrigeration is used to maintain certain processes at their needed low temperatures (for example, in <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">alkylation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Butene" title="Butene">butenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a> to produce a high-<a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane</a> gasoline component). Metal workers use refrigeration to temper steel and cutlery. When transporting temperature-sensitive foodstuffs and other materials by trucks, trains, airplanes and seagoing vessels, refrigeration is a necessity.
</p><p>Dairy products are constantly in need of refrigeration, and it was only discovered in the past few decades that eggs needed to be refrigerated during shipment rather than waiting to be refrigerated after arrival at the grocery store. Meats, poultry and fish all must be kept in climate-controlled environments before being sold. Refrigeration also helps keep fruits and vegetables edible longer.
</p><p>One of the most influential uses of refrigeration was in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">sushi</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sashimi" title="Sashimi">sashimi</a> industry in Japan. Before the discovery of refrigeration, many sushi connoisseurs were at risk of contracting diseases. The dangers of unrefrigerated sashimi were not brought to light for decades due to the lack of research and healthcare distribution across rural Japan. Around mid-century, the <a href="/wiki/Zojirushi" title="Zojirushi">Zojirushi</a> corporation, based in Kyoto, made breakthroughs in refrigerator designs, making refrigerators cheaper and more accessible for restaurant proprietors and the general public.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_refrigeration">Methods of refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Methods of refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methods of refrigeration can be classified as <i>non-cyclic</i>, <i>cyclic</i>, <i>thermoelectric</i> and <i>magnetic</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-cyclic_refrigeration">Non-cyclic refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Non-cyclic refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ice_trade" title="Ice trade">Ice trade</a></div>
<p>This refrigeration method cools a contained area by melting ice, or by sublimating <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Perhaps the simplest example of this is a portable cooler, where items are put in it, then ice is poured over the top. Regular ice can maintain temperatures near, but not below the freezing point, unless salt is used to cool the ice down further (as in a <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream_maker#Hand_Cranked_machines" title="Ice cream maker">traditional ice-cream maker</a>). Dry ice can reliably bring the temperature well below water freezing point.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclic_refrigeration">Cyclic refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cyclic refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump_and_refrigeration_cycle" title="Heat pump and refrigeration cycle">Heat pump and refrigeration cycle</a></div>
<p>This consists of a refrigeration cycle, where heat is removed from a low-temperature space or source and rejected to a high-temperature sink with the help of external work, and its inverse, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_power_cycle" title="Thermodynamic power cycle">thermodynamic power cycle</a>. In the power cycle, heat is supplied from a high-temperature source to the engine, part of the heat being used to produce work and the rest being rejected to a low-temperature sink. This satisfies the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>.
</p><p>A <i>refrigeration cycle</i> describes the changes that take place in the refrigerant as it alternately absorbs and rejects heat as it circulates through a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerator</a>. It is also applied to heating, ventilation, and air conditioning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVACR" title="HVACR">HVACR</a> work, when describing the "process" of refrigerant flow through an HVACR unit, whether it is a packaged or split system.
</p><p>Heat naturally flows from hot to cold. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">Work</a> is applied to cool a living space or storage volume by pumping heat from a lower temperature heat source into a higher temperature heat sink. <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">Insulation</a> is used to reduce the work and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> needed to achieve and maintain a lower temperature in the cooled space. The operating principle of the refrigeration cycle was described mathematically by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">Sadi Carnot</a> in 1824 as a <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">heat engine</a>.
</p><p>The most common types of refrigeration systems use the reverse-Rankine <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">vapor-compression refrigeration</a> cycle, although <a href="/wiki/Absorption_heat_pump" title="Absorption heat pump">absorption heat pumps</a> are used in a minority of applications.
</p><p>Cyclic refrigeration can be classified as:
</p>
<ol><li>Vapor cycle, and</li>
<li>Gas cycle</li></ol>
<p>Vapor cycle refrigeration can further be classified as:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">Vapor-compression refrigeration</a></li>
<li>Sorption Refrigeration
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Absorption_refrigerator" title="Absorption refrigerator">Vapor-absorption refrigeration</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adsorption_refrigeration" title="Adsorption refrigeration">Adsorption refrigeration</a></li></ol></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vapor-compression_cycle">Vapor-compression cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Vapor-compression cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">Vapor-compression refrigeration</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:301px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Refrigeration.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="281" data-file-width="299" decoding="async" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/Refrigeration.png" width="299"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Figure 1: Vapor compression refrigeration</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:389px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RefrigerationTS.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="387" decoding="async" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f7/RefrigerationTS.png" width="387"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Figure 2: Temperature–Entropy diagram</div></div></div>
<p>The vapor-compression cycle is used in most household refrigerators as well as in many large commercial and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_refrigerator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Industrial refrigerator (page does not exist)">industrial refrigeration</a> systems. Figure 1 provides a schematic diagram of the components of a typical vapor-compression refrigeration system.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> of the cycle can be analyzed on a diagram<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> as shown in Figure 2. In this cycle, a circulating refrigerant such as a low boiling hydrocarbon or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbons" title="Hydrofluorocarbons">hydrofluorocarbons</a> enters the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a> as a vapour. From point 1 to point 2, the vapor is compressed at constant <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> and exits the compressor as a vapor at a higher temperature, but still below the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> at that temperature. From point 2 to point 3 and on to point 4, the vapor travels through the <a href="/wiki/Condenser_(heat_transfer)" title="Condenser (heat transfer)">condenser</a> which cools the vapour until it starts condensing, and then condenses the vapor into a liquid by removing additional heat at constant pressure and temperature. Between points 4 and 5, the liquid refrigerant goes through the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion_valve" title="Thermal expansion valve">expansion valve</a> (also called a throttle valve) where its pressure abruptly decreases, causing <a href="/wiki/Flash_evaporation" title="Flash evaporation">flash evaporation</a> and auto-refrigeration of, typically, less than half of the liquid.
</p><p>That results in a mixture of liquid and vapour at a lower temperature and pressure as shown at point 5. The cold liquid-vapor mixture then travels through the evaporator coil or tubes and is completely vaporized by cooling the warm air (from the space being refrigerated) being blown by a fan across the evaporator coil or tubes. The resulting refrigerant vapour returns to the compressor inlet at point 1 to complete the thermodynamic cycle.
</p><p>The above discussion is based on the ideal vapour-compression refrigeration cycle, and does not take into account real-world effects like frictional pressure drop in the system, slight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_reversibility" title="Thermodynamic reversibility">thermodynamic irreversibility</a> during the compression of the refrigerant vapor, or <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">non-ideal gas</a> behavior, if any. Vapor compression refrigerators can be arranged in two stages in <a href="/wiki/Cascade_refrigeration" title="Cascade refrigeration">cascade refrigeration</a> systems, with the second stage cooling the condenser of the first stage. This can be used for achieving very low temperatures.
</p><p>More information about the design and performance of vapor-compression refrigeration systems is available in the classic <i><a href="/wiki/Perry%27s_Chemical_Engineers%27_Handbook" title="Perry's Chemical Engineers' Handbook">Perry's Chemical Engineers' Handbook</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sorption_cycle">Sorption cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sorption cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption_cycle">Absorption cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Absorption cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Absorption_refrigerator" title="Absorption refrigerator">Absorption refrigerator</a></div>
<p>In the early years of the twentieth century, the vapor absorption cycle using water-ammonia systems or <a href="/wiki/Lithium_bromide" title="Lithium bromide">LiBr</a>-water was popular and widely used. After the development of the vapor compression cycle, the vapor absorption cycle lost much of its importance because of its low <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">coefficient of performance</a> (about one fifth of that of the vapor compression cycle). Today, the vapor absorption cycle is used mainly where fuel for heating is available but electricity is not, such as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicles" title="Recreational vehicles">recreational vehicles</a> that carry <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LP gas</a>. It is also used in industrial environments where plentiful waste heat overcomes its inefficiency.
</p><p>The absorption cycle is similar to the compression cycle, except for the method of raising the pressure of the refrigerant vapor. In the absorption system, the compressor is replaced by an absorber which dissolves the refrigerant in a suitable liquid, a liquid pump which raises the pressure and a generator which, on heat addition, drives off the refrigerant vapor from the high-pressure liquid. Some work is needed by the liquid pump but, for a given quantity of refrigerant, it is much smaller than needed by the compressor in the vapor compression cycle. In an absorption refrigerator, a suitable combination of refrigerant and absorbent is used. The most common combinations are ammonia (refrigerant) with water (absorbent), and water (refrigerant) with lithium bromide (absorbent).
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_cycle">Adsorption cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Adsorption cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Adsorption_refrigeration" title="Adsorption refrigeration">Adsorption refrigeration</a></div>
<p>The main difference with absorption cycle, is that in adsorption cycle, the refrigerant (adsorbate) could be ammonia, water, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, etc., while the adsorbent is a solid, such as <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a>, unlike in the absorption cycle where absorbent is liquid.
</p><p>The reason adsorption refrigeration technology has been extensively researched in recent 30 years lies in that the operation of an adsorption refrigeration system is often noiseless, non-corrosive and environment friendly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_cycle">Gas cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Gas cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a> is a gas that is compressed and expanded but doesn't change phase, the refrigeration cycle is called a <i>gas cycle</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">Air</a> is most often this working fluid. As there is no condensation and evaporation intended in a gas cycle, components corresponding to the condenser and evaporator in a vapor compression cycle are the hot and cold gas-to-gas <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a> in gas cycles.
</p><p>The gas cycle is less efficient than the vapor compression cycle because the gas cycle works on the reverse <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">Brayton cycle</a> instead of the reverse <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a>. As such, the working fluid does not receive and reject heat at constant temperature. In the gas cycle, the refrigeration effect is equal to the product of the specific heat of the gas and the rise in temperature of the gas in the low temperature side. Therefore, for the same cooling load, a gas refrigeration cycle needs a large mass flow rate and is bulky.
</p><p>Because of their lower efficiency and larger bulk, <i>air cycle</i> coolers are not often used nowadays in terrestrial cooling devices. However, the <a href="/wiki/Air_cycle_machine" title="Air cycle machine">air cycle machine</a> is very common on <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a>-powered jet <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> as cooling and ventilation units, because compressed air is readily available from the engines' compressor sections. Such units also serve the purpose of pressurizing the aircraft.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoelectric_refrigeration">Thermoelectric refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Thermoelectric refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_cooling" title="Thermoelectric cooling">Thermoelectric cooling</a> uses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peltier_effect" title="Peltier effect">Peltier effect</a> to create a heat <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> between the junction of two types of material. This effect is commonly used in camping and portable coolers and for cooling electronic components and small instruments. Peltier coolers are often used where a traditional vapor-compression cycle refrigerator would be impractical or take up too much space, and in cooled image sensors as an easy, compact and lightweight, if inefficient, way to achieve very low temperatures, using two or more stage peltier coolers arranged in a <a href="/wiki/Cascade_refrigeration" title="Cascade refrigeration">cascade refrigeration</a> configuration, meaning that two or more Peltier elements are stacked on top of each other, with each stage being larger than the one before it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> in order to extract more heat and waste heat generated by the previous stages. Peltier cooling has a low COP (efficiency) when compared with that of the vapor-compression cycle, so it emits more waste heat (heat generated by the Peltier element or cooling mechanism) and consumes more power for a given cooling capacity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_refrigeration">Magnetic refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Magnetic refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_refrigeration" title="Magnetic refrigeration">Magnetic refrigeration</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Magnetic refrigeration, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic" title="Adiabatic">adiabatic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demagnetization" title="Demagnetization">demagnetization</a>, is a cooling technology based on the magnetocaloric effect, an <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_and_extrinsic_properties" title="Intrinsic and extrinsic properties">intrinsic property</a> of magnetic solids. The refrigerant is often a <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>. The active <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipoles</a> in this case are those of the <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell" title="Electron shell">electron shells</a> of the paramagnetic atoms.
</p><p>A strong magnetic field is applied to the refrigerant, forcing its various magnetic dipoles to align and putting these degrees of freedom of the refrigerant into a state of lowered <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>. A heat sink then absorbs the heat released by the refrigerant due to its loss of entropy. Thermal contact with the heat sink is then broken so that the system is insulated, and the magnetic field is switched off. This increases the heat capacity of the refrigerant, thus decreasing its temperature below the temperature of the heat sink.
</p><p>Because few materials exhibit the needed properties at room temperature, applications have so far been limited to <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a> and research.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods">Other methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Other methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Other methods of refrigeration include the <a href="/wiki/Air_cycle_machine" title="Air cycle machine">air cycle machine</a> used in aircraft; the <a href="/wiki/Vortex_tube" title="Vortex tube">vortex tube</a> used for spot cooling, when compressed air is available; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoacoustic_refrigeration" title="Thermoacoustic refrigeration">thermoacoustic refrigeration</a> using sound waves in a pressurized gas to drive heat transfer and heat exchange; <a href="/wiki/Steam_jet_cooling" title="Steam jet cooling">steam jet cooling</a> popular in the early 1930s for air conditioning large buildings; thermoelastic cooling using a smart metal alloy stretching and relaxing. Many <a href="/wiki/Stirling_cycle" title="Stirling cycle">Stirling cycle</a> heat engines can be run backwards to act as a refrigerator, and therefore these engines have a niche use in <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a>. In addition, there are other types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryocoolers" title="Cryocoolers">cryocoolers</a> such as Gifford-McMahon coolers, Joule-Thomson coolers, <a href="/wiki/Pulse_tube_refrigerator" title="Pulse tube refrigerator">pulse-tube refrigerators</a> and, for temperatures between 2 mK and 500 mK, <a href="/wiki/Dilution_refrigerator" title="Dilution refrigerator">dilution refrigerators</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elastocaloric_refrigeration">Elastocaloric refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Elastocaloric refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another potential solid-state refrigeration technique and a relatively new area of study comes from a special property of <a href="/wiki/Pseudoelasticity" title="Pseudoelasticity">super elastic</a> materials. These materials undergo a temperature change when experiencing an applied mechanical <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">stress</a> (called the elastocaloric effect). Since super elastic materials deform reversibly at high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strain_(mechanics)" title="Strain (mechanics)">strains</a>, the material experiences a flattened <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elastic</a> region in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress-strain_curve" title="Stress-strain curve">stress-strain curve</a> caused by a resulting phase transformation from an <a href="/wiki/Austenite" title="Austenite">austenitic</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Martensite" title="Martensite">martensitic</a> crystal phase.
</p><p>When a super elastic material experiences a stress in the austenitic phase, it undergoes an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transformation</a> to the martensitic phase, which causes the material to heat up. Removing the stress reverses the process, restores the material to its austenitic phase, and <a href="/wiki/Endothermic_process" title="Endothermic process">absorbs heat</a> from the surroundings cooling down the material.
</p><p>The most appealing part of this research is how potentially energy efficient and environmentally friendly this cooling technology is. The different materials used, commonly <a href="/wiki/Shape-memory_alloy" title="Shape-memory alloy">shape-memory alloys</a>, provide a non-toxic source of emission free refrigeration. The most commonly studied materials studied are shape-memory alloys, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitinol" title="Nitinol">nitinol</a> and Cu-Zn-Al. Nitinol is of the more promising alloys with output heat at about 66 J/cm<sup>3</sup> and a temperature change of about 16–20 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Due to the difficulty in manufacturing some of the shape memory alloys, alternative materials like <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">natural rubber</a> have been studied. Even though rubber may not give off as much heat per volume (12 J/cm<sup>3</sup> ) as the shape memory alloys, it still generates a comparable temperature change of about 12 K and operates at a suitable temperature range, low stresses, and low cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main challenge however comes from potential energy losses in the form of <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a>, often associated with this process. Since most of these losses comes from incompatibilities between the two phases, proper alloy tuning is necessary to reduce losses and increase reversibility and <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">efficiency</a>. Balancing the transformation strain of the material with the energy losses enables a large elastocaloric effect to occur and potentially a new alternative for refrigeration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fridge_Gate">Fridge Gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Fridge Gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Fridge Gate method is a theoretical application of using a single logic gate to drive a refrigerator in the most energy efficient way possible without violating the laws of thermodynamics. It operates on the fact that there are two energy states in which a particle can exist: the ground state and the excited state. The excited state carries a little more energy than the ground state, small enough so that the transition occurs with high probability. There are three components or particle types associated with the fridge gate. The first is on the interior of the fridge, the second on the outside and the third is connected to a power supply which heats up every so often that it can reach the E state and replenish the source. In the cooling step on the inside of the fridge, the g state particle absorbs energy from ambient particles, cooling them, and itself jumping to the e state. In the second step, on the outside of the fridge where the particles are also at an e state, the particle falls to the g state, releasing energy and heating the outside particles. In the third and final step, the power supply moves a particle at the e state, and when it falls to the g state it induces an energy-neutral swap where the interior e particle is replaced by a new g particle, restarting the cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_systems">Passive systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Passive systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When combining a <a href="/wiki/Passive_daytime_radiative_cooling" title="Passive daytime radiative cooling">passive daytime radiative cooling</a> system with <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">thermal insulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooler" title="Evaporative cooler">evaporative cooling</a>, one study found a 300% increase in ambient cooling power when compared to a stand-alone radiative cooling surface, which could extend the <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a> of food by 40% in <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humid climates</a> and 200% in <a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">desert climates</a> without refrigeration. The system's evaporative cooling layer would require water "re-charges" every 10 days to a month in humid areas and every 4 days in hot and dry areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacity_ratings">Capacity ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Capacity ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Refrigeration&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The refrigeration capacity of a refrigeration system is the product of the <a href="/wiki/Evaporator" title="Evaporator">evaporators</a>’ <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> rise and the evaporators’ <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">mass flow rate</a>. The measured capacity of refrigeration is often dimensioned in the unit of kW or BTU/h. Domestic and commercial refrigerators may be rated in kJ/s, or Btu/h of cooling. For commercial and industrial refrigeration systems, the <b>kilowatt</b> (kW) is the basic unit of refrigeration, except in North America, where both <b><a href="/wiki/Ton_of_refrigeration" title="Ton of refrigeration">ton of refrigeration</a></b> and BTU/h are used.
</p><p>A refrigeration system's <b><a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">coefficient of performance</a></b> (CoP) is very important in determining a system's overall efficiency. It is defined as refrigeration capacity in kW divided by the energy input in kW. While CoP is a very simple measure of performance, it is typically not used for industrial refrigeration in North America. Owners and manufacturers of these systems typically use <b>performance factor</b> (PF). A system's PF is defined as a system's energy input in horsepower divided by its refrigeration capacity in TR. Both CoP and PF can be applied to either the entire system or to system components. For example, an individual compressor can be rated by comparing the energy needed to run the compressor versus the expected refrigeration capacity based on inlet volume flow rate. It is important to note that both CoP and PF for a refrigeration system are only defined at specific operating conditions, including temperatures and thermal loads. Moving away from the specified operating conditions can dramatically change a system's performance.
</p><p>Air conditioning systems used in residential application typically use <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_energy_efficiency_ratio" title="Seasonal energy efficiency ratio">SEER</a> (Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio)for the energy performance rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Air conditioning systems for commercial application often use EER (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_ratio" title="Energy efficiency ratio">Energy Efficiency Ratio</a>) and IEER (Integrated Energy Efficiency Ratio) for the energy efficiency performance rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
