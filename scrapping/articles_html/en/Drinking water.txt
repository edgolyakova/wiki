<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Drinking water</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Water safe for consumption</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drinking_water.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Drinking_water.jpg/220px-Drinking_water.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Drinking_water.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drinking_water.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">Tap water</a> is drinking water supplied through <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">indoor plumbing</a> for home use in many countries.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Drinking water</b> is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> that is used in <a href="/wiki/Drinking" title="Drinking">drink</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_preparation" title="Food preparation">food preparation</a>; <b>potable water</b> is water that is safe to be used as drinking water. The amount of drinking water required to maintain good health varies, and depends on physical activity level, age, health-related issues, and environmental conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2004_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2004-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA2011-2">[2]</a></sup> For those who work in a hot climate, up to 16 litres (4.2 US gal) a day may be required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2004_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Typically in <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">tap water</a> meets drinking <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> standards, even though only a small proportion is actually consumed or used in food preparation. Other typical uses for tap water include washing, toilets, and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">Greywater</a> may also be used for toilets or irrigation. Its use for irrigation however may be associated with risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Water may also be unacceptable due to levels of toxins or suspended solids.
</p><p>Globally, by 2015, 89% of people had access to water from a source that is suitable for drinking – called <i><a href="/wiki/Improved_water_source" title="Improved water source">improved water source</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>, access to potable water ranged from 40% to 80% of the population. Nearly 4.2 <a href="/wiki/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a> people worldwide had access to tap water, while another 2.4 billion had access to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">wells</a> or public taps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> considers access to safe drinking-water a basic human right.
</p><p>About 1 to 2 billion people lack safe drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2018_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2018-4">[4]</a></sup> Water can <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">carry vectors of disease</a>. More people die from unsafe water than from war, then-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.N._secretary-general" title="U.N. secretary-general">U.N. secretary-general</a> <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> said in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Third world countries are most affected by lack of water, flooding, and water quality. Up to 80 percent of illnesses in developing countries are the direct result of inadequate water and sanitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to a reports by <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> and <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> has the best drinking water quality in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">Water resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_security" title="Water security">Water security</a></div>

<p>Water covers approximately 70% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%E2%80%99s_surface" title="Earth’s surface">Earth's surface</a>, where approximately 97.2% of it is <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a>, only 2.8% <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh</a>. Potable water is available in almost all populated areas of the Earth, although it may be expensive and the supply may not always be sustainable. Sources where water may be obtained include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_water" title="Spring water">springs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyporheic_zone" title="Hyporheic zone">hyporheic zones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, and:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">Precipitation</a> which includes rain, hail, snow, fog, etc.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">Surface water</a> such as rivers, streams, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">Biological</a> sources such as plants</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">Desalinated</a> <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Water_supply_network" title="Water supply network">Water supply network</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_water_generator" title="Atmospheric water generator">Atmospheric water generators</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10.1038/s41586-021-03900-w_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-10.1038/s41586-021-03900-w-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Threats for the availability of water resources include: <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_conflict" title="Water conflict">water conflict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquifer#Depth,_droughts_and_overpumping" title="Aquifer">insufficient well-depth, droughts and overpumping</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change" title="Effects of climate change">effects of climate change</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption">Consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Requirements_for_drinking">Requirements for drinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Requirements for drinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fluid_balance" title="Fluid balance">Fluid balance</a></div>
<p>The amount of drinking water required per day is variable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2004_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2004-1">[1]</a></sup> It depends on physical activity, age, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>, and environmental conditions. In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Reference_Daily_Intake" title="Reference Daily Intake">Adequate Intake</a> for total water, based on median intakes, is 3.7 litres (130 imp fl oz; 130 US fl oz) per day for human males older than 18, and 2.7 litres (95 imp fl oz; 91 US fl oz) per day for human females older than 18 which includes about 80% from beverages and 20% from food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a> recommends 2.0 litres (70 imp fl oz; 68 US fl oz) of total water per day for adult women and 2.5 litres (88 imp fl oz; 85 US fl oz) per day for adult men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efsa_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-efsa-14">[14]</a></sup> The common advice to drink 8 glasses (1,900 mL or 64 US fl oz) of plain water per day is not based on science, and an individual's thirst provides a better guide for how much water they require rather than a specific, fixed quantity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ajpregu.physiology.org_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ajpregu.physiology.org-15">[15]</a></sup> Americans age 21 and older, on average, drink 1,043 mL (36.7 imp fl oz; 35.3 US fl oz) of drinking water a day and 95% drink less than 2,958 mL (104.1 imp fl oz; 100.0 US fl oz) per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA2011-2">[2]</a></sup> Physical exercise and heat exposure cause loss of water and therefore may induce thirst and greater water intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iom_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-iom-16">[16]</a></sup> Physically active individuals in hot climates may have total daily water needs of 6 litres (210 imp fl oz; 200 US fl oz) or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iom_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-iom-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The drinking water contribution to mineral nutrients intake is also unclear. <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">Inorganic</a> minerals generally enter surface water and ground water via <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">storm water</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> or through the Earth's crust. Treatment processes also lead to the presence of some minerals. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Water generated from the <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of nutrients provides a significant proportion of the daily water requirements for some <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> animals, but provides only a small fraction of a human's necessary intake. There are a variety of trace elements present in virtually all potable water, some of which play a role in metabolism. For example, sodium, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> are common chemicals found in small quantities in most waters, and these elements play a role in body metabolism. Other elements such as <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, while beneficial in low concentrations, can cause dental problems and other issues when present at high levels.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fluid_balance" title="Fluid balance">Fluid balance</a> is key. Profuse sweating can increase the need for <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> (salt) replacement. <a href="/wiki/Water_intoxication" title="Water intoxication">Water intoxication</a> (which results in <a href="/wiki/Hyponatremia" title="Hyponatremia">hyponatremia</a>), the process of consuming too much water too quickly, can be fatal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Water makes up about 60% of the body weight in men and 55% of weight in women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A baby is composed of about 70% to 80% water while the elderly are composed of around 45%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Household_usage">Household usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Household usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_use" title="Water use">Water use</a> and <a href="/wiki/Residential_water_use_in_the_U.S._and_Canada" title="Residential water use in the U.S. and Canada">Residential water use in the U.S. and Canada</a></div>
<p>In the United States, the typical water consumption per capita, at home, is 69.3 US gallons (262 l; 57.7 imp gal) of water per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Of this, only 1% of the water provided by public water suppliers is for drinking and cooking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> Uses include (in decreasing order) toilets, washing machines, showers, baths, faucets, and leaks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The qualitative and quantitative aspects of drinking water requirements on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_animals" title="Domesticated animals">domesticated animals</a> are studied and described within the context of <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>. However, relatively few studies have been focused on the drinking behavior of wild animals. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_supply">Water supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">Water supply</a></div>
<p>The most efficient and convenient way to transport and deliver potable water is through pipes. Plumbing can require significant capital investment. Some systems suffer high operating costs. The cost to replace the deteriorating water and sanitation infrastructure of industrialized countries may be as high as $200 billion a year. Leakage of untreated and treated water from pipes reduces access to water. Leakage rates of 50% are not uncommon in urban systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Springs are often used as sources for bottled waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">Tap water</a>, delivered by domestic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_system" title="Water system">water systems</a> refers to water piped to homes and delivered to a <a href="/wiki/Tap_(valve)" title="Tap (valve)">tap</a> or spigot. For these water sources to be consumed safely, they must receive adequate treatment and meet drinking water regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of the high initial investments, many less wealthy nations cannot afford to develop or sustain appropriate infrastructure, and as a consequence people in these areas may spend a correspondingly higher fraction of their income on water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> 2003 statistics from El Salvador, for example, indicate that the poorest 20% of households spend more than 10% of their total income on water. In the United Kingdom, authorities define spending of more than 3% of one's income on water as a hardship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_quality">Water quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Water quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">Water quality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water_quality_standards" title="Drinking water quality standards">Drinking water quality standards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Water pollution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3300" data-file-width="2550" decoding="async" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg/220px-Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg/330px-Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg/440px-Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drinking_Water_Security_Poster_EPA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>EPA drinking water security poster from 2003</div></div></div>
<p>According to the World Health Organization's 2017 report, <i>safe</i> drinking-water is water that "does not represent any significant risk to health over a lifetime of consumption, including different sensitivities that may occur between life stages".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_2017_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_2017-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 2">: 2 </span></sup>
</p><p>Parameters for drinking water quality typically fall within three categories: physical, chemical, microbiological.
</p><p>Physical and chemical parameters include <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, trace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Total_suspended_solids" title="Total suspended solids">total suspended solids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>. Chemical parameters tend to pose more of a chronic health risk through buildup of heavy metals although some components like nitrates/nitrites and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> can have a more immediate impact. Physical parameters affect the aesthetics and taste of the drinking water and may complicate the removal of microbial pathogens.
</p><p>Microbiological parameters include <a href="/wiki/Coliform_bacteria" title="Coliform bacteria">coliform bacteria</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i>, and specific pathogenic species of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>-causing <i><a href="/wiki/Vibrio_cholerae" title="Vibrio cholerae">Vibrio cholerae</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viruses" title="Viruses">viruses</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protozoan" title="Protozoan">protozoan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasites" title="Parasites">parasites</a>. Originally, fecal contamination was determined with the presence of <a href="/wiki/Coliform_bacteria" title="Coliform bacteria">coliform bacteria</a>, a convenient marker for a class of harmful <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">fecal</a> <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. The presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fecal_coliforms" title="Fecal coliforms">fecal coliforms</a> (like <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. Coli</a></i>) serves as an indication of contamination by <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>. Additional contaminants include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protozoan" title="Protozoan">protozoan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oocysts" title="Oocysts">oocysts</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidium" title="Cryptosporidium">Cryptosporidium</a> sp.</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giardia_lamblia" title="Giardia lamblia">Giardia lamblia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Legionella" title="Legionella">Legionella</a></i>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viruses" title="Viruses">viruses</a> (enteric).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Microbial pathogenic parameters are typically of greatest concern because of their immediate health risk.
</p><p>Throughout most of the world, the most common contamination of raw water sources is from human sewage in particular human fecal pathogens and parasites. In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a> were estimated to cause 1.8 million deaths while about 1.1 billion people lacked proper drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In parts of the world, the only sources of water are from small streams that are often directly contaminated by sewage.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">Pesticides</a>, whether used in agriculture or domestically (e.g. homes, schools, businesses) are potential drinking water contaminants. Pesticides may be present in drinking water in low concentrations, but the <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> of the chemical and the extent of human exposure are factors that are used to determine the specific health risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfluorinated_alkylated_substances" title="Perfluorinated alkylated substances">Perfluorinated alkylated substances</a> (PFAS) are a group of synthetic compounds used in a large variety of consumer products, such as food packaging, waterproof fabrics, carpeting and cookware. PFAS are known to persist in the environment and are commonly described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutants" title="Persistent organic pollutants">persistent organic pollutants</a>. PFAS chemicals have been detected in blood, both humans and animals, worldwide, as well as in food products, water, air and soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Animal testing studies with PFAS have shown effects on growth and development, and possibly effects on reproduction, thyroid, the immune system and liver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> As of 2022 the health impacts of many PFAS compounds are not understood. Scientists are conducting research to determine the extent and severity of impacts from PFAS on human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> PFAS have been widely detected in drinking water worldwide and regulations have been developed, or are under development, in many countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_treatment">Water treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Water treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">Water purification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">Water treatment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg/220px-Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg/330px-Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg/440px-Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water treatment plant</div></div></div>
<p>Most water requires some treatment before use; even water from deep wells or springs. The extent of treatment depends on the source of the water. Appropriate technology options in water treatment include both community-scale and household-scale point-of-use (POU) designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cawstHWT_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-cawstHWT-37">[37]</a></sup> Only a few large urban areas such as <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> have access to sufficiently pure water of sufficient volume that no treatment of the raw water is required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In emergency situations when conventional treatment systems have been compromised, waterborne pathogens may be killed or inactivated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_water#Boiling_for_water_purification" title="Boiling water">boiling</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> but this requires abundant sources of fuel, and can be very onerous on consumers, especially where it is difficult to store boiled water in sterile conditions. Other techniques, such as filtration, chemical disinfection, and exposure to ultraviolet radiation (including solar UV) have been demonstrated in an array of randomized control trials to significantly reduce levels of water-borne disease among users in low-income countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> but these suffer from the same problems as boiling methods.
</p><p>Another type of water treatment is called <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalination</a> and is used mainly in dry areas with access to large bodies of saltwater.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Point_of_use_methods">Point of use methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Point of use methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Portable_water_purification" title="Portable water purification">Portable water purification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-supply_of_water_and_sanitation" title="Self-supply of water and sanitation">Self-supply of water and sanitation</a></div>
<p>The ability of point of use (POU) options to reduce disease is a function of both their ability to remove microbial pathogens if properly applied and such social factors as ease of use and cultural appropriateness. Technologies may generate more (or less) health benefit than their lab-based microbial removal performance would suggest.
</p><p>The current priority of the proponents of POU treatment is to reach large numbers of low-income households on a sustainable basis. Few POU measures have reached significant scale thus far, but efforts to promote and commercially distribute these products to the world's poor have only been under way for a few years.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Solar_water_disinfection" title="Solar water disinfection">Solar water disinfection</a> is a low-cost method of purifying water that can often be implemented with locally available materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Unlike methods that rely on <a href="/wiki/Firewood" title="Firewood">firewood</a>, it has low impact on the environment.
</p>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO), "access to safe drinking-water is essential to health, a basic human right and a component of effective policy for health protection."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_2017_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_2017-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 2">: 2 </span></sup> In 1990, only 76 percent of the global population had access to drinking water. By 2015 that number had increased to 91 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1990, most countries in Latin America, East and South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa were well below 90%. In Sub-Saharan Africa, where the rates are lowest, household access ranges from 40 to 80 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018-45">[45]</a></sup> Countries that experience violent conflict can have reductions in drinking water access: One study found that a conflict with about 2,500 battle deaths deprives 1.8% of the population of potable water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2015, 5.2 billion people representing 71% of the global population used safely managed drinking water services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017-47">[47]</a></sup> As of 2017, 90% of people having access to water from a source that is suitable for drinking – called <i><a href="/wiki/Improved_water_source" title="Improved water source">improved water source</a></i> – and 71% of the world could access safely managed drinking water that is clean and available on-demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Estimates suggest that at least 25% of improved sources contain fecal contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLoS_Bain_2014_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-PLoS_Bain_2014-48">[48]</a></sup> 1.8 billion people still use an unsafe drinking water source which may be contaminated by <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> This can result in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a>, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Reduction of waterborne diseases and development of safe water resources is a major <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> goal in developing countries. <a href="/wiki/Bottled_water" title="Bottled water">Bottled water</a> is sold for public consumption in most parts of the world.
</p><p>Improved sources are also monitored based on whether water is available when needed (5.8 billion people), located on premises (5.4 billion), free from contamination (5.4 billion), and within a 30-minute round trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 3">: 3 </span></sup> While improved water sources such as protected piped water are more likely to provide safe and adequate water as they may prevent contact with human excreta, for example, this is not always the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018-45">[45]</a></sup> According to a 2014 study, approximately 25% of improved sources contained fecal contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLoS_Bain_2014_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-PLoS_Bain_2014-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The population in Australia, New Zealand, North America and Europe have achieved nearly universal basic drinking water services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 3">: 3 </span></sup>
</p><p>As of 2015, American households use an average of 300 gallons of water a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring">Monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The WHO/<a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Program (JMP) for Water Supply and Sanitation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> is the official United Nations mechanism tasked with monitoring progress towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goal" title="Millennium Development Goal">Millennium Development Goal</a> (MDG) relating to drinking-water and sanitation (MDG 7, Target 7c), which is to: "Halve, by 2015, the proportion of people without sustainable access to safe drinking-water and basic sanitation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Access to safe drinking water is indicated by safe water sources. These improved drinking water sources include household connection, public <a href="/wiki/Standpipe_(street)" title="Standpipe (street)">standpipe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">borehole</a> condition, protected dug well, protected spring, and rain water collection. Sources that do not encourage improved drinking water to the same extent as previously mentioned include: unprotected wells, unprotected springs, rivers or ponds, vender-provided water, bottled water (consequential of limitations in quantity, not quality of water), and tanker truck water. Access to sanitary water comes hand in hand with access to <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">improved sanitation</a> facilities for excreta, such as connection to public sewer, connection to septic system, or a <a href="/wiki/Pit_latrine" title="Pit latrine">pit latrine</a> with a slab or water seal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to this indicator on improved water sources, the MDG was met in 2010, five years ahead of schedule. Over 2 billion more people used improved drinking water sources in 2010 than did in 1990. However, the job is far from finished. 780 million people are still without improved sources of drinking water, and many more people still lack safe drinking water. Estimates suggest that at least 25% of improved sources contain fecal contamination<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLoS_Bain_2014_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-PLoS_Bain_2014-48">[48]</a></sup> and an estimated 1.8 billion people globally use a source of drinking water that suffers from fecal contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The quality of these sources varies over time and often gets worse during the wet season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Continued efforts are needed to reduce urban-rural disparities and inequities associated with poverty; to dramatically increase safe drinking water coverage in countries in sub-Saharan Africa and Oceania; to promote global monitoring of drinking water quality; and to look beyond the MDG target towards universal coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="453" data-file-width="596" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png/220px-Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png/330px-Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png/440px-Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Illustration_of_a_typical_drinking_water_treatment_process.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of a typical tap water treatment process</div></div></div>
<p>A "safely managed drinking water service" is "one located on premises, available when needed and free from contamination".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>The terms 'improved water source' and 'unimproved water source' were coined in 2002 as a drinking water monitoring tool by the JMP of UNICEF and WHO. The term, improved water source refers to "piped water on premises (piped household water connection located inside the user’s dwelling, plot or yard), and other improved drinking water sources (public taps or standpipes, tube wells or boreholes, protected dug wells, protected springs, and rainwater collection)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-ourworldindata_Hannah_2018-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_development">International development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: International development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Expanding <a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a> (Water, Sanitation and Hygiene)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> coverage and monitoring in non-household settings such as schools, healthcare facilities, and work places, is included in <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/WaterAid" title="WaterAid">WaterAid</a> International is a <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organization</a> (NGO) that works on improving the availability of safe drinking water in some the world's poorest countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sanitation_and_Water_for_All" title="Sanitation and Water for All">Sanitation and Water for All</a> is a partnership that brings together national governments, donors, UN agencies, NGOs and other development partners. They work to improve sustainable access to sanitation and water supply to meet and go beyond the MDG target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> In 2014, 77 countries had already met the MDG sanitation target, 29 were on track and, 79 were not on-track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_UNICEF_Progress_2017-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_aspects">Health aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Health aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/WASH#Health_aspects" title="WASH">WASH § Health aspects</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Contaminated water</a> is estimated to result in more than half a million deaths per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2014_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Contaminated water with the lack of sanitation was estimated to cause about one percent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disability_adjusted_life_years" title="Disability adjusted life years">disability adjusted life years</a> worldwide in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> As contaminated water takes its toll on the health of those exposed, the duration of exposure plays a part in the effects of certain diseases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diarrheal_diseases">Diarrheal diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Diarrheal diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 90% of deaths from <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrheal</a> diseases in the developing world today occur in children under five years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whounicef_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-whounicef-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 11">: 11 </span></sup> According to the WHO, the most common diseases linked with poor water quality are cholera, diarrhoea, <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A" title="Hepatitis A">hepatitis A</a>, typhoid, and <a href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">polio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein-energy_malnutrition" title="Protein-energy malnutrition">protein-energy malnutrition</a>, can decrease the children's resistance to infections, including water-related diarrheal diseases. Between 2000 and 2003, 769,000 children under five years old in sub-Saharan Africa died each year from diarrheal diseases. As a result from poor water quality and bad sanitation, an estimated 829,000 people die each year from diarrhoea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-62">[62]</a></sup> Only thirty-six percent of the population in the sub-Saharan region have access to proper means of sanitation. More than 2,000 children's lives are lost every day. In South Asia, 683,000 children under five years old died each year from diarrheal disease from 2000 to 2003. During the same period, in developed countries, 700 children under five years old died from diarrheal disease. Improved water supply reduces diarrhea morbidity by 25% and improvements in drinking water through proper storage in the home and chlorination reduces diarrhea episodes by 39%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whounicef_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-whounicef-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groundwater_pollution">Groundwater pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Groundwater pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_pollution" title="Groundwater pollution">Groundwater pollution</a></div>
<p>Some efforts at increasing the availability of safe drinking water have been disastrous. When the 1980s were declared the "International Decade of Water" by the United Nations, the assumption was made that groundwater is inherently safer than water from rivers, ponds, and canals. While instances of cholera, typhoid and diarrhea were reduced, other problems emerged due to polluted groundwater.
</p><p>Sixty million people are estimated to have been poisoned by well water contaminated by excessive <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, which dissolved from granite rocks. The effects are particularly evident in the bone deformations of children. Similar or larger problems are anticipated in other countries including China, Uzbekistan, and Ethiopia. Although helpful for dental health in low dosage, fluoride in large amounts interferes with bone formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Half of Bangladesh's 12 million <a href="/wiki/Tube_well" title="Tube well">tube wells</a> contain unacceptable levels of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> due to the wells not dug deep enough (past 100 meters). The Bangladeshi government had spent less than US$7 million of the 34 million allocated for solving the problem by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_contamination_of_groundwater" title="Arsenic contamination of groundwater">Natural arsenic poisoning</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_threat" title="Global threat">global threat</a> with 140 million people affected in 70 countries globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> These examples illustrate the need to examine each location on a case-by-case basis and not assume what works in one area will work in another.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water_quality_standards" title="Drinking water quality standards">Drinking water quality standards</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_water_supply_and_sanitation_by_country" title="List of water supply and sanitation by country">List of water supply and sanitation by country</a></div>
<p>Guidelines for the assessment and improvement of service activities relating to drinking water have been published in the form of <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water_quality_standards" title="Drinking water quality standards">drinking water quality standards</a> such as ISO 24510.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> sets legislation on water quality. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directive_2000/60/EC" title="Directive 2000/60/EC">Directive 2000/60/EC</a> of the European Parliament and of the Council of 23 October 2000 establishing a framework for Community action in the field of water policy</i>, known as the water framework directive, is the primary piece of legislation governing water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterframework_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-waterframework-67">[67]</a></sup> This drinking water directive relates specifically to water intended for human consumption. Each member state is responsible for establishing the required policing measures to ensure that the legislation is implemented. For example, in the UK the <a href="/wiki/Water_Supply_(Water_Quality)_Regulations_1989" title="Water Supply (Water Quality) Regulations 1989">Water Quality Regulations</a> prescribe maximum values for substances that affect wholesomeness and the <a href="/wiki/Drinking_Water_Inspectorate" title="Drinking Water Inspectorate">Drinking Water Inspectorate</a> polices the water companies.
</p><p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Public_water_system" title="Public water system">public water systems</a>, defined as systems that serve more than 25 customers or 15 service connections, are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) under the <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> As of 2019 the EPA has issued 88 standards for microorganisms, chemicals and radionuclides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_DW_contaminants_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_DW_contaminants-69">[69]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> regulates bottled water as a food product under the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Food,_Drug,_and_Cosmetic_Act" title="Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act">Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> All public water suppliers in the US must uphold a certain standard of water quality. If the requirements are met, Americans can drink their local tap water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
