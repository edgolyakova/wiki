<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Codified systems and traditions of combat</div>
<p><b>Martial arts</b> are codified systems and traditions of <a href="/wiki/Combat" title="Combat">combat</a> practiced for a number of reasons such as <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a>; <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement" title="Law enforcement">law enforcement</a> applications; <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">competition</a>;  physical, mental, and spiritual development; <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a>; and the preservation of a nation's <a href="/wiki/Intangible_cultural_heritage" title="Intangible cultural heritage">intangible cultural heritage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Paul Bowman, the term <i>martial arts</i> was popularized by mainstream <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> during the 1960s to 1970s, notably by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_martial_arts_films" title="Hong Kong martial arts films">Hong Kong martial arts films</a> (most famously those of <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a>) during the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Chopsocky" title="Chopsocky">chopsocky</a>" wave of the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to John Clements, the term <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/martial_art" title="wikt:martial art">martial arts</a></i> itself is derived from an older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_(language)" title="Latin (language)">Latin</a> term meaning "arts of <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>", the <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman</a> god of war, and was used to refer to the combat systems of Europe (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_martial_arts" title="European martial arts">European martial arts</a>) as early as the 1550s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <b>martial science</b>, or <b>martial sciences</b>, was commonly used to refer to the fighting arts of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_martial_arts" title="Asian martial arts">Asian martial arts</a>) up until the 1970s, while the term <i>Chinese boxing</i> was also used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a> up until then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some authors have argued that <b>fighting arts</b> or <b>fighting systems</b> would be more appropriate terms on the basis that many martial arts were never "martial" in the sense of being used or created by professional <a href="/wiki/Warrior" title="Warrior">warriors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Martial arts may be categorized using a variety of criteria, including:
</p>
<ul><li>Traditional/historical arts vs. contemporary styles: e.g., <a href="/wiki/Folk_wrestling" title="Folk wrestling">folk wrestling</a> compared to modern <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_martial_arts" title="Hybrid martial arts">hybrid martial arts</a>.</li>
<li>Techniques taught: armed vs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unarmed_combat" title="Unarmed combat">unarmed</a>, and within these categories
<ul><li>armed: by type of weapon (<a href="/wiki/Swordsmanship" title="Swordsmanship">swordsmanship</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stick_fighting" title="Stick fighting">stick fighting</a> etc.)</li>
<li>unarmed: by type of combat (<a href="/wiki/Grappling" title="Grappling">grappling</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Strike_(attack)" title="Strike (attack)">striking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_fighting" title="Stand-up fighting">stand-up fighting</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Ground_fighting" title="Ground fighting">ground fighting</a>)</li></ul></li>
<li>By application or intent: <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">combat sport</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choreographed_fight" title="Choreographed fight">choreography</a> or demonstration of forms, <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">physical fitness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, etc.</li>
<li>Within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_tradition" title="Chinese tradition">Chinese tradition</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/External_martial_arts" title="External martial arts">"external"</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Neijia" title="Neijia">"internal"</a> styles</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_technical_focus">By technical focus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By technical focus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unarmed">Unarmed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unarmed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unarmed martial arts can be broadly grouped into those focusing on <a href="/wiki/Strike_(attack)" title="Strike (attack)">strikes</a>, those focusing on <a href="/wiki/Grappling" title="Grappling">grappling</a>, and those that cover both fields, often described as <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_martial_arts" title="Hybrid martial arts">hybrid martial arts</a>.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Strike_(attack)" title="Strike (attack)">Strikes</a></b>
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Punch_(combat)" title="Punch (combat)">Punching</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">Boxing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wing_Chun" title="Wing Chun">Wing Chun</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kick" title="Kick">Kicking</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Kickboxing" title="Kickboxing">Kickboxing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taekwondo" title="Taekwondo">Taekwondo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capoeira" title="Capoeira">Capoeira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savate" title="Savate">Savate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karate" title="Karate">Karate</a></li>
<li>Others using strikes: <a href="/wiki/Lethwei" title="Lethwei">Lethwei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">Muay Thai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kung_fu_(term)" title="Kung fu (term)">Kung Fu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pencak_Silat" title="Pencak Silat">Pencak Silat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalaripayattu" title="Kalaripayattu">Kalaripayattu</a></li></ul>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Grappling" title="Grappling">Grappling</a></b>
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Throw_(grappling)" title="Throw (grappling)">Throwing</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Hapkido" title="Hapkido">Hapkido</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">Judo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumo" title="Sumo">Sumo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">Wrestling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">Aikido</a></li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Armed">Armed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Armed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The traditional martial arts that cover <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_combat" title="Armed combat">armed combat</a> often encompass a wide spectrum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melee_weapons" title="Melee weapons">melee weapons</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bladed_weapon" title="Bladed weapon">bladed weapons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polearms" title="Polearms">polearms</a>. Such traditions include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eskrima" title="Eskrima">eskrima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silat" title="Silat">silat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalaripayat" title="Kalaripayat">kalaripayat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawan_kobudo" title="Okinawan kobudo">kobudo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Historical_European_martial_arts" title="Historical European martial arts">historical European martial arts</a>, especially those of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a> also feature weapons as part of their curriculum.
</p><p>Sometimes, training with one specific weapon may be considered a style in its own right, especially in the case of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_martial_arts" title="Japanese martial arts">Japanese martial arts</a>, with disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">kenjutsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kendo" title="Kendo">kendo</a> (sword), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bojutsu" title="Bojutsu">bojutsu</a> (staff), and <a href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABd%C5%8D" title="Kyūdō">kyūdō</a> (archery). Similarly, modern martial arts and sports include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_fencing" title="Modern fencing">modern fencing</a>, stick-fighting systems like <a href="/wiki/Canne_de_combat" title="Canne de combat">canne de combat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modern_competitive_archery" title="Modern competitive archery">modern competitive archery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Practical_shooting" title="Practical shooting">practical shooting</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_application_or_intent">By application or intent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: By application or intent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat-oriented">Combat-oriented</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Combat-oriented">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">Combat sport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">Self-defense</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Health-oriented">Health-oriented</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Health-oriented">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many martial arts, especially those from Asia, also teach side disciplines which pertain to medicinal practices. This is particularly prevalent in traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_martial_arts" title="Asian martial arts">Asian martial arts</a> which may teach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone-setting" title="Bone-setting">bone-setting</a>, herbalism, and other aspects of traditional medicine.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spirituality-oriented"><span class="anchor" id="Spirituality"></span>Spirituality-oriented</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spirituality-oriented">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Martial arts can also be linked with religion and spirituality. Numerous systems are reputed to have been founded, disseminated, or practiced by monks or nuns.
</p><p>Throughout the Asian arts, meditation may be incorporated as a part of training. In the arts influenced by a mix of <a href="/wiki/Chan_Buddhism" title="Chan Buddhism">Chan Buddhist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> philosophy, the practice itself may be used as an aid to attaining mindfulness.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> styles, when concerning non-physical qualities of the combat, are often strongly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> Buddhist philosophy. Concepts like "<a href="/wiki/Mushin_(mental_state)" title="Mushin (mental state)">empty mind</a>" and "beginner's mind" are recurrent. <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">Aikido</a> practitioners for instance, can have a strong philosophical belief of the flow of energy and peace fostering, as idealised by the art's founder <a href="/wiki/Morihei_Ueshiba" title="Morihei Ueshiba">Morihei Ueshiba</a>.
</p><p>Traditional Korean martial arts place emphasis on the development of the practitioner's spiritual and philosophical development. A common theme in most Korean styles, such as <a href="/wiki/Taekkyeon" title="Taekkyeon">Taekkyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taekwondo" title="Taekwondo">taekwondo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hapkido" title="Hapkido">Hapkido</a> is the value of "inner peace" in a practitioner, which is stressed to be only achievable through individual meditation and training. The Koreans believe that the use of physical force is only justifiable for self defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Systema" title="Systema">Systema</a> draws upon breathing and relaxation techniques, as well as elements of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox</a> thought, to foster self-conscience and calmness, and to benefit the practitioner in different levels: the physical, the psychological and the spiritual.
</p><p>Some martial arts in various cultures can be performed in dance-like settings for various reasons, such as for evoking ferocity in preparation for battle or showing off skill in a more stylized manner, with capoeira being the most prominent example. Many such martial arts incorporate music, especially strong percussive rhythms (see also <a href="/wiki/War_dance" title="War dance">war dance</a>).
</p><p><b>Pahlevani and zourkhaneh rituals</b> is the name of a Persian Martial arts inscribed by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> for <b>varzesh-e pahlavāni</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa">آیین پهلوانی و زورخانه‌ای</span>, "heroic sport")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or <b>varzesh-e bāstāni</b> (<span title="Persian-language text"><span dir="rtl" lang="fa">ورزش باستانی</span></span>; <i>varzeš-e bāstānī</i>, "ancient sport"), a traditional system of athletics originally used to train warriors in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> (<a href="/wiki/Name_of_Iran" title="Name of Iran">Persia</a>), and first appearing under this name and form in the Safavid era, with similarities to systems in adjacent lands under other names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Human warfare dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Epipalaeolithic" title="Epipalaeolithic">Epipalaeolithic</a> to early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> era. The oldest works of art depicting scenes of battle are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_paintings" title="Cave paintings">cave paintings</a> from eastern Spain (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Levante" title="Spanish Levante">Spanish Levante</a>) dated between 10,000 and 6,000 BCE that show organized groups fighting with bows and arrows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamblin_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Similar evidence of warfare has been found in Epipalaeolithic to early Neolithic era <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_burial" title="Mass burial">mass burials</a>, excavated in Germany and at <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Sahaba" title="Jebel Sahaba">Jebel Sahaba</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Sudan" title="Northern Sudan">Northern Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamblin_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">Wrestling</a> is the oldest <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">combat sport</a>, with origins in <a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">hand-to-hand combat</a>. <a href="/wiki/Belt_wrestling" title="Belt wrestling">Belt wrestling</a> was depicted in works of art from <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 3000 BC</span>, and later in the <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_literature" title="Sumerian literature">Sumerian</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The earliest known depiction of <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a> comes from a <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> relief in Mesopotamia (modern Iraq) from the 3rd millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncyclopaediaBritannicaEntry_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EncyclopaediaBritannicaEntry-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leitai.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Leitai.jpg/150px-Leitai.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Leitai.jpg/225px-Leitai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Leitai.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leitai.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_artist" title="Chinese martial artist">Chinese martial artist</a> preparing to throw his opponent during a <a href="/wiki/Lei_tai" title="Lei tai">lei tai</a> contest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">Ancient China</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The foundation of modern <a href="/wiki/List_of_martial_arts#East_Asia" title="List of martial arts">East Asian martial arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_martial_arts#South_Asia" title="List of martial arts">South Asian martial arts</a> is likely facilitated by cultural exchanges of early <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_martial_arts" title="Indian martial arts">Indian martial arts</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a> of <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">Chinese history</a> (480–221 BC) extensive development in martial philosophy and strategy emerged, as described by <a href="/wiki/Sun_Tzu" title="Sun Tzu">Sun Tzu</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_War" title="The Art of War">The Art of War</a></i> (c. 350 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Legendary accounts link the origin of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaolinquan" title="Shaolinquan">Shaolinquan</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spread_of_Buddhism" title="Spread of Buddhism">spread of Buddhism</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_India" title="Ancient India">ancient India</a> during the early 5th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_era" title="Common era">CE</a>, with the figure of <a href="/wiki/Bodhidharma" title="Bodhidharma">Bodhidharma</a>, to China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Written evidence of martial arts in Southern India dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a> of about the 2nd century BCE to the 2nd century AD.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The combat techniques of the <a href="/wiki/Sangam_period" title="Sangam period">Sangam period</a> were the earliest precursors to <a href="/wiki/Kalaripayattu" title="Kalaripayattu">Kalaripayattu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2616" data-file-width="2907" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg/200px-Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg/300px-Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg/400px-Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pankration_Met_16.71.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pankration" title="Pankration">Pankratiasts</a> fighting under the eyes of a judge. Side B of a <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_Games" title="Panathenaic Games">Panathenaic</a> prize <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_amphora" title="Panathenaic amphora">amphora</a>, c. 500 BC.</div></div></div>
<p>In Europe, the earliest sources of martial arts traditions date to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Boxing" title="Ancient Greek Boxing">Boxing</a> (<i>pygme</i>, <i>pyx</i>), <a href="/wiki/Greek_wrestling" title="Greek wrestling">wrestling</a> (<i>pale</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Pankration" title="Pankration">pankration</a>  were represented in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Olympic_Games" title="Ancient Olympic Games">Ancient Olympic Games</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> produced <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiatorial combat</a> as a public spectacle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_manual" title="Combat manual">combat manuals</a> have survived from the European <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. This includes such styles as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_fencing" title="Historical fencing">sword and shield</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-handed_swordfighting" title="Two-handed swordfighting">two-handed swordfighting</a> and other types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melee_weapons" title="Melee weapons">melee weapons</a> besides unarmed combat. Amongst these are transcriptions of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Liechtenauer" title="Johannes Liechtenauer">Johannes Liechtenauer</a>'s mnemonic poem on the longsword dating back to the late fourteenth century. Likewise, Asian martial arts became well-documented during the medieval period, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_martial_arts" title="Japanese martial arts">Japanese martial arts</a> beginning with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> nobility in the 12th century, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming era</a> treatises such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ji_Xiao_Xin_Shu" title="Ji Xiao Xin Shu">Ji Xiao Xin Shu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_martial_arts" title="Indian martial arts">Indian martial arts</a> in medieval texts such as the <a href="/wiki/Agni_Purana" title="Agni Purana">Agni Purana</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malla_Purana" title="Malla Purana">Malla Purana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Korean_martial_arts" title="Korean martial arts">Korean martial arts</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> era and texts such as <a href="/wiki/Muyejebo" title="Muyejebo">Muyejebo</a> (1598).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>European swordsmanship always had a sportive component, but the duel was always a possibility until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Modern <a href="/wiki/Fencing" title="Fencing">sport fencing</a> began developing during the 19th century as the French and Italian military academies began codifying instruction. The Olympic games led to standard international rules, with the Féderation Internationale d'Escrime founded in 1913. Modern boxing originates with <a href="/wiki/Jack_Broughton" title="Jack Broughton">Jack Broughton</a>'s rules in the 18th century, and reaches its present form with the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Queensberry_Rules" title="Marquess of Queensberry Rules">Marquess of Queensberry Rules</a> of 1867.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_styles">Folk styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Folk styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Folk_wrestling" title="Folk wrestling">Folk wrestling</a></div>
<p>Certain traditional combat sports and fighting styles exist all over the world, rooted in local culture and folklore. The most common of these are styles of <a href="/wiki/Folk_wrestling" title="Folk wrestling">folk wrestling</a>, some of which have been practiced since antiquity and are found in the most remote areas. Other examples include forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stick_fighting" title="Stick fighting">stick fighting</a> and boxing. While these arts are based on historical traditions of folklore, they are not "historical" in the sense that they reconstruct or preserve a historical system from a specific era. They are rather contemporary regional sports that coexist with the modern forms of martial arts sports as they have developed since the 19th century, often including cross-fertilization between sports and folk styles; thus, the traditional Thai art of <a href="/wiki/Muay_boran" title="Muay boran">muay boran</a> developed into the modern national sport of <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">muay Thai</a>, which in turn came to be practiced worldwide and contributed significantly to modern hybrid styles like <a href="/wiki/Kickboxing" title="Kickboxing">kickboxing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mixed_martial_arts" title="Mixed martial arts">mixed martial arts</a>. <a href="/wiki/Singlestick" title="Singlestick">Singlestick</a>, an English martial art can be seen often utilized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_dancing" title="Morris dancing">morris dancing</a>. Many European dances share elements of martial arts with examples including Ukrainian <a href="/wiki/Hopak" title="Hopak">Hopak</a>, Polish Zbójnicki (use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciupaga" title="Ciupaga">ciupaga</a>), the Czech dance <a href="/wiki/Odzemek" title="Odzemek">odzemek</a>, and the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Halling_(dance)" title="Halling (dance)">Halling</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Modern_history_of_East_Asian_martial_arts" title="Modern history of East Asian martial arts">Modern history of East Asian martial arts</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_19th_to_early_20th_century">Late 19th to early 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Late 19th to early 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The mid to late 19th century marks the beginning of the history of martial arts as modern sports developed out of earlier traditional fighting systems. In Europe, this concerns the developments of <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fencing" title="Fencing">fencing</a> as sports. In Japan, the same period marks the formation of the modern forms of <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jujutsu" title="Jujutsu">jujutsu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karate" title="Karate">karate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kendo" title="Kendo">kendo</a> (among others) based on revivals of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koryu" title="Koryu">old schools</a> of <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> martial arts which had been suppressed during the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Modern <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">muay Thai</a> rules date to the 1920s. In China, the modern history of martial arts begins in the <a href="/wiki/Nanjing_decade" title="Nanjing decade">Nanjing decade</a> (1930s) following the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Guoshu_Institute" title="Central Guoshu Institute">Central Guoshu Institute</a> in 1928 under the <a href="/wiki/Kuomintang" title="Kuomintang">Kuomintang</a> government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Western interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_martial_arts" title="Asian martial arts">Asian martial arts</a> arises towards the end of the 19th century, due to the increase in trade between the United States with China and Japan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Relatively few Westerners actually practiced the arts, considering it to be mere performance. <a href="/wiki/Edward_William_Barton-Wright" title="Edward William Barton-Wright">Edward William Barton-Wright</a>, a railway engineer who had studied <a href="/wiki/Jujutsu" title="Jujutsu">jujutsu</a> while working in Japan between 1894 and 1897, was the first man known to have taught Asian martial arts in Europe. He also founded an eclectic style named <a href="/wiki/Bartitsu" title="Bartitsu">Bartitsu</a> which combined jujutsu, judo, wrestling, boxing, <a href="/wiki/Savate" title="Savate">savate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stick_fighting" title="Stick fighting">stick fighting</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Fencing and <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_wrestling" title="Greco-Roman wrestling">Greco-Roman wrestling</a> was included in the <a href="/wiki/1896_Summer_Olympics" title="1896 Summer Olympics">1896 Summer Olympics</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FILA_Wrestling_World_Championships" title="FILA Wrestling World Championships">FILA Wrestling World Championships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boxing_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Boxing at the Summer Olympics">Boxing at the Summer Olympics</a> were introduced in 1904. The tradition of awarding <a href="/wiki/Championship_belt" title="Championship belt">championship belts</a> in wrestling and boxing can be traced to the <a href="/wiki/Lonsdale_Belt" title="Lonsdale Belt">Lonsdale Belt</a>, introduced in 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="20th_century_.281914_to_1989.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_(1914_to_1989)">20th century (1914 to 1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 20th century (1914 to 1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2021</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="276" data-file-width="450" decoding="async" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg/220px-The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg/330px-The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg/440px-The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_age_of_18_Bruce_Lee_and_Ye_Wen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a> (right) and his teacher <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Ip_Man" title="Ip Man">Ip Man</a></span> (left).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="604" data-file-width="434" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg/150px-Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg/225px-Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg/300px-Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jackie_Chan_Cannes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jackie_Chan" title="Jackie Chan">Jackie Chan</a>, one of the best known actors and martial artists.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Boxing_Association_(amateur)" title="International Boxing Association (amateur)">International Boxing Association</a> was established in 1920. <a href="/wiki/World_Fencing_Championships" title="World Fencing Championships">World Fencing Championships</a> have been held since 1921.
</p><p>As Western influence grew in Asia a greater number of military personnel spent time in China, Japan and South Korea during World War II and the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and were exposed to local fighting styles. Jujutsu, judo and karate first became popular among the mainstream from the 1950s–1960s. Due in part to Asian and Hollywood <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martial_arts_movies" title="Martial arts movies">martial arts movies</a>, most modern American martial arts are either Asian-derived or Asian influenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The term <a href="/wiki/Kickboxing" title="Kickboxing">kickboxing</a> (キックボクシング) was created by the Japanese boxing promoter Osamu Noguchi for a variant of muay Thai and karate that he created in the 1950s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_kickboxing" title="American kickboxing">American kickboxing</a> was developed in the 1970s, as a combination of boxing and karate. <a href="/wiki/Taekwondo" title="Taekwondo">Taekwondo</a> was developed in the context of the Korean War in the 1950s.
</p><p>The later 1960s and 1970s witnessed an increased media interest in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a>, influenced by martial artist <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a>. Bruce Lee is credited as one of the first instructors to openly teach Chinese martial arts to Westerners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-absolutedefense.net_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-absolutedefense.net-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/World_Judo_Championships" title="World Judo Championships">World Judo Championships</a> have been held since 1956, <a href="/wiki/Judo_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Judo at the Summer Olympics">Judo at the Summer Olympics</a> was introduced in 1964. <a href="/wiki/Karate_World_Championships" title="Karate World Championships">Karate World Championships</a> were introduced in 1970.
</p><p>The "<a href="/wiki/Chopsocky" title="Chopsocky">kung fu wave</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_action_cinema" title="Hong Kong action cinema">Hong Kong action cinema</a> in the 1970s, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee_films" title="Bruce Lee films">Bruce Lee films</a>, popularized martial arts in global <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>. A number of mainstream films produced during the 1980s also contributed significantly to the perception of martial arts in Western popular culture. These include <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Karate_Kid_(1984_film)" title="The Karate Kid (1984 film)">The Karate Kid</a></i> (1984) and <i><a href="/wiki/Bloodsport_(film)" title="Bloodsport (film)">Bloodsport</a></i> (1988). This era produced some <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood</a> action stars with martial arts background, such as <a href="/wiki/Jean-Claude_Van_Damme" title="Jean-Claude Van Damme">Jean-Claude Van Damme</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Norris" title="Chuck Norris">Chuck Norris</a>.
</p><p>Also during the 20th century, a number of martial arts were adapted for <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a> purposes for military <a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">hand-to-hand combat</a>. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II_combatives" title="World War II combatives">World War II combatives</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KAPAP" title="KAPAP">KAPAP</a> (1930s) and <a href="/wiki/Krav_Maga" title="Krav Maga">Krav Maga</a> (1950s) in Israel, <a href="/wiki/Systema" title="Systema">Systema</a> in Soviet-era Russia, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanshou" title="Sanshou">Sanshou</a> in the People's Republic of China are examples of such systems. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_military" title="US military">US military</a> de-emphasized hand-to-hand combat training during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_War_period" title="Cold War period">Cold War period</a>, but revived it with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/LINE_(combat_system)" title="LINE (combat system)">LINE</a> in 1989.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_to_present">1990 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 1990 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1993, the first <a href="/wiki/Pancrase" title="Pancrase">Pancrase</a> event was held in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloodyelbow_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloodyelbow-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/K-1" title="K-1">K-1</a> rules of kickboxing were introduced, based on 1980s <a href="/wiki/Seidokaikan" title="Seidokaikan">Seidokaikan</a> karate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_jiu-jitsu" title="Brazilian jiu-jitsu">Brazilian jiu-jitsu</a> became popular and proved to be effective in <a href="/wiki/Mixed_martial_arts" title="Mixed martial arts">mixed martial arts</a> (MMA) competitions such as the <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_Fighting_Championship" title="Ultimate Fighting Championship">UFC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pride_Fighting_Championships" title="Pride Fighting Championships">PRIDE</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNCstyles_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNCstyles-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jackie_Chan" title="Jackie Chan">Jackie Chan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jet_Li" title="Jet Li">Jet Li</a> are prominent martial artists who have become major movie figures. Their popularity and media presence has been at the forefront for promoting Chinese martial arts since the late 20th and early 21st centuries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>With the continual discovery of more medieval and Renaissance fighting manuals, the practice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_European_Martial_Arts" title="Historical European Martial Arts">Historical European Martial Arts</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Martial_Arts" title="Western Martial Arts">Western Martial Arts</a> have been growing<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in popularity across the United States and Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>On 29 November 2011, <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> inscribed <a href="/wiki/Taekkyeon" title="Taekkyeon">Taekkyon</a> onto its Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity List.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many styles of <a href="/wiki/Indian_martial_arts" title="Indian martial arts">Indian martial arts</a> were banned by the colonial authorities during the period of <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British rule in India</a>, which led to a decline in their popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Some, such as <a href="/wiki/Kalaripayattu" title="Kalaripayattu">Kalaripayattu</a>, did not undergo such declines since they were mostly practised in areas of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princely_states" title="Princely states">outside direct British control</a>. Other Indian martial art, such as <a href="/wiki/Silambam" title="Silambam">Silambam</a>, while not widely practiced in India, continue to be practiced in other countries in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">Indian cultural sphere</a> such as Indonesia and Malaysia. Many other Indian martial arts such as <a href="/wiki/Mardani_khel" title="Mardani khel">Mardhani Khel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paika_akhada" title="Paika akhada">Paika Akhada</a> survived by practitioners practicing the art in secret, or by telling the colonial authorities that it was a form of dance. While many regional Indian martial arts forms are fading into obscurity, martial arts such as <a href="/wiki/Gatka" title="Gatka">Gatka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalaripayattu" title="Kalaripayattu">Kalaripayattu</a> are experiencing a gradual resurgence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_and_competition">Testing and competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Testing and competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Testing or evaluation is important to martial artists of many disciplines who wish to determine their progression or own level of skill in specific contexts. Students often undergo periodic testing and grading by their own teacher in order to advance to a higher level of recognized achievement, such as a different <a href="/wiki/Belt_(clothing)" title="Belt (clothing)">belt</a> color or title. The type of testing used varies from system to system but may include forms or <a href="/wiki/Sparring" title="Sparring">sparring</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1290" data-file-width="1479" decoding="async" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg/220px-Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg/330px-Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg/440px-Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steven_Ho_Martial_Arts_Kick.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Steven_Ho_(martial_artist)" title="Steven Ho (martial artist)">Steven Ho</a> executing a Jump Spin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hook_Kick" title="Hook Kick">Hook Kick</a></div></div></div>
<p>Various forms and sparring are commonly used in martial art exhibitions and tournaments. Some competitions pit practitioners of different disciplines against each other using a common set of rules, these are referred to as mixed martial arts competitions. Rules for sparring vary between art and organization but can generally be divided into <i>light-contact</i>, <i>medium-contact</i>, and <i>full-contact</i> variants, reflecting the amount of force that should be used on an opponent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light-_and_medium-contact">Light- and medium-contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Light- and medium-contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These types of sparring restrict the amount of force that may be used to hit an opponent, in the case of light sparring this is usually to 'touch' contact, e.g. a punch should be 'pulled' as soon as or before contact is made. In medium-contact (sometimes referred to as semi-contact) the punch would not be 'pulled' but not hit with full force. As the amount of force used is restricted, the aim of these types of sparring is not to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knock_out" title="Knock out">knock out</a> an opponent; a point system is used in competitions.
</p><p>A referee acts to monitor for fouls and to control the match, while judges mark down scores, as in boxing. Particular targets may be prohibited, certain techniques may be forbidden (such as headbutting or groin hits), and fighters may be required to wear <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">protective equipment</a> on their head, hands, chest, groin, shins or feet. Some grappling arts, such as aikido, use a similar method of compliant training that is equivalent to light or medium contact.
</p><p>In some styles (such as fencing and some styles of taekwondo sparring), competitors score points based on the landing of a single technique or strike as judged by the referee, whereupon the referee will briefly stop the match, award a point, then restart the match. Alternatively, sparring may continue with the point noted by the judges. Some critics of point sparring feel that this method of training teaches habits that result in lower combat effectiveness. Lighter-contact sparring may be used exclusively, for children or in other situations when heavy contact would be inappropriate (such as beginners), medium-contact sparring is often used as training for full contact.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full-contact">Full-contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Full-contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-contact" title="Full-contact">Full-contact</a></div>
<p>Full-contact sparring or competition, where strikes or techniques are not pulled but used with full force as the name implies, has a number of tactical differences from light and medium-contact sparring. It is considered by some to be requisite in learning realistic unarmed combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SB_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-SB-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In full-contact sparring, the aim of a competitive match is to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knock_out" title="Knock out">knock out</a> the opponent or to force the opponent to <a href="/wiki/Submission_wrestling" title="Submission wrestling">submit</a>.
Where scoring takes place it may be a subsidiary measure, only used if no clear winner has been established by other means; in some competitions, such as the <a href="/wiki/UFC_1" title="UFC 1">UFC 1</a>, there was no scoring, though most now use some form of judging as a backup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UFC1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-UFC1-28">[28]</a></sup> Due to these factors, full-contact matches tend to be more aggressive in character, but rule sets may still mandate the use of protective equipment, or limit the techniques allowed.
</p><p>Nearly all mixed martial arts organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_Fighting_Championship" title="Ultimate Fighting Championship">UFC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancrase" title="Pancrase">Pancrase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shooto" title="Shooto">Shooto</a> use a form of full-contact rules as do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_Boxing" title="Professional Boxing">professional boxing</a> organizations and <a href="/wiki/K-1" title="K-1">K-1</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kyokushin" title="Kyokushin">Kyokushin</a> <a href="/wiki/Karate" title="Karate">karate</a> requires advanced practitioners to engage in bare-knuckled, full-contact sparring allowing kicks, knees and punching although punching to the head is disallowed while wearing only a karate <i>gi</i> and groin protector. Brazilian jiu-jitsu and judo matches do not allow striking, but are full-contact in the sense that full force is applied in the permitted grappling and submission techniques. Competitions held by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Taekwondo_Federation#Sparring" title="World Taekwondo Federation">World Taekwondo</a> requires the use of <a href="/wiki/Headgear_(martial_arts)" title="Headgear (martial arts)">Headgear</a> and padded vest, but are full contact in the sense that full force is applied to strikes to the head and body, and win by <a href="/wiki/Knockout" title="Knockout">knockout</a> is possible.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Martial_sport">Martial sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Martial sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">Combat sport</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2634" data-file-width="1989" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg/170px-050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg/255px-050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg/340px-050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:050907-M-7747B-002-Judo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Several martial arts, such as <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Olympic_sports" title="Olympic sports">Olympic sports</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Martial arts have crossed over into sports when forms of <a href="/wiki/Sparring" title="Sparring">sparring</a> become competitive, becoming a sport in its own right that is dissociated from the original combative origin, such as with western fencing. The <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympic_Games" title="Summer Olympic Games">Summer Olympic Games</a> includes judo, taekwondo, western archery, boxing, javelin, wrestling and fencing as events, while <a href="/wiki/Wushu_(sport)" title="Wushu (sport)">Chinese wushu</a> recently failed in its bid to be included, but is still actively performed in tournaments across the world. Practitioners in some arts such as <a href="/wiki/Kickboxing" title="Kickboxing">kickboxing</a> and Brazilian jiu-jitsu often train for sport matches, whereas those in other arts such as <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">aikido</a> generally spurn such competitions. Some schools believe that competition breeds better and more efficient practitioners, and gives a sense of good sportsmanship. Others believe that the rules under which competition takes place have diminished the combat effectiveness of martial arts or encourage a kind of practice which focuses on winning trophies rather than a focus such as cultivating a particular moral character.
</p><p>The question of "which is the best martial art" has led to inter style competitions fought with very few rules allowing a variety of fighting styles to enter with few limitations. This was the origin of the first <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_Fighting_Championship" title="Ultimate Fighting Championship">Ultimate Fighting Championship</a> tournament (later renamed <a href="/wiki/UFC_1" title="UFC 1">UFC 1: The Beginning</a>) in the USA inspired by the Brazilian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vale_tudo" title="Vale tudo">Vale tudo</a> tradition and along with other minimal rule competitions, most notably those from Japan such as <a href="/wiki/Shooto" title="Shooto">Shooto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pancrase" title="Pancrase">Pancrase</a>, have evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">combat sport</a> of Mixed Martial Arts (MMA).
</p><p>Some martial artists compete in non-sparring competitions such as <a href="/wiki/Breaking_(martial_arts)" title="Breaking (martial arts)">breaking</a> or choreographed routines of techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poomse" title="Poomse">poomse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kata" title="Kata">kata</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aka_(burmese)" title="Aka (burmese)">aka</a>, or modern variations of the martial arts which include dance-influenced competitions such as tricking. Martial traditions have been influenced by governments to become more sport-like for political purposes; the central impetus for the attempt by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> in transforming Chinese martial arts into the committee-regulated sport of <a href="/wiki/Wushu_(sport)" title="Wushu (sport)">wushu</a> was suppressing what they saw as the potentially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subversion_(politics)" title="Subversion (politics)">subversive</a> aspects of martial training, especially under the traditional system of family lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mastering_Yang_Style_Taijiquan_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mastering_Yang_Style_Taijiquan-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_fitness_benefits">Health and fitness benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Health and fitness benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martial arts training aims to result in several benefits to trainees, such as their physical, mental, emotional and spiritual health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Through systematic practice in the martial arts a person's <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">physical fitness</a> may be boosted (strength, stamina, speed, flexibility, movement coordination, etc.) as the whole body is exercised and the entire muscular system is activated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
Beyond contributing to physical fitness, martial arts training also has benefits for <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a>, contributing to <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self-control" title="Self-control">self-control</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emotional" title="Emotional">emotional</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a> well-being. For this reason, a number of martial arts schools have focused purely on therapeutic aspects, de-emphasizing the historical aspect of self-defense or combat completely.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a>, martial arts also have the nature of an art, since there is emotional communication and complete emotional expression.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Self-defense.2C_military_and_law_enforcement_applications"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-defense,_military_and_law_enforcement_applications">Self-defense, military and law enforcement applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Self-defense, military and law enforcement applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">Hand-to-hand combat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">Self-defense</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="979" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg/150px-ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg/225px-ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg/300px-ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ArmyMilCombativesChokehold.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> combatives instructor demonstrates a <a href="/wiki/Chokehold" title="Chokehold">chokehold</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Some traditional martial concepts have seen new use within modern military training. Perhaps the most recent example of this is <a href="/wiki/Point_shooting" title="Point shooting">point shooting</a> which relies on <a href="/wiki/Muscle_memory" title="Muscle memory">muscle memory</a> to more effectively utilize a <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearm</a> in a variety of awkward situations, much the way an <a href="/wiki/Iaido" title="Iaido">iaidoka</a> would master movements with their sword.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504,_Berlin,_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="570" decoding="async" height="309" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504%2C_Berlin%2C_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504%2C_Berlin%2C_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504%2C_Berlin%2C_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504%2C_Berlin%2C_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504%2C_Berlin%2C_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504%2C_Berlin%2C_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-00504,_Berlin,_Turn-_und_Sportwoche_im_Lustgarten.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Demonstration of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ju-Jitsu" title="Ju-Jitsu">Ju-Jitsu</a> defense against a <a href="/wiki/Knife_fight" title="Knife fight">knife attack</a>. <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> 1924</div></div></div>
<p>During the World War II era <a href="/wiki/William_E._Fairbairn" title="William E. Fairbairn">William E. Fairbairn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_A._Sykes" title="Eric A. Sykes">Eric A. Sykes</a> were recruited by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">Special Operations Executive</a> (SOE) to teach their martial art of <a href="/wiki/Defendu" title="Defendu">Defendu</a> (itself drawing on Western boxing and Jujutsu) and pistol shooting to UK, US, and Canadian special forces. The book <i>Kill or Get Killed</i>, written by Colonel <a href="/wiki/Rex_Applegate" title="Rex Applegate">Rex Applegate</a>, was based on the Defendu taught by Sykes and Fairbairn. Both Fairbairn's <i>Get Tough</i> and Appelgate's <i>Kill or Get Killed</i> became classic works on hand-to-hand combat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Traditional hand-to-hand, knife, and spear techniques continue to see use in the composite systems developed for today's wars. Examples of this include European <a href="/wiki/Unifight" title="Unifight">Unifight</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army" title="US Army">US Army</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Combatives" title="Combatives">Combatives</a> system developed by <a href="/wiki/Matt_Larsen" title="Matt Larsen">Matt Larsen</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israeli_army" title="Israeli army">Israeli army</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KAPAP" title="KAPAP">KAPAP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krav_Maga" title="Krav Maga">Krav Maga</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marine_Corps" title="US Marine Corps">US Marine Corps</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Martial_Arts_Program" title="Marine Corps Martial Arts Program">Marine Corps Martial Arts Program</a></i> (MCMAP). Unarmed dagger defenses identical to those found in the manual of <a href="/wiki/Fiore_dei_Liberi" title="Fiore dei Liberi">Fiore dei Liberi</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Codex_Wallerstein" title="Codex Wallerstein">Codex Wallerstein</a> were integrated into the U.S. Army's <a href="/wiki/Training_manual" title="Training manual">training manuals</a> in 1942<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
and continue to influence today's systems along with other traditional systems such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eskrima" title="Eskrima">eskrima</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silat" title="Silat">silat</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The rifle-mounted <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonet</a> which has its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spear</a>, has seen use by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> as recently as the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many martial arts are also seen and used in Law Enforcement hand-to-hand training. For example, the Tokyo Riot Police's use of <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">aikido</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Twigger1997_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Twigger1997-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Martial_arts_industry">Martial arts industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Martial arts industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martial arts since the 1970s has become a significant industry, a subset of the wider <a href="/wiki/Sport_industry" title="Sport industry">sport industry</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martial_arts_films" title="Martial arts films">cinema</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports_television" title="Sports television">sports television</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Hundreds of millions of people worldwide practice some form of martial art.
Web Japan (sponsored by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs" title="Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs">Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a>) claims there are 50 million karate practitioners worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
The South Korean government in 2009 published an estimate that taekwondo is practiced by 70 million people in 190 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kim2009_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kim2009-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The wholesale value of martial arts related sporting equipment shipped in the United States was estimated at US$314 million in 2007; participation in the same year was estimated at 6.9 million (ages 6 or older, 2% of US population).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> R. A. Court, CEO of Martial Arts Channel, stated the total revenue of the US martial arts industry at US$40 billion and the number of US practitioners at 30 million in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Martial arts equipment can include that which is used for conditioning, protection and <a href="/wiki/List_of_martial_arts_weapons" title="List of martial arts weapons">weapons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hojo_und%C5%8D" title="Hojo undō">Specialized conditioning equipment</a> can include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breaking_board" title="Breaking board">breaking boards</a>, dummy partners such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muk_Yan_Jong" title="Muk Yan Jong">wooden dummy</a>, and targets such as <a href="/wiki/Punching_bag" title="Punching bag">punching bags</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Makiwara" title="Makiwara">makiwara</a>. <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">Protective equipment</a> for sparring and competition includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boxing_gloves" title="Boxing gloves">boxing gloves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Headgear_(martial_arts)" title="Headgear (martial arts)">headgear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mouthguard" title="Mouthguard">mouthguards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Martial_arts_fraud">Martial arts fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martial_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Martial arts fraud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="anchor" id="martial_arts_fraud"></span>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_martial_arts" title="Asian martial arts">Asian martial arts</a> experienced a surge of popularity in the West during the 1970s, and the rising demand resulted in numerous low quality or fraudulent schools. Fueled by fictional depictions in martial arts movies, this led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninja_craze" title="Ninja craze">ninja craze</a> of the 1980s in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> There were also numerous fraudulent ads for martial arts training programs, inserted into comic books circa the 1960s and 1970s, which were read primarily by adolescent boys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the seventies, lower ranks (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyu" title="Kyu">kyu</a>) began to be given colorful belts to show progress. This proved to be commercially viable and colored-belt systems were adopted in many martial arts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degree_mill" title="Degree mill">degree mills</a> (also known as <i>McDojos</i> and <i>belt factories</i>) as a means to generate additional cash. This was covered in the <i><a href="/wiki/Penn_%26_Teller:_Bullshit!" title="Penn &amp; Teller: Bullshit!">Penn &amp; Teller: Bullshit!</a></i> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Penn_%26_Teller:_Bullshit!_episodes#Season_8:_2010" title="List of Penn &amp; Teller: Bullshit! episodes">episode "Martial Arts"</a> (June 2010).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
