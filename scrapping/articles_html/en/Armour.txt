<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Armour</span></h1>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Covering used to protect from physical injury or damage</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Armour_(disambiguation)" title="Armour (disambiguation)">Armour (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_(41985701161).jpg"><img alt="Torso-covering mail armour on a black mannequin" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6000" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_%2841985701161%29.jpg/220px-Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_%2841985701161%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_%2841985701161%29.jpg/330px-Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_%2841985701161%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_%2841985701161%29.jpg/440px-Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_%2841985701161%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Xixia_Chainmail_Armor_(41985701161).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Western_Xia" title="Western Xia">Western Xia</a> mail armour</div></div></div>
<p><b>Armour</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) or <b>armor</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>; see <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-our,_-or" title="American and British English spelling differences">spelling differences</a>) is a covering used to protect an object, individual, or vehicle from physical injury or damage, especially direct contact <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a> during <a href="/wiki/Combat" title="Combat">combat</a>, or from a potentially dangerous environment or activity (e.g. cycling, construction sites, etc.).  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_armour" title="Personal armour">Personal armour</a> is used to protect soldiers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_animal" title="War animal">war animals</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_armour" title="Vehicle armour">Vehicle armour</a> is used on <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">armoured fighting vehicles</a>, and some mostly ground attack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_aircraft" title="Combat aircraft">combat aircraft</a>.
</p><p>A second use of the term <i>armour</i> describes <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)#Armoured_division" title="Division (military)">armoured forces</a>, <a href="#Armoured_fighting_vehicles">armoured weapons</a>, and their role in combat. After the development of <a href="/wiki/Armoured_warfare" title="Armoured warfare">armoured warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tanks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanised_infantry" title="Mechanised infantry">mechanised infantry</a> and their combat formations came to be referred to collectively as "armour".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_bordone_016.jpg"><img alt="An oil painting depicts a partially-armoured man who is assisted by two boys, one of whom is tying on some armour onto his arm while the other holds his helmet. A group of soldiers are amassed in the background." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4452" data-file-width="6050" decoding="async" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Paris_bordone_016.jpg/220px-Paris_bordone_016.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Paris_bordone_016.jpg/330px-Paris_bordone_016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Paris_bordone_016.jpg/440px-Paris_bordone_016.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paris_bordone_016.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Portrait of a Gentleman in Armour with Two Pages</i> by <a href="/wiki/Paris_Bordone" title="Paris Bordone">Paris Bordone</a></div></div></div>
<p>The word "armour" began to appear in the Middle Ages as a derivative of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_French_language" title="Old French language">Old French</a>. It is dated from 1297 as a "mail, defensive covering worn in combat". The word originates from the Old French <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">armure</i></span>, itself derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">armatura</i></span> meaning "arms and/or equipment", with the root <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">armare</i></span> meaning "arms or gear".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Body_armor" title="Body armor">Body armor</a></div>
<p>Armour has been used throughout <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>. It has been made from a variety of materials, beginning with the use of leathers or fabrics as protection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and evolving through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">chain mail</a> and metal plate into today's modern <a href="/wiki/Composite_armour" title="Composite armour">composites</a>. For much of <a href="/wiki/Military_history" title="Military history">military history</a> the manufacture of metal personal armour has dominated the technology and employment of armour.
</p><p>Armour drove the development of many important technologies of the Ancient World, including wood <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laminate" title="Laminate">lamination</a>, mining, <a href="/wiki/Refining_(metallurgy)" title="Refining (metallurgy)">metal refining</a>, vehicle manufacture, leather processing, and later decorative metal working. Its production was influential in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>, and furthered commercial development of metallurgy and engineering. Armour was the single most influential factor in the development of <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a>, which in turn revolutionised warfare.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg"><img alt="Bronze armour and a helmet with pieces of boar's tusk" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4632" data-file-width="3464" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg/170px-%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg/255px-%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg/340px-%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%CE%A0%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B1_%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD_%CE%94%CE%AD%CE%BD%CF%84%CF%81%CF%89%CE%BD_8005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Dendra_panoply" title="Dendra panoply">Dendra panoply</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean Greek</a> armour, c. 1400 BC</div></div></div>
<p>Significant factors in the development of armour include the economic and technological necessities of its production. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Plate_armour" title="Plate armour">plate armour</a> first appeared in <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Medieval Europe</a> when water-powered trip hammers made the formation of plates faster and cheaper. Also, modern militaries usually do not equip their forces with the best armour available because it would be prohibitively expensive. At times the development of armour has paralleled the development of increasingly effective weaponry on the battlefield, with armourers seeking to create better protection without sacrificing mobility.
</p><p>Well-known armour types in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_history" title="European history">European history</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Lorica_hamata" title="Lorica hamata">lorica hamata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorica_squamata" title="Lorica squamata">lorica squamata</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lorica_segmentata" title="Lorica segmentata">lorica segmentata</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legions</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">mail</a> <a href="/wiki/Hauberk" title="Hauberk">hauberk</a> of the early medieval age, and the full steel plate harness worn by later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a>, and breast and back plates worn by heavy cavalry in several European countries until the first year of World War I (1914–15). The <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> warriors of feudal Japan utilised <a href="/wiki/Japanese_armour" title="Japanese armour">many types of armour</a> for hundreds of years up to the 19th century.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early">Early</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg"><img alt="Statue depicting a man in colorful armour" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4032" data-file-width="3024" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg/220px-China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg/330px-China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg/440px-China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:China_Shanxi_Ming_dynasty_-_Buddhist_deity_GuanGong_or_Guan_Yu_-_wood_IMG_9575_Museum_of_Asian_Civilisation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wooden statue of <a href="/wiki/Guan_Yu" title="Guan Yu">Guan Yu</a> in mountain pattern armour, 16th c. <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cuirass" title="Cuirass">Cuirasses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Combat_helmet" title="Combat helmet">helmets</a> were manufactured in Japan as early as the 4th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Tankō</i>, worn by foot soldiers and <i>keikō</i>, worn by horsemen were both pre-samurai types of early <a href="/wiki/Japanese_armour" title="Japanese armour">Japanese armour</a> constructed from iron plates connected together by leather thongs. Japanese lamellar armour (<i>keiko</i>) passed through Korea and reached Japan around the 5th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These early Japanese lamellar armours took the form of a sleeveless jacket, leggings and a helmet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Armour did not always cover all of the body; sometimes no more than a helmet and leg plates were worn. The rest of the body was generally protected by means of a large <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shield</a>. Examples of armies equipping their troops in this fashion were the Aztecs (13th to 15th century CE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In East Asia many types of armour were commonly used at different times by various cultures, including <a href="/wiki/Scale_armour" title="Scale armour">scale armour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamellar_armour" title="Lamellar armour">lamellar armour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laminar_armour" title="Laminar armour">laminar armour</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plated_mail" title="Plated mail">plated mail</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chainmail" title="Chainmail">mail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plate_armour" title="Plate armour">plate armour</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brigandine" title="Brigandine">brigandine</a>. Around the dynastic Tang, Song, and early Ming Period, cuirasses and plates (mingguangjia) were also used, with more elaborate versions for officers in war. The Chinese, during that time used partial plates for "important" body parts instead of covering their whole body since too much plate armour hinders their martial arts movement. The other body parts were covered in cloth, leather, lamellar, or Mountain pattern. In pre-Qin dynasty times, leather armour was made out of various animals, with more exotic ones such as the rhinoceros.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">Mail</a>, sometimes called "chainmail", made of interlocking iron rings is believed to have first appeared some time after 300 BC. Its invention is credited to the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> are thought to have adopted their design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gradually, small additional plates or discs of iron were added to the mail to protect vulnerable areas. Hardened leather and <a href="/wiki/Splint_armour" title="Splint armour">splinted</a> construction were used for arm and leg pieces. The <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_plates" title="Coat of plates">coat of plates</a> was developed, an armour made of large plates sewn inside a textile or leather coat.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="13th_to_18th_century_Europe">13th to 18th century Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 13th to 18th century Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Components_of_medieval_armour" title="Components of medieval armour">Components of medieval armour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armour_in_the_18th_century" title="Armour in the 18th century">Armour in the 18th century</a></div>
<p>Early plate in Italy, and elsewhere in the 13th–15th century, were made of iron. Iron armour could be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carburising" title="Carburising">carburised</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case_hardening" title="Case hardening">case hardened</a> to give a surface of harder steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Plate armour became cheaper than mail by the 15th century as it required much less labour and labour had become much more expensive after the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>, though it did require larger furnaces to produce larger <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">blooms</a>. Mail continued to be used to protect those joints which could not be adequately protected by plate, such as the armpit, crook of the elbow and groin. Another advantage of plate was that a lance rest could be fitted to the breast plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The small skull cap evolved into a bigger true helmet, the <a href="/wiki/Bascinet" title="Bascinet">bascinet</a>, as it was lengthened downward to protect the back of the neck and the sides of the head. Additionally, several new forms of fully enclosed helmets were introduced in the late 14th century.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers,_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle,_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art,_New_York.jpg"><img alt="Three statues of riders and horses in armour" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers%2C_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle%2C_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg/220px-Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers%2C_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle%2C_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers%2C_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle%2C_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg/330px-Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers%2C_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle%2C_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers%2C_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle%2C_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg/440px-Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers%2C_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle%2C_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reconstitution_d%27armures_de_chevaliers_et_de_destriers,_XVIe_si%C3%A8cle,_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art,_New_York.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Heavily armoured riders and their <a href="/wiki/Barding" title="Barding">barded</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_horse" title="War horse">war horses</a>, 16th century</div></div></div>
<p>Probably the most recognised style of armour in the world became the <a href="/wiki/Plate_armour" title="Plate armour">plate armour</a> associated with the <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a> of the European <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>, but continuing to the early 17th century <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> in all European countries.
</p><p>By about 1400 the full harness of plate armour had been developed in armouries of Lombardy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Heavy cavalry dominated the battlefield for centuries in part because of their armour.
</p><p>In the early 15th century, advances in weaponry allowed infantry to defeat armoured knights on the battlefield. The quality of the metal used in armour deteriorated as armies became bigger and armour was made thicker, necessitating breeding of larger cavalry horses. If during the 14–15th centuries armour seldom weighed more than 15 kg, then by the late 16th century it weighed 25 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The increasing weight and thickness of late 16th century armour therefore gave substantial resistance.
</p><p>In the early years of low velocity firearms, full suits of armour, or breast plates actually stopped bullets fired from a modest distance. Crossbow bolts, if still used, would seldom penetrate good plate, nor would any bullet unless fired from close range. In effect, rather than making plate armour obsolete, the use of firearms stimulated the development of plate armour into its later stages. For most of that period, it allowed horsemen to fight while being the targets of defending arquebusiers without being easily killed. Full suits of armour were actually worn by generals and princely commanders right up to the second decade of the 18th century. It was the only way they could be mounted and survey the overall battlefield with safety from distant musket fire.
</p><p>The horse was afforded protection from lances and infantry weapons by steel plate <a href="/wiki/Barding" title="Barding">barding</a>. This gave the horse protection and enhanced the visual impression of a mounted knight. Late in the era, elaborate barding was used in parade armour.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Later">Later</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg"><img alt="Metal armour for torso and arms" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3722" data-file-width="2978" decoding="async" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg/220px-Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg/330px-Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg/440px-Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elements_of_a_Light-Cavalry_Armor_MET_DT780.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Elements of a Light-Cavalry Armor</i>, c. 1510</div></div></div>
<p>Gradually, starting in the mid-16th century, one plate element after another was discarded to save weight for foot soldiers.
</p><p>Back and breast plates continued to be used throughout the entire period of the 18th century and through Napoleonic times, in many European (heavy) cavalry units, until the early 20th century. From their introduction, <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">muskets</a> could pierce plate armour, so cavalry had to be far more mindful of the fire. In Japan armour continued to be used until the end of the samurai era, with the last major fighting in which armour was used happening in 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_armour" title="Japanese armour">Samurai armour</a> had one last short lived use in 1877 during the <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Rebellion" title="Satsuma Rebellion">Satsuma Rebellion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though the age of the knight was over, armour continued to be used in many capacities. Soldiers in the American Civil War bought iron and steel vests from peddlers (both sides had considered but rejected body armour for standard issue). The effectiveness of the vests varied widely—some successfully deflected bullets and saved lives, but others were poorly made and resulted in tragedy for the soldiers. In any case the vests were abandoned by many soldiers due to their weight on long marches as well as the stigma they got for being cowards from their fellow troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the start of World War I, thousands of the French <a href="/wiki/Cuirassier" title="Cuirassier">Cuirassiers</a> rode out to engage the German Cavalry. By that period, the shiny armour plate was covered in dark paint and a canvas wrap covered their elaborate Napoleonic style helmets. Their armour was only meant for protection against edged weapons such as <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabre" title="Sabre">sabres</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lance" title="Lance">lances</a>. Cavalry had to be wary of <a href="/wiki/Repeating_rifle" title="Repeating rifle">repeating rifles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine guns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a>, unlike the foot soldiers, who at least had a <a href="/wiki/Trench" title="Trench">trench</a> to give them some protection.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present">Present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistic_vest" title="Ballistic vest">ballistic vests</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Flak_jacket" title="Flak jacket">flak jackets</a>, made of ballistic cloth (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyneema" title="Dyneema">dyneema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twaron" title="Twaron">twaron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">spectra</a> etc.) and ceramic or metal plates are common among police forces, <a href="/wiki/Security_guard" title="Security guard">security staff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prison_officer" title="Prison officer">corrections officers</a> and some branches of the military.
</p><p>The US Army has adopted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interceptor_body_armour" title="Interceptor body armour">Interceptor body armour</a>, which uses Enhanced Small Arms Protective Inserts (ESAPIs) in the chest, sides, and back of the armour. Each plate is rated to stop a range of ammunition including 3 hits from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751_NATO" title="7.62×51 NATO">7.62×51 NATO</a> AP round at a range of 10 m (33 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dragon_Skin" title="Dragon Skin">Dragon Skin</a> is another ballistic vest which is currently in testing with mixed results. As of 2019, it has been deemed too heavy, expensive, and unreliable, in comparison to more traditional plates, and it is outdated in protection compared to modern US IOTV armour, and even in testing was deemed a downgrade from the IBA.
</p><p>The British Armed Forces also have their own armour, known as Osprey. It is rated to the same general equivalent standard as the US counterpart, the <a href="/wiki/Improved_Outer_Tactical_Vest" title="Improved Outer Tactical Vest">Improved Outer Tactical Vest</a>, and now the <a href="/wiki/Soldier_Plate_Carrier_System" title="Soldier Plate Carrier System">Soldier Plate Carrier System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modular_Tactical_Vest" title="Modular Tactical Vest">Modular Tactical Vest</a>.
</p><p>The Russian Armed Forces also have armour, known as the 6B43, all the way to 6B45, depending on variant. Their armour runs on the GOST system, which, due to regional conditions, has resulted in a technically higher protective level overall.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle">Vehicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vehicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_armour" title="Vehicle armour">Vehicle armour</a></div>
<p>The first modern production technology for armour plating was used by navies in the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Ironclad_warship" title="Ironclad warship">ironclad warship</a>, reaching its pinnacle of development with the <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a>. The first <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tanks</a> were produced during World War I. Aerial armour has been used to protect pilots and aircraft systems since the First World War.
</p><p>In modern ground forces' usage, the meaning of armour has expanded to include the role of troops in combat. After the evolution of <a href="/wiki/Armoured_warfare" title="Armoured warfare">armoured warfare</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanised_infantry" title="Mechanised infantry">mechanised infantry</a> were mounted in armoured fighting vehicles and replaced <a href="/wiki/Light_infantry" title="Light infantry">light infantry</a> in many situations. In modern armoured warfare, armoured units equipped with tanks and <a href="/wiki/Infantry_fighting_vehicle" title="Infantry fighting vehicle">infantry fighting vehicles</a> serve the historic role of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cavalry" title="Heavy cavalry">heavy cavalry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light_cavalry" title="Light cavalry">light cavalry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dragoon" title="Dragoon">dragoons</a>, and belong to the armoured branch of warfare.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ships">Ships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">Warship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naval_armour" title="Naval armour">Naval armour</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Warrior_(1860).jpg"><img alt="A ship with an iron hull" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="990" data-file-width="1491" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/HMS_Warrior_%281860%29.jpg/220px-HMS_Warrior_%281860%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/HMS_Warrior_%281860%29.jpg/330px-HMS_Warrior_%281860%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/HMS_Warrior_%281860%29.jpg/440px-HMS_Warrior_%281860%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Warrior_(1860).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/HMS_Warrior_(1860)" title="HMS Warrior (1860)">HMS <i>Warrior</i></a> during her third commission between 1867 and 1871</div></div></div>
<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ironclad" title="Ironclad">ironclad</a> battleship, with <a href="/wiki/Iron_armour" title="Iron armour">iron armour</a> over a wooden hull, <a href="/wiki/French_ironclad_Gloire" title="French ironclad Gloire"><i>Gloire</i></a>, was launched by the <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a> in 1859<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> prompting the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> to build a counter. The following year they launched <a href="/wiki/HMS_Warrior_(1860)" title="HMS Warrior (1860)">HMS <i>Warrior</i></a>, which was twice the size and had iron armour over an iron hull. After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hampton_Roads" title="Battle of Hampton Roads">first battle between two ironclads</a> took place in 1862 during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, it became clear that the ironclad had replaced the <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">unarmoured line-of-battle ship</a> as the most powerful warship afloat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ironclads were designed for several roles, including as high seas battleships, <a href="/wiki/Coastal_defence_ship" title="Coastal defence ship">coastal defence ships</a>, and long-range <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a>. The rapid evolution of warship design in the late 19th century transformed the ironclad from a wooden-hulled vessel which carried sails to supplement its steam engines into the steel-built, turreted battleships and cruisers familiar in the 20th century. This change was pushed forward by the development of heavier naval guns (the ironclads of the 1880s carried some of the heaviest guns ever mounted at sea),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> more sophisticated steam engines, and advances in metallurgy which made steel shipbuilding possible.
</p><p>The rapid pace of change in the ironclad period meant that many ships were obsolete as soon as they were complete, and that naval tactics were in a state of flux. Many ironclads were built to make use of the <a href="/wiki/Naval_ram" title="Naval ram">ram</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedo</a>, which a number of naval designers considered the crucial weapons of naval combat. There is no clear end to the ironclad period, but towards the end of the 1890s the term <i>ironclad</i> dropped out of use. New ships were increasingly constructed to a standard pattern and designated battleships or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armoured_cruiser" title="Armoured cruiser">armoured cruisers</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trains">Trains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Trains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg"><img alt="A train with metal plates affixed to the exterior" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="514" data-file-width="380" decoding="async" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg/170px-Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg/255px-Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg/340px-Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wagon_pancerny_s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An armoured train from 1915</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Armoured_train" title="Armoured train">Armoured trains</a> saw use during the 19th century and early 20th century in the American Civil War (1861–1865), the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> (1870–1871), the <a href="/wiki/First_Boer_War" title="First Boer War">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second</a> Boer Wars (1880–81 and 1899–1902), the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Soviet_War" title="Polish–Soviet War">Polish–Soviet War</a> (1919–1921), the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First</a> (1914–1918) and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World Wars</a> (1939–1945) and the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a> (1946–1954). The most intensive use of armoured trains was during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a> (1918–1920).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Armoured_fighting_vehicles">Armoured fighting vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Armoured fighting vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_armour" title="Vehicle armour">Vehicle armour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">Armoured fighting vehicle</a></div>
<p>Ancient <a href="/wiki/Siege_engine" title="Siege engine">siege engines</a> were usually protected by wooden armour, often covered with wet hides or thin metal to prevent being easily burned.
</p><p>Medieval <a href="/wiki/War_wagon" title="War wagon">war wagons</a> were horse-drawn wagons that were similarly armoured.  These contained guns or crossbowmen that could fire through gun-slits.
</p><p>The first modern <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">armoured fighting vehicles</a> were <a href="/wiki/Armored_car_(military)" title="Armored car (military)">armoured cars</a>, developed circa 1900.  These started as ordinary wheeled motor-cars protected by iron shields, typically mounting a <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the First World War, the stalemate of <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a> during on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> spurred the development of the tank. It was envisioned as an armoured machine that could advance under fire from enemy rifles and machine guns, and respond with its own heavy guns. It utilized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caterpillar_track" title="Caterpillar track">caterpillar tracks</a> to cross ground broken up by shellfire and trenches.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With the development of effective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_artillery" title="Anti-aircraft artillery">anti-aircraft artillery</a> in the period before the Second World War, military pilots, once the "knights of the air" during the First World War, became far more vulnerable to ground fire. As a response armour plating was added to aircraft to protect aircrew and vulnerable areas such as engines and fuel tanks. <a href="/wiki/Self-sealing_fuel_tank" title="Self-sealing fuel tank">Self-sealing fuel tanks</a> functioned like armour in that they added protection but also increased weight and cost.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_2">Present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abrams-transparent.png"><img alt="A tank" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="691" data-file-width="1542" decoding="async" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Abrams-transparent.png/220px-Abrams-transparent.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Abrams-transparent.png/330px-Abrams-transparent.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Abrams-transparent.png/440px-Abrams-transparent.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abrams-transparent.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Military" title="United States Military">US Military</a>'s <a href="/wiki/M1_Abrams" title="M1 Abrams">M1 Abrams</a> <a href="/wiki/Main_battle_tank" title="Main battle tank">MBT</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Composite_armour" title="Composite armour">composite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reactive_armour" title="Reactive armour">reactive</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cage_armour" title="Cage armour">cage armour</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png"><img alt="Metal plate with five dents" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6782" data-file-width="5963" decoding="async" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png/220px-Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png/330px-Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png/440px-Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pannzerung_plastische_deformation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Swebor-brand high-strength low alloy steel plate, showing both sides, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_deformation" title="Plastic deformation">plastic deformation</a> from defeating <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a> testing. Note: When exposed to fire, steel first expands and then loses its strength, exceeding critical temperature at 538 °C (1,000 °F) per <a class="external text" href="https://www.astm.org/Standards/E119.htm" rel="nofollow">ASTM E119</a> unless treated with <a href="/wiki/Fireproofing" title="Fireproofing">fireproofing</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Tank armour has progressed from the Second World War armour forms, now incorporating not only harder <a href="/wiki/Composite_armour" title="Composite armour">composites</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Reactive_armour" title="Reactive armour">reactive armour</a> designed to defeat <a href="/wiki/Shaped_charge" title="Shaped charge">shaped charges</a>. As a result of this, the <a href="/wiki/Main_battle_tank" title="Main battle tank">main battle tank</a> (MBT) conceived in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era can survive multiple <a href="/wiki/Rocket-propelled_grenade" title="Rocket-propelled grenade">rocket-propelled grenade</a> strikes with minimal effect on the crew or the operation of the vehicle. The <a href="/wiki/Light_tank" title="Light tank">light tanks</a> that were the last descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Light_cavalry" title="Light cavalry">light cavalry</a> during the Second World War have almost completely disappeared from the world's militaries due to increased lethality of the weapons available to the vehicle-mounted infantry.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">armoured personnel carrier</a> (APC) was devised during the First World War. It allows the safe and rapid movement of infantry in a combat zone, minimising casualties and maximising mobility. APCs are fundamentally different from the previously used armoured <a href="/wiki/Half-track" title="Half-track">half-tracks</a> in that they offer a higher level of protection from artillery <a href="/wiki/Air_burst" title="Air burst">burst</a> fragments, and greater mobility in more terrain types. The basic APC design was substantially expanded to an <a href="/wiki/Infantry_fighting_vehicle" title="Infantry fighting vehicle">infantry fighting vehicle</a> (IFV) when properties of an APC and a light tank were combined in one vehicle.
</p><p>Naval armour has fundamentally changed from the Second World War doctrine of thicker plating to defend against <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)" title="Shell (projectile)">shells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">bombs</a> and torpedoes. Passive defence naval armour is limited to <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a> or steel (either single layer or as <a href="/wiki/Spaced_armour" title="Spaced armour">spaced armour</a>) protecting particularly vital areas from the effects of nearby impacts. Since ships cannot carry enough armour to completely protect against <a href="/wiki/Anti-ship_missile" title="Anti-ship missile">anti-ship missiles</a>, they depend more on defensive weapons destroying incoming missiles, or causing them to miss by confusing their guidance systems with <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare" title="Electronic warfare">electronic warfare</a>.
</p><p>Although the role of the ground attack aircraft significantly diminished after the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, it re-emerged during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, and in the recognition of this, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a> authorised the design and production of what became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-10_Thunderbolt_II" title="A-10 Thunderbolt II">A-10</a> dedicated anti-armour and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground-attack_aircraft" title="Ground-attack aircraft">ground-attack aircraft</a> that first saw action in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/High-voltage_transformer_fire_barriers" title="High-voltage transformer fire barriers">High-voltage transformer fire barriers</a> are often required to defeat <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a> from small arms as well as projectiles from <a href="/wiki/Bushing_(electrical)" title="Bushing (electrical)">transformer bushings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lightning_arrester" title="Lightning arrester">lightning arresters</a>, which form part of large electrical <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a>, per <a class="external text" href="https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/all-codes-and-standards/list-of-codes-and-standards/detail?code=850" rel="nofollow">NFPA 850</a>. Such fire barriers may be designed to inherently function as armour, or may be <a href="/wiki/Passive_fire_protection" title="Passive fire protection">passive fire protection</a> materials <i>augmented by armour</i>, where care must be taken to ensure that the armour's reaction to fire does not cause issues with regards to the fire barrier being armoured to defeat explosions and projectiles in addition to fire, especially since both functions must be provided simultaneously, meaning they must be fire-tested together to provide realistic evidence of fitness for purpose.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Unmanned_combat_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned combat aerial vehicle">Combat drones</a> use little to no vehicular armour as they are not manned vessels, this results in them being lightweight and small in size.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_armour">Animal armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Animal armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horse_armour">Horse armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Horse armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Barding" title="Barding">Barding</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG"><img alt="Statue of horse and rider in armour" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG/220px-Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG/330px-Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG/440px-Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dresden-Zwinger-Armoury-Armor.02.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 16th-century knight with a horse in full barding</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_armour" title="Body armour">Body armour</a> for <a href="/wiki/Horses_in_warfare" title="Horses in warfare">war horses</a> has been used since at least 2000 BC. Cloth, leather, and metal protection covered cavalry horses in ancient civilisations, including ancient Egypt, Assyria, Persia, and Rome. Some formed <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cavalry" title="Heavy cavalry">heavy cavalry</a> units of armoured horses and riders used to attack infantry and mounted archers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Armour for horses is called <i>barding</i> (also spelled <i>bard</i> or <i>barb</i>) especially when used by European <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a>.
</p><p>During the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> as armour protection for knights became more effective, their mounts became targets. This vulnerability was exploited by the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scots</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bannockburn" title="Battle of Bannockburn">Battle of Bannockburn</a> in the 14th century, when horses were killed by the infantry, and for the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cr%C3%A9cy" title="Battle of Crécy">Battle of Crécy</a> in the same century where <a href="/wiki/English_longbow" title="English longbow">longbowmen</a> shot horses and the then dismounted <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> knights were killed by <a href="/wiki/Heavy_infantry" title="Heavy infantry">heavy infantry</a>. Barding developed as a response to such events.
</p><p>Examples of armour for horses could be found as far back as <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataphracts" title="Cataphracts">Cataphracts</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Scale_armour" title="Scale armour">scale armour</a> for both rider and horse, are believed by many historians to have influenced the later European knights, via contact with the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nell,_Grant_S._1995_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nell,_Grant_S._1995-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Surviving period examples of barding are rare; however, complete sets are on display at the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">Philadelphia Museum of Art</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Collection" title="Wallace Collection">Wallace Collection</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Armouries" title="Royal Armouries">Royal Armouries</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>.  Horse armour could be made in whole or in part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuir_bouilli" title="Cuir bouilli">cuir bouilli</a> (hardened leather), but surviving examples of this are especially rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elephant_armour">Elephant armour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Elephant armour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Armouries,_Leeds_20190201_123036_(49307523651).jpg"><img alt="War elephant display in a museum" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3024" data-file-width="3024" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Royal_Armouries%2C_Leeds_20190201_123036_%2849307523651%29.jpg/220px-Royal_Armouries%2C_Leeds_20190201_123036_%2849307523651%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Royal_Armouries%2C_Leeds_20190201_123036_%2849307523651%29.jpg/330px-Royal_Armouries%2C_Leeds_20190201_123036_%2849307523651%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Royal_Armouries%2C_Leeds_20190201_123036_%2849307523651%29.jpg/440px-Royal_Armouries%2C_Leeds_20190201_123036_%2849307523651%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Armouries,_Leeds_20190201_123036_(49307523651).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Model of an armoured elephant at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Armouries_Museum" title="Royal Armouries Museum">Royal Armouries Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/War_elephant" title="War elephant">War elephants</a> were first used in ancient times without armour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKistler20079_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKistler20079-23">[23]</a></sup> but armour was introduced because elephants injured by enemy weapons would often flee the battlefield. Elephant armour was often made from hardened leather, which was fitted onto an individual elephant while moist, then dryed to create a hardened shell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKistler200713_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKistler200713-24">[24]</a></sup> Alternatively, metal armour pieces were sometimes sewn into heavy cloth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKistler200722_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKistler200722-25">[25]</a></sup> Later <a href="/wiki/Lamellar_armour" title="Lamellar armour">lamellar armour</a> (small overlapping metal plates) was introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKistler2007211_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKistler2007211-26">[26]</a></sup> Full plate armour was not typically used due to its expense and the danger of the animal overheating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKistler200721_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKistler200721-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
