<p><b>Probability</b> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> concerning numerical descriptions of how likely an <a href="/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)" title="Event (probability theory)">event</a> is to occur, or how likely it is that a proposition is true.  The probability of an event is a number between 0 and 1, where, roughly speaking, 0 indicates impossibility of the event and 1 indicates certainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[note 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart_and_Ord_2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart_and_Ord_2009-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feller_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feller-3">[2]</a></sup> The higher the probability of an event, the more likely it is that the event will occur. A simple example is the tossing of a fair (unbiased) coin. Since the coin is fair, the two outcomes ("heads" and "tails") are both equally probable; the probability of "heads" equals the probability of "tails"; and since no other outcomes are possible, the probability of either "heads" or "tails" is 1/2 (which could also be written as 0.5 or 50%).
</p><p>These concepts have been given an <a href="/wiki/Probability_axioms" title="Probability axioms">axiomatic</a> mathematical formalization in <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>, which is used widely in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Areas_of_study" title="Areas of study">areas of study</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> to, for example, draw inferences about the expected frequency of events. Probability theory is also used to describe the underlying mechanics and regularities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_systems" title="Complex systems">complex systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Probability_interpretations" title="Probability interpretations">Probability interpretations</a></div>
<p>When dealing with <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random" title="Random">random</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Well-defined" title="Well-defined">well-defined</a> in a purely theoretical setting (like tossing a coin), probabilities can be numerically described by the number of desired outcomes, divided by the total number of all outcomes. For example, tossing a coin twice will yield "head-head", "head-tail", "tail-head", and "tail-tail" outcomes. The probability of getting an outcome of "head-head" is 1 out of 4 outcomes, or, in numerical terms, 1/4, 0.25 or 25%. However, when it comes to practical application, there are two major competing categories of probability interpretations, whose adherents hold different views about the fundamental nature of probability:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(philosophy)" title="Objectivity (philosophy)">Objectivists</a> assign numbers to describe some objective or physical state of affairs. The most popular version of objective probability is <a href="/wiki/Frequentist_probability" title="Frequentist probability">frequentist probability</a>, which claims that the probability of a random event denotes the <i>relative frequency of occurrence</i> of an experiment's outcome when the experiment is repeated indefinitely. This interpretation considers probability to be the relative frequency "in the long run" of outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> A modification of this is <a href="/wiki/Propensity_probability" title="Propensity probability">propensity probability</a>, which interprets probability as the tendency of some experiment to yield a certain outcome, even if it is performed only once.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjective_probability#Objective_and_subjective_Bayesian_probabilities" title="Subjective probability">Subjectivists</a> assign numbers per subjective probability, that is, as a degree of belief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> The degree of belief has been interpreted as "the price at which you would buy or sell a bet that pays 1 unit of utility if E, 0 if not E",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> although that interpretation is not universally agreed upon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>  The most popular version of subjective probability is <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_probability" title="Bayesian probability">Bayesian probability</a>, which includes expert knowledge as well as experimental data to produce probabilities.  The expert knowledge is represented by some (subjective) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prior_probability_distribution" title="Prior probability distribution">prior probability distribution</a>.  These data are incorporated in a <a href="/wiki/Likelihood_function" title="Likelihood function">likelihood function</a>. The product of the prior and the likelihood, when normalized, results in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posterior_probability_distribution" title="Posterior probability distribution">posterior probability distribution</a> that incorporates all the information known to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> By <a href="/wiki/Aumann%27s_agreement_theorem" title="Aumann's agreement theorem">Aumann's agreement theorem</a>, Bayesian agents whose prior beliefs are similar will end up with similar posterior beliefs. However, sufficiently different priors can lead to different conclusions, regardless of how much information the agents share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_probability#Etymology" title="History of probability">History of probability § Etymology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_probability_and_statistics" title="Glossary of probability and statistics">Glossary of probability and statistics</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Likelihood" title="Likelihood">Likelihood</a></div>
<p>The word <i>probability</i> <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">derives</a> from the Latin <i>probabilitas</i>, which can also mean "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/probity" title="wiktionary:probity">probity</a>", a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Witness" title="Witness">witness</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Legal_case" title="Legal case">legal case</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and often correlated with the witness's <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>. In a sense, this differs much from the modern meaning of <i>probability</i>, which in contrast is a measure of the weight of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a>, and is arrived at from <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">inductive reasoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_inference" title="Statistical inference">statistical inference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emergence_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emergence-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_probability" title="History of probability">History of probability</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_statistics" title="History of statistics">History of statistics</a></div>
<p>The scientific study of probability is a modern development of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">Gambling</a> shows that there has been an interest in quantifying the ideas of probability for millennia, but exact mathematical descriptions arose much later. There are reasons for the slow development of the mathematics of probability. Whereas games of chance provided the impetus for the mathematical study of probability, fundamental issues <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[note 2]</a></sup> are still obscured by the superstitions of gamblers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Jeffrey" title="Richard Jeffrey">Richard Jeffrey</a>, "Before the middle of the seventeenth century, the term 'probable' (Latin <i>probabilis</i>) meant <i>approvable</i>, and was applied in that sense, univocally, to opinion and to action. A probable action or opinion was one such as sensible people would undertake or hold, in the circumstances."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeffrey_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffrey-14">[12]</a></sup> However, in legal contexts especially, 'probable' could also apply to propositions for which there was good evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklin_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-15">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cardano.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="268" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Cardano.jpg/140px-Cardano.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Cardano.jpg/210px-Cardano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/03/Cardano.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cardano.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano" title="Gerolamo Cardano">Gerolamo Cardano</a> (16th century)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2204" data-file-width="1682" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg/140px-Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg/210px-Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg/280px-Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens-painting.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a> published one of the first books on probability (17th century)</div></div></div>
<p>The sixteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> polymath <a href="/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano" title="Gerolamo Cardano">Gerolamo Cardano</a> demonstrated the efficacy of defining <a href="/wiki/Odds" title="Odds">odds</a> as the ratio of favourable to unfavourable outcomes (which implies that the probability of an event is given by the ratio of favourable outcomes to the total number of possible outcomes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup>).
Aside from the elementary work by Cardano, the doctrine of probabilities dates to the correspondence of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat" title="Pierre de Fermat">Pierre de Fermat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a> (1654). <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a> (1657) gave the earliest known scientific treatment of the subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakob_Bernoulli" title="Jakob Bernoulli">Jakob Bernoulli</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ars_Conjectandi" title="Ars Conjectandi">Ars Conjectandi</a></i> (posthumous, 1713) and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_de_Moivre" title="Abraham de Moivre">Abraham de Moivre</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Doctrine_of_Chances" title="The Doctrine of Chances">Doctrine of Chances</a></i> (1718) treated the subject as a branch of mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/Ian_Hacking" title="Ian Hacking">Ian Hacking</a>'s <i>The Emergence of Probability</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emergence_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emergence-11">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/James_Franklin_(philosopher)" title="James Franklin (philosopher)">James Franklin's</a> <i>The Science of Conjecture</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> for histories of the early development of the very concept of mathematical probability.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_errors" title="Theory of errors">theory of errors</a> may be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Roger_Cotes" title="Roger Cotes">Roger Cotes</a>'s <i>Opera Miscellanea</i> (posthumous, 1722), but a memoir prepared by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Simpson" title="Thomas Simpson">Thomas Simpson</a> in 1755 (printed 1756) first applied the theory to the discussion of errors of observation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> The reprint (1757) of this memoir lays down the axioms that positive and negative errors are equally probable, and that certain assignable limits define the range of all errors. Simpson also discusses continuous errors and describes a probability curve.
</p><p>The first two laws of error that were proposed both originated with <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon Laplace">Pierre-Simon Laplace</a>. The first law was published in 1774, and stated that the frequency of an error could be expressed as an exponential function of the numerical magnitude of the error—disregarding sign. The second law of error was proposed in 1778 by Laplace, and stated that the frequency of the error is an exponential function of the square of the error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson1923_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1923-21">[19]</a></sup> The second law of error is called the normal distribution or the Gauss law. "It is difficult historically to attribute that law to Gauss, who in spite of his well-known precocity had probably not made this discovery before he was two years old."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson1923_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1923-21">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">Daniel Bernoulli</a> (1778) introduced the principle of the maximum product of the probabilities of a system of concurrent errors.
</p>

</p><p>In the nineteenth century, authors on the general theory included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laplace" title="Laplace">Laplace</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sylvestre_Lacroix" title="Sylvestre Lacroix">Sylvestre Lacroix</a> (1816), Littrow (1833), <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Quetelet" title="Adolphe Quetelet">Adolphe Quetelet</a> (1853), <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dedekind" title="Richard Dedekind">Richard Dedekind</a> (1860), Helmert (1872), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Laurent" title="Hermann Laurent">Hermann Laurent</a> (1873), Liagre, Didion and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Pearson" title="Karl Pearson">Karl Pearson</a>. <a href="/wiki/Augustus_De_Morgan" title="Augustus De Morgan">Augustus De Morgan</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Boole" title="George Boole">George Boole</a> improved the exposition of the theory.
</p><p>In 1906, <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Markov" title="Andrey Markov">Andrey Markov</a> introduced<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> the notion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markov_chains" title="Markov chains">Markov chains</a>, which played an important role in <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">stochastic processes</a> theory and its applications. The modern theory of probability based on the <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measure theory</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Kolmogorov" title="Andrey Kolmogorov">Andrey Kolmogorov</a> in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the geometric side, contributors to <i>The Educational Times</i> were influential (Miller, Crofton, McColl, Wolstenholme, Watson, and <a href="/wiki/Artemas_Martin" title="Artemas Martin">Artemas Martin</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/Integral_geometry" title="Integral geometry">integral geometry</a> for more info.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">Probability theory</a></div>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theories</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">theory of probability</a> is a representation of its concepts in formal terms—that is, in terms that can be considered separately from their meaning. These formal terms are manipulated by the rules of mathematics and logic, and any results are interpreted or translated back into the problem domain.
</p><p>There have been at least two successful attempts to formalize probability, namely the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kolmogorov" title="Kolmogorov">Kolmogorov</a> formulation and the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Threlkeld_Cox" title="Richard Threlkeld Cox">Cox</a> formulation. In Kolmogorov's formulation (see also <a href="/wiki/Probability_space" title="Probability space">probability space</a>), <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">sets</a> are interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)" title="Event (probability theory)">events</a> and probability as a <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measure</a> on a class of sets. In <a href="/wiki/Cox%27s_theorem" title="Cox's theorem">Cox's theorem</a>, probability is taken as a primitive (i.e., not further analyzed), and the emphasis is on constructing a consistent assignment of probability values to propositions. In both cases, the <a href="/wiki/Probability_axioms" title="Probability axioms">laws of probability</a> are the same, except for technical details.
</p><p>There are other methods for quantifying uncertainty, such as the <a href="/wiki/Dempster%E2%80%93Shafer_theory" title="Dempster–Shafer theory">Dempster–Shafer theory</a> or <a href="/wiki/Possibility_theory" title="Possibility theory">possibility theory</a>, but those are essentially different and not compatible with the usually-understood laws of probability.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Probability theory is applied in everyday life in <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> assessment and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">modeling</a>. The insurance industry and <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">markets</a> use <a href="/wiki/Actuarial_science" title="Actuarial science">actuarial science</a> to determine pricing and make trading decisions. Governments apply probabilistic methods in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_regulation" title="Environmental regulation">environmental regulation</a>, entitlement analysis, and <a href="/wiki/Financial_regulation" title="Financial regulation">financial regulation</a>.
</p><p>An example of the use of probability theory in equity trading is the effect of the perceived probability of any widespread Middle East conflict on oil prices, which have ripple effects in the economy as a whole. An assessment by a commodity trader that a war is more likely can send that commodity's prices up or down, and signals other traders of that opinion. Accordingly, the probabilities are neither assessed independently nor necessarily rationally. The theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral_finance" title="Behavioral finance">behavioral finance</a> emerged to describe the effect of such <a href="/wiki/Groupthink" title="Groupthink">groupthink</a> on pricing, on policy, and on peace and conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to financial assessment, probability can be used to analyze trends in biology (e.g., disease spread) as well as ecology (e.g., biological Punnett squares). As with finance, risk assessment can be used as a statistical tool to calculate the likelihood of undesirable events occurring, and can assist with implementing protocols to avoid encountering such circumstances. Probability is used to design <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Games_of_chance" title="Games of chance">games of chance</a> so that casinos can make a guaranteed profit, yet provide payouts to players that are frequent enough to encourage continued play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another significant application of probability theory in everyday life is <a href="/wiki/Reliability_(statistics)" title="Reliability (statistics)">reliability</a>. Many consumer products, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobiles" title="Automobiles">automobiles</a> and consumer electronics, use reliability theory in product design to reduce the probability of failure. Failure probability may influence a manufacturer's decisions on a product's <a href="/wiki/Warranty" title="Warranty">warranty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cache_language_model" title="Cache language model">cache language model</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_Language_Model" title="Statistical Language Model">statistical language models</a> that are used in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> are also examples of applications of probability theory.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutually_exclusive_events">Mutually exclusive events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mutually exclusive events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_randomness_and_probability_in_quantum_mechanics">Relation to randomness and probability in quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relation to randomness and probability in quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">Randomness</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation#Interpretations" title="Quantum fluctuation">Quantum fluctuation § Interpretations</a></div>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">deterministic</a> universe, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics" title="Newtonian mechanics">Newtonian</a> concepts, there would be no probability if all conditions were known (<a href="/wiki/Laplace%27s_demon" title="Laplace's demon">Laplace's demon</a>), (but there are situations in which <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">sensitivity to initial conditions</a> exceeds our ability to measure them, i.e. know them).  In the case of a <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a> wheel, if the force of the hand and the period of that force are known, the number on which the ball will stop would be a certainty (though as a practical matter, this would likely be true only of a roulette wheel that had not been exactly levelled – as Thomas A. Bass' <a href="/wiki/Eudaemons" title="Eudaemons">Newtonian Casino</a> revealed).  This also assumes knowledge of inertia and friction of the wheel, weight, smoothness, and roundness of the ball, variations in hand speed during the turning, and so forth. A probabilistic description can thus be more useful than Newtonian mechanics for analyzing the pattern of outcomes of repeated rolls of a roulette wheel. Physicists face the same situation in the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">kinetic theory of gases</a>, where the system, while deterministic <i>in principle</i>, is so complex (with the number of molecules typically the order of magnitude of the <a href="/wiki/Avogadro_constant" title="Avogadro constant">Avogadro constant</a> <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7023602000000000000♠"></span>6.02<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup></span>) that only a statistical description of its properties is feasible.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">Probability theory</a> is required to describe quantum phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> A revolutionary discovery of early 20th century <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> was the random character of all physical processes that occur at sub-atomic scales and are governed by the laws of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>. The objective <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> evolves deterministically but, according to the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a>, it deals with probabilities of observing, the outcome being explained by a <a href="/wiki/Wave_function_collapse" title="Wave function collapse">wave function collapse</a> when an observation is made. However, the loss of <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">determinism</a> for the sake of <a href="/wiki/Instrumentalism" title="Instrumentalism">instrumentalism</a> did not meet with universal approval. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> famously <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein#Quellenangaben_und_Anmerkungen" title="de:Albert Einstein">remarked</a> in a letter to <a href="/wiki/Max_Born" title="Max Born">Max Born</a>: "I am convinced that God does not play dice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> Like Einstein, <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Erwin Schrödinger</a>, who <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation#Historical_background_and_development" title="Schrödinger equation">discovered</a> the wave function, believed quantum mechanics is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical" title="Statistical">statistical</a> approximation of an underlying deterministic <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup> In some modern interpretations of the statistical mechanics of measurement, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">quantum decoherence</a> is invoked to account for the appearance of subjectively probabilistic experimental outcomes.
</p>

<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Strictly speaking, a probability of 0 indicates that an event <a href="/wiki/Almost_surely" title="Almost surely"><i>almost</i> never</a> takes place, whereas a probability of 1 indicates than an event <a href="/wiki/Almost_surely" title="Almost surely"><i>almost</i> certainly</a> takes place. This is an important distinction when the <a href="/wiki/Sample_space" title="Sample space">sample space</a> is infinite. For example, for the <a href="/wiki/Continuous_uniform_distribution" title="Continuous uniform distribution">continuous uniform distribution</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real</a> interval [5, 10], there are an infinite number of possible outcomes, and the probability of any given outcome being observed — for instance, exactly 7 — is 0. This means that when we make an observation, it will <i>almost surely not</i> be exactly 7. However, it does <b>not</b> mean that exactly 7 is <i>impossible</i>. Ultimately some specific outcome (with probability 0) will be observed, and one possibility for that specific outcome is exactly 7.</span>
</li>
