<p><b>Friendship</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">relationship</a> of mutual <a href="/wiki/Affection" title="Affection">affection</a> between people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is a stronger form of interpersonal bond than an "acquaintance" or an "association", such as a classmate, neighbor, coworker, or colleague.
</p><p>In some cultures, the concept of friendship is restricted to a small number of very deep relationships; in others, such as the U.S. and Canada, a person could have many friends, plus perhaps a more intense relationship with one or two people, who may be called <i>good friends</i> or <i>best friends</i>. Other colloquial terms include <i>besties</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Best_friends_forever" title="Best friends forever">Best Friends Forever</a></i> (<i>BFF</i>s). Although there are many forms of friendship, some of which may vary from place to place, certain characteristics are present in many such bonds. Such features include choosing to be with one another, enjoying time spent together, and being able to engage in a positive and supportive role to one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sometimes friends are distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>, as in the saying "friends and family", and sometimes from <a href="/wiki/Sexual_partner" title="Sexual partner">lovers</a> (e.g., "lovers and friends"), although the line is blurred with <a href="/wiki/Friends_with_benefits_relationships" title="Friends with benefits relationships">friends with benefits</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Friend_zone" title="Friend zone">friend zone</a></i> is a place where someone is restricted from rising up to the status of lover (see also <a href="/wiki/Unrequited_love" title="Unrequited love">Unrequited love</a>).
</p><p>Friendship has been studied in academic fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Communication_studies" title="Communication studies">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_psychology" title="Social psychology">social psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. Various academic theories of friendship have been proposed, including <a href="/wiki/Social_exchange_theory" title="Social exchange theory">social exchange theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equity_theory" title="Equity theory">equity theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Relational_dialectics" title="Relational dialectics">relational dialectics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attachment_styles" title="Attachment styles">attachment styles</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developmental_psychology">Developmental psychology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1064" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg/220px-Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg/330px-Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg/440px-Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Childhood_friends_at_a_carnival.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young childhood friends</div></div></div>
<p>The understanding of friendship in children tends to be more heavily focused on areas such as common activities, physical proximity, and shared expectations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bremner_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bremner-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 498">: 498 </span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[a]</a></sup> These friendships provide opportunity for <a href="/wiki/Play_(activity)" title="Play (activity)">playing</a> and practicing <a href="/wiki/Emotional_self-regulation" title="Emotional self-regulation">self-regulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 246">: 246 </span></sup> Most children tend to describe friendship in terms of things like <a href="/wiki/Sharing" title="Sharing">sharing</a>, and children are more likely to share with someone they consider to be a friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 246">: 246 </span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman,_B._M._2012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newman,_B._M._2012-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> As children mature, they become less individualized and are more aware of others. They gain the ability to empathize with their friends, and enjoy playing in groups. They also experience peer rejection as they move through the middle childhood years. Establishing good friendships at a young age helps a child to be better acclimated in society later on in their life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman,_B._M._2012_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newman,_B._M._2012-6">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Based upon the reports of teachers and mothers, 75% of preschool children had at least one friend. This figure rose to 78% through the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_grade" title="Fifth grade">fifth grade</a>, as measured by co-nomination as friends, and 55% had a mutual best friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 247">: 247 </span></sup> About 15% of children were found to be chronically friendless, reporting periods without mutual friends at least six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 250">: 250 </span></sup>
</p><p>Potential benefits of friendship include the opportunity to learn about empathy and problem solving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>
Coaching from parents can be useful in helping children to make friends. <a href="/wiki/Eileen_Kennedy-Moore" title="Eileen Kennedy-Moore">Eileen Kennedy-Moore</a> describes three key ingredients of children's friendship formation: (1) openness, (2) similarity, and (3) shared fun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Parents can also help children understand social guidelines they haven't learned on their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Drawing from research by Robert Selman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> and others, Kennedy-Moore outlines developmental stages in children's friendship, reflecting an increasing capacity to understand others' perspectives: "I Want It My Way", "What's In It For Me?", "By the Rules", "Caring and Sharing", and "Friends Through Thick and Thin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adolescence">Adolescence</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bhutan,_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1085" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Bhutan%2C_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg/220px-Bhutan%2C_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Bhutan%2C_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg/330px-Bhutan%2C_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Bhutan%2C_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg/440px-Bhutan%2C_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bhutan,_Friends_-_Flickr_-_babasteve.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two friends in <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a></div></div></div>
<p>In adolescence, friendships become "more giving, sharing, frank, supportive, and spontaneous." Adolescents tend to seek out peers who can provide such qualities in a <a href="/wiki/Reciprocity_(social_and_political_philosophy)" title="Reciprocity (social and political philosophy)">reciprocal relationship</a>, and to avoid peers whose problematic behavior suggest they may not be able to satisfy these needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reisman_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-reisman-15">[14]</a></sup> Personal characteristics and <a href="/wiki/Disposition" title="Disposition">dispositions</a> are also features sought by adolescents, when choosing whom to begin a friendship with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[15]</a></sup> Relationships begin to maintain a focus on shared values, <a href="/wiki/Loyalty" title="Loyalty">loyalty</a>, and common interests, rather than physical concerns like proximity and access to play things that more characterize childhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 246">: 246 </span></sup>
</p><p>One large study of American adolescents determined how their engagement in problematic behavior (such as stealing, fighting, and truancy) was related to their friendships. Findings indicated that adolescents were less likely to engage in problem behavior when their friends did well in school, participated in school activities, avoided drinking, and had good <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a>. The opposite was found regarding adolescents who did engage in problematic behavior. Whether adolescents were influenced by their friends to engage in problem behavior depended on how much they were exposed to those friends, and whether they and their friendship groups "fit in" at school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Friendships formed during <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">post-secondary education</a> last longer than friendships formed earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> In late adolescence, cross-racial friendships tend to be uncommon, likely due to prejudice and cultural differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adulthood">Adulthood</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1067" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg/220px-3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg/330px-3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg/440px-3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:3_barefoot_females_smiling_and_sitting_on_a_bench.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Friendship in adulthood</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="348" data-file-width="416" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg/220px-Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg/330px-Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jonathan_Lovingly_Taketh_His_Leave_of_David_by_Julius_Schnorr_von_Carolsfeld.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Freundschaft zwischen Jonathan und David</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julius_Schnorr_von_Karolsfeld" title="Julius Schnorr von Karolsfeld">Julius Schnorr von Karolsfeld</a> (1860), which translates in English as <i>Friendship between Jonathan and David</i></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1525" data-file-width="1525" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG/220px-Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG/330px-Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG/440px-Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tudor_and_Delia_Summer_2015_coloured.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two friends before posing for a picture.</div></div></div>
<p>Friendship in adulthood provides companionship, affection, as well as emotional support, and contributes positively to mental well-being and improved physical health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-enc-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 426">: 426 </span></sup>
</p><p>Adults may find it particularly difficult to maintain meaningful friendships in the workplace. "The workplace can crackle with competition, so people learn to hide vulnerabilities and quirks from colleagues. Work friendships often take on a transactional feel; it is difficult to say where networking ends and real friendship begins."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Most adults value the financial security of their jobs more than friendship with coworkers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The majority of adults have an average of two close friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Numerous studies with adults suggest that friendships and other supportive relationships do enhance self-esteem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www2.psych.purdue.edu_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-www2.psych.purdue.edu-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Older_adults">Older adults</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Old_age" title="Old age">Older adults</a> continue to report high levels of personal satisfaction in their friendships as they age, even as the overall number of friends tends to decline. This satisfaction is associated with an increased ability to accomplish <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">activities of daily living</a>, as well as a reduced decline in <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive abilities</a>, decreased instances of hospitalization, and better outcomes related to <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">rehabilitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-enc-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 427">: 427 </span></sup> The overall number of reported friends in later life may be mediated by increased lucidity, better speech and vision, and marital status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 53">: 53 </span></sup> A decline in the number of friends an individual has as they become older has been explained by Carstensen's Socioemotional Selectivity Theory, which describes a change in motivation that adults experience when socializing. The theory states that an increase in age is characterized by a shift from information-gathering to emotional regulation; in order to maintain positive emotions, older adults restrict their social groups to those whom they share an emotional bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>As one review phrased it:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Research within the past four decades has now consistently found that older adults reporting the highest levels of happiness and general well being also report strong, close ties to numerous friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brain_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-brain-26">[25]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>As family responsibilities and vocational pressures lessen, friendships become more important. Among the elderly, friendships can provide links to the larger community, serve as a protective factor against depression and loneliness, and compensate for potential losses in social support previously given by family members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yearbook_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-yearbook-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 32–33">: 32–33 </span></sup> Especially for people who cannot go out as often, interactions with friends allow for continued societal interaction. Additionally, older adults in declining health who remain in contact with friends show improved psychological well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forming_and_maintaining">Forming and maintaining</span></h2>
<p>Forming and maintaining friendships often requires time and effort.
</p><p>Some factors affect most people. For example, most people underestimate how much other people like them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Liking_gap" title="Liking gap">liking gap</a> can make it difficult to form friendships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developmental_issues">Developmental issues</span></h3>
<p>People with certain types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developmental_disorders" title="Developmental disorders">developmental disorders</a> may struggle more than usual to make and maintain friendships.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder">Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder</span></h4>
<p>Children with <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">attention deficit hyperactivity disorder</a> (ADHD) may have difficulty forming and maintaining friendships, due to a limited ability to build <a href="/wiki/Social_skills" title="Social skills">social skills</a> through <a href="/wiki/Observational_learning" title="Observational learning">observational learning</a>, difficulties attending to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_cues" title="Social cues">social cues</a>, and because of the social impacts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impulsive_behavior" title="Impulsive behavior">impulsive behavior</a> and a greater tendency to engage in behavior that may be seen as disruptive by their peers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoza_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoza-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> In a 2007 review, no treatment was identified which effectively address peer functioning in children with ADHD, and treatments which addressed other aspects of the disorder were not found to eliminate issues related to peer functioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoza_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-hoza-33">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Autism">Autism</span></h4>
<p>Certain symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Autism_spectrum" title="Autism spectrum">autism spectrum</a> disorders can interfere with the formation of interpersonal relations, such as a preference for routine actions, resistance to change, obsession with particular interests or rituals, and a lack of <a href="/wiki/Social_skills" title="Social skills">social skills</a>. Autistic children have been found to be more likely to be close friends of one person, rather than having groups of friends. Additionally, they are more likely to be close friends of other children with some sort of a disability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> A sense of parental attachment aids in the quality of friendships in children with autism spectrum disorders; a sense of attachment with one's parents compensates for a lack of social skills that would usually inhibit friendships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study done by Frankel et al. showed that parental intervention and instruction plays an important role in such children developing friendships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Along with parental intervention, school professionals play an important role in teaching social skills and peer interaction. Paraprofessionals, specifically one-on-one aides and classroom aides, are often placed with children with autism spectrum disorders in order to facilitate friendships and guide the child in making and maintaining substantial friendships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although lessons and training may help peers of children with autism, bullying is still a major concern in social situations. According to Anahad O'Connor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, bullying is most likely to occur against children with autism spectrum disorders who have the most potential to live independently. Such children are more at risk because they have as many of the rituals and lack of social skills as children who are more visibly autistic, but they are more likely to be <a href="/wiki/Mainstreaming_(education)" title="Mainstreaming (education)">mainstreamed</a> in school. Autistic children have more difficulty attending to <a href="/wiki/Social_cue" title="Social cue">social cues</a>, and so may not always recognize when they are being bullied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Down_syndrome">Down syndrome</span></h4>
<p>Children with <a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down syndrome</a> have increased difficulty forming friendships. They experience a <a href="/wiki/Language_delay" title="Language delay">language delay</a> causing them to have a harder time playing with other children. Most children with Down syndrome may prefer to watch other students and play <i>alongside</i> a friend but not <i>with</i> them, mostly because they understand more than they can outwardly express. In preschool years, children with Down syndrome can benefit from the classroom setting, surrounded by other children and less dependent on adult aid. Children with this disability benefit from a variety of interactions with both adults and children. At school, ensuring an inclusive environment in the classroom can be difficult, but proximity to close friends can be crucial for social development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h2>
<p>Studies have found that strong social supports improve a person's prospects for good health and longevity. Conversely, loneliness and a lack of social supports have been linked to an increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">heart disease</a>, viral <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infections</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, as well as higher mortality rates overall. Two researchers have even termed friendship networks a "behavioral <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccine</a>" that boosts both physical and mental health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a large body of research linking friendship and health, but the precise reasons for the connection remain unclear. Most of the studies in this area are large <a href="/wiki/Prospective_cohort_study" title="Prospective cohort study">prospective studies</a> that follow people over time, and while there may be a <a href="/wiki/Correlation_does_not_imply_causation" title="Correlation does not imply causation">correlation</a> between the two variables (friendship and health status), researchers still do not know if there is a cause and effect relationship, such as the notion that good friendships actually improve health. A number of theories have attempted to explain this link. These theories have included that good friends encourage their friends to lead more healthy lifestyles; that good friends encourage their friends to seek help and access services when needed; that good friends enhance their friends' coping skills in dealing with illness and other health problems; and that good friends actually affect physiological pathways that are protective of health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_health">Mental health</span></h3>
<p>The lack of friendship has been found to play a role in increasing risk of <a href="/wiki/Suicidal_ideation" title="Suicidal ideation">suicidal ideation</a> among female adolescents, including having more friends who were not themselves friends with one another. However, no similar effect was observed for males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> Having few or no friends is a major indicator in the diagnosis of a range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_disorders" title="Mental disorders">mental disorders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reisman_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-reisman-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Higher friendship quality directly contributes to self-esteem, self-confidence, and social development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www2.psych.purdue.edu_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-www2.psych.purdue.edu-23">[22]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/World_Database_of_Happiness" title="World Database of Happiness">World Happiness Database</a> study found that people with close friendships are happier, although the absolute number of friends did not increase happiness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Other studies have suggested that children who have friendships of a high quality may be protected against the development of certain disorders, such as anxiety and depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Conversely, having few friends is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school_dropouts" title="High school dropouts">dropping out of school</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Aggression" title="Aggression">aggression</a>, and adult <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bremner_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bremner-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 500">: 500 </span></sup> Peer rejection is also associated with lower later aspiration in the <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">workforce</a>, and participation in social activities, while higher levels of friendship was associated with higher adult <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bremner_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-bremner-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 500–01">: 500–01 </span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution">Dissolution</span></h2>
<p>The dissolution of a friendship may be viewed as a personal rejection, or may be the result of natural changes over time, as friends grow more distant both physically and emotionally. The disruption of friendships has been associated with increased <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(emotion)" title="Guilt (emotion)">guilt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, and may be highly stressful events, especially in childhood. However, potential negative effects can be mitigated if the dissolution of a friendship is replaced with another close relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 248">: 248 </span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span></h2>
<p>Friends tend to be more similar to one another in terms of age, gender, behavior, <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a>, personal disposition, and academic performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 248">: 248 </span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enc_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-enc-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 426">: 426 </span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brain_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-brain-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 55–56">: 55–56 </span></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">ethnically diverse</a> countries, there is broad evidence that children and adolescents tend to form friendships with others of the same race or ethnicity, beginning in <a href="/wiki/Preschool" title="Preschool">preschool</a>, and peaking in middle or late childhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 264">: 264 </span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_differences">Gender differences</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Cleanup_rewrite plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg"><img alt="Crystal Clear app kedit.svg" data-file-height="128" data-file-width="128" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/40px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/60px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/80px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section may need to be <b>rewritten</b> to comply with Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">quality standards</a>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Friendship&amp;action=edit">You can help</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Talk:Friendship" title="Talk:Friendship">talk page</a> may contain suggestions.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2020</span>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In general, female-female friendship interactions among children tend to focus on interpersonal connections and <a href="/wiki/Social_support" title="Social support">mutual support</a>. In contrast, male-male interaction tends to be more focused on <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">social status</a>. As a result, they may actively discourage the expression of emotional needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 320–02">: 320–02 </span></sup>
</p><p>Females report more anxiety, jealousy, and relational victimization and less stability related to their friendships. Males, on the other hand, report higher levels of physical victimization. Nevertheless, males and females tend to report relative satisfaction levels with their friendships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 249–50">: 249–50 </span></sup>
</p><p>Women tend to be more expressive and intimate in their same-sex friendships and have a smaller range of friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[15]</a></sup> Males are more likely to define intimacy in terms of shared physical experiences. In contrast, females are more likely to define it in shared emotional ones. Males are less likely to make emotional or personal disclosures to other males because they could use this information against them. However, they will disclose this information to females (as they are not in competition with them), and males tend to regard friendships with females as more meaningful, intimate, and pleasant. Male-male friendships are generally more like alliances, while female-female friendships are much more attachment-based. As a result, this also means that the end of male-male friendships tends to be less emotionally upsetting than that of female-female friendships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Women tend to be more socially adept than their male peers among older adults. As a result, many older men may rely upon a female companion, such as a spouse, to compensate for their comparative lack of social skills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brain_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-brain-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 55">: 55 </span></sup> One study found that women in Europe and North America were slightly more likely than men to self-report having a best friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span></h3>
<p>Which relationships count as true friendships, rather than as an acquaintance or a co-worker, varies by culture.  In English-speaking cultures, it is not unusual for people to include weaker relationships as being friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-53">[52]</a></sup>  In other cultures, such as the Russian and Polish cultures, only the most significant relationships are considered friends.  A Russian might have one or two friends plus a large number of "pals" or acquaintances; a Canadian in similar circumstances might count all of these relationships as being friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-53">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western cultures</a>, friendships are often seen as lesser to familial or romantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>When discussing taboos of friendship it was found that Chinese respondents found more than their British counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag is ambiguous, and needs clarification. (November 2021)">ambiguous</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interspecies">Interspecies</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">Ethology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altruism_in_animals" title="Altruism in animals">Altruism in animals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sociobiology" title="Sociobiology">Sociobiology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="3648" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG/220px-CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG/330px-CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG/440px-CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CUTE_FRIENDSHIP.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A man with an <a href="/wiki/Indian_palm_squirrel" title="Indian palm squirrel">Indian palm squirrel</a>, (<i>Funambulus palmarum</i>)</div></div></div>
<p>Friendship is found among animals of higher intelligence, such as higher <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. Cross-species friendships are common between humans and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_animal" title="Domestic animal">domestic animals</a>. Cross-species friendships may also occur between two non-human animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>.
</p>
