<p>An <b>island</b> (or <b>isle</b>) is an isolated piece of habitat that is surrounded by a dramatically different habitat, such as water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Very small islands such as emergent land features on <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a> can be called <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skerry" title="Skerry">skerries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cay" title="Cay">cays</a> or keys. An <a href="/wiki/River_island" title="River island">island in a river</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Lake_island" title="Lake island">lake island</a> may be called an <a href="/wiki/Ait" title="Ait">eyot or ait</a>, and a small island off the coast may be called a <a href="/wiki/Holm_(island)" title="Holm (island)">holm</a>. Sedimentary islands in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganges_delta" title="Ganges delta">Ganges delta</a> are called <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Bangladesh" title="List of islands of Bangladesh">chars</a>. A grouping of geographically or geologically related islands, such as the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, is referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>.
</p><p>There are two main types of islands in the sea: continental and oceanic. There are also <a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">artificial islands</a>, which are man-made.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>island</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>iland</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>igland</i> (from <i>ig</i> or <i>ieg</i>, similarly meaning 'island' when used independently, and <a href="/wiki/-land" title="-land">-land</a> carrying its contemporary meaning; cf. <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>eiland</i> ("island"), <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Eiland</i> ("small island")). However, the spelling of the word was modified in the 15th century because of a <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">false etymology</a> caused by an incorrect association with the <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymologically</a> unrelated Old French loanword <i>isle</i>, which itself comes from the Latin word <i>insula</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Old English <i>ieg</i> is actually a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> of Swedish <i>ö</i> and German <i>Aue</i>, and related to Latin <i>aqua</i> (water).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_continents">Relationships with continents</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differentiation_from_continents">Differentiation from continents</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg/300px-Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg/450px-Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg/600px-Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuller_projection_with_largest_islands.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuller_projection" title="Fuller projection">Dymaxion world map</a> with the continental landmasses (Roman numerals) and 30 largest islands (Arabic numerals) roughly to scale</div></div></div>
<p>There is no standard of size that distinguishes islands from <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1-5">[5]</a></sup> or from <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google7_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-google7-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a widely accepted difference between islands and continents in terms of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Continents are often considered to be the largest <a href="/wiki/Landmass" title="Landmass">landmass</a> of a particular <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">continental plate</a>; this holds true for <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australia</a>, which sits on its own continental lithosphere and tectonic plate (the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Plate" title="Australian Plate">Australian Plate</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, islands are usually seen as being extensions of the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> (e.g. volcanic islands), or as belonging to a continental plate containing a larger landmass (continental islands); the latter is the case of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, which sits on the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">North American Plate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_islands">Continental islands</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">Continental shelf</a></div>
<p>Continental islands are bodies of land that lie on the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Examples are <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_(island)" title="Java (island)">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a> off <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>; <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a> off <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> off <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>; <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sable_Island" title="Sable Island">Sable Island</a> off <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> off <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Microcontinental_islands">Microcontinental islands</span></h4>
<p>A special type of continental island is the microcontinental island, which is created when a continent is horizontally displaced or <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rifted</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-microcont_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-microcont-12">[12]</a></sup> Examples are <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a> off <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_New_Caledonia" title="Geography of New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and some of the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-microcont_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-microcont-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bars">Bars</span></h4>
<p>Another subtype is an island or <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">bar</a> formed by deposition of tiny rocks where water current loses some of its carrying capacity. This includes:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier islands</a>, which are accumulations of <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> deposited by sea currents on the continental shelves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluvial_processes" title="Fluvial processes">fluvial</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvial</a> islands formed in <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> or midstream within large rivers. While some are transitory and may disappear if the volume or speed of the current changes, others are stable and long-lived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_islands">Oceanic islands</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/High_island" title="High island">High island</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonic_versus_volcanic">Tectonic versus volcanic</span></h4>
<p>Oceanic islands are typically considered to be islands that do not sit on continental shelves. Other definitions limit the term to only refer to islands with no past geological connections to a continental landmass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zug_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-zug-16">[16]</a></sup> The vast majority are <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> in origin, such as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Ocean" title="South Atlantic Ocean">South Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The few oceanic islands that are not volcanic are <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic</a> in origin and arise where plate movements have lifted up the ocean floor above the surface. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter_and_Saint_Paul_Archipelago" title="Saint Peter and Saint Paul Archipelago">Saint Peter and Saint Paul Archipelago</a> in the North Atlantic Ocean and <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Island" title="Macquarie Island">Macquarie Island</a> in the South Pacific Ocean.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_islands">Volcanic islands</span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Arcs">Arcs</span></h5>
<p>One type of volcanic oceanic island is found in a volcanic island arc. These islands arise from volcanoes where the <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> of one plate under another is occurring. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>, and most of <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The only examples in the Atlantic Ocean are some of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Sandwich Islands">South Sandwich Islands</a>.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_Rifts">Oceanic Rifts</span></h5>
<p>Another type of volcanic oceanic island occurs where an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_rift" title="Oceanic rift">oceanic rift</a> reaches the surface. There are two examples: <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, which is the world's second largest volcanic island, and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a>. Both are in the Atlantic.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Hotspots">Hotspots</span></h5>
<p>A third type of volcanic oceanic island is formed over volcanic <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a>. A hotspot is more or less stationary relative to the moving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> above it, so a chain of islands results as the plate drifts. Over long periods of time, this type of island is eventually "drowned" by <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic adjustment</a> and eroded, becoming a <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamount</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Plate movement across a hot-spot produces a line of islands oriented in the direction of the plate movement. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kure_Atoll" title="Kure Atoll">Kure</a>, which continue beneath the sea surface in a more northerly direction as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Seamounts" title="Emperor Seamounts">Emperor Seamounts</a>. Another chain with similar orientation is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuamotu_Archipelago" title="Tuamotu Archipelago">Tuamotu Archipelago</a>; its older, northerly trend is the <a href="/wiki/Line_Islands" title="Line Islands">Line Islands</a>. The southernmost chain is the <a href="/wiki/Austral_Islands" title="Austral Islands">Austral Islands</a>, with its northerly trending part the atolls in the nation of <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha</a> is an example of a hotspot volcano in the Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Another hotspot in the Atlantic is the island of <a href="/wiki/Surtsey" title="Surtsey">Surtsey</a>, which was formed in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Atolls">Atolls</span></h5>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">Atoll</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atoll</a> is an island formed from a <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a> that has grown on an eroded and submerged volcanic island. The <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reef</a> rises to the surface of the water and forms a new island. Atolls are typically ring-shaped with a central <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoon</a>. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Line_Islands" title="Line Islands">Line Islands</a> in the Pacific and the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_islands">Tropical islands</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Landing_Runway_18_(2121588367).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Landing_Runway_18_%282121588367%29.jpg/220px-Landing_Runway_18_%282121588367%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Landing_Runway_18_%282121588367%29.jpg/330px-Landing_Runway_18_%282121588367%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Landing_Runway_18_%282121588367%29.jpg/440px-Landing_Runway_18_%282121588367%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Landing_Runway_18_(2121588367).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plane landing on an airport island, <a href="/wiki/Velana_International_Airport" title="Velana International Airport">Velana International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hulhul%C3%A9_Island" title="Hulhulé Island">Hulhulé Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Low_island" title="Low island">Low island</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coral_island" title="Coral island">Coral island</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef#Formation" title="Coral reef">Coral reef § Formation</a></div>
<p>Approximately 45,000 <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> islands with an <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> of at least 5 hectares (12 acres) exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TI_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TI-24">[24]</a></sup> Examples <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef#Formation" title="Coral reef">formed from coral reefs</a> include <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TI_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-TI-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">Granite</a> islands include <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tioman_Island" title="Tioman Island">Tioman</a>.
</p><p>The socio-economic diversity of tropical islands ranges from the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> societies in the interior of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Sentinel" title="North Sentinel">North Sentinel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> to the high-tech lifestyles of the city-islands of <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> International <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> is a significant factor in the economy of many tropical islands including Seychelles, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>,  <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="De-islanding">De-islanding</span></h2>
<p>The process of de-islandisation is often concerning <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridging</a>, but there are other forms of linkages such as <a href="/wiki/Causeway" title="Causeway">causeways</a>: fixed transport links across narrow necks of water, some of which are only operative at low tides (e.g. that connecting Cornwall's <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Mount" title="St Michael's Mount">St Michael's Mount</a> to the peninsular mainland) while others (such as the <a href="/wiki/Canso_Causeway" title="Canso Causeway">Canso Causeway</a> connecting <a href="/wiki/Cape_Breton_Island" title="Cape Breton Island">Cape Breton</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> mainland), are usable all-year-round (aside from interruptions during storm surge periods).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-istudies_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-istudies-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some places may retain "island" in their names for historical reasons after being connected to a larger landmass by a land bridge or landfill, such as <a href="/wiki/Coney_Island" title="Coney Island">Coney Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coronado,_California" title="Coronado, California">Coronado Island</a>, though these are, strictly speaking, <a href="/wiki/Tied_island" title="Tied island">tied islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-istudies_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-istudies-28">[28]</a></sup> Conversely, when a piece of land is separated from the <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">mainland</a> by a man-made canal, for example the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Corinth_Canal" title="Corinth Canal">Corinth Canal</a>, more or less the entirety of <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Sea_Canal" title="White Sea Canal">White Sea Canal</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Marble_Hill,_Manhattan" title="Marble Hill, Manhattan">Marble Hill</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> during the time between the building of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Ship_Canal" title="United States Ship Canal">United States Ship Canal</a> and the filling-in of the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_River" title="Harlem River">Harlem River</a> which surrounded the area, it is generally not considered an island.
</p><p>Another type of connection is fostered by harbour walls/breakwaters that incorporate offshore islets into their structures, such as those in Sai harbour in northern <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a>, Japan, and the connection to the mainland which transformed Ilhéu do Diego from an islet. De-islanded through its fixed link to the mainland, the former islet's name, Ilhéu do Diego, became functionally redundant (and thereby archaic) and the location took the fort as its namesake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Some former island sites have retained designations as islands after the draining/subsidence of surrounding waters and their fixed linkage to land (England’s <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a> and Vancouver’s <a href="/wiki/Granville_Island" title="Granville Island">Granville Island</a> being respective cases in point). Their names are thereby archaic in that they reflect the islands’ pasts rather than their present structures and/or transport logistics. Other examples include <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Johor%E2%80%93Singapore_Causeway" title="Johor–Singapore Causeway">causeway</a>, and the various <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> delta islands, such as <a href="/wiki/IJsselmonde_(island)" title="IJsselmonde (island)">IJsselmonde</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_islands">Artificial islands</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">Artificial island</a></div>
<p>Almost all of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s islands are natural and have been formed by tectonic forces or volcanic eruptions. However, artificial (man-made) islands also exist, such as the island in <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Bay" title="Osaka Bay">Osaka Bay</a> off the Japanese island of <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a>, on which <a href="/wiki/Kansai_International_Airport" title="Kansai International Airport">Kansai International Airport</a> is located. Artificial islands can be built using natural materials (e.g., earth, rock, or sand) or artificial ones (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> slabs or recycled <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sometimes natural islands are artificially enlarged, such as <a href="/wiki/Vasilyevsky_Island" title="Vasilyevsky Island">Vasilyevsky Island</a> in the Russian city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>, which had its western shore extended westward by some 0.5 km in the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Passenger_Port_of_St._Petersburg" title="Passenger Port of St. Petersburg">Passenger Port of St. Petersburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3676" data-file-width="5000" decoding="async" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg/220px-Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg/330px-Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg/440px-Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kansai_International_Airport_Aerial_photograph.2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kansai_International_Airport" title="Kansai International Airport">Kansai International Airport</a>, on an artificial island</div></div></div>
<p>Artificial islands are sometimes built on pre-existing "low-tide elevation," a naturally formed area of land which is surrounded by and above water at low <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a> but submerged at high tide.  Legally these are not islands and have no territorial sea of their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
