<p>A <b>river</b> is a natural flowing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercourse" title="Watercourse">watercourse</a>, usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>, flowing towards an <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> or another river. In some cases, a river flows into the ground and becomes dry at the end of its course without reaching another body of water. Small rivers can be referred to using names such as creek, brook, rivulet, and <a href="/wiki/Rill" title="Rill">rill</a>. There are no official definitions for the generic term river as applied to <a href="/wiki/Geographical_feature" title="Geographical feature">geographic features</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GNIS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GNIS-1">[1]</a></sup> although in some countries or communities a <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> is defined by its size. Many names for small rivers are specific to geographic location; examples are "run" in some parts of the United States, "<a href="/wiki/Burn_(landform)" title="Burn (landform)">burn</a>" in Scotland and northeast England, and "beck" in northern England. Sometimes a river is defined as being larger than a creek,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> but not always: the language is vague.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GNIS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GNIS-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg/220px-Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg/330px-Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg/440px-Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Melting_Toe_of_Athabasca_Glacier.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Melting toe of Athabasca Glacier, <a href="/wiki/Jasper_National_Park" title="Jasper National Park">Jasper National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta, Canada</a></div></div></div>
<p>Rivers are part of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>. Water generally collects in a river from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> from <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> and other sources such as <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrology)" title="Spring (hydrology)">springs</a>, and the release of stored water in natural ice and <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">snowpacks</a>.
</p><p>Rivers are often considered major features within a landscape; however, they actually only cover around 0.1% of the land on Earth. They are made more obvious and significant to humans since many human cities and civilizations are built around the freshwater supplied by rivers and streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Most of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_cities" title="List of largest cities">major cities of the world</a> are situated on the banks of rivers, as they are, or were, used as a <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">source of water</a>, for obtaining food, for <a href="/wiki/Maritime_transport" title="Maritime transport">transport</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a>, as a defensive measure, as a source of <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> to drive machinery, for <a href="/wiki/Bathing" title="Bathing">bathing</a>, and as a means of disposing of <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>. In the pre-industrial era, larger rivers were a major obstruction to the movement of people, goods, and armies across them. Towns often developed at the few locations they could be crossed. Many major cities such as <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> are located at the lowest point at which a river could be bridged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Potamology is the scientific study of rivers, while <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnology</a> is the study of inland waters in general.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_and_drainage_basin">Source and drainage basin</span></h3>
<p>A river begins at a <a href="/wiki/River_source" title="River source">source</a> (or more often several sources) which is usually a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">watershed</a>, drains all the streams in its <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>, follows a watercourse, and ends either at a <a href="/wiki/River_mouth" title="River mouth">mouth</a> or mouths which could be a <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river delta</a>, etc. The water in a river is usually confined to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_channel" title="Stream channel">channel</a>, made up of a <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream bed</a> between <a href="/wiki/Bank_(geography)" title="Bank (geography)">banks</a>. In larger rivers there is often also a wider <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplain</a> shaped by <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a>-waters over-topping the channel. Floodplains may be very wide in relation to the size of the river channel. This distinction between river channel and floodplain can be blurred, especially in urban areas where the floodplain of a river channel can become greatly developed by housing and industry. 
</p><p>The term upriver (or upstream) refers to the direction towards the source of the river, i.e. against the direction of <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">flow</a>. Likewise, the term downriver (or downstream) describes the direction towards the mouth of the river, in which the <a href="/wiki/Current_(stream)" title="Current (stream)">current</a> flows. The term left bank refers to the left bank in the direction of flow, right bank to the right in the direction of flow.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Channels">Channels</span></h3>
<p>Rivers can flow down mountains, through <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valleys</a> or along <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plains</a>, and can create <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">canyons</a> or gorges. The river channel typically contains a single stream, but some rivers flow as several interconnecting streams, producing a <a href="/wiki/Braided_river" title="Braided river">braided river</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Braided rivers occur on <a href="/wiki/Peneplain" title="Peneplain">peneplains</a> and some of the larger <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anastomosis" title="Anastomosis">Anastamosing rivers</a> are similar to braided rivers and are quite rare; they have multiple sinuous channel - 1 carrying large volumes of sediment. There are rare cases of <a href="/wiki/River_bifurcation" title="River bifurcation">river bifurcation</a> in which a river divides, and the resultant flows end in different seas. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Nerodime_River" title="Nerodime River">Nerodime River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a>.
</p><p>A river flowing in its channel is a source of energy that acts on the river channel to change its shape and form. In 1757, German hydrologist <a href="/wiki/Albert_Brahms" title="Albert Brahms">Albert Brahms</a> empirically observed that the submerged weight of objects that may be carried away by a river is proportional to the sixth power of the river flow speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garde,_1995,_14_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garde,_1995,_14-6">[6]</a></sup> This formulation is also sometimes called Airy's law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garde,_1995,_19_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garde,_1995,_19-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus, if the speed of flow is doubled, the flow would dislodge objects with 64 times as much submerged weight. In mountainous torrential zones, this can be seen as erosion channels through hard rocks and the creation of sands and gravels from the destruction of larger rocks. A river valley that was created from a U-shaped <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciated</a> valley can often easily be identified by the V-shaped channel that it has carved. 
</p><p>In the middle reaches where a river flows over flatter land, <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meanders</a> may form through erosion of the river banks and deposition on the inside of bends. Sometimes the river will cut off a loop, shortening the channel and forming an <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">oxbow lake</a> or <a href="/wiki/Billabong" title="Billabong">billabong</a>. Rivers that carry large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> may develop conspicuous deltas at their mouths. Rivers whose mouths are in saline <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal</a> waters may form <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>.
</p><p>Throughout the course of the river, the total volume of water transported downstream will often be a combination of the free water flow together with a substantial volume flowing through sub-surface rocks and gravels that underlie the river and its floodplain (called the <a href="/wiki/Hyporheic_zone" title="Hyporheic zone">hyporheic zone</a>). For many rivers in large valleys, this unseen component of flow may greatly exceed the visible flow.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_and_ratings">Types and ratings</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NileDelta-EO.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="565" data-file-width="496" decoding="async" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/NileDelta-EO.JPG/220px-NileDelta-EO.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/NileDelta-EO.JPG/330px-NileDelta-EO.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/NileDelta-EO.JPG/440px-NileDelta-EO.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NileDelta-EO.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River_delta" title="Nile River delta">Nile River delta</a>, as seen from Earth orbit. The Nile is an example of a wave-dominated delta that has the classic Greek letter delta (Δ) shape after which river deltas were named.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1957" data-file-width="372" decoding="async" height="894" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg/170px-PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg/255px-PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg/340px-PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA16197_Titan_river.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A radar image of a 400-kilometre (250 mi) river of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> near the north pole of Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Lakes_of_Titan" title="Lakes of Titan">Titan</a></div></div></div>
<p>Rivers have been classified by many criteria including their <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, their <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">biotic</a> status, and their relevance to white water <a href="/wiki/Rafting" title="Rafting">rafting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoeing</a> activities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsurface_rivers:_subterranean_and_subglacial">Subsurface rivers: subterranean and subglacial</span></h3>
<p>Most but not all rivers flow on the surface. <a href="/wiki/Subterranean_river" title="Subterranean river">Subterranean rivers</a> flow underground in <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>. Such rivers are frequently found in regions with <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> <a href="/wiki/Geological_formation" title="Geological formation">geologic formations</a>. <a href="/wiki/Subglacial_stream" title="Subglacial stream">Subglacial streams</a> are the braided rivers that flow at the beds of glaciers and <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, permitting meltwater to be discharged at the front of the glacier. Because of the gradient in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> from the overlying weight of the glacier, such streams can even flow uphill.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanence_of_flow:_perennial_and_ephemeral">Permanence of flow: perennial and ephemeral</span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_river" title="Intermittent river">intermittent river</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Ephemerality" title="Ephemerality">ephemeral</a> river) only flows occasionally and can be dry for several years at a time. These rivers are found in regions with limited or highly variable rainfall, or can occur because of geologic conditions such as a highly <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(Earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (Earth sciences)">permeable</a> river bed. Some ephemeral rivers flow during the summer months but not in the winter. Such rivers are typically fed from <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> aquifers which recharge from winter rainfall. In England these rivers are called <i>bournes</i> and give their name to places such as <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastbourne" title="Eastbourne">Eastbourne</a>. Even in humid regions, the location where flow begins in the smallest <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributary</a> streams generally moves upstream in response to precipitation and downstream in its absence or when active summer vegetation diverts water for evapotranspiration. Normally dry rivers in arid zones are often identified as <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_(creek)" title="Arroyo (creek)">arroyos</a> or other regional names.
</p><p>The meltwater from large hailstorms can create a <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">slurry</a> of water, hail and sand or soil, forming temporary rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stream_order_classification">Stream order classification</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Strahler_number" title="Strahler number">Strahler Stream Order</a> ranks rivers based on the connectivity and hierarchy of contributing tributaries. Headwaters are first order while the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> is twelfth order. Approximately 80% of the rivers in the world are of the first and second order.
</p><p>The ways in which a river's characteristics vary between its upper and lower course are summarized by the <a href="/wiki/Bradshaw_model" title="Bradshaw model">Bradshaw model</a>. Power-law relationships between channel slope, depth, and width are given as a function of discharge by "<a href="/wiki/River_regime" title="River regime">river regime</a>".
</p><p>In certain languages, distinctions are made among rivers based on their stream order. In French, for example, rivers that run to the sea are called <i>fleuve</i>, while other rivers are called <i>rivière</i>. For example, in Canada, the <a href="/wiki/Churchill_River_(Hudson_Bay)" title="Churchill River (Hudson Bay)">Churchill River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> is called <i>la rivière Churchill</i> as it runs to <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Churchill_River_(Atlantic)" title="Churchill River (Atlantic)">Churchill River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> is called <i>le fleuve Churchill</i> as it runs to the Atlantic Ocean. As most rivers in France are known by their names only without the word <i>rivière</i> or <i>fleuve</i> (e.g. <i>la <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">Seine</a></i>, not <i>le fleuve Seine</i>, even though the Seine is classed as a <i>fleuve</i>), one of the most prominent rivers in the Francophone commonly known as <i>fleuve</i> is <i>le fleuve Saint-Laurent</i> (the <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a>). Since many <i>fleuves</i> are large and prominent, receiving many tributaries, the word is sometimes used to refer to certain large rivers that flow into other <i>fleuves</i>; however, even small streams that run to the sea are called <i>fleuve</i> (e.g. <i><a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fleuve_c%C3%B4tier" title="fr:Fleuve côtier">fleuve côtier</a></i>, "coastal <i>fleuve</i>").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topographical_classification">Topographical classification</span></h3>
<p>Rivers can generally be classified as either <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_river" title="Alluvial river">alluvial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bedrock_river" title="Bedrock river">bedrock</a>, or some mix of the two. Alluvial rivers have channels and floodplains that are self-formed in unconsolidated or weakly consolidated sediments. They <a href="/wiki/Bank_erosion" title="Bank erosion">erode</a> their banks and deposit material on <a href="/wiki/Bar_(river_morphology)" title="Bar (river morphology)">bars</a> and their floodplains. 
</p><p>Bedrock rivers form when the river downcuts through the modern sediments and into the underlying bedrock. This occurs in regions that have experienced some kind of uplift (thereby steepening river gradients) or in which a particularly hard <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">lithology</a> causes a river to have a steepened reach that has not been covered in modern <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvium</a>. Bedrock rivers very often contain alluvium on their beds; this material is important in eroding and sculpting the channel. Rivers that go through patches of bedrock and patches of deep alluvial cover are classified as mixed bedrock-alluvial.
</p><p>Alluvial rivers can be further classified by their <a href="/wiki/Channel_pattern" title="Channel pattern">channel pattern</a> as meandering, braided, wandering, anastomose, or straight. The morphology of an alluvial river reach is controlled by a combination of sediment supply, substrate composition, discharge, vegetation, and bed <a href="/wiki/Aggradation" title="Aggradation">aggradation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotic_classification">Biotic classification</span></h3>
<p>There are several systems of classification based on ecological conditions typically assigning classes from the most <a href="/wiki/Oligotroph" title="Oligotroph">oligotrophic</a> or unpolluted through to the most <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophic</a> or polluted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other systems are based on a whole eco-system approach such as developed by the New Zealand Ministry for the Environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In Europe, the requirements of the <a href="/wiki/Water_Framework_Directive" title="Water Framework Directive">Water Framework Directive</a> has led to the development of a wide range of classification methods including classifications based on fishery status<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>A system of river zonation used in francophone communities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IlliesBotosaneanu1963_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IlliesBotosaneanu1963-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hawkes1975_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hawkes1975-13">[13]</a></sup>  divides rivers into three primary zones:
</p>
<ul><li>The <i>crenon</i> is the uppermost zone at the source of the river. It is further divided into the eucrenon (spring or boil zone) and the hypocrenon (brook or headstream zone). These areas have low temperatures, reduced oxygen content and slow moving water.</li>
<li>The <i>rhithron</i> is the upstream portion of the river that follows the crenon. It has relatively cool temperatures, high oxygen levels, and fast, turbulent, swift flow.</li>
<li>The <i>potamon</i> is the remaining downstream stretch of river. It has warmer temperatures, lower oxygen levels, slow flow and sandier bottoms.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigability">Navigability</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_scale_of_river_difficulty" title="International scale of river difficulty">international scale of river difficulty</a> is used to rate the challenges of navigation—particularly those with <a href="/wiki/Rapids" title="Rapids">rapids</a>. Class I is the easiest and Class VI is the hardest.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Streamflow">Streamflow</span></h2>
<p>Studying the flows of rivers is one aspect of <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cave_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cave-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Direction">Direction</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:217px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RiverMeanderingCourse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="215" decoding="async" height="384" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/RiverMeanderingCourse.jpg" width="215"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RiverMeanderingCourse.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>River meandering course</div></div></div>
<p>Rivers flow downhill with their power derived from gravity. The direction can involve all directions of the compass and can be a complex meandering path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenberg2006_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenberg2006-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenberg1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenberg1-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rydell1997_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rydell1997-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rivers flowing downhill, from river source to river mouth, do not necessarily take the shortest path. For alluvial streams, straight and braided rivers have very low sinuosity and flow directly down hill, while meandering rivers flow from side to side across a valley. Bedrock rivers typically flow in either a <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a> pattern, or a pattern that is determined by weaknesses in the bedrock, such as <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faults</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">fractures</a>, or more erodible layers.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate">Rate</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">Volumetric flow rate</a>, also known as discharge, volume flow rate, and rate of water flow, is the volume of water which passes through a given cross-section of the river channel per unit time. It is typically measured in <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre_per_second" title="Cubic metre per second">cubic metres per second</a> (cumec) or cubic feet per second (cfs).
</p><p>Volumetric flow rate can be thought of as the mean velocity of the flow through a given cross-section, times that cross-sectional area. Mean velocity can be approximated through the use of the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_wall" title="Law of the wall">law of the wall</a>. In general, velocity increases with the depth (or <a href="/wiki/Manning_formula" title="Manning formula">hydraulic radius</a>) and slope of the river channel, while the cross-sectional area scales with the depth and the width: the double-counting of depth shows the importance of this variable in determining the discharge through the channel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluvial_erosion">Fluvial erosion</span></h4>
<p>In its youthful stage, a river causes erosion in the watercourse, deepening the valley. <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_action" title="Hydraulic action">Hydraulic action</a> loosens and dislodges aggregate which further erodes the banks and the river bed. Over time, this deepens the river bed and creates steeper sides which are then weathered. The steepened nature of the banks causes the sides of the valley to move downslope causing the valley to become <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">V-shaped</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">Waterfalls</a> also form in the youthful river valley where a band of hard rock overlays a layer of soft rock. <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">Differential erosion</a> occurs as the river erodes the soft rock more readily than the hard rock, this leaves the hard rock more elevated and stands out from the river below. A <a href="/wiki/Plunge_pool" title="Plunge pool">plunge pool</a> forms at the bottom and deepens as a result of hydraulic action and abrasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flooding">Flooding</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="511" data-file-width="770" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg/220px-Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg/330px-Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg/440px-Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trapped_woman_on_a_car_roof_during_flash_flooding_in_Toowoomba_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">Flash flooding</a> caused by a large amount of rain falling in a short amount of time</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg"><img alt="The mouth of the River Seaton in Cornwall after heavy rain caused flooding and significant erosion of the beach" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg/220px-Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg/330px-Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg/440px-Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seaton_Beach_after_heavy_rainfall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Seaton" title="River Seaton">River Seaton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> after heavy rain caused flooding and significant erosion of the beach.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">Flooding</a> is a natural part of a river's cycle. The majority of the erosion of river channels and the erosion and deposition on the associated floodplains occur during the <a href="/wiki/Flood_stage" title="Flood stage">flood stage</a>. In many developed areas, human activity has changed the form of river channels, altering magnitudes and frequencies of flooding. Some examples of this are the building of levees, the straightening of channels, and the draining of natural wetlands. 
</p><p>In many cases human activities in rivers and floodplains have dramatically increased the risk of flooding. Straightening rivers allows water to flow more rapidly downstream, increasing the risk of flooding places further downstream. Building on flood plains removes flood storage, which again exacerbates downstream flooding. The building of levees only protects the area behind the levees and not those further downstream. Levees and flood-banks can also increase flooding upstream because of the back-water pressure as the river flow is impeded by the narrow channel banks. <a href="/wiki/Detention_basin" title="Detention basin">Detention basins</a> finally also reduce the risk of flooding significantly by being able to take up some of the flood water.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sediment_yield">Sediment yield</span></h4>
<p>Sediment yield is the total quantity of particulate matter (suspended or bedload) reaching the outlet of a drainage basin over a fixed time frame. Yield is usually expressed as kilograms per square kilometre per year. Sediment delivery processes are affected by a myriad of factors such as drainage area size, basin slope, climate, sediment type (lithology), vegetation cover, and human land use / management practices.
</p><p>The theoretical concept of the 'sediment delivery ratio' (ratio between yield and total amount of sediment eroded) indicates that not all of the sediment is eroded within a certain catchment that reaches out to the outlet (e.g., deposition on floodplains). Such storage opportunities are typically increased in catchments of larger size, thus leading to a lower yield and sediment delivery ratio.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brackish_water">Brackish water</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">Brackish water</a> occurs in most rivers where they meet the sea. The extent of brackish water may extend a significant distance upstream, especially in areas with high tidal ranges.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="River_biota">River biota</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/River_ecosystem" title="River ecosystem">River ecosystem</a></div>
<p>The organisms in the <a href="/wiki/Riparian_zone" title="Riparian zone">riparian zone</a> respond to changes in river channel location and patterns of flow. The ecosystem of rivers is generally described by the <a href="/wiki/River_Continuum_Concept" title="River Continuum Concept">river continuum concept</a>, which has some additions and refinements to allow for dams and waterfalls and temporary extensive flooding. The concept describes the river as a system in which the physical parameters, the availability of food particles and the composition of the ecosystem are continuously changing along its length. The food (energy) that remains from the upstream part is used downstream.
</p><p>The general pattern is that the first order streams contain particulate matter (decaying leaves from the surrounding forests) which is processed there by shredders like <a href="/wiki/Plecoptera" title="Plecoptera">Plecoptera</a> larvae. The products of these shredders are used by collectors, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydropsychidae" title="Hydropsychidae">Hydropsychidae</a>, and further downstream algae that create the <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a> become the main food source of the organisms. All changes are gradual and the distribution of each species can be described as a <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">normal curve</a>, with the highest density where the conditions are optimal. In rivers <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a> is virtually absent and the composition of the ecosystem stays fixed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_chemistry" title="River chemistry">River chemistry</a></div>
<p>The chemistry of rivers is complex and depends on inputs from the atmosphere, the geology through which it travels and the inputs from man's activities. The chemical composition of the water has a large impact on the ecology of that water for both plants and animals and it also affects the uses that may be made of the river water. Understanding and characterizing river water chemistry requires a well designed and managed sampling and analysis.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1502" data-file-width="2122" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg/220px-River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg/330px-River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg/440px-River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:River_avon_at_keynsham_arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leisure activities on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Avon_(Bristol)" title="River Avon (Bristol)">River Avon</a> at Avon Valley Country Park, <a href="/wiki/Keynsham" title="Keynsham">Keynsham</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. A boat giving trips to the public passes a moored private boat.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_material">Construction material</span></h3>
<p>The coarse sediments, <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, generated and moved by rivers are extensively used in construction. In parts of the world this can generate extensive new lake habitats as <a href="/wiki/Gravel_pit" title="Gravel pit">gravel pits</a> fill with water. In other circumstances it can destabilize the river bed, and the course of the river and cause severe damage to <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> fish populations which rely on stable gravel formations for egg laying. In upland rivers, rapids with <a href="/wiki/Whitewater" title="Whitewater">whitewater</a> or even waterfalls occur. Rapids are often used for recreation, such as <a href="/wiki/Whitewater_kayaking" title="Whitewater kayaking">whitewater kayaking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_production">Energy production</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg/220px-Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg/330px-Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg/440px-Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Watermill in Belgium.</div></div></div>
<p>Fast flowing rivers and waterfalls are widely used as sources of energy, via <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric plants</a>. Evidence of watermills shows them in use for many hundreds of years, for instance in <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dounby_Click_Mill" title="Dounby Click Mill">Dounby Click Mill</a>. Prior to the invention of <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam power</a>, watermills for grinding cereals and for processing wool and other textiles were common across Europe. In the 1890s the first machines to generate power from river water were established at places such as <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">Cragside</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> and in recent decades there has been a significant increase in the development of large scale power generation from water, especially in wet mountainous regions such as Norway.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_source">Food source</span></h3>
<p>Rivers have been a source of food since pre-history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> They are often a rich source of fish and other edible aquatic life and are a major source of fresh water, which can be used for drinking and irrigation. Rivers help to determine the <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban form</a> of cities and neighborhoods, and their corridors often present opportunities for <a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">urban renewal</a> through the development of <a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">foreshoreways</a> such as river walks. Rivers also provide an easy means of disposing of <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater</a> and, in much of the less developed world, other wastes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation_and_transport">Navigation and transport</span></h3>
<p>Rivers have been used for <a href="/wiki/Inland_navigation" title="Inland navigation">navigation</a> for thousands of years. The earliest evidence of navigation is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilisation" title="Indus Valley civilisation">Indus Valley civilization</a>, which existed in northwestern India around 3300 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Riverine navigation provides a cheap means of transport and is still used extensively on most major rivers of the world like the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganges_River" title="Ganges River">Ganges</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Indus" title="River Indus">Indus</a>. 
</p><p>In some heavily forested regions such as <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and Canada, <a href="/wiki/Lumberjack" title="Lumberjack">lumberjacks</a> use rivers to float felled trees downstream to <a href="/wiki/Logging_camp" title="Logging camp">lumber camps</a> for further processing, saving much effort and cost by transporting the huge heavy logs by natural means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnr.state.mn.us_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnr.state.mn.us-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_borders">Political borders</span></h3>
<p>Rivers have been important in determining political boundaries and defending countries. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> was a long-standing border of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, and today it forms most of the border between <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>. The Mississippi in North America and the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> in Europe are major east–west boundaries in those continents. The <a href="/wiki/Orange_River" title="Orange River">Orange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limpopo_River" title="Limpopo River">Limpopo</a> Rivers in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern Africa</a> form the boundaries between provinces and countries along their routes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacred_rivers">Sacred rivers</span></h3>
<p><span class="anchor" id="Sacred"></span>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rigvedic_rivers" title="Rigvedic rivers">Rigvedic rivers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sapta_Sindhu" title="Sapta Sindhu">Sapta Sindhu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacred_mountains" title="Sacred mountains">Sacred mountains</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_groves" title="Sacred groves">Sacred groves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacred_natural_site" title="Sacred natural site">Sacred natural site</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_site" title="Sacred site">Sacred site</a></div>
<p><b>Sacred rivers</b> and their reverence is a phenomenon found in several religions, especially religions in which nature is revered. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">Indian-origin religions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> revere and preserve <a href="/wiki/Sacred_groves_of_India" title="Sacred groves of India">groves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Banyan_trees_in_India" title="List of Banyan trees in India">trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacred_mountains#India" title="Sacred mountains">mountains</a> and rivers as sacred. Among the most sacred rivers in Hinduism are the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alter2001_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alter2001-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yamuna" title="Yamuna">Yamuna</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hy_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-hy-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-brit-25">[25]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sarasvati_River" title="Sarasvati River">Sarasvati</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB-Sarasvati_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB-Sarasvati-26">[26]</a></sup> rivers. Other sacred rivers for Indian religions include the <a href="/wiki/Rigvedic_rivers" title="Rigvedic rivers">Rigvedic rivers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Narmada_River" title="Narmada River">Narmada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Godavari_River" title="Godavari River">Godavari</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kaveri" title="Kaveri">Kaveri</a> rivers. The <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gita" title="Gita">Gita</a>, the most sacred of <a href="/wiki/Hindu_texts" title="Hindu texts">Hindu texts</a>, were written on the banks of the Sarasvati river.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon,_Saltford.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon%2C_Saltford.jpg/220px-River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon%2C_Saltford.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon%2C_Saltford.jpg/330px-River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon%2C_Saltford.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon%2C_Saltford.jpg/440px-River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon%2C_Saltford.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:River_bank_repair_on_the_River_Avon,_Saltford.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>River bank repair</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/River_engineering" title="River engineering">River engineering</a></div>
<p>Rivers are often managed or controlled to make them more useful or less disruptive to human activity.
</p>
<ul><li>Dams or weirs may be built to control the flow, store water, or extract energy.</li>
<li>Levees, known as dikes in Europe, may be built to prevent river water from flowing on floodplains or floodways.</li>
<li>Canals connect rivers to one another for <a href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(water_supply)" title="Aqueduct (water supply)">water transfer</a> or navigation.</li>
<li>River courses may be modified to improve navigation, or straightened to increase the flow rate.</li></ul>
<p>River management is a continuous activity as rivers tend to 'undo' the modifications made by people. <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">Dredged</a> channels silt up, <a href="/wiki/Sluice" title="Sluice">sluice mechanisms</a> deteriorate with age, levees and dams may suffer seepage or catastrophic failure. The benefits sought through managing rivers may often be offset by the social and economic costs of mitigating the bad effects of such management. As an example, in parts of the developed world, rivers have been confined within channels to free up flat flood-plain land for development. Floods can inundate such development at high financial cost and often with loss of life.
</p><p>Rivers are increasingly managed for <a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">habitat conservation</a>, as they are critical for many <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">aquatic</a> and riparian plants, <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">resident</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migratory fishes</a>, waterfowl, <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating birds</a>, and many mammals.
</p>

Human livelihoods around rivers are also impacted by plastic pollution through direct damage to shipping and transport vessels, effects on tourism or real estate value, and the clogging of drains and other hydraulic infrastructure leading to increased flood risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
