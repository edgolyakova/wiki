<p><b>Fiction</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Creative_work" title="Creative work">creative work</a>, chiefly any <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a> work, portraying <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">individuals</a>, events, or <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">places</a> in ways that are <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">imaginary</a> or inconsistent with <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">fact</a>, or plausibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C._Hugh_Holman_1990,_p._212_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-C._Hugh_Holman_1990,_p._212-3">[3]</a></sup> In a narrow sense, "fiction" refers to <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">written narratives</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">prose</a> – often referring specifically to <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novellas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short stories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> More broadly, however, fiction encompasses imaginary narratives expressed in any <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">medium</a>, including not just writings but also <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">live theatrical performances</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">films</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_dramas" title="Radio dramas">radio dramas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">comics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role-playing_games" title="Role-playing games">role-playing games</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The creation of a work of fiction implies the <a href="/wiki/Worldbuilding" title="Worldbuilding">construction of an imaginary world</a>. Typically, the fictionality of the work is publicly acknowledged and the audience expects the work to deviate in some ways from the real world rather than presenting only factually accurate portrayals or <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">characters</a> who are actual people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-litfiction_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-litfiction-6">[6]</a></sup> Because fiction is generally understood to not fully adhere to the real world, the <a href="/wiki/Theme_(narrative)" title="Theme (narrative)">themes</a> and context of a work, such as if and how it relates to real-world issues or events, are open to interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Characters and events within some fictional works may even exist in their own context entirely separate from the known physical universe: an independent <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">fictional universe</a>.
</p><p>In contrast to fiction, creators of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">non-fiction</a> works assume responsibility for presenting only the historical and factual truth. Despite the traditional distinction between fiction and non-fiction, some modern works blur this boundary, particularly ones that fall under certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental_fiction" title="Experimental fiction">experimental</a> storytelling genres—including some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodern_fiction" title="Postmodern fiction">postmodern fiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autofiction" title="Autofiction">autofiction</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Creative_nonfiction" title="Creative nonfiction">creative nonfiction</a> like <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction_novel" title="Non-fiction novel">non-fiction novels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Docudrama" title="Docudrama">docudramas</a>—as well as the deliberate <a href="/wiki/Literary_forgery" title="Literary forgery">literary fraud</a> of falsely marketing fiction as nonfiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_literature" title="History of literature">History of literature</a></div>
<p>Storytelling has existed in all human cultures, and each culture incorporates different elements of truth and fiction into storytelling. Early fiction was closely associated with history and <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myth</a>. Greek poets such as <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">Aesop</a> developed fictional stories that were told first through <a href="/wiki/Oral_storytelling" title="Oral storytelling">oral storytelling</a> and then in writing. Prose fiction was developed in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>, influenced by the storytelling traditions of Asia and Egypt. Distinctly fictional work was not recognized as separate from historical or mythological stories until the imperial period. Plasmatic narrative, following entirely invented characters and events, was developed through ancient <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_comedy" title="New comedy">New Comedy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> One common structure among early fiction is a series of strange and fantastic adventures as early writers test the limits of fiction writing. <a href="/wiki/Milesian_tale" title="Milesian tale">Milesian tales</a> were an early example of fiction writing in Ancient Greece and Italy. As fiction writing developed in Ancient Greece, relatable characters and plausible scenarios were emphasized to better connect with the audience, including elements such as <a href="/wiki/Romance_(love)" title="Romance (love)">romance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ceremony" title="Ceremony"> religious ceremonies</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heroic_romance" title="Heroic romance">Heroic romance</a> was developed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">medieval Europe</a>, incorporating elements associated with <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> elements and <a href="/wiki/Chivalry" title="Chivalry">chivalry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The structure of the modern novel was developed by <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quixote</a></i> in the early-17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The novel became a primary medium of fiction in the 18th and 19th centuries. They were often associated with <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> ideas such as <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnosticism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">Realism</a> developed as a literary style at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> New forms of mass media developed in the late-19th and early-20th centuries, including popular-fiction magazines and early film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Interactive_fiction" title="Interactive fiction">Interactive fiction</a> was developed in the late-20th century through video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">Elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Character">Character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">Character (arts)</a></div>
<p>Characters are the main actors within a work of fiction that carry out the story. They typically take the form of humans with names, identities, and character traits, and they typically engage in actions and speech to convey their motives. Characters may be entirely imaginary, or they may have a basis in real-life individuals. In literary fiction, study of the characters is the work's purpose. Memorable characters provide value and iconography to fictional stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Fictional characters can be perceived similarly to real people by the audience. <a href="/wiki/First_impression_(psychology)" title="First impression (psychology)">First impressions</a> are influential in how a character is initially perceived, while familiarity with a character results in expected behaviors. Characters that behave contrary to their previous characterization can be confusing or jarring to the audience. The audience can also form social connections with characters, feeling for them as if they were real.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plot_(narrative)" title="Plot (narrative)">Plot (narrative)</a></div>
<p>Plot is the sequence of events that occurs in a work of fiction. It occurs through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cause_and_effect" title="Cause and effect">cause and effect</a> in which actions produce reactions and cause the story to progress. The plot often corresponds to a conflict between characters or within a character and presents stakes that are at risk within the story. Plot is structured through a series of scenes in which related events occur that lead to subsequent scenes. These events form plot points that cause changes to the story or the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">Setting (narrative)</a></div>
<p>Setting is the time, place, and circumstance in which a story takes place. It includes the physical surroundings that the characters experience and the social conventions that affect characters. The setting may resemble a character in that it has specific traits, undergoes actions that affect the plot, and develops over the course of the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theme">Theme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Theme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Theme_(narrative)" title="Theme (narrative)">Theme (narrative)</a></div>
<p>Theme is the underlying idea or message presented by a work. It is more abstract than other elements and can be applied to other circumstances as a broader concept. Theme is a subjective element that is interpreted by the audience and may or may not be intended by the work's creator. The audience may come to different conclusions about a work's theme or develop new ideas about its theme as the work progresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction_writing">Fiction writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fiction writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fiction_writing" title="Fiction writing">Fiction writing</a></div>
<p>Fiction writing is the process by which an author or creator produces a fictional work. Some elements of the writing process may be planned in advance, while others may come about spontaneously. Fiction writers use different <a href="/wiki/Writing_style" title="Writing style">writing styles</a> and have distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writer%27s_voice" title="Writer's voice">writers' voices</a> when writing fictional stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formats">Formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_genres" title="List of fictional genres">List of fictional genres</a></div>
<p>Traditionally, fiction includes novels, short stories, <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narrative_poetry" title="Narrative poetry">narrative poetry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">plays</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operas" title="Operas">operas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">dramas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puppet_play" title="Puppet play">puppet plays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Concert_dance" title="Concert dance">various kinds of theatrical dances</a>). However, fiction may also encompass <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>, and many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animated_cartoon" title="Animated cartoon">animated cartoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">stop motions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_film" title="Fictional film">films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_drama" title="Radio drama">radio programs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Television_comedy" title="Television comedy">comedies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dramatic_programming" title="Dramatic programming">dramas</a>), etc.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> has had a major impact on the creation and distribution of fiction, calling into question the feasibility of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> as a means to ensure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royalties" title="Royalties">royalties</a> are paid to copyright holders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Also, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_libraries" title="Digital libraries">digital libraries</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Project_Gutenberg" title="Project Gutenberg">Project Gutenberg</a> make <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> texts more readily available. The combination of inexpensive home computers, the Internet, and the creativity of its users has also led to new forms of fiction, such as interactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_game" title="Computer game">computer games</a> or computer-generated comics. Countless forums for <a href="/wiki/Fan_fiction" title="Fan fiction">fan fiction</a> can be found online, where loyal followers of specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_realm" title="Fictional realm">fictional realms</a> create and distribute derivative stories. The Internet is also used for the development of <a href="/wiki/Blog_fiction" title="Blog fiction">blog fiction</a>, where a story is delivered through a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> either as flash fiction or serial blog, and <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_fiction" title="Collaborative fiction">collaborative fiction</a>, where a story is written sequentially by different authors, or the entire text can be revised by anyone using a <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a>.
</p><p>Types of <a href="/wiki/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">literary fiction</a> in prose are distinguished by relative length and include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Short story: the boundary between a long short story and a novella is vague,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> although a short story commonly comprises fewer than 7,500 words</li>
<li>Novella: typically, 17,500 to 40,000 words in length; examples include <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Case_of_Dr_Jekyll_and_Mr_Hyde" title="Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde">Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde</a></i> (1886) or <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Darkness" title="Heart of Darkness">Heart of Darkness</a></i> (1899)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>Novel: 40,000 words or more in length</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre_fiction">Genre fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Genre fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Genre_fiction" title="Genre fiction">Genre fiction</a></div>
<p>Fiction is commonly broken down into a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_genres" title="List of fictional genres">genres</a>: subsets of fiction, each differentiated by a particular unifying <a href="/wiki/Tone_(literature)" title="Tone (literature)">tone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Writing_style" title="Writing style">style</a>; set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_technique#literary_techniques" title="Literary technique">narrative techniques</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetypes</a>, or other tropes; <a href="/wiki/Content_(media)" title="Content (media)">media content</a>; or other popularly defined criterion. <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">Science fiction</a>, for example, predicts or supposes technologies that are not realities at the time of the work's creation: <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i> was published in 1865, while in 1969 astronauts <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> became the first persons to land on the Moon.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Historical_fiction" title="Historical fiction">Historical fiction</a> places imaginary characters into real historical events. In the 1814 historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Waverley_(novel)" title="Waverley (novel)">Waverley</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Scott" title="Sir Walter Scott">Sir Walter Scott</a>'s fictional character Edward Waverley meets a figure from history, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonnie_Prince_Charlie" title="Bonnie Prince Charlie">Bonnie Prince Charlie</a>, and takes part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Prestonpans" title="Battle of Prestonpans">Battle of Prestonpans</a>. Some works of fiction are slightly or greatly re-imagined based on some originally true story, or a reconstructed biography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Role_of_Narrative_Fiction_and_Semi-Fiction_in_Organizational_Studies_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Role_of_Narrative_Fiction_and_Semi-Fiction_in_Organizational_Studies-27">[27]</a></sup> Often, even when the fictional story is based on fact, there may be additions and subtractions from the true story to make it more interesting. An example is <a href="/wiki/Tim_O%27Brien_(author)" title="Tim O'Brien (author)">Tim O'Brien</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Things_They_Carried" title="The Things They Carried">The Things They Carried</a></i>, a 1990 series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_stories" title="Short stories">short stories</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.
</p><p>Fictional works that explicitly involve supernatural, magical, or scientifically impossible elements are often classified under the genre of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s 1865 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> series. Creators of fantasy sometimes introduce imaginary creatures and beings such as dragons and fairies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C._Hugh_Holman_1990,_p._212_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-C._Hugh_Holman_1990,_p._212-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_fiction">Literary fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Literary fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">Literary fiction</a></div>
<p>The definition of literary fiction is controversial. In the book trade, it is used to market novels which do not fit neatly into an established genre (see <a href="/wiki/Genre_fiction" title="Genre fiction">genre fiction</a>). Other definitions are that a novel is literary fiction if it is character-driven rather than plot-driven, or if it examines the human condition, or if it uses language in an experimental or poetic fashion, or simply if it is "serious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Literary fiction is often used as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, in the narrow sense of writings specifically considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OD_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-OD-29">[29]</a></sup> While literary fiction is sometimes regarded as superior to genre fiction, the two are not mutually exclusive, and major literary figures have employed the genres of science fiction, <a href="/wiki/Crime_fiction" title="Crime fiction">crime fiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romance_novel" title="Romance novel">romance</a>, etc, to create works of literature. Furthermore, the study of genre fiction has developed within academia in recent decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term is sometimes used such as to equate literary fiction to literature.  The accuracy of this is debated. <a href="/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" title="Neal Stephenson">Neal Stephenson</a> has suggested that while any definition will be simplistic there is today a general cultural difference between literary and genre fiction. On the one hand literary authors nowadays are frequently supported by patronage, with employment at a university or a similar institution, and with the continuation of such positions determined not by book sales but by critical acclaim by other established literary authors and critics. On the other hand, he suggests, genre fiction writers tend to support themselves by book sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, in an interview, <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a> lamented that "the category of 'literary fiction' has sprung up recently to torment people like me who just set out to write books, and if anybody wanted to read them, terrific, the more the merrier. ... I'm a genre writer of a sort. I write literary fiction, which is like spy fiction or chick lit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrossman2006_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrossman2006-32">[32]</a></sup> Likewise, on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Charlie_Rose_Show" title="The Charlie Rose Show">The Charlie Rose Show</a></i>, he argued that this term, when applied to his work, greatly limited him and his expectations of what might come of his writing, so he does not really like it. He suggested that all his works are literary, simply because "they are written in words".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Literary fiction often involves <a href="/wiki/Social_commentary" title="Social commentary">social commentary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_criticism" title="Political criticism">political criticism</a>, or reflection on the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180-34">[34]</a></sup> In general, it focuses on "introspective, in-depth character studies" of "interesting, complex and developed" characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColes20097_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColes20097-35">[35]</a></sup> This contrasts with genre fiction where plot is the central concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009181–82_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009181–82-36">[36]</a></sup> Usually in literary fiction the focus is on the "inner story" of the characters who drive the plot, with detailed motivations to elicit "emotional involvement" in the reader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColes200726_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColes200726-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColes20098_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColes20098-38">[38]</a></sup> The style of literary fiction is often described as "elegantly written, lyrical, and ... layered".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009179_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009179-39">[39]</a></sup> The tone of literary fiction can be darker than genre fiction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182-40">[40]</a></sup> while the pacing of literary fiction may be slower than popular fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182-40">[40]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Rafferty" title="Terrence Rafferty">Terrence Rafferty</a> notes, "literary fiction, by its nature, allows itself to dawdle, to linger on stray beauties even at the risk of losing its way".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERafferty2011_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERafferty2011-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Realism">Realism<span class="anchor" id="Reality_and_fiction"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">Literary realism</a></div>
<p>Realistic fiction typically involves a story whose basic <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">setting</a> (time and location in the world) is real and whose events could feasibly happen in a real-world setting; in contrast, <a href="/wiki/Speculative_fiction" title="Speculative fiction">speculative fiction</a> typically involves a story where the opposite is the case, often being set in an entirely <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/alternate_universe" title="wikt:alternate universe">imaginary universe</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_history" title="Alternative history">alternative history</a> of the world other than that currently understood as true, or some other non-existent location or time-period, sometimes even presenting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impossible_technology" title="Impossible technology">impossible technology</a> or defiance of the currently understood laws of nature. However, all types of fiction arguably invite their audience to explore real ideas, issues, or possibilities in an otherwise imaginary setting or using what is understood about reality to mentally construct something similar to reality, though still distinct from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[note 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[note 2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of the traditional separation between fiction and non-fiction, the lines are now commonly understood as blurred, showing more overlap than mutual exclusion. Even fiction usually has elements of or grounding in, truth. The distinction between the two may be best defined from the perspective of the audience, according to whom a work is regarded as non-fiction if its people, places, and events are all historically or factually real, while a work is regarded as fiction if it deviates from reality in any of those areas. The distinction between fiction and non-fiction is further obscured by an understanding, on the one hand, that the truth can be presented through imaginary channels and constructions, while, on the other hand, imagination can just as well bring about significant conclusions about truth and reality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Literary critic <a href="/wiki/James_Wood_(critic)" title="James Wood (critic)">James Wood</a> argues that "fiction is both artifice and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verisimilitude_(narrative)" title="Verisimilitude (narrative)">verisimilitude</a>", meaning that it requires both creative inventions as well as some acceptable degree of believability,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup> a notion often encapsulated in poet <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>'s term: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Willing_suspension_of_disbelief" title="Willing suspension of disbelief">willing suspension of disbelief</a>. Also, infinite fictional possibilities themselves signal the impossibility of fully knowing reality, provocatively demonstrating that there is no criterion to measure constructs of reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subversive_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-subversive-47">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
