<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Orchestra</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Large instrumental ensemble</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Orchestra_(disambiguation)" title="Orchestra (disambiguation)">Orchestra (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orchestre_symphonique_(dessus).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4016" data-file-width="6016" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Orchestre_symphonique_%28dessus%29.jpg/300px-Orchestre_symphonique_%28dessus%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Orchestre_symphonique_%28dessus%29.jpg/450px-Orchestre_symphonique_%28dessus%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Orchestre_symphonique_%28dessus%29.jpg/600px-Orchestre_symphonique_%28dessus%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orchestre_symphonique_(dessus).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Orchestre_national_du_Capitole_de_Toulouse" title="Orchestre national du Capitole de Toulouse">Orchestre national du Capitole de Toulouse</a> in public performance at the Grain Hall of Toulouse</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>orchestra</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" lang="it-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[orˈkɛstra]</a></span>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">instrumental ensemble</a> typical of <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, which combines instruments from different families, including
</p>
<ul><li>bowed <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string instruments</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instrument" title="Woodwind instrument">woodwinds</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Western_concert_flute" title="Western concert flute">flute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">brass</a> instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornet" title="Cornet">cornet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">percussion instruments</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare drum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cymbal" title="Cymbal">cymbals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triangle_(musical_instrument)" title="Triangle (musical instrument)">triangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tambourine" title="Tambourine">tambourine</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mallet_percussion" title="Mallet percussion">mallet percussion instruments</a></li></ul>
<p>each grouped in sections.
</p><p>Other instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Celesta" title="Celesta">celesta</a> may sometimes appear in a fifth <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_section" title="Keyboard section">keyboard section</a> or may stand alone as soloist instruments, as may the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_harp" title="Concert harp">concert harp</a> and, for performances of some modern compositions, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_musical_instrument" title="Electronic musical instrument">electronic instruments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[note 1]</a></sup>
</p><p>A full-size Western orchestra may sometimes be called a <b><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1023754711">.mw-parser-output .vanchor>:target~.vanchor-text{background-color:#b1d2ff}</style><span class="vanchor"><span id="symphony_orchestra"></span><span class="vanchor-text">symphony orchestra</span></span></b> or <b>philharmonic orchestra</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>phil-</i>, "loving", and "harmony"). The actual number of musicians employed in a given performance may vary from seventy to over one hundred musicians, depending on the work being played and the size of the venue. A <i><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1023754711" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="vanchor"><span id="chamber_orchestra"></span><span class="vanchor-text">chamber orchestra</span></span></i> (sometimes <i>concert orchestra</i>) is a smaller ensemble of not more than about fifty musicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludwig-2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludwig-2014-2">[1]</a></sup> Orchestras that specialize in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a> of, for example, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">George Frideric Handel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Classical_period_(music)" title="Classical period (music)">Classical</a> repertoire, such as that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haydn" title="Haydn">Haydn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>, tend to be smaller than orchestras performing a <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic music</a> repertoire,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphonies" title="Symphonies">symphonies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Johannes Brahms</a>. The typical orchestra grew in size throughout the 18th and 19th centuries, reaching a peak with the large orchestras (of as many as 120 players) called for in the works of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>, and later, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Gustav Mahler</a>.
</p><p>Orchestras are usually led by a <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a> who directs the performance with movements of the hands and arms, often made easier for the musicians to see by use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductor%27s_baton" title="Conductor's baton">conductor's baton</a>. The conductor unifies the orchestra, sets the <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> and shapes the sound of the ensemble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-3">[2]</a></sup> The conductor also prepares the orchestra by leading rehearsals before the public concert, in which the conductor provides instructions to the musicians on their interpretation of the music being performed.
</p><p>The leader of the first violin section – commonly called the <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a> – also plays an important role in leading the musicians. In the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a> era (1600–1750), orchestras were often led by the concertmaster, or by a chord-playing musician performing the <a href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" title="Basso continuo">basso continuo</a> parts on a <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">pipe organ</a>, a tradition that some 20th century and 21st century <a href="/wiki/Early_music" title="Early music">early music</a> ensembles continue. Orchestras play a wide range of repertoire, including symphonies, <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballet</a> <a href="/wiki/Overture" title="Overture">overtures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">concertos</a> for solo instruments, and as <a href="/wiki/Pit_orchestra" title="Pit orchestra">pit ensembles</a> for <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballets</a>, and some types of <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical theatre</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a> <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">operettas</a>).
</p><p>Amateur orchestras include those made up of students from an elementary school or a high school, <a href="/wiki/Youth_orchestra" title="Youth orchestra">youth orchestras</a>, and community orchestras; the latter two typically being made up of amateur musicians from a particular city or region.
</p><p>The term <i>orchestra</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ὀρχήστρα (<i>orchestra</i>), the name for the area in front of a stage in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_theatre" title="Ancient Greek theatre">ancient Greek theatre</a> reserved for the <a href="/wiki/Greek_chorus" title="Greek chorus">Greek chorus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baroque_and_classical_eras">Baroque and classical eras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Baroque and classical eras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Baroque era, the size and composition of an orchestra was not standardised. There were large differences in size, instrumentation and playing styles—and therefore in orchestral soundscapes and palettes — between the various European regions. The <i>Baroque orchestra</i> ranged from smaller orchestras (or ensembles) with one player per part, to larger scale orchestras with many players per part. Examples of the smaller variety were Bach's orchestras, for example in Koethen, where he had access to an ensemble of up to 18 players. Examples of large scale Baroque orchestras would include Corelli's orchestra in Rome which ranged between 35 and 80 players for day-to-day performances, being enlarged to 150 players for special occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the classical era, the orchestra became more standardized with a small to medium sized string section and a core wind section consisting of pairs of oboes, flutes, bassoons and horns, sometimes supplemented by percussion and pairs of clarinets and trumpets.
</p>
<h3><span id="Beethoven.27s_influence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Beethoven's_influence">Beethoven's influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beethoven's influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The so-called "standard complement" of doubled winds and brass in the orchestra pioneered in the late 18th century and consolidated during the first half of the 19th century is generally attributed to the forces called for by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> after Haydn and Mozart.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Beethoven's instrumentation almost always included paired <a href="/wiki/Western_concert_flute" title="Western concert flute">flutes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">horns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>. The exceptions to this are his <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 4 (Beethoven)">Symphony No. 4</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Beethoven)" title="Violin Concerto (Beethoven)">Violin Concerto</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Concerto No. 4 (Beethoven)">Piano Concerto No. 4</a>, which each specify a single <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>. Beethoven carefully calculated the expansion of this particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timbral" title="Timbral">timbral</a> "palette" in Symphonies 3, 5, 6, and 9 for an innovative effect. The third horn in the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Beethoven)">"Eroica"</a> Symphony arrives to provide not only some harmonic flexibility, but also the effect of "choral" brass in the Trio movement. <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">Piccolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contrabassoon" title="Contrabassoon">contrabassoon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombones</a> add to the triumphal finale of his <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven)">Symphony No. 5</a>. A piccolo and a pair of trombones help deliver the effect of storm and sunshine in the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Beethoven)">Sixth</a>, also known as the <i>Pastoral Symphony</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Ninth</a> asks for a second pair of horns, for reasons similar to the "Eroica" (four horns has since become standard); Beethoven's use of piccolo, contrabassoon, trombones, and untuned <a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">percussion</a> — plus <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">chorus</a> and vocal soloists — in his finale, are his earliest suggestion that the timbral boundaries of symphony might be expanded. For several decades after his death, symphonic <a href="/wiki/Instrumentation_(music)" title="Instrumentation (music)">instrumentation</a> was faithful to Beethoven's well-established model, with few exceptions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumental_technology">Instrumental technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Instrumental technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_(1916).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="864" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_%281916%29.jpg/290px-Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_%281916%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_%281916%29.jpg/435px-Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_%281916%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_%281916%29.jpg/580px-Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_%281916%29.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia_Orchestra_at_American_premiere_of_Mahler%27s_8th_Symphony_(1916).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Leopold_Stokowski" title="Leopold Stokowski">Stokowski</a> and the Philadelphia Orchestra at the 2 March 1916 American premiere of <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahler</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphony_of_a_Thousand" title="Symphony of a Thousand">8th Symphony</a></div></div></div>
<p>The invention of the piston and rotary valve by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_St%C3%B6lzel" title="Heinrich Stölzel">Heinrich Stölzel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bl%C3%BChmel" title="Friedrich Blühmel">Friedrich Blühmel</a>, both <a href="/wiki/Silesians" title="Silesians">Silesians</a>, in 1815, was the first in a series of innovations which impacted the orchestra, including the development of modern keywork for the flute by <a href="/wiki/Theobald_Boehm" title="Theobald Boehm">Theobald Boehm</a> and the innovations of <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Sax" title="Adolphe Sax">Adolphe Sax</a> in the woodwinds, notably the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophone</a>. These advances would lead <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a> to write a landmark book on <a href="/wiki/Instrumentation_(music)" title="Instrumentation (music)">instrumentation</a>, which was the first systematic treatise on the use of instrumental sound as an expressive element of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Wagner.27s_influence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Wagner's_influence">Wagner's influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wagner's influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The next major expansion of symphonic practice came from <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festspielhaus" title="Bayreuth Festspielhaus">Bayreuth</a> orchestra, founded to accompany his musical dramas. Wagner's works for the stage were scored with unprecedented scope and complexity: indeed, his score to <i><a href="/wiki/Das_Rheingold" title="Das Rheingold">Das Rheingold</a></i> calls for six <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harps</a>. Thus, Wagner envisioned an ever-more-demanding role for the conductor of the theatre orchestra, as he elaborated in his influential work <i>On Conducting</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> This brought about a revolution in orchestral <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">composition</a>, and set the style for orchestral performance for the next eighty years. Wagner's theories re-examined the importance of <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">dynamics</a>, bowing of string instruments and the role of principals in the orchestra.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_orchestra">20th-century orchestra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th-century orchestra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the beginning of the 20th century, symphony orchestras were larger, better funded, and better trained than previously; consequently, composers could compose larger and more ambitious works. The works of <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Gustav Mahler</a> were particularly innovative; in his later symphonies, such as the mammoth <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Mahler)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Mahler)">Symphony No. 8</a>, Mahler pushes the furthest boundaries of orchestral size, employing huge forces. By the late Romantic era, orchestras could support the most enormous forms of symphonic expression, with huge string sections, massive brass sections and an expanded range of percussion instruments. With the recording era beginning, the standards of performance were pushed to a new level, because a recorded symphony could be listened to closely and even minor errors in intonation or ensemble, which might not be noticeable in a live performance, could be heard by critics. As recording technologies improved over the 20th and 21st centuries, eventually small errors in a recording could be "fixed" by audio editing or <a href="/wiki/Overdubbing" title="Overdubbing">overdubbing</a>. Some older conductors and composers could remember a time when simply "getting through" the music as best as possible was the standard. Combined with the wider audience made possible by recording, this led to a renewed focus on particular star conductors and on a high standard of orchestral execution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The typical symphony orchestra consists of four groups of related <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instruments</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_section" title="Woodwind section">woodwinds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brass_section" title="Brass section">brass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Percussion_section" title="Percussion section">percussion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">strings</a>. Other instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celesta" title="Celesta">celesta</a> may sometimes be grouped into a fifth section such as a <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_section" title="Keyboard section">keyboard section</a> or may stand alone, as may the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_harp" title="Concert harp">concert harp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electric and electronic</a> instruments. The orchestra, depending on the size, contains almost all of the standard instruments in each group.
</p><p>In the history of the orchestra, its instrumentation has been expanded over time, often agreed to have been standardized by the classical period<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>'s influence on the classical model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> In the 20th and 21st century, new repertory demands expanded the instrumentation of the orchestra, resulting in a flexible use of the classical-model instruments and newly developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_instrument" title="Electric instrument">electric</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_instrument" title="Electronic instrument">electronic instruments</a> in various combinations.
</p><p>The terms <i>symphony orchestra</i> and <i>philharmonic orchestra</i> may be used to distinguish different ensembles from the same locality, such as the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/London_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="London Philharmonic Orchestra">London Philharmonic Orchestra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[note 2]</a></sup> A symphony or philharmonic orchestra will usually have over eighty musicians on its roster, in some cases over a hundred, but the actual number of musicians employed in a particular performance may vary according to the work being played and the size of the venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludwig-2014_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ludwig-2014-2">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <i>chamber orchestra</i> is usually a smaller ensemble; a major chamber orchestra might employ as many as fifty musicians, but some are much smaller. <i>Concert orchestra</i> is an alternative term, as in the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Concert_Orchestra" title="BBC Concert Orchestra">BBC Concert Orchestra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89_Concert_Orchestra" title="RTÉ Concert Orchestra">RTÉ Concert Orchestra</a>.
</p>

<p>Apart from the core orchestral complement, various other instruments are called for occasionally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins-Griffiths-2001_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-Griffiths-2001-12">[10]</a></sup> These include the <a href="/wiki/Flugelhorn" title="Flugelhorn">flugelhorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornet" title="Cornet">cornet</a>. <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">Saxophones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_guitar" title="Classical guitar">classical guitars</a>, for example, appear in some 19th- through 21st-century scores. While appearing only as featured solo instruments in some works, for example <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">Maurice Ravel</a>'s orchestration of <a href="/wiki/Modest_Mussorgsky" title="Modest Mussorgsky">Modest Mussorgsky</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pictures_at_an_Exhibition" title="Pictures at an Exhibition">Pictures at an Exhibition</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Rachmaninoff" title="Sergei Rachmaninoff">Sergei Rachmaninoff</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Symphonic_Dances_(Rachmaninoff)" title="Symphonic Dances (Rachmaninoff)">Symphonic Dances</a></i>, the saxophone is included in other works, such as Ravel's <i><a href="/wiki/Bol%C3%A9ro" title="Boléro">Boléro</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet_(Prokofiev)#Orchestral_suites_extracted_from_Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet (Prokofiev)">Romeo and Juliet Suites 1 and 2</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaughan_Williams" title="Vaughan Williams">Vaughan Williams</a>' Symphonies <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Vaughan_Williams)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Vaughan Williams)">No. 6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Vaughan_Williams)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Vaughan Williams)">No. 9</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Walton" title="William Walton">William Walton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Belshazzar%27s_Feast_(Walton)" title="Belshazzar's Feast (Walton)"><i>Belshazzar's Feast</i></a>, and many other works as a member of the orchestral ensemble. The <a href="/wiki/Euphonium" title="Euphonium">euphonium</a> is featured in a few late <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/20th-century_classical_music" title="20th-century classical music">20th century works</a>, usually playing parts marked "tenor tuba", including <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">Gustav Holst</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Planets" title="The Planets">The Planets</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ein_Heldenleben" title="Ein Heldenleben">Ein Heldenleben</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Wagner_tuba" title="Wagner tuba">Wagner tuba</a>, a modified member of the horn family, appears in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s cycle <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">Der Ring des Nibelungen</a></i> and several other works by Strauss, <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a> (as featured in <a href="/wiki/The_Rite_of_Spring" title="The Rite of Spring">The Rite of Spring</a>), <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>, and others; it also has a notably prominent role in <a href="/wiki/Anton_Bruckner" title="Anton Bruckner">Anton Bruckner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Bruckner)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Bruckner)">Symphony No. 7 in E Major</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> Cornets appear in <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky</a>'s ballet <i><a href="/wiki/Swan_Lake" title="Swan Lake">Swan Lake</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Mer_(Debussy)" title="La Mer (Debussy)">La Mer</a></i>, and several orchestral works by <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a>. Unless these instruments are played by members "doubling" on another instrument (for example, a trombone player changing to euphonium or a bassoon player switching to <a href="/wiki/Contrabassoon" title="Contrabassoon">contrabassoon</a> for a certain passage), orchestras typically hire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freelance" title="Freelance">freelance</a> musicians to augment their regular ensemble.
</p><p>The 20th century orchestra was far more flexible than its predecessors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins-Griffiths-2001_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-Griffiths-2001-12">[10]</a></sup> In Beethoven's and <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Felix Mendelssohn</a>'s time, the orchestra was composed of a fairly standard core of instruments, which was very rarely modified by composers. As time progressed, and as the Romantic period saw changes in accepted modification with composers such as Berlioz and Mahler; some composers used multiple harps and <a href="/wiki/Sound_effect" title="Sound effect">sound effect</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Wind_machine" title="Wind machine">wind machine</a>. During the 20th century, the modern orchestra was generally standardized with the modern instrumentation listed below. Nevertheless, by the mid- to late 20th century, with the development of <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_classical_music" title="Contemporary classical music">contemporary classical music</a>, instrumentation could practically be hand-picked by the composer (e.g., to add <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_instrument" title="Electric instrument">electric instruments</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_instrument" title="Electronic instrument">electronic instruments</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizers</a>, non-Western instruments, or other instruments not traditionally used in orchestra).
</p><p>With this history in mind, the orchestra can be analysed in five eras: the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque era</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_music_period" title="Classical music period">Classical era</a>, early/mid-<a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic music</a> era, late-Romantic era and combined <a href="/wiki/Modernism_(music)" title="Modernism (music)">Modern/Postmodern eras</a>. The first is a <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> orchestra (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">J.S. Bach</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handel" title="Handel">Handel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaldi" title="Vivaldi">Vivaldi</a>), which generally had a smaller number of performers, and in which one or more chord-playing instruments, the <a href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" title="Basso continuo">basso continuo</a> group (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">pipe organ</a> and assorted bass instruments to perform the <a href="/wiki/Bassline" title="Bassline">bassline</a>), played an important role; the second is a typical classical period orchestra (e.g., early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beethoven" title="Beethoven">Beethoven</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a>), which used a smaller group of performers than a <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic music</a> orchestra and a fairly standardized instrumentation; the third is typical of an early/mid-Romantic era (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Schubert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Berlioz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Schumann</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahms" title="Brahms">Brahms</a>); the fourth is a late-Romantic/early 20th-century orchestra (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wagner" title="Wagner">Wagner</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahler" title="Mahler">Mahler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Stravinsky</a>), to the common complement of a 2010-era modern orchestra (e.g., <a href="/wiki/John_Adams_(composer)" title="John Adams (composer)">Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Barber" title="Samuel Barber">Barber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krzysztof_Penderecki" title="Krzysztof Penderecki">Penderecki</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laredo_Philharmonic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="254" data-file-width="170" decoding="async" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/49/Laredo_Philharmonic.jpg" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laredo_Philharmonic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Conducting an orchestra</div></div></div>
<p>Among the instrument groups and within each group of instruments, there is a generally accepted hierarchy. Every instrumental group (or section) has a <b>principal</b> who is generally responsible for leading the group and playing orchestral solos. The violins are divided into two groups, first violin and second violin, with the second violins playing in lower registers than the first violins, playing an <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompaniment</a> part, or harmonizing the melody played by the first violins. The principal first violin is called the <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a> (or orchestra "leader" in the U.K.) and is not only considered the leader of the string section, but the second-in-command of the entire orchestra, behind only the <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a>. The concertmaster leads the pre-concert <a href="/wiki/Musical_tuning" title="Musical tuning">tuning</a> and handles musical aspects of orchestra management, such as determining the bowings for the violins or for all of the string section. The concertmaster usually sits to the conductor's left, closest to the audience. There is also a principal second violin, a principal viola, a principal cello, and a principal bass.
</p><p>The principal trombone is considered the leader of the low brass section, while the principal trumpet is generally considered the leader of the entire brass section. While the oboe often provides the tuning note for the orchestra (due to a 300 year-old convention), there is generally no designated principal of the woodwind section (though in woodwind ensembles, the flute is often the presumptive leader.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> Instead, each principal confers with the others as equals in the case of musical differences of opinion. Most sections also have an assistant principal (or co-principal or associate principal), or in the case of the first violins, an assistant concertmaster, who often plays a <a href="/wiki/Tutti" title="Tutti">tutti</a> part in addition to replacing the principal in his or her absence.
</p><p>A section string player plays in <a href="/wiki/Unison" title="Unison">unison</a> with the rest of the section, except in the case of divided (<i>divisi</i>) parts, where upper and lower parts in the music are often assigned to "outside" (nearer the audience) and "inside" seated players. Where a solo part is called for in a string section, the section leader invariably plays that part. The section leader (or principal) of a string section is also responsible for determining the bowings, often based on the bowings set out by the concertmaster. In some cases, the principal of a string section may use a slightly different bowing than the concertmaster, to accommodate the requirements of playing their instrument (e.g., the double-bass section). Principals of a string section will also lead entrances for their section, typically by lifting the bow before the entrance, to ensure the section plays together. Tutti wind and brass players generally play a unique but non-solo part. Section percussionists play parts assigned to them by the principal percussionist.
</p><p>In modern times, the musicians are usually directed by a <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a>, although early orchestras did not have one, giving this role instead to the <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichordist</a> playing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basso_Continuo" title="Basso Continuo">continuo</a>. Some modern orchestras also do <a href="/wiki/Conductorless_orchestra" title="Conductorless orchestra">without conductors</a>, particularly smaller orchestras and those specializing in historically accurate (so-called "period") performances of <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">baroque</a> and earlier music.
</p><p>The most frequently performed repertoire for a <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a> orchestra is Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_classical_music" title="European classical music">classical music</a> or <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>. However, orchestras are used sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a> (e.g., to accompany a rock or pop band in a concert), extensively in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_music" title="Film music">film music</a>, and increasingly often in <a href="/wiki/Video_game_music" title="Video game music">video game music</a>. Orchestras are also used in the <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_metal" title="Symphonic metal">symphonic metal</a> genre. The term "orchestra" can also be applied to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jazz_ensemble" title="Jazz ensemble">jazz ensemble</a>, for example in the performance of <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big-band</a> music.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_and_appointment_of_members">Selection and appointment of members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Selection and appointment of members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 2000s, all tenured members of a professional orchestra normally <a href="/wiki/Audition" title="Audition">audition</a> for positions in the ensemble. Performers typically play one or more solo pieces of the auditionee's choice, such as a movement of a <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">concerto</a>, a solo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.S._Bach" title="J.S. Bach">Bach</a> movement, and a variety of excerpts from the orchestral literature that are advertised in the audition poster (so the auditionees can prepare). The excerpts are typically the most technically challenging parts and solos from the orchestral literature. Orchestral auditions are typically held in front of a panel that includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductor_(music)" title="Conductor (music)">conductor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a>, the principal player of the section for which the auditionee is applying, and possibly other principal players.
</p><p>The most promising candidates from the first round of auditions are invited to return for a second or third round of auditions, which allows the conductor and the panel to compare the best candidates. Performers may be asked to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sight_read" title="Sight read">sight read</a> orchestral music. The final stage of the audition process in some orchestras is a <i>test week</i>, in which the performer plays with the orchestra for a week or two, which allows the conductor and principal players to see if the individual can function well in an actual rehearsal and performance setting.
</p><p>There are a range of different employment arrangements. The most sought-after positions are permanent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenure" title="Tenure">tenured</a> positions in the orchestra. Orchestras also hire musicians on contracts, ranging in length from a single concert to a full season or more. Contract performers may be hired for individual concerts when the orchestra is doing an exceptionally large late-Romantic era orchestral work, or to substitute for a permanent member who is sick. A professional musician who is hired to perform for a single concert is sometimes called a "sub". Some contract musicians may be hired to replace permanent members for the period that the permanent member is on <a href="/wiki/Parental_leave" title="Parental leave">parental leave</a> or <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a> leave.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_gender_in_ensembles">History of gender in ensembles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History of gender in ensembles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Historically, major professional orchestras have been mostly or entirely composed of men. The first <a href="/wiki/Women_in_music" title="Women in music">women members hired in professional orchestras</a> have been <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harpists</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Philharmonic" title="Vienna Philharmonic">Vienna Philharmonic</a>, for example, did not accept women to permanent membership until 1997, far later than comparable orchestras (the other orchestras ranked among the world's top five by <i><a href="/wiki/Gramophone_(magazine)" title="Gramophone (magazine)">Gramophone</a></i> in 2008).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_world's_greatest_orchestras_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_world's_greatest_orchestras-15">[13]</a></sup> The last major orchestra to appoint a woman to a permanent position was the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Philharmonic" title="Berlin Philharmonic">Berlin Philharmonic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> In February 1996, the Vienna Philharmonic's principal flute, <a href="/wiki/Dieter_Flury" title="Dieter Flury">Dieter Flury</a>, told <i><a href="/wiki/Westdeutscher_Rundfunk" title="Westdeutscher Rundfunk">Westdeutscher Rundfunk</a></i> that accepting women would be "gambling with the emotional unity (<span title="German-language text"><i lang="de">emotionelle Geschlossenheit</i></span>) that this organism currently has".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDR5_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-WDR5-17">[15]</a></sup> In April 1996, the orchestra's press secretary wrote that "compensating for the expected leaves of absence" of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maternity_leave" title="Maternity leave">maternity leave</a> would be a problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, the Vienna Philharmonic was "facing protests during a [US] tour" by the <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Alliance_for_Women_in_Music" title="International Alliance for Women in Music">International Alliance for Women in Music</a>. Finally, "after being held up to increasing ridicule even in socially conservative Austria, members of the orchestra gathered [on 28 February 1997] in an extraordinary meeting on the eve of their departure and agreed to admit a woman, Anna Lelkes, as harpist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JP_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-JP-19">[17]</a></sup> As of 2013, the orchestra has six female members; one of them, violinist Albena Danailova, became one of the orchestra's <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmasters</a> in 2008, the first woman to hold that position in that orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> In 2012, women made up 6% of the orchestra's membership. VPO president <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Hellsberg" title="Clemens Hellsberg">Clemens Hellsberg</a> said the VPO now uses completely screened <a href="/wiki/Blind_audition" title="Blind audition">blind auditions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, an article in <i>Mother Jones</i> stated that while "[m]any prestigious orchestras have significant female membership — women outnumber men in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic" title="New York Philharmonic">New York Philharmonic</a>'s violin section — and several renowned ensembles, including the <a href="/wiki/National_Symphony_Orchestra" title="National Symphony Orchestra">National Symphony Orchestra</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit_Symphony" title="Detroit Symphony">Detroit Symphony</a>, and the Minnesota Symphony, are led by women violinists", the <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>, brass, and percussion sections of major orchestras "... are still predominantly male."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> A 2014 BBC article stated that the "... introduction of 'blind' auditions, where a prospective instrumentalist performs behind a screen so that the judging panel can exercise no gender or racial prejudice, has seen the gender balance of traditionally male-dominated symphony orchestras gradually shift."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_ensembles">Amateur ensembles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Amateur ensembles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are also a variety of amateur orchestras:
</p>
<dl><dt>School orchestras</dt>
<dd>These orchestras consist of students from elementary or secondary school. They may be students from a music class or program or they may be drawn from the entire school body. School orchestras are typically led by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_teacher" title="Music teacher">music teacher</a>. In some cases, school orchestras are <a href="/wiki/String_orchestra" title="String orchestra">string orchestras</a>, consisting only of students playing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/String_instruments" title="String instruments">string instruments</a>, with students playing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodwinds" title="Woodwinds">woodwinds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percussion" title="Percussion">percussion</a> grouped together as a <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert band</a>.</dd>
<dt>University or conservatory orchestras</dt>
<dd>These orchestras consist of students from a university or music conservatory. In some cases, university orchestras are open to all students from a university, from all programs. Larger universities may have two or more university orchestras: one or more orchestras made up of music majors (or, for major music programs, several tiers of music major orchestras, ranked by skill level) and a second orchestra open to university students from all academic programs (e.g., science, business, etc.) who have previous classical music experience on an orchestral instrument. University and conservatory orchestras are led by a conductor who is typically a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> or instructor at the university or conservatory.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Youth_orchestra" title="Youth orchestra">Youth orchestras</a></dt>
<dd>These orchestras consist of teens and young adults drawn from an entire city or region. The age range in youth orchestras varies between different ensembles. In some cases, youth orchestras may consist of teens or young adults from an entire country (e.g., Canada's National Youth Orchestra).</dd>
<dt>Community orchestras</dt>
<dd>These orchestras consist of amateur performers drawn from an entire city or region. Community orchestras typically consist mainly of adult amateur musicians. Community orchestras range in level from beginner-level orchestras which rehearse music without doing formal performances in front of an audience to intermediate-level ensembles to advanced amateur groups which play standard professional orchestra repertoire. In some cases, university or conservatory music students may also be members of community orchestras. While community orchestra members are mostly unpaid amateurs, in some orchestras, a small number of professionals may be hired to act as principal players and section leaders.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Repertoire_and_performances">Repertoire and performances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Repertoire and performances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Orchestras play a wide range of repertoire ranging from 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music#Dance_suite" title="Baroque music">dance suites</a>, 18th century <a href="/wiki/Divertimento" title="Divertimento">divertimentos</a> to 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">film scores</a> and 21st-century symphonies. Orchestras have become synonymous with the <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a>, an extended <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">musical composition</a> in Western <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a> that typically contains multiple movements which provide contrasting keys and tempos. Symphonies are notated in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_score" title="Full score">musical score</a>, which contains all the instrument parts. The <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a> uses the score to study the symphony before rehearsals and decide on their interpretation (e.g., tempos, articulation, phrasing, etc.), and to follow the music during rehearsals and concerts, while leading the ensemble. Orchestral musicians play from parts containing just the notated music for their instrument. A small number of symphonies also contain vocal parts (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beethoven" title="Beethoven">Beethoven</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beethoven%27s_Ninth_Symphony" title="Beethoven's Ninth Symphony">Ninth Symphony</a>).
</p><p>Orchestras also perform <a href="/wiki/Overture" title="Overture">overtures</a>, a term originally applied to the instrumental introduction to an opera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blom_1954_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blom_1954-24">[22]</a></sup> During the early Romantic era, composers such as <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Mendelssohn</a> began to use the term to refer to independent, self-existing instrumental, programmatic works that presaged genres such as the <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a>, a form devised by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> in several works that began as dramatic overtures. These were "at first undoubtedly intended to be played at the head of a programme".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blom_1954_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blom_1954-24">[22]</a></sup> In the 1850s the concert overture began to be supplanted by the symphonic poem.
</p><p>Orchestras also play with instrumental soloists in <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">concertos</a>. During concertos, the orchestra plays an <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompaniment</a> role to the soloist (e.g., a solo violinist or pianist) and, at times, introduces musical themes or interludes while the soloist is not playing. Orchestras also play during <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballets</a>, some musical theatre works and some choral works (both sacred works such as Masses and secular works). In operas and ballets, the orchestra accompanies the singers and dancers, respectively, and plays overtures and interludes where the melodies played by the orchestra take centre stage.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performances">Performances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Performances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Baroque era, orchestras performed in a range of venues, including at the fine houses of aristocrats, in opera halls and in churches. Some wealthy aristocrats had an orchestra in residence at their estate, to entertain them and their guests with performances. During the Classical era, as composers increasingly sought out financial support from the general public, orchestra concerts were increasingly held in public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_hall" title="Concert hall">concert halls</a>, where music lovers could buy tickets to hear the orchestra. Aristocratic patronage of orchestras continued during the Classical era, but this went on alongside public concerts. In the 20th and 21st century, orchestras found a new patron: governments. Many orchestras in North America and Europe receive part of their funding from national, regional level governments (e.g., state governments in the U.S.) or city governments. These government subsidies make up part of orchestra revenue, along with ticket sales, charitable donations (if the orchestra is registered as a charity) and other fundraising activities. With the invention of successive technologies, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_recording" title="Sound recording">sound recording</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio broadcasting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Television_broadcasting" title="Television broadcasting">television broadcasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streaming_video" title="Streaming video">streaming</a> and downloading of concert videos, orchestras have been able to find new revenue sources.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_in_performance">Issues in performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Issues in performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Faking">Faking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Faking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faking_(Western_classical_music)" title="Faking (Western classical music)">Faking (Western classical music)</a></div>
<p>One of the "great unmentionable [topics] of orchestral playing" is "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faking_(Western_classical_music)" title="Faking (Western classical music)">faking</a>", the process by which an orchestral musician gives the false "... impression of playing every note as written", typically for a very challenging passage that is very high or very fast, while not actually playing the notes that are in the printed music part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestrad.com_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-thestrad.com-25">[23]</a></sup> An article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Strad" title="The Strad">The Strad</a></i> states that all orchestral musicians, even those in the top orchestras, occasionally fake certain passages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestrad.com_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-thestrad.com-25">[23]</a></sup> One reason that musicians fake is because there are not enough rehearsals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestrad.com_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-thestrad.com-25">[23]</a></sup> Another factor is the extreme challenges in 20th century and 21st century contemporary pieces; some professionals said "faking" was "necessary in anything from ten to almost ninety per cent of some modern works".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestrad.com_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-thestrad.com-25">[23]</a></sup> Professional players who were interviewed were of a consensus that faking may be acceptable when a part is not written well for the instrument, but faking "just because you haven't practised" the music is not acceptable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestrad.com_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-thestrad.com-25">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-revolution">Counter-revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Counter-revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the advent of the early music movement, smaller orchestras where players worked on execution of works in styles derived from the study of older treatises on playing became common. These include the <a href="/wiki/Orchestra_of_the_Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment">Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment</a>, the <a href="/wiki/London_Classical_Players" title="London Classical Players">London Classical Players</a> under the direction of Sir <a href="/wiki/Roger_Norrington" title="Roger Norrington">Roger Norrington</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Ancient_Music" title="Academy of Ancient Music">Academy of Ancient Music</a> under <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hogwood" title="Christopher Hogwood">Christopher Hogwood</a>, among others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_trends_in_the_United_States">Recent trends in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Recent trends in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, the late 20th century saw a crisis of funding and support for orchestras. The size and cost of a symphony orchestra, compared to the size of the base of supporters, became an issue that struck at the core of the institution. Few orchestras could fill auditoriums, and the time-honored season-subscription system became increasingly anachronistic, as more and more listeners would buy tickets on an <i>ad-hoc</i> basis for individual events. Orchestral endowments and — more centrally to the daily operation of American orchestras — orchestral donors have seen investment portfolios shrink, or produce lower yields, reducing the ability of donors to contribute; further, there has been a trend toward donors finding other social causes more compelling. While government funding is less central to American than European orchestras, cuts in such funding are still significant for American ensembles. Finally, the drastic falling-off of revenues from recording, tied to no small extent to changes in the recording industry itself, began a period of change that has yet to reach its conclusion.
</p><p>U.S. orchestras that have gone into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11_bankruptcy" title="Chapter 11 bankruptcy">Chapter 11 bankruptcy</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Orchestra" title="Philadelphia Orchestra">Philadelphia Orchestra</a> (April 2011), and the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Orchestra" title="Louisville Orchestra">Louisville Orchestra</a> (December 2010); orchestras that have gone into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_7_bankruptcy" title="Chapter 7 bankruptcy">Chapter 7 bankruptcy</a> and have ceased operations include the Northwest Chamber Orchestra in 2006, the Honolulu Orchestra in March 2011, the <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_Symphony_Orchestra" title="New Mexico Symphony Orchestra">New Mexico Symphony Orchestra</a> in April 2011, and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Syracuse_Symphony&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Syracuse Symphony (page does not exist)">Syracuse Symphony</a> in June 2011. The Festival of Orchestras in Orlando, Florida, ceased operations at the end of March 2011.
</p><p>One source of financial difficulties that received notice and criticism was high salaries for music directors of US orchestras,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> which led several high-profile conductors to take pay cuts in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup> Music administrators such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_Tilson_Thomas" title="Michael Tilson Thomas">Michael Tilson Thomas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esa-Pekka_Salonen" title="Esa-Pekka Salonen">Esa-Pekka Salonen</a> argued that new music, new means of presenting it, and a renewed relationship with the community could revitalize the symphony orchestra. The American critic <a href="/wiki/Greg_Sandow" title="Greg Sandow">Greg Sandow</a> has argued in detail that orchestras must revise their approach to music, performance, the concert experience, marketing, public relations, community involvement, and presentation to bring them in line with the expectations of 21st century audiences immersed in popular culture.
</p><p>It is not uncommon for contemporary composers to use unconventional instruments, including various synthesizers, to achieve desired effects. Many, however, find more conventional orchestral configuration to provide better possibilities for color and depth. Composers like <a href="/wiki/John_Adams_(composer)" title="John Adams (composer)">John Adams</a> often employ Romantic-size orchestras, as in Adams' opera <i><a href="/wiki/Nixon_in_China" title="Nixon in China">Nixon in China</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Philip Glass</a> and others may be more free, yet still identify size-boundaries. Glass in particular has recently turned to conventional orchestras in works like the <i><a href="/wiki/Cello_Concerto_No._1_(Glass)" title="Cello Concerto No. 1 (Glass)">Concerto for Cello and Orchestra</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_No._2_(Glass)" title="Violin Concerto No. 2 (Glass)">Violin Concerto No. 2</a>.
</p><p>Along with a decrease in funding, some U.S. orchestras have reduced their overall personnel, as well as the number of players appearing in performances. The reduced numbers in performance are usually confined to the <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a>, since the numbers here have traditionally been flexible (as multiple players typically play from the same part).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_conductor">Role of conductor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Role of conductor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">Conducting</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="418" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg/220px-Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg/330px-Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg/440px-Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taipei.apo-hsu.altonthompson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Apo_Hsu" title="Apo Hsu">Apo Hsu</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Baton_(conducting)" title="Baton (conducting)">baton</a>, conducts the <a href="/wiki/National_Taiwan_Normal_University" title="National Taiwan Normal University">NTNU Symphony Orchestra</a> in Taipei, Republic of China</div></div></div>
<p>Conducting is the art of directing a <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">musical</a> performance, such as an orchestral or <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choral</a> <a href="/wiki/Concert" title="Concert">concert</a>. The primary duties of the conductor are to set the <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, ensure correct entries by various members of the ensemble, and "shape" the phrasing where appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-3">[2]</a></sup> To convey their ideas and interpretation, a conductor communicates with their musicians primarily through hand gestures, typically (though not invariably) with the aid of a <a href="/wiki/Baton_(conducting)" title="Baton (conducting)">baton</a>, and may use other gestures or signals, such as eye contact with relevant performers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CambComp3_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CambComp3-30">[28]</a></sup> A conductor's directions will almost invariably be supplemented or reinforced by verbal instructions or suggestions to their musicians in rehearsal prior to a performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CambComp3_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-CambComp3-30">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The conductor typically stands on a raised podium with a large music stand for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_score" title="Full score">full score</a>, which contains the <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a> for all the instruments and voices. Since the mid-18th century, most conductors have not played an instrument when conducting,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although in earlier periods of <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a> history, leading an ensemble while playing an instrument was common. In <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a> from the 1600s to the 1750s, the group would typically be led by the <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichordist</a> or first violinist (see <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a>), an approach that in modern times has been revived by several music directors for music from this period. Conducting while playing a <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> or <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizer</a> may also be done with musical theatre <a href="/wiki/Pit_orchestra" title="Pit orchestra">pit orchestras</a>. Communication is typically non-verbal during a performance (this is strictly the case in <a href="/wiki/Art_music" title="Art music">art music</a>, but in jazz big bands or large pop ensembles, there may be occasional spoken instructions, such as a "count in"). However, in <a href="/wiki/Rehearsal" title="Rehearsal">rehearsals</a>, frequent interruptions allow the conductor to give verbal directions as to how the music should be played or sung.
</p><p>Conductors act as guides to the orchestras or choirs they conduct. They choose the works to be performed and study their <a href="/wiki/Sheet_music" title="Sheet music">scores</a>, to which they may make certain adjustments (e.g., regarding tempo, articulation, phrasing, repetitions of sections, and so on), work out their interpretation, and relay their vision to the performers. They may also attend to organizational matters, such as scheduling rehearsals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> planning a concert season, hearing <a href="/wiki/Audition" title="Audition">auditions</a> and selecting members, and promoting their ensemble in the media. Orchestras, <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choirs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concert_band" title="Concert band">concert bands</a> and other sizable <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">musical ensembles</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big bands</a> are usually led by conductors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conductorless_orchestras">Conductorless orchestras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Conductorless orchestras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conductorless_orchestra" title="Conductorless orchestra">Conductorless orchestra</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque music</a> era (1600–1750), most orchestras were led by one of the musicians, typically the principal first violin, called the <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a>. The concertmaster would lead the tempo of pieces by lifting his or her bow in a rhythmic manner. Leadership might also be provided by one of the chord-playing instrumentalists playing the <a href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" title="Basso continuo">basso continuo</a> part which was the core of most Baroque instrumental ensemble pieces. Typically, this would be a <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> player, a <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">pipe organist</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Lute" title="Lute">lutist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Theorbo" title="Theorbo">theorbo</a> player. A keyboard player could lead the ensemble with his or her head, or by taking one of the hands off the keyboard to lead a more difficult tempo change. A lutenist or theorbo player could lead by lifting the instrument neck up and down to indicate the tempo of a piece, or to lead a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ritard" title="Ritard">ritard</a> during a cadence or ending. In some works which combined <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choirs</a> and instrumental ensembles, two leaders were sometimes used: A concertmaster to lead the instrumentalists and a chord-playing performer to lead the singers. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_music_period" title="Classical music period">Classical music period</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1720–1800</span>), the practice of using chordal instruments to play basso continuo was gradually phased out, and it disappeared completely by 1800. Instead, ensembles began to use conductors to lead the orchestra's tempos and playing style, while the concertmaster played an additional leadership role for the musicians, especially the string players, who imitate the bowstroke and playing style of the concertmaster, to the degree that is feasible for the different stringed instruments.
</p><p>In 1922, the idea of a conductor-less orchestra was revived in post-<a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolutionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. The symphony orchestra <a href="/wiki/Persimfans" title="Persimfans">Persimfans</a> was formed without a conductor, because the founders believed that the ensemble should be modeled on the ideal <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> state, in which all people are equal. As such, its members felt that there was no need to be led by the dictatorial baton of a conductor; instead they were led by a <a href="/wiki/Committee" title="Committee">committee</a>, which determined tempos and playing styles. Although it was a partial success within the Soviet Union, the principal difficulty with the concept was in changing <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> during performances, because even if the committee had issued a decree about where a tempo change should take place, there was no leader in the ensemble to guide this tempo change. The orchestra survived for ten years before <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Stalin's</a> cultural politics disbanded it by taking away its funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Western nations, some ensembles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Orpheus_Chamber_Orchestra" title="Orpheus Chamber Orchestra">Orpheus Chamber Orchestra</a>, based in New York City, have had more success with conductorless orchestras, although decisions are likely to be deferred to some sense of leadership within the ensemble (for example, the principal wind and string players, notably the concertmaster). Others have returned to the tradition of a principal player, usually a violinist, being the artistic director and running rehearsal and leading concerts. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Chamber_Orchestra" title="Australian Chamber Orchestra">Australian Chamber Orchestra</a>, Amsterdam Sinfonietta &amp; Candida Thompson and the <a href="/wiki/New_Century_Chamber_Orchestra" title="New Century Chamber Orchestra">New Century Chamber Orchestra</a>. As well, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Early_music" title="Early music">early music</a> movement, some 20th and 21st century orchestras have revived the Baroque practice of having no conductor on the podium for Baroque pieces, using the concertmaster or a chord-playing <a href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" title="Basso continuo">basso continuo</a> performer (e.g., harpsichord or organ) to lead the group.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_conductors">Multiple conductors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Multiple conductors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Offstage_instruments">Offstage instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Offstage instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some orchestral works specify that an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Offstage_trumpet" title="Offstage trumpet">offstage trumpet</a> should be used or that other instruments from the orchestra should be positioned off-stage or behind the stage, to create a haunted, mystical effect. To ensure that the offstage instrumentalist(s) play in time, sometimes a sub-conductor will be stationed offstage with a clear view of the principal conductor. Examples include the ending of "<a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">Gustav Holst</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Planets" title="The Planets">The Planets</a></i>. The principal conductor leads the large orchestra, and the sub-conductor relays the principal conductor's tempo and gestures to the offstage musician (or musicians). One of the challenges with using two conductors is that the second conductor may get out of synchronization with the main conductor, or may mis-convey (or misunderstand) the principal conductor's gestures, which can lead to the offstage instruments being out of time. In the late 20th century and early 21st century, some orchestras use a <a href="/wiki/Video_camera" title="Video camera">video camera</a> pointed at the principal conductor and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_TV" title="Closed-circuit TV">closed-circuit TV</a> set in front of the offstage performers, instead of using two conductors.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_music">Contemporary music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Contemporary music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The techniques of <a href="/wiki/Polystylism" title="Polystylism">polystylism</a> and polytempo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> music have led a few 20th and 21st century composers to write music where multiple orchestras or ensembles perform simultaneously. These trends have brought about the phenomenon of polyconductor music, wherein separate sub-conductors conduct each group of musicians. Usually, one principal conductor conducts the sub-conductors, thereby shaping the overall performance. In <a href="/wiki/Percy_Grainger" title="Percy Grainger">Percy Grainger</a>'s <i>The Warriors</i> which includes three conductors: the primary conductor of the orchestra, a secondary conductor directing an off-stage <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brass_ensemble&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brass ensemble (page does not exist)">brass ensemble</a>, and a tertiary conductor directing percussion and harp. One example in the late-century orchestral music is <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Stockhausen" title="Karlheinz Stockhausen">Karlheinz Stockhausen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gruppen" title="Gruppen">Gruppen</a></i>, for three orchestras, which are placed around the audience. This way, the "sound masses" could be spatialized, as in an <a href="/wiki/Electroacoustic_music" title="Electroacoustic music">electroacoustic work</a>. <i>Gruppen</i> was premiered in Cologne, in 1958, conducted by Stockhausen, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Maderna" title="Bruno Maderna">Bruno Maderna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a>. It has been performed in 1996 by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Rattle" title="Simon Rattle">Simon Rattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Carewe" title="John Carewe">John Carewe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Harding" title="Daniel Harding">Daniel Harding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
