<p><b>Fire</b> is the rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of a material (the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> chemical process of <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>, releasing <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>, and various reaction <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup>
At a certain point in the combustion reaction, called the ignition point, flames are produced. The <i><a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">flame</a></i> is the visible portion of the fire. Flames consist primarily of carbon dioxide, water vapor, oxygen and nitrogen. If hot enough, the gases may become ionized to produce <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>. Depending on the substances alight, and any impurities outside, the <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a> of the flame and the fire's <a href="/wiki/Intensity_(heat_transfer)" title="Intensity (heat transfer)">intensity</a> will be different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fire in its most common form can result in <a href="/wiki/Conflagration" title="Conflagration">conflagration</a>, which has the potential to cause physical damage through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burning" title="Burning">burning</a>.  Fire is an important process that affects ecological systems around the globe. The positive effects of fire include stimulating growth and maintaining various ecological systems.
Its negative effects include hazard to life and property, atmospheric pollution, and water contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> If fire removes <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion#Vegetative_cover" title="Soil erosion">protective vegetation</a>, heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> may lead to an increase in <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion#Rainfall_and_runoff" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion by water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Also, when vegetation is burned, the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> it contains is released into the atmosphere, unlike elements such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> which remain in the <a href="/wiki/Wood_ash" title="Wood ash">ash</a> and are quickly recycled into the soil. This loss of nitrogen caused by a fire produces a long-term reduction in the fertility of the soil, but this fecundity can potentially be recovered as molecular nitrogen in the atmosphere is "<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">fixed</a>" and converted to <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> by natural phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">leguminous</a> plants that are "nitrogen-fixing" such as <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peas" title="Peas">peas</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_beans" title="Green beans">green beans</a>.
</p><p>Fire has been used by humans in <a href="/wiki/Fire_worship" title="Fire worship">rituals</a>, in agriculture for clearing land, for cooking, generating heat and light, for signaling, propulsion purposes, <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">forging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incineration</a> of waste, <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremation</a>, and as a weapon or mode of destruction.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">Combustion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fire_tetrahedron.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="260" data-file-width="270" decoding="async" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Fire_tetrahedron.svg/220px-Fire_tetrahedron.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Fire_tetrahedron.svg/330px-Fire_tetrahedron.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Fire_tetrahedron.svg/440px-Fire_tetrahedron.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fire_tetrahedron.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The fire <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron" title="Tetrahedron">tetrahedron</a></div></div></div>
<p>Fires start when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">flammable</a> or a combustible material, in combination with a sufficient quantity of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> such as oxygen gas or another oxygen-rich compound (though non-oxygen oxidizers exist), is exposed to a source of heat or ambient <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> above the <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>/oxidizer mix, and is able to sustain a rate of rapid oxidation that produces a <a href="/wiki/Chain_reaction" title="Chain reaction">chain reaction</a>. This is commonly called the <a href="/wiki/Fire_triangle" title="Fire triangle">fire tetrahedron</a>. Fire cannot exist without all of these elements in place and in the right proportions. For example, a flammable liquid will start burning only if the fuel and oxygen are in the right proportions. Some fuel-oxygen mixes may require a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, a substance that is not consumed, when added, in any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> reaction during combustion, but which enables the reactants to combust more readily.
</p><p>Once ignited, a chain reaction must take place whereby fires can sustain their own heat by the further release of heat energy in the process of combustion and may propagate, provided there is a continuous supply of an oxidizer and fuel.
</p><p>If the oxidizer is oxygen from the surrounding air, the presence of a force of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>, or of some similar force caused by acceleration, is necessary to produce <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>, which removes combustion products and brings a supply of oxygen to the fire. Without gravity, a fire rapidly surrounds itself with its own combustion products and non-oxidizing gases from the air, which exclude oxygen and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/extinguish" title="wikt:extinguish">extinguish</a> the fire. Because of this, the risk of fire in a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> is small when it is <a href="/wiki/Orbital_maneuver" title="Orbital maneuver">coasting</a> in inertial flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> This does not apply if oxygen is supplied to the fire by some process other than thermal convection.
</p><p>Fire can be <a href="/wiki/Fire_protection" title="Fire protection">extinguished</a> by removing any one of the elements of the fire tetrahedron. Consider a natural gas flame, such as from a stove-top burner. The fire can be extinguished by any of the following:
</p>
<ul><li>turning off the gas supply, which removes the fuel source;</li>
<li>covering the flame completely, which smothers the flame as the combustion both uses the available oxidizer (the oxygen in the air) and displaces it from the area around the flame with CO<sub>2</sub>;</li>
<li>application of water, which removes heat from the fire faster than the fire can produce it (similarly, blowing hard on a flame will displace the heat of the currently burning gas from its fuel source, to the same end), or</li>
<li>application of a retardant chemical such as <a href="/wiki/Halomethane#Fire_extinguishing" title="Halomethane">Halon</a> to the flame, which retards the chemical reaction itself until the rate of combustion is too slow to maintain the chain reaction.</li></ul>
<p>In contrast, fire is intensified by increasing the overall rate of combustion. Methods to do this include balancing the input of fuel and oxidizer to <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometric</a> proportions, increasing fuel and oxidizer input in this balanced mix, increasing the ambient temperature so the fire's own heat is better able to sustain combustion, or providing a catalyst, a non-reactant medium in which the fuel and oxidizer can more readily react.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flame">Flame</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">Flame</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">Flame test</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Candle-light-animated.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="624" data-file-width="468" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Candle-light-animated.gif/170px-Candle-light-animated.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Candle-light-animated.gif/255px-Candle-light-animated.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Candle-light-animated.gif/340px-Candle-light-animated.gif 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Candle-light-animated.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">flame</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1402" data-file-width="2095" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png/220px-Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png/330px-Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png/440px-Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northwest_Crown_Fire_Experiment.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Northwest Crown Fire Experiment, Canada</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fire.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2896" data-file-width="1944" decoding="async" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Fire.JPG/170px-Fire.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Fire.JPG/255px-Fire.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Fire.JPG/340px-Fire.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fire.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photo of a fire taken with a 1/4000th of a second exposure</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Space_Fire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1750" data-file-width="2250" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Space_Fire.jpg/220px-Space_Fire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Space_Fire.jpg/330px-Space_Fire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Space_Fire.jpg/440px-Space_Fire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Space_Fire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fire is affected by gravity. Left: Flame on Earth; Right: Flame on the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a></div></div></div>
<p>A flame is a mixture of reacting gases and solids emitting visible, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">frequency spectrum</a> of which depends on the chemical composition of the burning material and intermediate reaction products. In many cases, such as the burning of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>, for example wood, or the incomplete <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of gas, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent" title="Incandescent">incandescent</a> solid particles called <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> produce the familiar red-orange glow of "fire". This light has a <a href="/wiki/Continuous_spectrum" title="Continuous spectrum">continuous spectrum</a>. Complete combustion of gas has a dim blue color due to the emission of single-wavelength radiation from various electron transitions in the excited molecules formed in the flame. Usually oxygen is involved, but <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> burning in <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> also produces a flame, producing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> (HCl). Other possible combinations producing flames, amongst many, are <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_tetroxide" title="Nitrogen tetroxide">nitrogen tetroxide</a>. Hydrogen and hydrazine/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">UDMH</a> flames are similarly pale blue, while burning <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> and its compounds, evaluated in mid-20th century as a <a href="/wiki/Zip_fuel" title="Zip fuel">high energy fuel</a> for <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a>, emits intense green flame, leading to its informal nickname of "Green Dragon".
</p><p>The glow of a flame is complex. <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">Black-body radiation</a> is emitted from soot, gas, and fuel particles, though the soot particles are too small to behave like perfect blackbodies. There is also <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> emission by de-excited <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in the gases. Much of the radiation is emitted in the visible and infrared bands. The color depends on temperature for the black-body radiation, and on chemical makeup for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_spectra" title="Emission spectra">emission spectra</a>. The dominant color in a flame changes with temperature. The photo of the forest fire in Canada is an excellent example of this variation. Near the ground, where most burning is occurring, the fire is white, the hottest color possible for organic material in general, or yellow. Above the yellow region, the color changes to orange, which is cooler, then red, which is cooler still. Above the red region, combustion no longer occurs, and the uncombusted carbon particles are visible as black <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a>.
</p><p>The common distribution of a flame under normal gravity conditions depends on <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>, as soot tends to rise to the top of a general flame, as in a <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candle</a> in normal gravity conditions, making it yellow. In <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">micro gravity or zero gravity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> such as an environment in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>, convection no longer occurs, and the flame becomes spherical, with a tendency to become more blue and more efficient (although it may go out if not moved steadily, as the CO<sub>2</sub> from combustion does not disperse as readily in micro gravity, and tends to smother the flame). There are several possible explanations for this difference, of which the most likely is that the temperature is sufficiently evenly distributed that soot is not formed and complete combustion occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> Experiments by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">NASA</a> reveal that <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_flame" title="Diffusion flame">diffusion flames</a> in micro gravity allow more soot to be completely oxidized after they are produced than diffusion flames on Earth, because of a series of mechanisms that behave differently in micro gravity when compared to normal gravity conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> These discoveries have potential applications in <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_industry" title="Private industry">industry</a>, especially concerning <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">combustion engines</a>, various steps are taken to eliminate a flame. The method depends mainly on whether the fuel is oil, wood, or a high-energy fuel such as <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Fire_science_.26_ecology"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_science_&amp;_ecology">Fire science &amp; ecology</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fire_ecology" title="Fire ecology">Fire ecology</a></div>
<p>Every natural ecosystem has its own <a href="/wiki/Fire_regime" title="Fire regime">fire regime</a>, and the organisms in those ecosystems are adapted to or dependent upon that fire regime. Fire creates a mosaic of different <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> patches, each at a different stage of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Different species of plants, animals, and microbes specialize in exploiting a particular stage, and by creating these different types of patches, fire allows a greater number of species to exist within a landscape.
</p><p>Fire science is a branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_science" title="Physical science">physical science</a> which includes fire behavior, dynamics, and <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>. Applications of fire science include <a href="/wiki/Fire_protection" title="Fire protection">fire protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_investigation" title="Fire investigation">fire investigation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> management.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fossil_record_of_fire" title="Fossil record of fire">Fossil record of fire</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fossil_record_of_fire" title="Fossil record of fire">fossil record of fire</a> first appears with the establishment of a land-based flora in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Ordovician" title="Middle Ordovician">Middle Ordovician</a> period, <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=470">470</a> million years ago</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wellman2000_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wellman2000-13">[12]</a></sup> permitting the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the atmosphere as never before, as the new hordes of land plants pumped it out as a waste product. When this concentration rose above 13%, it permitted the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1991_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1991-14">[13]</a></sup>   Wildfire is first recorded in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Silurian" title="Late Silurian">Late Silurian</a> fossil record, <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=420">420</a> million years ago</span>, by fossils of <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoalified</a> plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoiGMissing_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DoiGMissing-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott2006-16">[15]</a></sup> Apart from a controversial gap in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Devonian" title="Late Devonian">Late Devonian</a>, charcoal is present ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott2006_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scott2006-16">[15]</a></sup> The level of atmospheric oxygen is closely related to the prevalence of charcoal: clearly oxygen is the key factor in the abundance of wildfire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2009-17">[16]</a></sup>  Fire also became more abundant when grasses radiated and became the dominant component of many ecosystems, around <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=6–7">6 to 7</a> million years ago</span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retallack1997_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retallack1997-18">[17]</a></sup> this kindling provided <a href="/wiki/Tinder" title="Tinder">tinder</a> which allowed for the more rapid spread of fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2009_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2009-17">[16]</a></sup>  These widespread fires may have initiated a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> process, whereby they produced a warmer, drier climate more conducive to fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2009_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2009-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_control">Human control</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_human_control">Early human control</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Control_of_fire_by_early_humans" title="Control of fire by early humans">Control of fire by early humans</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Des8.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2964" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Des8.jpg/220px-Des8.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Des8.jpg/330px-Des8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Des8.jpg/440px-Des8.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Des8.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bushman starting a fire in <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ignition_of_a_match.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2079" data-file-width="2073" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Ignition_of_a_match.jpg/220px-Ignition_of_a_match.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Ignition_of_a_match.jpg/330px-Ignition_of_a_match.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Ignition_of_a_match.jpg/440px-Ignition_of_a_match.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ignition_of_a_match.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Process of ignition of a match</div></div></div>
<p>The ability to <a href="/wiki/Control_of_fire_by_early_humans" title="Control of fire by early humans">control fire</a> was a dramatic change in the habits of early humans. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Making_fire" title="Making fire">Making fire</a> to generate heat and light made it possible for people to <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cook</a> food, simultaneously increasing the variety and availability of nutrients and reducing disease by killing organisms in the food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The heat produced would also help people stay warm in cold weather, enabling them to live in cooler climates. Fire also kept nocturnal predators at bay. Evidence of cooked food is found from <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=1.0">1</a> million years ago</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Although there is some evidence that fire may have been used in a controlled fashion about 1 million years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> other sources put the date at 400,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2009b_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2009b-23">[22]</a></sup>  Evidence becomes widespread around 50 to 100 thousand years ago, suggesting regular use from this time; interestingly, resistance to <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> started to evolve in human populations at a similar point in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2009b_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2009b-23">[22]</a></sup>  The use of fire became progressively more sophisticated, with it being used to create charcoal and to control wildlife from 'tens of thousands' of years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2009b_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2009b-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fire has also been used for centuries as a method of torture and execution, as evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Death_by_burning" title="Death by burning">death by burning</a> as well as torture devices such as the <a href="/wiki/Boot_(torture)" title="Boot (torture)">iron boot</a>, which could be filled with water, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a>, or even <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and then heated over an open fire to the agony of the wearer.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turun_palo_1827.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2614" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Turun_palo_1827.jpg/220px-Turun_palo_1827.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Turun_palo_1827.jpg/330px-Turun_palo_1827.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Turun_palo_1827.jpg/440px-Turun_palo_1827.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turun_palo_1827.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting of the <a href="/wiki/Turku_Cathedral" title="Turku Cathedral">Cathedral</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Turku" title="Academy of Turku">Academy building</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_Turku" title="Great Fire of Turku">Great Fire of Turku</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Wilhelm_Finnberg" title="Gustaf Wilhelm Finnberg">Gustaf Wilhelm Finnberg</a>, 1827</div></div></div>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a>, during the introduction of grain-based agriculture, people all over the world used fire as a tool in <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a> management. These fires were typically <a href="/wiki/Controlled_burn" title="Controlled burn">controlled burns</a> or "cool fires", as opposed to uncontrolled "hot fires", which damage the soil. Hot fires destroy plants and animals, and endanger communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> This is especially a problem in the forests of today where traditional burning is prevented in order to encourage the growth of timber crops. Cool fires are generally conducted in the spring and autumn. They clear undergrowth, burning up <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> that could trigger a hot fire should it get too dense. They provide a greater variety of environments, which encourages game and plant diversity. For humans, they make dense, impassable forests traversable. Another human use for fire in regards to landscape management is its use to clear land for agriculture. Slash-and-burn agriculture is still common across much of tropical Africa, Asia and South America. "For small farmers, it is a convenient way to clear overgrown areas and release nutrients from standing vegetation back into the soil", said Miguel Pinedo-Vasquez, an ecologist at the <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20121205080303/http://www.cerc.columbia.edu/" rel="nofollow">Earth Institute’s Center for Environmental Research and Conservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blogs.ei.columbia.edu_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-blogs.ei.columbia.edu-25">[24]</a></sup> However, this useful strategy is also problematic. Growing population, fragmentation of forests and warming climate are making the earth's surface more prone to ever-larger escaped fires. These harm ecosystems and human infrastructure, cause health problems, and send up spirals of carbon and soot that may encourage even more warming of the atmosphere – and thus feed back into more fires. Globally today, as much as 5 million square kilometres – an area more than half the size of the United States – burns in a given year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blogs.ei.columbia.edu_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-blogs.ei.columbia.edu-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_human_control">Later human control</span></h3>
<p>There are numerous modern applications of fire. In its broadest sense, fire is used by nearly every human being on earth in a controlled setting every day. Users of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_combustion" title="Internal combustion">internal combustion</a> vehicles employ fire every time they drive. Thermal <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a> provide <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> for a large percentage of humanity by igniting fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, then using the resultant heat to boil water into <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>, which then drives <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbines</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command,_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1772" data-file-width="2143" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command%2C_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg/220px-Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command%2C_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command%2C_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg/330px-Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command%2C_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command%2C_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg/440px-Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command%2C_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Air_Force_Bomber_Command,_1942-1945._CL3400.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> after four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire-bombing" title="Fire-bombing">fire-bombing</a> raids in July 1943, which killed an estimated 50,000 people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The use of fire in <a href="/wiki/Conventional_warfare" title="Conventional warfare">warfare</a> has a long <a href="/wiki/Military_history" title="Military history">history</a>. Fire was the basis of all <a href="/wiki/Early_thermal_weapons" title="Early thermal weapons">early thermal weapons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> detailed the use of fire by Greek soldiers who hid in a <a href="/wiki/Trojan_Horse" title="Trojan Horse">wooden horse</a> to burn <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_war" title="Trojan war">Trojan war</a>. Later the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> fleet used <a href="/wiki/Greek_fire" title="Greek fire">Greek fire</a> to attack ships and men. In the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, the first modern <a href="/wiki/Flamethrower" title="Flamethrower">flamethrowers</a> were used by infantry, and were successfully mounted on armoured vehicles in the Second World War. In the latter war, <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">incendiary bombs</a> were used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" title="Axis Powers">Axis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> alike, notably on Tokyo, Rotterdam, London, Hamburg and, notoriously, at <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>; in the latter two cases <a href="/wiki/Firestorm" title="Firestorm">firestorms</a> were deliberately caused in which a ring of fire surrounding each city was drawn inward by an updraft caused by a central cluster of fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarashWebel2008_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BarashWebel2008-27">[26]</a></sup> The United States Army Air Force also extensively used incendiaries against Japanese targets in the latter months of the war, devastating entire cities constructed primarily of wood and paper houses. The use of <a href="/wiki/Napalm" title="Napalm">napalm</a> was employed in July 1944, towards the end of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-napalm_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-napalm-28">[27]</a></sup> although its use did not gain public attention until the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-napalm_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-napalm-28">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Molotov_cocktail" title="Molotov cocktail">Molotov cocktails</a> were also used.
</p>

<p>Setting <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> aflame releases usable energy. <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">Wood</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric</a> fuel, and is still viable today. The use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">power plants</a> supplies the vast majority of the world's electricity today; the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> states that nearly 80% of the world's power came from these sources in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> The fire in a <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power station</a> is used to heat water, creating steam that drives <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbines</a>. The turbines then spin an <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generator</a> to produce electricity. Fire is also used to provide <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">mechanical work</a> directly, in both <a href="/wiki/External_combustion_engine" title="External combustion engine">external</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">unburnable</a> solid remains of a combustible material left after a fire is called <i>clinker</i> if its <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> is below the flame temperature, so that it fuses and then solidifies as it cools, and <i>ash</i> if its melting point is above the flame temperature.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_management">Fire management</span></h4>
<p>Deftly controlling a fire to optimize its size, shape, and intensity is generally called <i>fire management</i>, and the more advanced forms of it, as traditionally (and sometimes still) practiced by skilled cooks, <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ironmaster" title="Ironmaster">ironmasters</a>, and others, are highly <a href="/wiki/Skill" title="Skill">skilled</a> activities. They include knowledge about selecting which types of wood, charcoal, or mineral coal to burn; how to arrange the fuel; how to stoke the fire both in early phases and in maintenance phases; how to modulate the amount of heat, flames, and smoke as suited to the desired application; how best to bank a fire to be revived later; how to choose, design, or modify woodstoves, coalstoves, bakery ovens, and industrial <a href="/wiki/Industrial_furnace" title="Industrial furnace">furnaces</a>; and so on. Detailed expositions of fire management are available in various books about blacksmithing, about skilled <a href="/wiki/Camping" title="Camping">camping</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">military scouting</a>, and about the <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">domestic arts</a> in previous centuries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection_and_prevention">Protection and prevention</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Wildfire#Prevention" title="Wildfire">Wildfire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fire_protection" title="Fire protection">Fire protection</a></div>
<p>Wildfire prevention programs around the world may employ techniques such as <i>wildland fire use</i> and <i>prescribed or <a href="/wiki/Controlled_burn" title="Controlled burn">controlled burns</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-operations1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-operations1-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> <i>Wildland fire use</i> refers to any fire of natural causes that is monitored but allowed to burn. <i>Controlled burns</i> are fires ignited by government agencies under less dangerous weather conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_fighting" title="Fire fighting">Fire fighting</a> services are provided in most developed areas to extinguish or contain uncontrolled fires. Trained <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">firefighters</a> use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" title="Fire apparatus">fire apparatus</a>, water supply resources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_main" title="Water main">water mains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fire_hydrant" title="Fire hydrant">fire hydrants</a> or they might use A and B class foam depending on what is feeding the fire.
</p><p>Fire prevention is intended to reduce sources of ignition.  Fire prevention also includes education to teach people how to avoid causing fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Buildings, especially schools and tall buildings, often conduct <a href="/wiki/Fire_drill" title="Fire drill">fire drills</a> to inform and prepare citizens on how to react to a building fire. Purposely starting destructive fires constitutes <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">arson</a> and is a crime in most jurisdictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Model <a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">building codes</a> require <a href="/wiki/Passive_fire_protection" title="Passive fire protection">passive fire protection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_fire_protection" title="Active fire protection">active fire protection</a> systems to minimize damage resulting from a fire. The most common form of active fire protection is <a href="/wiki/Fire_sprinkler" title="Fire sprinkler">fire sprinklers</a>. To maximize passive fire protection of buildings, building materials and furnishings in most developed countries are tested for <a href="/wiki/Fire-resistance_rating" title="Fire-resistance rating">fire-resistance</a>, combustibility and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammability</a>. <a href="/wiki/Upholstery" title="Upholstery">Upholstery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpeting" title="Carpeting">carpeting</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastics" title="Plastics">plastics</a> used in vehicles and vessels are also tested.
</p><p>Where fire prevention and fire protection have failed to prevent damage, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_insurance" title="Fire insurance">fire insurance</a> can mitigate the financial impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fire-damaged_restaurant,_Balingen_(Zollernalbkreis).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3098" data-file-width="4555" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Fire-damaged_restaurant%2C_Balingen_%28Zollernalbkreis%29.jpg/220px-Fire-damaged_restaurant%2C_Balingen_%28Zollernalbkreis%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Fire-damaged_restaurant%2C_Balingen_%28Zollernalbkreis%29.jpg/330px-Fire-damaged_restaurant%2C_Balingen_%28Zollernalbkreis%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Fire-damaged_restaurant%2C_Balingen_%28Zollernalbkreis%29.jpg/440px-Fire-damaged_restaurant%2C_Balingen_%28Zollernalbkreis%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fire-damaged_restaurant,_Balingen_(Zollernalbkreis).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fire-damaged <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> waiting for <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolition</a></div></div></div>
<p>Different restoration methods and measures are used depending on the type of fire damage that occurred. Restoration after fire damage can be performed by <a href="/wiki/Property_management" title="Property management">property management</a> teams, building maintenance personnel, or by the homeowners themselves; however, contacting a certified professional fire damage restoration specialist is often regarded as the safest way to restore fire damaged property due to their training and extensive experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Most are usually listed under "Fire and Water Restoration" and they can help speed repairs, whether for individual homeowners or for the largest of institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fire and Water Restoration companies are regulated by the appropriate state's Department of Consumer Affairs – usually the state contractors license board. In California, all Fire and Water Restoration companies must register with the California Contractors State License Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Presently, the California Contractors State License Board has no specific classification for "water and fire damage restoration." Hence, the Contractor's State License Board requires both an asbestos certification (ASB) as well as a demolition classification (C-21) in order to perform Fire and Water Restoration work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
