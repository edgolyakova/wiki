</p>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Illegal behavior defined by existing criminal law</div><p>In ordinary language, a <b>crime</b> is an unlawful act punishable by a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> or other authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup> The term <i>crime</i> does not, in modern <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>, have any simple and universally accepted definition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farmer_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farmer-2">[2]</a></sup> though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statutory" title="Statutory">statutory</a> definitions have been provided for certain purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The most popular view is that crime is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_of_being" title="Category of being">category</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>; in other words, something is a crime if declared as such by the relevant and applicable law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farmer_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Farmer-2">[2]</a></sup> One proposed definition is that a crime or <b>offence</b> (or <b>criminal offence</b>) is an act harmful not only to some individual but also to a community, society, or the state ("a public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrong" title="Wrong">wrong</a>"). Such acts are forbidden and punishable by law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxdic_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxdic-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The notion that acts such as <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">theft</a> are to be prohibited exists worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-5">[5]</a></sup> What precisely is a criminal offence is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a> of each relevant <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>. While many have a catalogue of crimes called the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_code" title="Criminal code">criminal code</a>, in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_law_(legal_system)" title="Common law (legal system)">common law</a> nations no such comprehensive statute exists.
</p><p>The state (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forms_of_government" title="Forms of government">government</a>) has the power to severely restrict one's <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a> for committing a crime. In modern societies, there are <a href="/wiki/Criminal_procedure" title="Criminal procedure">procedures</a> to which investigations and <a href="/wiki/Trial" title="Trial">trials</a> must adhere. If found <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(law)" title="Guilt (law)">guilty</a>, an offender may be <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentenced</a> to a form of reparation such as a <a href="/wiki/Community_sentence" title="Community sentence">community sentence</a>, or, depending on the nature of their offence, to undergo <a href="/wiki/Imprisonment" title="Imprisonment">imprisonment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life imprisonment</a> or, in some <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdictions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">death</a>. Some jurisdictions sentence individuals to programs to emphasize or provide for their <a href="/wiki/Rehabilitation_(penology)" title="Rehabilitation (penology)">rehabilitation</a> while most jurisdictions sentence individuals with the goal of <a href="/wiki/Incapacitation_(penology)" title="Incapacitation (penology)">punishing</a> them or a mix of the aforementioned practices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Usually, to be classified as a crime, the "act of doing something criminal" (<i><a href="/wiki/Actus_reus" title="Actus reus">actus reus</a></i>) must – with <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">certain exceptions</a> – be accompanied by the "intention to do something criminal" (<i><a href="/wiki/Mens_rea" title="Mens rea">mens rea</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxdic_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxdic-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>While every crime violates the law, not every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Violation_of_the_law" title="Violation of the law">violation of the law</a> counts as a crime. Breaches of <a href="/wiki/Private_law" title="Private law">private law</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">torts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breaches of contract</a>) are not automatically punished by the state, but can be enforced through <a href="/wiki/Civil_procedure" title="Civil procedure">civil procedure</a>.
</p>

<p>When informal relationships prove insufficient to establish and maintain a desired <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a>, a government or a state may impose more formalized or stricter systems of <a href="/wiki/Social_control" title="Social control">social control</a>. With institutional and legal machinery at their disposal, agents of the state can compel <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">populations</a> to conform to codes and can opt to punish or attempt to reform those who do not conform.
</p><p>Authorities employ various mechanisms to regulate (encouraging or discouraging) certain behaviors in general. Governing or administering agencies may for example codify rules into laws, police citizens and visitors to ensure that they comply with those laws, and implement other policies and practices that legislators or administrators have prescribed with the aim of discouraging or <a href="/wiki/Crime_prevention" title="Crime prevention">preventing crime</a>. In addition, authorities provide <a href="/wiki/Legal_remedy" title="Legal remedy">remedies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanctions_(law)" title="Sanctions (law)">sanctions</a>, and collectively these constitute a <a href="/wiki/Criminal_justice" title="Criminal justice">criminal justice</a> system. Legal sanctions vary widely in their severity; they may include (for example) incarceration of temporary character aimed at reforming the <a href="/wiki/Convict" title="Convict">convict</a>. Some jurisdictions have penal codes written to inflict permanent harsh punishments: legal <a href="/wiki/Mutilation" title="Mutilation">mutilation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life without parole</a>.
</p><p>Usually, a <a href="/wiki/Natural_person" title="Natural person">natural person</a> perpetrates a crime, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_personality" title="Legal personality">legal persons</a> may also commit crimes. Historically, several premodern societies believed that non-human <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> were capable of committing crimes, and prosecuted and punished them accordingly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sociologist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Quinney" title="Richard Quinney">Richard Quinney</a> has written about the relationship between society and crime. When Quinney states "crime is a <a href="/wiki/Social_phenomenon" title="Social phenomenon">social phenomenon</a>" he envisages both how individuals conceive crime and how populations perceive it, based on <a href="/wiki/Social_norm" title="Social norm">societal norms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>crime</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> root <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">cernō</i></span>, meaning "I decide, I give judgment". Originally the Latin word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/crimen#Latin" title="wiktionary:crimen">crīmen</a></i> meant "<a href="/wiki/Criminal_charge" title="Criminal charge">charge</a>" or "cry of distress".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> word <span title="Ancient Greek-language text"><span lang="grc">κρίμα</span></span>, <span title="Ancient Greek-language romanization"><i lang="grc-Latn">krima</i></span>, from which the Latin <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> derives, typically referred to an intellectual mistake or an offense against the community, rather than a private or moral wrong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 13th century <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <i>crime</i> meant "sinfulness", according to the <a href="/wiki/Online_Etymology_Dictionary" title="Online Etymology Dictionary">Online Etymology Dictionary</a>. It was probably brought to England as Old French <span title="Old French (842-ca. 1400)-language text"><i lang="fro">crimne</i></span> (12th century form of <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">Modern French</a> <i>crime</i>), from Latin <i>crimen</i> (in the genitive case: <i>criminis</i>). In Latin, <i>crimen</i> could have signified any one of the following: "<a href="/wiki/Criminal_charge" title="Criminal charge">charge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Criminal_accusation" title="Criminal accusation">accusation</a>; crime, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fault_(legal)" title="Fault (legal)">fault</a>, offense".
</p><p>The word may derive from the Latin <i>cernere</i> – "to decide, to sift" (see <a href="/wiki/Crisis" title="Crisis">crisis</a>, mapped on <a href="/wiki/Kairos" title="Kairos">Kairos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chronos" title="Chronos">Chronos</a>). But <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Klein" title="Ernest Klein">Ernest Klein</a> (citing <a href="/wiki/Karl_Brugmann" title="Karl Brugmann">Karl Brugmann</a>) rejects this and suggests *cri-men, which originally would have meant "cry of distress". Thomas G. Tucker suggests a root in "<a href="/wiki/Crying" title="Crying">cry</a>" words and refers to English <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">plaint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">plaintiff</a>, and so on. The meaning "offense punishable by law" dates from the late 14th century. The Latin word is <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">glossed</a> in Old English by <i>facen</i>, also "deceit, fraud, treachery", [cf. fake]. <i>Crime wave</i> is first attested in 1893 in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span></h3>
<p>Whether a given act or <a href="/wiki/Omission_(law)" title="Omission (law)">omission</a> constitutes a crime does not depend on the nature of that act or omission; it depends on the nature of the legal consequences that may follow it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> An act or omission is a crime if it is capable of being followed by what are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_proceedings" title="Criminal proceedings">criminal proceedings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law,_Eleventh_Edition_1982,_page_3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Law,_Eleventh_Edition_1982,_page_3-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The following definition of <i>crime</i> was provided by the Prevention of Crimes Act 1871, and applied<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> for the purposes of section 10 of the Prevention of Crime Act 1908:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The expression "crime" means, in England and Ireland, any <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felony</a> or the offence of <a href="/wiki/Uttering" title="Uttering">uttering</a> false or counterfeit coin, or of possessing counterfeit gold or silver coin, or the offence of obtaining goods or money by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_pretences" title="False pretences">false pretences</a>, or the offence of <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_to_defraud" title="Conspiracy to defraud">conspiracy to defraud</a>, or any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misdemeanour" title="Misdemeanour">misdemeanour</a> under the fifty-eighth section of the <a href="/wiki/Larceny_Act_1861" title="Larceny Act 1861">Larceny Act, 1861</a>.</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span></h3>
<p>For the purpose of section 243 of the <a href="/wiki/Trade_Union_and_Labour_Relations_(Consolidation)_Act_1992" title="Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992">Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992</a>, a crime means an offence punishable on <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictment</a>, or an offence punishable on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summary_conviction" title="Summary conviction">summary conviction</a>, and for the commission of which the offender is liable under the <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a> making the offence punishable to be imprisoned either absolutely or at the discretion of the court as an alternative for some other punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span></h3>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" title="Norm (sociology)">normative</a> <a href="/wiki/Definition" title="Definition">definition</a> views crime as <a href="/wiki/Deviance_(sociology)" title="Deviance (sociology)">deviant behavior</a> that violates prevailing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" title="Norm (sociology)">norms</a> – <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a> standards prescribing how humans ought to behave normally. This approach considers the complex realities surrounding the concept of crime and seeks to understand how changing <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">social</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychological</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic</a> conditions may affect changing definitions of crime and the form of the legal, <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">law-enforcement</a>, and penal responses made by society.
</p><p>These <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">structural</a> realities remain fluid and often contentious. For example: as cultures change and the political environment shifts, societies may <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminalisation" title="Criminalisation">criminalise</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decriminalisation" title="Decriminalisation">decriminalise</a> certain behaviours, which directly affects the <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crime_rate" title="Crime rate">crime rates</a>, influence the allocation of resources for the enforcement of laws, and (re-)influence the general <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">public opinion</a>.
</p><p>Similarly, changes in the collection and/or calculation of data on crime may affect the public perceptions of the extent of any given "crime problem". All such adjustments to <a href="/wiki/Crime_statistics" title="Crime statistics">crime statistics</a>, allied with the experience of people in their everyday lives, shape attitudes on the extent to which the state should use law or <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_(political_science)" title="Social engineering (political science)">social engineering</a> to enforce or encourage any particular <a href="/wiki/Social_norm" title="Social norm">social norm</a>. Behaviour can be controlled and influenced by a society in many ways without having to resort to the criminal justice system.
</p><p>Indeed, in those cases where no clear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consensus" title="Consensus">consensus</a> exists on a given norm, the drafting of <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a> by the group in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">power</a> to prohibit the behaviour of another group may seem to some observers an improper limitation of the second group's <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">freedom</a>, and the ordinary members of society have less respect for the law or laws in general – whether the authorities actually enforce the disputed law or not.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_definitions">Other definitions</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">Legislatures</a> can pass laws (called <a href="/wiki/Malum_prohibitum" title="Malum prohibitum"><i>mala prohibita</i></a>) that define crimes against social norms. These laws vary from time to time and from place to place: note variations in <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a> laws, for example, and the prohibition or encouragement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duelling" title="Duelling">duelling</a> in history. Other crimes, called <a href="/wiki/Malum_in_se" title="Malum in se"><i>mala in se</i></a>, count as outlawed in almost all societies, (<a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">theft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, for example).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/English_criminal_law" title="English criminal law">English criminal law</a> and the related criminal law of <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries can define offences that the courts alone have developed over the years, without any actual legislation: <a href="/wiki/Common_law_offence" title="Common law offence">common law offences</a>. The courts used the concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Malum_in_se" title="Malum in se">malum in se</a></i> to develop various common law offences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminalization">Criminalization</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Criminalization" title="Criminalization">Criminalization</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="505" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg/220px-Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg/330px-Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg/440px-Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Traitors_heads_on_old_london_bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The spiked heads of executed criminals once adorned the gatehouse of the medieval <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>One can view criminalization as a procedure deployed by society as a preemptive harm-reduction device, using the threat of punishment as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterrence_(legal)" title="Deterrence (legal)">deterrent</a> to anyone proposing to engage in the behavior causing harm. The state becomes involved because governing entities can become convinced that the costs of not criminalizing (through allowing the harms to continue unabated) outweigh the costs of criminalizing it (restricting individual liberty, for example, to minimize harm to others).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>States control the process of criminalization because:
</p>
<ul><li>Even if <a href="/wiki/Victimology" title="Victimology">victims</a> recognize their own role as victims, they may not have the resources to investigate and seek legal redress for the injuries suffered: the enforcers formally appointed by the state often have better access to expertise and resources.</li>
<li>The victims may only want compensation for the injuries suffered, while remaining indifferent to a possible desire for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterrence_(psychology)" title="Deterrence (psychology)">deterrence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Fear of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retaliate" title="Retaliate">retaliation</a> may deter victims or witnesses of crimes from taking any action. Even in policed societies, fear may inhibit from reporting incidents or from co-operating in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" title="Trial (law)">trial</a>.</li>
<li>Victims, on their own, may lack the economies of scale that could allow them to administer a penal system, let alone to collect any fines levied by a court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Garoupa and Klerman (2002) warn that a <a href="/wiki/Rent-seeking" title="Rent-seeking">rent-seeking</a> government has as its primary motivation to maximize revenue and so, if offenders have sufficient wealth, a rent-seeking government will act more aggressively than a <a href="/wiki/Social_welfare_function" title="Social welfare function">social-welfare</a>-maximizing government in enforcing laws against minor crimes (usually with a fixed penalty such as parking and routine traffic violations), but more laxly in enforcing laws against major crimes.</li>
<li>As a result of the crime, victims may die or become incapacitated.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labelling_theory">Labelling theory</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href='/wiki/Labeling_theory#The_"criminal"' title="Labeling theory">Labeling theory § The "criminal"</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Label_(sociology)" title="Label (sociology)">label</a> of "crime" and the accompanying <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">social stigma</a> normally confine their scope to those activities seen as injurious to the general population or to the state, including some that cause serious loss or damage to individuals. Those who apply the labels of "crime" or "criminal" intend to assert the <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> of a dominant population, or to reflect a consensus of condemnation for the identified behavior and to justify any punishments prescribed by the state (if <a href="/wiki/Due_process" title="Due process">standard processing</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" title="Trial (law)">tries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conviction_(law)" title="Conviction (law)">convicts</a> an accused person of a crime).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural-law_theory">Natural-law theory</span></h2>
<p>Justifying the state's use of <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">force</a> to coerce compliance with its laws has proven a consistent theoretical problem. One of the earliest justifications involved the theory of <a href="/wiki/Natural_law" title="Natural law">natural law</a>. This posits that the nature of the world or of human beings underlies the standards of <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a> or constructs them. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> wrote in the 13th century: "the rule and measure of human acts is the <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>, which is the first principle of human acts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He regarded people as by nature <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rational</a> beings, concluding that it becomes morally appropriate that they should behave in a way that conforms to their rational nature. Thus, to be valid, any law must conform to natural law and coercing people to conform to that law is morally acceptable. In the 1760s, <a href="/wiki/William_Blackstone" title="William Blackstone">William Blackstone</a> described the thesis:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>"This law of nature, being co-eval with mankind and dictated by God himself, is of course superior in obligation to any other. It is binding over all the globe, in all countries, and at all times: no human laws are of any validity, if contrary to this; and such of them as are valid derive all their force, and all their authority, mediately or immediately, from this original."</dd></dl>
<p>But <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Austin_(legal_philosophy)" title="John Austin (legal philosophy)">John Austin</a> (1790–1859), an early <a href="/wiki/Legal_positivism" title="Legal positivism">positivist</a>, applied <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a> in accepting the calculating nature of human beings and the existence of an objective morality. He denied that the legal validity of a norm depends on whether its content conforms to morality. Thus in Austinian terms, a moral code can objectively determine what people ought to do, the law can embody whatever norms the legislature decrees to achieve social utility, but every individual remains free to choose what to do. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/H._L._A._Hart" title="H. L. A. Hart">H.L.A. Hart</a> saw the law as an aspect of <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>, with lawmakers able to adopt any law as a means to a moral end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thus the necessary and sufficient conditions for the truth of a proposition of law simply involved internal <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consistency" title="Consistency">consistency</a>, and that the state's agents used state power with <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">responsibility</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Dworkin" title="Ronald Dworkin">Ronald Dworkin</a> rejects Hart's theory and proposes that all individuals should expect the equal respect and concern of those who govern them as a fundamental political right. He offers a theory of compliance overlaid by a theory of <a href="/wiki/Deference" title="Deference">deference</a> (the citizen's duty to obey the law) and a theory of enforcement, which identifies the legitimate goals of enforcement and punishment. Legislation must conform to a theory of legitimacy, which describes the circumstances under which a particular person or group is entitled to make law, and a theory of legislative justice, which describes the law they are entitled or obliged to make.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are natural-law theorists who have accepted the idea of enforcing the prevailing morality as a primary function of the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This view entails the problem that it makes any moral criticism of the law impossible: if conformity with natural law forms a necessary condition for legal validity, all valid law must, by definition, count as morally just. Thus, on this line of reasoning, the legal validity of a norm necessarily entails its moral justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>One can solve this problem by granting some degree of <a href="/wiki/Moral_relativism" title="Moral relativism">moral relativism</a> and accepting that norms may evolve over time and, therefore, one can criticize the continued enforcement of old laws in the light of the current norms. People may find such law acceptable, but the use of state power to coerce citizens to comply with that law lacks moral justification. More recent conceptions of the theory characterise crime as the violation of <a href="/wiki/Individual_and_group_rights" title="Individual and group rights">individual rights</a>.
</p><p>Since society considers so many rights as natural (hence the term <i><a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">right</a></i>) rather than man-made, what constitutes a crime also counts as natural, in contrast to laws (seen as man-made). <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> illustrates this view, saying that a <a href="/wiki/Smuggling" title="Smuggling">smuggler</a> would be an excellent citizen, "...had not the laws of his country made that a crime which nature never meant to be so."
</p><p>Natural-law theory therefore distinguishes between "criminality" (which derives from human nature) and "illegality" (which originates with the interests of those in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">power</a>). Lawyers sometimes express the two concepts with the phrases <i><a href="/wiki/Malum_in_se" title="Malum in se">malum in se</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Malum_prohibitum" title="Malum prohibitum">malum prohibitum</a></i> respectively. They regard a "crime <i>malum in se</i>" as inherently criminal; whereas a "crime <i>malum prohibitum</i>" (the argument goes) counts as criminal only because the law has decreed it so.
</p><p>It follows from this view that one can perform an illegal act without committing a crime, while a criminal act could be perfectly legal. Many <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> thinkers (such as Adam Smith and the American <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_founders" title="List of national founders">Founding Fathers</a>) subscribed to this view to some extent, and it remains influential among so-called <a href="/wiki/Liberalism#Classical_and_modern" title="Liberalism">classical liberals</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarians</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Some religious communities regard <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a> as a crime; some may even highlight the crime of sin very early in legendary or mythological accounts of origins – note the tale of <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a> and the theory of <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">original sin</a>. What one group considers a crime may cause or ignite war or conflict. However, the earliest known <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a> had codes of <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, containing both <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil</a> and penal rules mixed together, though not always in recorded form.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">Ancient Near East</span></h3><p>
The <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerians</a> produced the earliest surviving written codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Urukagina" title="Urukagina">Urukagina</a> (reigned <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 2380 BC</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 2360 BC</span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_chronology_timeline" title="Short chronology timeline">short chronology</a>) had an early code that has not survived; a later king, <a href="/wiki/Ur-Nammu" title="Ur-Nammu">Ur-Nammu</a>, left the earliest extant written law system, the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Ur-Nammu" title="Code of Ur-Nammu">Code of Ur-Nammu</a> (<span title="circa">c.</span><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 2100</span> – c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 2050 BC</span>), which prescribed a formal system of penalties for specific cases in 57 articles. The Sumerians later issued other codes, including the "code of <a href="/wiki/Lipit-Ishtar" title="Lipit-Ishtar">Lipit-Ishtar</a>". This code, from the 20th century BCE, contains some fifty articles, and scholars have reconstructed it by comparing several sources. <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Sumerian was deeply conscious of his personal rights and resented any encroachment on them, whether by his King, his superior, or his equal. No wonder that the Sumerians were the first to compile laws and law codes.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Kramer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote>
<p>Successive legal codes in <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi" title="Code of Hammurabi">code of Hammurabi</a> (<span title="circa">c.</span><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1790 BC</span>), reflected <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a> society's belief that law derived from the will of the <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">gods</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_law" title="Babylonian law">Babylonian law</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
Many states at this time functioned as <a href="/wiki/Theocracy" title="Theocracy">theocracies</a>, with codes of conduct largely religious in origin or reference. In the Sanskrit texts of <a href="/wiki/Dharma%C5%9B%C4%81stra" title="Dharmaśāstra">Dharmaśāstra</a> (<span title="circa">c.</span><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1250 BC</span>), issues such as legal and religious duties, code of conduct, penalties and remedies, etc. have been discussed and forms one of the elaborate and earliest source of legal code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Henry_Maine" title="Sir Henry Maine">Sir Henry Maine</a> studied the ancient codes available in his day, and failed to find any criminal law in the "modern" sense of the word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> While modern systems distinguish between offences against the "state" or "community", and offences against the "individual", the so-called penal law of ancient communities did not deal with "crimes" (Latin: <i>crimina</i>), but with "wrongs" (Latin: <i>delicta</i>). Thus the Hellenic laws treated all forms of <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">theft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assault" title="Assault">assault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, and murder as private wrongs, and left action for enforcement up to the victims or their survivors. The earliest systems seem to have lacked formal courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> systematized law and applied their system across the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. Again, the initial rules of <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> regarded assaults as a matter of private compensation. The most significant Roman law concept involved <i>dominion</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Pater_familias" title="Pater familias">pater familias</a></i> owned all the family and its property (including slaves); the <i>pater</i> enforced matters involving interference with any property. The <i>Commentaries</i> of <a href="/wiki/Gaius_(jurist)" title="Gaius (jurist)">Gaius</a> (written between 130 and 180 AD) on the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Tables" title="Twelve Tables">Twelve Tables</a> treated <i>furtum</i> (in modern parlance: "theft") as a <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a>.
</p><p>Similarly, assault and violent <a href="/wiki/Robbery" title="Robbery">robbery</a> involved <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespass</a> as to the <i>pater's</i> property (so, for example, the rape of a slave could become the subject of compensation to the <i>pater</i> as having trespassed on his "property"), and breach of such laws created a <i>vinculum juris</i> (an obligation of law) that only the payment of monetary compensation (modern "<a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a>") could discharge. Similarly, the consolidated <a href="/wiki/Germanic_law" title="Germanic law">Teutonic laws</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_tribes" title="Germanic tribes">Germanic tribes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> included a complex system of monetary compensations for what courts would now<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crime&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> consider the complete<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> range of criminal offences against the person, from murder down.
</p><p>Even though Rome abandoned its <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannic provinces</a> around 400 AD, the Germanic mercenaries – who had largely become instrumental in enforcing Roman rule in Britannia – acquired ownership of land there and continued to use a mixture of Roman and Teutonic Law, with much written down under the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_kings" title="Anglo-Saxon kings">Anglo-Saxon kings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> But only when a more centralized English monarchy emerged following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman invasion</a>, and when the kings of England attempted to assert power over the land and its peoples, did the modern concept emerge, namely of a crime not only as an offence against the "individual", but also as a wrong against the "state".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>This idea came from <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, and the earliest conception of a criminal act involved events of such major significance that the "state" had to usurp the usual functions of the civil tribunals, and direct a special law or <i>privilegium</i> against the perpetrator. All the earliest English criminal trials involved wholly extraordinary and arbitrary courts without any settled law to apply, whereas the civil (delictual) law operated in a highly developed and consistent manner (except where a king wanted to raise money by selling a new form of <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">writ</a>). The development of the idea that the "state" dispenses <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a> in a court only emerges in parallel with or after the emergence of the concept of <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>.
</p><p>In continental Europe, Roman law persisted, but with a stronger influence from the Christian Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Coupled with the more diffuse political structure based on smaller feudal units, various legal traditions emerged, remaining more strongly rooted in Roman <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a>, but modified to meet the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Political_climate" title="Political climate">political climate</a>.
</p><p>In Scandinavia the effect of Roman law did not become apparent until the 17th century, and the courts grew out of the <i><a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">things</a></i> – the assemblies of the people. The people decided the cases (usually with largest freeholders dominating). This system later gradually developed into a system with a royal judge nominating a number of the most esteemed men of the parish as his board, fulfilling the function of "the people" of yore.
</p><p>From the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Hellenic</a> system onwards, the policy rationale for requiring the payment of monetary compensation for wrongs committed has involved the avoidance of <a href="/wiki/Feud" title="Feud">feuding</a> between <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">families</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> If compensation could mollify families' feelings, this would help to keep the peace. On the other hand, the institution of oaths also played down the threat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_warfare" title="Endemic warfare">feudal warfare</a>. Both in archaic Greece and in <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, an accused person walked free if he could get a sufficient number of male relatives to swear him not guilty. (Compare the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a>, in which the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_veto_power" title="United Nations Security Council veto power">veto power</a> of the permanent members ensures that the organization does not become involved in crises where it could not enforce its decisions.)
</p><p>These means of restraining private <a href="/wiki/Feud" title="Feud">feuds</a> did not always work, and sometimes prevented the fulfillment of justice. But in the earliest times the "state" did not always provide an independent policing force. Thus criminal law grew out of what 21st-century lawyers would call torts; and, in real terms, many acts and omissions classified as crimes actually overlap with civil-law concepts.
</p><p>The development of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> thought from the 19th century onwards prompted some fresh views on crime and criminality, and fostered the beginnings of <a href="/wiki/Criminology" title="Criminology">criminology</a> as a study of crime in society. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Nietzsche</a> noted a link between crime and <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity</a> – in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_Tragedy" title="The Birth of Tragedy">The Birth of Tragedy</a></i> he asserted:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:PCR" title="Wikipedia:PCR"><span title="The material near this tag may lack sufficient context (November 2016)">needs context</span></a></i>]</sup> "The best and brightest that man can acquire he must obtain by crime". In the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Discipline_and_Punish" title="Discipline and Punish">Discipline and Punish</a></i> made a study of <a href="/wiki/Criminalization" title="Criminalization">criminalization</a> as a coercive method of state control.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>

</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_penalty">By penalty</span></h3>
<p>One can categorise crimes depending on the related punishment, with <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentencing</a> <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> prescribed in line with the perceived seriousness of the offence. Thus <a href="/wiki/Fine_(penalty)" title="Fine (penalty)">fines</a> and noncustodial sentences may address the crimes seen as least serious, with lengthy imprisonment or (in some jurisdictions) <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> reserved for the most serious.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_law">Common law</span></h3>
<p>Under the common law of England, crimes were classified as either <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felony</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misdemeanour" title="Misdemeanour">misdemeanour</a>, with treason sometimes being included with the felonies. This system was based on the perceived seriousness of the offence. It is still used in the United States but the distinction between felony and misdemeanour is abolished in England, Wales and Northern Ireland.
</p>

</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indictable-only_offence" title="Indictable-only offence">Indictable-only offence</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Indictable_offence" title="Indictable offence">Indictable offence</a></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_origin">By origin</span></h3>
<p>In common law countries, crimes may be categorised into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_law_offences" title="Common law offences">common law offences</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statutory" title="Statutory">statutory</a> offences. In the US, Australia and Canada (in particular), they are divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_crime" title="Federal crime">federal crimes</a> and under state crimes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1650" data-file-width="1275" decoding="async" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf/page1-220px-Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf/page1-330px-Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf/page1-440px-Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Felony_Sentences_in_State_Courts.pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Felony Sentences in State Courts</i>, study by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In the United States since 1930, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a> has tabulated <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Crime_Reports" title="Uniform Crime Reports">Uniform Crime Reports</a> (UCR) annually from crime data submitted by <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_agency" title="Law enforcement agency">law enforcement</a> agencies across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
Officials compile this data at the city, county, and <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a> levels into the UCR. They classify violations of laws based on <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> as Part I (index) crimes in UCR data. These are further categorized as violent or property crimes. Part I violent crimes include murder and criminal homicide (voluntary manslaughter), forcible rape, aggravated assault, and robbery; while Part I property crimes include burglary, arson, larceny/theft, and motor-vehicle theft. All other crimes count come under Part II.
</p><p>For convenience, such lists usually include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summary_offense" title="Summary offense">infractions</a> although, in the U.S., they may come into the sphere not of the criminal law, but rather of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_law_(private_law)" title="Civil law (private law)">civil law</a>. Compare <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tortfeasance</a>.
</p><p>Booking <a href="/wiki/Arrest" title="Arrest">arrests</a> require detention for a time-frame ranging 1 to 24 hours.
</p>
<h2><span id="Reports.2C_studies_and_organizations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Reports,_studies_and_organizations">Reports, studies and organizations</span></h2>
<p>There are several national and International organizations offering studies and statistics about global and local crime activity, such as <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Office_on_Drugs_and_Crime" title="United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime">United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime</a>, the United States of America Overseas Security Advisory Council (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OSAC" title="OSAC">OSAC</a>) safety report or national reports generated by the law-enforcement authorities of EU state member reported to the <a href="/wiki/Europol" title="Europol">Europol</a>.
</p>

<p>In England and Wales, as well as in Hong Kong, the term "offence" means the same thing as "<a href="#England_and_Wales">crime</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law,_Eleventh_Edition_1982,_page_3_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Law,_Eleventh_Edition_1982,_page_3-11">[11]</a></sup> They are further split into:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Summary_offence" title="Summary offence">Summary offences</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Indictable_offence" title="Indictable offence">Indictable offences</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_and_correlates">Causes and correlates</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Causes_and_correlates_of_crime" title="Causes and correlates of crime">Causes and correlates of crime</a></div>
<p>Many different <a href="/wiki/Correlates_of_crime" title="Correlates of crime">causes and correlates of crime</a> have been proposed with varying degree of empirical support. They include socioeconomic, psychological, biological, and behavioral factors. Controversial topics include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_violence_research" title="Media violence research">media violence research</a> and effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun_politics" title="Gun politics">gun politics</a>.
</p><p>Emotional state (both chronic and current) have a tremendous impact on individual thought processes and, as a result, can be linked to criminal activities. The positive psychology concept of Broaden and Build posits that cognitive functioning expands when an individual is in a good-feeling emotional state and contracts as emotional state declines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In positive emotional states an individual is able to consider more possible solutions to problems, but in lower emotional states fewer solutions can be ascertained. The narrowed thought-action repertoires can result in the only paths perceptible to an individual being ones they would never use if they saw an alternative, but if they can't conceive of the alternatives that carry less risk they will choose one that they can see. Criminals who commit even the most horrendous of crimes, such as mass murders, did not see another solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religious_law" title="Religious law">Religious law</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Crimes_in_religion" title="Category:Crimes in religion">Category:Crimes in religion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg"><img alt="Two peasant women are assaulting a Jewish man with pitchfork and broom. A man wearing spectacles, tails, and a six-button waistcoat, perhaps a pharmacist or a schoolteacher, holds another Jewish man by the throat and is about to hit him with a stick, while a woman in a window above him throws the wet and solid contents of a basin at him, possibly the contents of a chamberpot. More chaos can be seen in the background, including a man with a raised sword and riding a horse towards the foreground." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1824" data-file-width="2310" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg/220px-Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg/330px-Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg/440px-Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hep_Hep_Krawalle_1819.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious" title="Religious">Religious</a> sentiment often becomes a contributory factor of crime. In the 1819 anti-Jewish <a href="/wiki/Hep-Hep_riots" title="Hep-Hep riots">Hep-Hep riots</a> in <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, rioters attacked Jewish businesses and destroyed property.</div></div></div>
<p>Different religious traditions may promote distinct norms of behaviour, and these in turn may clash or harmonise with the perceived interests of a state. Socially accepted or imposed religious morality has influenced secular jurisdictions on issues that may otherwise concern only an individual's conscience. Activities sometimes criminalized on religious grounds include (for example) <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a> consumption (<a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">prohibition</a>), <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem-cell</a> research. In various historical and present-day societies, institutionalized religions have established systems of earthly justice that punish crimes against the divine will and against specific devotional, organizational and other rules under specific codes, such as Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic Shariah Law</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_jurisdictions_and_states_of_emergency">Military jurisdictions and states of emergency</span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> sphere, authorities can prosecute both regular crimes and specific acts (such as <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutiny</a> or <a href="/wiki/Desertion" title="Desertion">desertion</a>) under <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial-law</a> codes that either supplant or extend civil codes in times of (for example) war.
</p><p>Many constitutions contain provisions to curtail freedoms and criminalize otherwise tolerated behaviors under a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> in case of war, natural disaster or civil unrest. Undesired activities at such times may include <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_assembly" title="Freedom of assembly">assembly</a> in the streets, violation of <a href="/wiki/Curfew" title="Curfew">curfew</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun_politics" title="Gun politics">possession of firearms</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational">Occupational</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Occupational_crime" title="Occupational crime">Occupational crime</a></div>
<p>Two common types of <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employee</a> crime exist: <a href="/wiki/Embezzlement" title="Embezzlement">embezzlement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wage_theft" title="Wage theft">wage theft</a>.
</p><p>The complexity and anonymity of computer systems may help criminal employees camouflage their operations. The victims of the most costly <a href="/wiki/Confidence_trick" title="Confidence trick">scams</a> include banks, brokerage houses, insurance companies, and other large financial institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computing1_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Computing1-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, it is estimated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2021)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that $40 billion to $60 billion are lost annually due to all forms of wage theft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groote_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Groote-58">[58]</a></sup> This compares to national annual losses of $340 million due to <a href="/wiki/Robbery" title="Robbery">robbery</a>, $4.1 billion due to <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">burglary</a>, $5.3 billion due to <a href="/wiki/Larceny" title="Larceny">larceny</a>, and $3.8 billion due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto_theft" title="Auto theft">auto theft</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation-59">[59]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, as in the United States, wage theft was found to be widespread and severe. In a 2014 survey it was found that as many as one-third of low wage male foreign workers in Singapore, or about 130,000, were affected by wage theft from partial to full denial of pay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
