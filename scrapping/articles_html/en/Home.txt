<p>A <b>home</b>, or <b>domicile</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> used as a permanent or semi-permanent residence for one or many <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>. It is a fully or semi sheltered space and can have both interior and exterior aspects to it. Homes provide sheltered spaces, for instance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rooms" title="Rooms">rooms</a>, where domestic activity can be performed such as sleeping, preparing food, eating and hygiene as well as providing spaces for work and leisure such as remote working, studying and playing.
</p><p>Physical forms of homes can be static such as a <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>, mobile such as a <a href="/wiki/Houseboat" title="Houseboat">houseboat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trailer_(vehicle)" title="Trailer (vehicle)">trailer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Yurt" title="Yurt">yurt</a> or digital such as virtual space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The aspect of ‘home’ can be considered across scales; from the micro scale showcasing the most intimate spaces of the individual dwelling and direct surrounding area to the macro scale of the geographic area such as <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a>, <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> or <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>.
</p><p>The concept of ‘home’ has been researched and theorized across disciplines – topics ranging from the idea of home, the interior, the psyche, liminal space, contested space to gender and politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The home as a concept expands beyond residence as contemporary lifestyles and technological advances redefine the way the global population lives and works.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The concept and experience encompasses the likes of <a href="/wiki/Exile" title="Exile">exile</a>, yearning, <a href="/wiki/Belongingness" title="Belongingness">belonging</a>, homesickness and homelessness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_era">Prehistoric era</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1016" data-file-width="1013" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/220px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/330px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/440px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Taíno people">Taíno</a> <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a> in a cave in Puerto Rico</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest homes that humans inhabited were likely naturally occurring features such as <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>. The earliest human <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> found in caves come from a series of caves near Krugersdorp and Mokopane in South Africa. The cave sites of <a href="/wiki/Sterkfontein" title="Sterkfontein">Sterkfontein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swartkrans" title="Swartkrans">Swartkrans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kromdraai" title="Kromdraai">Kromdraai</a> B, <a href="/wiki/Drimolen" title="Drimolen">Drimolen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malapa" title="Malapa">Malapa</a>, Cooper's D, Gladysvale, Gondolin and Makapansgat have yielded a range of early human species dating back to between three and one million years ago, including <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus_africanus" title="Australopithecus africanus">Australopithecus africanus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus_sediba" title="Australopithecus sediba">Australopithecus sediba</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Paranthropus_robustus" title="Paranthropus robustus">Paranthropus robustus</a></i>. However, it is not generally thought that these early humans were living in the caves, but that they were brought into the caves by carnivores that had killed them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The first early hominid ever found in Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Taung_Child" title="Taung Child">Taung Child</a> in 1924, was also thought for many years to come from a cave, where it had been deposited after being preyed upon by an eagle. However, this is now debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Caves do form in the dolomite of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ghaap_Plateau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ghaap Plateau (page does not exist)">Ghaap Plateau</a>, including the Early, Middle and Later Stone Age site of <a href="/wiki/Wonderwerk_Cave" title="Wonderwerk Cave">Wonderwerk Cave</a>; however, the caves that form along the escarpment's edge, like that hypothesized for the Taung Child, are formed within a secondary limestone deposit called <a href="/wiki/Tufa" title="Tufa">tufa</a>. There is numerous evidence for other early human species inhabiting caves from at least one million years ago in different parts of the world, including <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> in China at <a href="/wiki/Zhoukoudian" title="Zhoukoudian">Zhoukoudian</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_rhodesiensis" title="Homo rhodesiensis">Homo rhodesiensis</a></i> in South Africa at the Cave of Hearths (<a href="/wiki/Makapansgat" title="Makapansgat">Makapansgat</a>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_neanderthalensis" title="Homo neanderthalensis">Homo neanderthalensis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis" title="Homo heidelbergensis">Homo heidelbergensis</a></i> in Europe at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological_Site_of_Atapuerca" title="Archaeological Site of Atapuerca">Archaeological Site of Atapuerca</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_floresiensis" title="Homo floresiensis">Homo floresiensis</a></i> in Indonesia, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denisovans" title="Denisovans">Denisovans</a> in southern Siberia.
</p><p>In southern Africa, early modern humans regularly used sea caves as shelter starting about 180,000 years ago when they learned to exploit the sea for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The oldest known site is PP13B at <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Point" title="Pinnacle Point">Pinnacle Point</a>. This may have allowed rapid expansion of humans out of Africa and colonization of areas of the world such as Australia by 60–50,000 years ago. Throughout southern Africa, Australia, and Europe, early modern humans used caves and rock shelters as sites for rock art, such as those at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giants_Castle" title="Giants Castle">Giants Castle</a>. Caves such as the <a href="/wiki/Yaodong" title="Yaodong">yaodong</a> in China were used for shelter; other caves were used for burials (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock-cut_tombs" title="Rock-cut tombs">rock-cut tombs</a>), or as religious sites (such as <a href="/wiki/Category:Buddhist_caves" title="Category:Buddhist caves">Buddhist caves</a>). Among the known sacred caves are China's Cave of a Thousand Buddhas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_caves_of_Crete" title="Sacred caves of Crete">sacred caves of Crete</a>. As technology progressed, humans and other hominids began constructing their own dwellings. Buildings such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hut_(dwelling)" title="Hut (dwelling)">huts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">longhouses</a> have been used for living since the late <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skara_Brae_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skara_Brae-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_era">Ancient era</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Empty_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" decoding="async" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span"><b>This section is empty.</b>  You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Home&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2021</span>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-classical_era">Post-classical era</span></h3>
<p>From the 14th to the 16th century, homelessness was perceived of as a "vagrancy problem" and legislative responses to the problem were predicated upon the threat it may pose to the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1301" data-file-width="1734" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg/220px-Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg/330px-Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg/440px-Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Industrialization brought mass migration to cities. This one-room worker home from <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a> is typical to late 19th century and early 20th century, often housing large families.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div></div></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Kirsten_Gram-Hanssen" title="Kirsten Gram-Hanssen">Kirsten Gram-Hanssen</a>, "It can be argued that historically and cross-culturally there is not always [a] strong relation between the concept of home and the physical building, and that this mode of thinking is rooted in <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">the Enlightenment</a> of the seventeenth century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> Before, one's home was more public than private; traits such as privacy, intimacy and familiarity would proceed to achieve greater prominence, aligning the concept with the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The connection between home and house was reinforced by a <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a> declaration from <a href="/wiki/Edward_Coke" title="Edward Coke">Edward Coke</a>: "The house of everyman is to him as his castle and fortresse, as well as his defense against injury and violence, as for his repose". Colloquially, this was adapted into the phrase "The Englishman's home is his castle" which popularised the notion of home as house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>A result of the longstanding association between home and women, 18th century English women, of upper-class status, were scorned for pursing activites outside of the home, thus seen to be of undesirable character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:10-13">[13]</a></sup> The concept of home took on unprecedent prominence by the 18th century, reified by cultural practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:11_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:11-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of a <a href="/wiki/Home_automation" title="Home automation">smart home</a> arose in the 19th century in turn with electricity having been introduced to homes in a limited capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> The distinction between home and work formulated in the 20th century, with home acting as sanctuary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Modern definitions portray home as a site of supreme comfort and familial intimacy, operating as a buffer to the greater world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:10-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_types">Common types</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_house_types" title="List of house types">List of house types</a></div><p>The concept of home is one with multiple interpretations, influenced by one's history and identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> People of differing ages, genders, ethnicities and classes may have resultingly different meanings of home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:9-17">[17]</a></sup> Commonly, it is associated with various forms of abodes such as wagons, cars, boats or tents although it is equally considered to extend beyond the space, in mind and emotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-19">[19]</a></sup> The space of a home need not be significant or fixed though the boundaries of home are often tied to the space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-19">[19]</a></sup> There have been multiple theories regarding one's choice of home with the residential conditions of their childhood often reflected in their later choice of home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Paul_Oliver" title="Paul Oliver">Paul Oliver</a>, the vast majority of abodes are <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">vernacular</a>, constructed in accordance with the residents' needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoolenEditorial_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoolenEditorial-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House">House</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">House</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:8A,_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_,_Bucharest_(Romania).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2786" data-file-width="3715" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg/220px-8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg/330px-8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg/440px-8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:8A,_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_,_Bucharest_(Romania).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>House at 8A, Bulevardul Aviatorilor, Bucharest, Romania</div></div></div>
<p>A house is a single-unit residential <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a>. It may range in complexity from a rudimentary <a href="/wiki/Hut" title="Hut">hut</a> to a complex structure of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> or other material, outfitted with <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">plumbing</a>, electrical, and <a href="/wiki/Heating,_ventilation,_and_air_conditioning" title="Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning">heating, ventilation, and air conditioning</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schoenauer_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-schoenauer-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The social unit that lives in a house is known as a <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a>. Most commonly, a household is a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> unit of some kind, although households may also be other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_(sociology)" title="Group (sociology)">social groups</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Roommate" title="Roommate">roommates</a> or, in a <a href="/wiki/Rooming_house" title="Rooming house">rooming house</a>, unconnected individuals. Some houses only have a dwelling space for one family or similar-sized group; larger houses called <a href="/wiki/Townhouse" title="Townhouse">townhouses</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Row_house" title="Row house">row houses</a> may contain numerous family dwellings in the same structure. A house may be accompanied by outbuildings, such as a <a href="/wiki/Garage_(residential)" title="Garage (residential)">garage</a> for vehicles or a <a href="/wiki/Shed" title="Shed">shed</a> for gardening equipment and tools. A house may have a <a href="/wiki/Backyard" title="Backyard">backyard</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Front_yard" title="Front yard">front yard</a> or both, which serve as additional areas where inhabitants can relax or eat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Houses may provide "certain activities, which gradually accumulate meaning until they become homes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-:8-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rykwert" title="Joseph Rykwert">Joseph Rykwert</a> distinguished between home and house in their physicality; a house requires a building whereas a home does not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <i>Home</i> and <i>house</i> are often used interchangeably, although their connotations may differ: <i>house</i> being "emotionally netural" and <i>home</i> evoking "personal, cognitive aspects".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-:8-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-24">[24]</a></sup> By the mid-18th century, the definition of home had extended beyond a house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:11_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:11-14">[14]</a></sup> "Few English words are filled with the emotional meaning of the word home".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-:10-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moveable_structures">Moveable structures</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1581" data-file-width="2194" decoding="async" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg/220px-LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg/330px-LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg/440px-LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A houseboat on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Union" title="Lake Union">Lake Union</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, US</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kazakh_yurt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3752" data-file-width="5624" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kazakh_yurt.jpg/220px-Kazakh_yurt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kazakh_yurt.jpg/330px-Kazakh_yurt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kazakh_yurt.jpg/440px-Kazakh_yurt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kazakh_yurt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A traditional Kazakh yurt on a wagon</div></div></div>
<p>Home as constitutionally mobile and transient has been contended by anthropologists and sociologist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile home</a> (also known as a house trailer, park home, trailer, or trailer home) is a <a href="/wiki/Prefabrication" title="Prefabrication">prefabricated</a> structure, built in a factory on a permanently attached chassis before being transported to site (either by being towed or on a trailer). Used as permanent homes, or for holiday or temporary accommodation, they are often left permanently or semi-permanently in one place, but can be moved, and may be required to move from time to time for legal reasons.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Houseboat" title="Houseboat">houseboat</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a> that has been designed or modified to be used primarily as a home. Some houseboats are not motorized, because they are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moored" title="Moored">moored</a>, kept stationary at a fixed point and often tethered to land to provide utilities. However, many are capable of operation under their own power. Float house is a Canadian and American term for a house on a float (raft); a rough house may be called a shanty boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In Western countries, houseboats tend to be either owned privately or rented out to holiday-goers, and on some canals in Europe, people dwell in houseboats all year round. Examples of this include, but are not limited to, Amsterdam, London, and Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>A traditional <a href="/wiki/Yurt" title="Yurt">yurt</a> or ger is a portable round tent covered with skins or <a href="/wiki/Felt" title="Felt">felt</a> and used as a dwelling by several distinct <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomadic groups</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe" title="Eurasian Steppe">steppes of Central Asia</a>. The structure consists of an angled assembly or latticework of wood or <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> for walls, a door frame, ribs (poles, rafters), and a wheel (crown, compression ring) possibly steam-bent. The roof structure is often self-supporting, but large yurts may have interior posts supporting the crown. The top of the wall of self-supporting yurts is prevented from spreading by means of a tension band which opposes the force of the roof ribs. Modern yurts may be permanently built on a wooden platform; they may use modern materials such as steam-bent wooden framing or metal framing, canvas or tarpaulin, plexiglass dome, wire rope, or <a href="/wiki/Radiant_barrier" title="Radiant barrier">radiant insulation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing_cooperative">Housing cooperative</span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1066933788">.mw-parser-output .excerpt-hat .mw-editsection-like{font-style:normal}</style><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Housing_cooperative" title="Housing cooperative">Housing cooperative</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housing_cooperative&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="533" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg/325px-999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg/488px-999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg/650px-999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg 2x" width="325"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>999 N. Lake Shore Drive, a co-op–owned residential building in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Housing_cooperative" title="Housing cooperative">housing cooperative</a>, or housing co-op, is a legal entity, usually a <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a>, which owns <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a>, consisting of one or more residential buildings; it is one type of <a href="/wiki/Housing_tenure" title="Housing tenure">housing tenure</a>. Housing cooperatives are a distinctive form of home ownership that have many characteristics that differ from other residential arrangements such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_family_home" title="Single family home">single family home</a> ownership, <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominiums</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">renting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housing_cooperative_nationalcooperativelawcenter.com_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housing_cooperative_nationalcooperativelawcenter.com-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
The corporation is membership based, with membership granted by way of a share purchase in the cooperative. Each <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholder</a> in the legal entity is granted the right to occupy one housing unit. A primary advantage of the housing cooperative is the pooling of the members' resources so that their buying power is leveraged; thus lowering the cost per member in all the services and products associated with home ownership.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repair">Repair</span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Home_repair" title="Home repair">Home repair</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Home_repair&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="1996" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg/220px-FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg/330px-FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg/440px-FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A man making repairs to a house after a flood</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Home_repair" title="Home repair">Home repair</a> involves the diagnosis and resolution of problems in a home, and is related to home maintenance to avoid such problems. Many types of repairs are "<a href="/wiki/Do_it_yourself" title="Do it yourself">do it yourself</a>" (DIY) projects, while others may be so complicated, time-consuming or risky as to require the assistance of a qualified <a href="/wiki/Handyman" title="Handyman">handyperson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Property_manager" title="Property manager">property manager</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">contractor</a>/builder, or other <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professionals</a>.
</p>
Home repair is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Renovation" title="Renovation">renovation</a>, although many improvements can result from repairs or maintenance. Often the costs of larger repairs will justify the alternative of investment in full-scale improvements. It may make just as much sense to upgrade a home system (with an improved one) as to repair it or incur ever-more-frequent and expensive maintenance for an inefficient, obsolete or dying system.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Housekeeping">Housekeeping</span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Housekeeping" title="Housekeeping">Housekeeping</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housekeeping&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<p><a href="/wiki/Housekeeping" title="Housekeeping">Housekeeping</a> refers to the management of duties and chores involved in the running of a <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a>, such as cleaning, <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>, home maintenance, shopping, and bill payment. These tasks may be performed by members of the household, or by other persons hired for the purpose. This is a more broad role than a cleaner, who is focused only on the cleaning aspect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housekeeping_House_Keeper_vs_Cleaner_differentiation_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housekeeping_House_Keeper_vs_Cleaner_differentiation-29">[29]</a></sup> The term is also used to refer to the money allocated for such use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housekeeping_oxford_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housekeeping_oxford-30">[30]</a></sup> By extension, it may also refer to an office or organization, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Housekeeping_(computing)" title="Housekeeping (computing)">maintenance</a> of <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">computer storage</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
A <a href="/wiki/Housekeeper_(domestic_worker)" title="Housekeeper (domestic worker)">housekeeper</a> is a person employed to manage a household<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_staff" title="Domestic staff">domestic staff</a>. According to the 1861 Victorian era <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs._Beeton%27s_Book_of_Household_Management" title="Mrs. Beeton's Book of Household Management">Mrs. Beeton's Book of Household Management</a></i>, the housekeeper is second in command in the house and "except in large establishments, where there is a house steward, the housekeeper must consider his/herself as the immediate representative of her mistress".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tenure">Tenure</span></h2>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Housing_tenure" title="Housing tenure">Housing tenure</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housing_tenure&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<p><a href="/wiki/Housing_tenure" title="Housing tenure">Housing tenure</a> refers to the financial arrangements under which someone has the right to live in a <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>. The most frequent forms are <a href="/wiki/Leasehold_estate" title="Leasehold estate">tenancy</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">rent</a> is paid by the occupant to a <a href="/wiki/Landlord" title="Landlord">landlord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Owner-occupancy" title="Owner-occupancy">owner-occupancy</a>, where the occupant owns their own home. Mixed forms of tenure are also possible.
</p><p>The basic forms of tenure can be subdivided, for example an owner-occupier may own a house outright, or it may be <a href="/wiki/Mortgage_law" title="Mortgage law">mortgaged</a>.  In the case of tenancy, the landlord may be a private individual, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organization</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Housing_association" title="Housing association">housing association</a>, or a government body, as in <a href="/wiki/Public_housing" title="Public housing">public housing</a>.
</p>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_survey" title="Statistical survey">Surveys</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> research frequently include questions about housing tenure, because it is a useful <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(statistics)" title="Proxy (statistics)">proxy</a> for income or wealth, and people are less reluctant to give information about it.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Owner-occupancy">Owner-occupancy</span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Owner-occupancy" title="Owner-occupancy">Owner-occupancy</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Owner-occupancy&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<a href="/wiki/Owner-occupancy" title="Owner-occupancy">Owner-occupancy</a> or home-ownership is a form of <a href="/wiki/Housing_tenure" title="Housing tenure">housing tenure</a> in which a person, called the owner-occupier, owner-occupant, or home owner, owns the home in which they live. The home can be a <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a>, such as  a <a href="/wiki/Single-family_detached_home" title="Single-family detached home">single-family house</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominium</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Housing_cooperative" title="Housing cooperative">housing cooperative</a>. In addition to providing <a href="/wiki/Housing" title="Housing">housing</a>, owner-occupancy also functions as a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investing" title="Real estate investing">real estate investment</a>.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rental_accommodation">Rental accommodation</span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">Renting</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Renting&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MEZ_1911_10_01_20_object_594901_(2).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="164" data-file-width="232" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/MEZ_1911_10_01_20_object_594901_%282%29.png/220px-MEZ_1911_10_01_20_object_594901_%282%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f4/MEZ_1911_10_01_20_object_594901_%282%29.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MEZ_1911_10_01_20_object_594901_(2).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Notice of renting availability at the Villa Freischütz in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meran" title="Meran">Meran</a> in 1911</div></div></div>
<a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">Renting</a>, also known as hiring or letting, is an agreement where a payment is made for the temporary use of a good, service or <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a> owned by another. A <a href="/wiki/Gross_lease" title="Gross lease">gross lease</a> is when the <a href="/wiki/Tenement_(law)" title="Tenement (law)">tenant</a> pays a flat rental amount and the <a href="/wiki/Landlord" title="Landlord">landlord</a> pays for all property charges regularly incurred by the ownership. An example of renting is <a href="/wiki/Equipment_rental" title="Equipment rental">equipment rental</a>. Renting can be an example of the <a href="/wiki/Sharing_economy" title="Sharing economy">sharing economy</a>.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Squatting">Squatting</span></h3>
<div class="excerpt-block"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066933788" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Squatting" title="Squatting">Squatting</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Squatting&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:No_Forvced_Removals.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/No_Forvced_Removals.jpg/220px-No_Forvced_Removals.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/No_Forvced_Removals.jpg/330px-No_Forvced_Removals.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/No_Forvced_Removals.jpg/440px-No_Forvced_Removals.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:No_Forvced_Removals.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Abahlali_baseMjondolo" title="Abahlali baseMjondolo">Abahlali baseMjondolo</a> protest in <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Squatting" title="Squatting">Squatting</a> is the action of occupying an abandoned or unoccupied area of land or a building, usually residential, that the squatter does not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_ownership_and_tenure" title="Land ownership and tenure">own, rent or otherwise have lawful permission to use</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> estimated in 2003 that there were one billion slum residents and squatters globally. Squatting occurs worldwide and tends to occur when people who are poor and homeless find empty buildings or land to occupy for housing. It has a long history, broken down by country below.
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Least_developed_countries" title="Least developed countries">least developed countries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shanty_towns" title="Shanty towns">shanty towns</a> often begin as squatted settlements. In African cities such as <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a> much of the population lives in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slums" title="Slums">slums</a>. There are <a href="/wiki/Pavement_dwellers" title="Pavement dwellers">pavement dwellers</a> in India and in Hong Kong as well as <a href="/wiki/Rooftop_slum" title="Rooftop slum">rooftop slums</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informal_settlements" title="Informal settlements">Informal settlements</a> in Latin America are known by names such as <a href="/wiki/Villa_miseria" title="Villa miseria">villa miseria</a> (Argentina), <a href="/wiki/Pueblos_j%C3%B3venes" title="Pueblos jóvenes">pueblos jóvenes</a> (Peru) and <a href="/wiki/Asentamiento" title="Asentamiento">asentamientos</a> irregulares (Guatemala, Uruguay). In Brazil, there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Favelas" title="Favelas">favelas</a> in the major cities and land-based movements.
</p>
In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialized_countries" title="Industrialized countries">industrialized countries</a>, there are often residential squats and also political squatting movements, which can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarchist" title="Anarchist">anarchist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomist" title="Autonomist">autonomist</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> in nature, for example in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-managed_social_centers_in_Italy" title="Self-managed social centers in Italy">self-managed social centres of Italy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Squatting_in_the_United_States" title="Squatting in the United States">squats in the United States</a>. Oppositional movements from the 1960s and 1970s created freespaces in Denmark or squatting village in the <a href="/wiki/Squatting_in_the_Netherlands" title="Squatting in the Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Squatting_in_England_and_Wales" title="Squatting in England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, there were estimated to be 50,000 squatters in the late 1970s. Each local situation determines the context: in Athens, Greece, there are <a href="/wiki/Athens_refugee_squats" title="Athens refugee squats">refugee squats</a>; Germany has social centres; in <a href="/wiki/Squatting_in_Spain" title="Squatting in Spain">Spain</a> there are many squats.</div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homelessness">Homelessness</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Homelessness" title="Homelessness">Homelessness</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/220px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/330px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/440px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Homeless people in <a href="/wiki/San%27ya" title="San'ya">San'ya</a> district, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, Japan</div></div></div>
<p>The state of being without a home can occur in may ways,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-34">[34]</a></sup> ranging from the upheavals of <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> fraud, theft, <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">arson</a>, or war-related destruction, to the more common voluntary sale, loss for one or more occupants on relationship breakdown, <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">expropriation</a> by government or legislated cause, repossession or <a href="/wiki/Foreclosure" title="Foreclosure">foreclosure</a> to pay secured debts, <a href="/wiki/Eviction" title="Eviction">eviction</a> by landlords, disposal by time-limited means – <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">lease</a>, or absolute gift. Jurisdiction-dependent means of home loss include <a href="/wiki/Adverse_possession" title="Adverse possession">adverse possession</a>, unpaid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_taxation" title="Property taxation">property taxation</a> and corruption such as in circumstances of a <a href="/wiki/Failed_state" title="Failed state">failed state</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_insolvency" title="Personal insolvency">Personal insolvency</a>, development or sustaining of <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">mental illness</a> or severe physical incapacity without affordable <a href="/wiki/Care_work" title="Care work">domestic care</a> commonly lead to a change of home. The underlying character of a home may be debased by <a href="/wiki/Structural_integrity_and_failure" title="Structural integrity and failure">structural defects</a>, natural <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Property_maintenance" title="Property maintenance">neglect</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>. <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">Refugees</a> are people who have fled their homes due to violence or <a href="/wiki/Persecution" title="Persecution">persecution</a>. They may seek temporary housing in a shelter or they may claim <a href="/wiki/Right_of_asylum" title="Right of asylum">asylum</a> in another country in an attempt to relocate permanently.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A dysfunctional home life commonly precipitates one's homelessness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The dichotomy between home and homelessness is to the extent that the concept of home, scholars have said, is dependent on homelessness: "in a sense, without homelessness, we would not be concerned with what home means".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_significance">Anthropogenic significance</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3372" data-file-width="2699" decoding="async" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg/220px-Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg/330px-Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg/440px-Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A celebratory poster for soldiers and marines returning home</div></div></div>
<p>The connection between humans and dwelling is profound, such that, the likes of <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Bachelard" title="Gaston Bachelard">Gaston Bachelard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Martin Heidegger</a> consider it an "essential characteristic" of humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-24">[24]</a></sup> A home is generally a place that is close to the heart of the owner, and can become a prized possession. It has been argued that psychologically "The strongest sense of home commonly coincides geographically with a dwelling. Usually, the sense of home attenuates as one moves away from that point, but it does not do so in a fixed or regular way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A person's conception of home can be dependent on congealing conditions, such as culture, geography or emotion; the sense of being at home may be contingent upon the presence of multiple emotions, such as joy, sorrow, nostalgia and pride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Further psychological interperation contends that homes serve the purpose of satisfying identity-based desires and expression and that it functions as a "symbol of the self", bound to the events of one's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:9-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Levinas" title="Emmanuel Levinas">Emmanuel Levinas</a> wrote of home as where, upon seclusion from the greater world, a sense of self can be regained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>There exist many connotations regarding the concept of a home, including of security, identity, ritual and socialisation, varied definitions and residents may associate their home with meanings, emotions, experiences and relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Home has been described as an "<a href="/wiki/Essentially_contested_concept" title="Essentially contested concept">essentially contested concept</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Common connotations of home are espoused by both those with or without a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup> It is the sociality and action of homes which some scholars have said conditions a house in to a home, which is, according to Gram-Hanssen, "a <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> made by its residents".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Dysfunctional sociality may negate the sense of a residence being a home whereas the physical contents may endow the sense; alienated from home one may feel "metaphorically homeless".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[a]</a></sup> Romantic or nostalgic notions are typical in the conceptions of "ideal homes", at once a cultural and individual concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> An ideal working-class home in <a href="/wiki/Postwar_Britain_(1945%E2%80%931979)" title="Postwar Britain (1945–1979)">Postwar Britain</a> was one of comfort and cleanliness, plentiful with food and compassion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>In modern America, an owned house has greater cachet as a home than other residences; debate exists as to if a rooming house can provide a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> Some housing scholars have contended that a conflation of house and home is the result of popular media and capitalist interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup> Differing cultures may perceive the concept of a home differently, ascribing less value to the privacy of a residence or the residence itself – although <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Housing_crisis" title="Housing crisis">housing issues</a> have been seen as of great concern to immigrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[b]</a></sup> The home can render to men and women in significant differences: men conditioned to experience great control and little labour and vice versa for women; homelessness too can be subject to differences per gender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-:4-34">[34]</a></sup> Sociologist Shelley Mallett preposed the idea of home as abstractions: space, feeling, <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">praxis</a> or "a way of being in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> Abstract notions of home are present in the proverb "A house is not a home".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_34-5"><a href="#cite_note-:4-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since it can be said that humans are generally creatures of <a href="/wiki/Habituation" title="Habituation">habit</a>, the state of a person's home has been known to physiologically influence their <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotions</a>, and overall <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marianne_Gullestad" title="Marianne Gullestad">Marianne Gullestad</a> wrote of the home as the center of and as an attempt to amalgamate everyday life; one's conduct there, she said, can reflect greater culture or social values, such as <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> insinuating the home to be the domain of women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[c]</a></sup> To be <a href="/wiki/Homesickness" title="Homesickness">homesick</a> is to desire <a href="/wiki/Belongingness" title="Belongingness">belonging</a>, said <a href="/wiki/Zygmunt_Bauman" title="Zygmunt Bauman">Zygmunt Bauman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup> Places like homes can trigger self-reflection, thoughts about who someone is or used to be or who they might become.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[51]</a></sup> These types of reflections also occur in places where there is a collective historical identity, such as <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Gettysburg</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_Zero" title="Ground Zero">Ground Zero</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[52]</a></sup> The time spent with one's home is a considerable element in establishing one's attachment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> Those without significant time spent of their life in a residence often struggle to consider home as a feature of residences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup> The perception of one's home can extend beyond the residence itself, to their neighbourhood, family, workplace or nation and one may feel as though they have multiple homes; to have felt at home beyond residence can be a significant element in one's appraisal of their life, a time in which notions of home, it has been observed, are more profound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-52">[50]</a></sup> The connection between home and family is pertinent, to the extent that some scholars consider the terms to be synonymous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
