<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Tool_(disambiguation)" title="Tool (disambiguation)">Tool (disambiguation)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equipment" title="Equipment">Equipment</a>.</div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the object. For the American rock band, see <a href="/wiki/Tool_(band)" title="Tool (band)">Tool (band)</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Object can be used to achieve a goal</div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2178" data-file-width="2652" decoding="async" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg/250px-MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg/375px-MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg/500px-MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MaryRose-carpentry_tools1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carpentry tools recovered from the wreck of a 16th-century sailing ship, the <a href="/wiki/Mary_Rose" title="Mary Rose">Mary Rose</a>. From the top, a <a href="/wiki/Mallet" title="Mallet">mallet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brace_(tool)" title="Brace (tool)">brace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plane_(tool)" title="Plane (tool)">plane</a>, handle of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auger_(drill)" title="Auger (drill)">T-auger</a>, handle of a <a href="/wiki/Gimlet_(tool)" title="Gimlet (tool)">gimlet</a>, possible handle of a <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">rule</a>.</div></div></div><p>A <b>tool</b> is an object that can extend an individual's ability to modify features of the surrounding environment. Although many animals use <a href="/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals" title="Tool use by animals">simple tools</a>, only <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human beings</a>, whose use of <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a> dates back <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">hundreds of millennia</a>, have been observed using tools to make other tools. 
</p><p>Early tools, made of such materials as <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, were used for preparation of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a>, manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a>, and working of materials to produce <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">clothing</a> and useful artifacts. The development of <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a> made additional types of tools possible. Harnessing energy sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">animal power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>, allowed increasingly complex tools to produce an even larger range of items, with the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> marking an inflection point in the use of tools. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a> allowed tools to operate with minimal human supervision, further increasing the productivity of human labor.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While a common-sense understanding of the meaning of tool is widespread, several formal definitions have been proposed.
</p><p>
In 1981, Benjamin Beck published a widely used definition of tool use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This has been modified to:<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The external employment of an unattached or manipulable attached environmental object to alter more efficiently the form, position, or condition of another object, another organism, or the user itself, when the user holds and directly manipulates the tool during or prior to use and is responsible for the proper and effective orientation of the tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shumaker_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shumaker-2">[2]</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Other, briefer definitions have been proposed:<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>An object carried or maintained for future use.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Finn, Tregenza, and Norman, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The use of physical objects other than the animal's own body or appendages as a means to extend the physical influence realized by the animal.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Jones and Kamil, 1973<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_and_Kamil_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_and_Kamil-4">[4]</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>An object that has been modified to fit a purpose ... [or] An inanimate object that one uses or modifies in some way to cause a change in the environment, thereby facilitating one's achievement of a target goal.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Hauser, 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2468" data-file-width="3290" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg/250px-Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg/375px-Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg/500px-Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prehistoric_Tools_-_Les_Combarelles_-_Les_Eyzies_de_Tayac_-_MNP.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a> over 10,000 years old, found in <a href="/wiki/Les_Combarelles" title="Les Combarelles">Les Combarelles</a> cave, France</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:180px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UpholsteryRegulator.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="134" data-file-width="178" decoding="async" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a3/UpholsteryRegulator.jpg" width="178"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UpholsteryRegulator.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Upholstery_regulator" title="Upholstery regulator">upholstery regulator</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_technology" title="History of technology">History of technology</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">Anthropologists</a> believe that the use of tools was an important step in the <a href="/wiki/Human_evolution" title="Human evolution">evolution of mankind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lilley_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-lilley-6">[6]</a></sup> Because tools are used extensively by both humans and wild <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a>, it is widely assumed that the first routine use of tools took place prior to the divergence between the two species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These early tools, however, were likely made of perishable materials such as sticks, or consisted of unmodified stones that cannot be distinguished from other stones as tools.
</p><p>Stone artifacts date back to about 2.5 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encylopediahumanevolution_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-encylopediahumanevolution-8">[8]</a></sup> However, a 2010 study suggests the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominin" title="Hominin">hominin</a> species <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus_afarensis" title="Australopithecus afarensis">Australopithecus afarensis</a></i> ate meat by carving animal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcasses_of_animals" title="Carcasses of animals">carcasses</a> with stone implements. This finding pushes back the earliest known use of stone tools among hominins to about 3.4 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Finds of actual tools date back at least 2.6 million years in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> One of the earliest distinguishable stone tool forms is the <a href="/wiki/Hand_axe" title="Hand axe">hand axe</a>.
</p><p>Up until recently, weapons found in digs were the only tools of "early man" that were studied and given importance. Now, more tools are recognized as culturally and historically relevant. As well as hunting, other activities required tools such as preparing food, "...nutting, leatherworking, grain harvesting and woodworking..."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Inline_citation#When_you_must_use_inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Inline citation"><span title="The text near this tag needs a citation. (June 2022)">quote citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Included in this group are "flake stone tools".
</p><p>Tools are the most important items that the ancient humans used to climb to the top of the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>; by inventing tools, they were able to accomplish tasks that human bodies could not, such as using a <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spear</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_arrow" title="Bow and arrow">bow</a> to kill <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">prey</a>, since their teeth were not sharp enough to pierce many animals' skins. “Man the hunter” as the catalyst for Hominin change has been questioned. Based on marks on the bones at archaeological sites, it is now more evident that pre-humans were scavenging off of other predators' carcasses rather than killing their own food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline_of_ancient_tool_development">Timeline of ancient tool development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Timeline of ancient tool development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
Many tools were made in <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> or in the early centuries of recorded history, but archaeological evidence can provide dates of development and use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r998391716">.mw-parser-output .div-col{margin-top:0.3em;column-width:30em}.mw-parser-output .div-col-small{font-size:90%}.mw-parser-output .div-col-rules{column-rule:1px solid #aaa}.mw-parser-output .div-col dl,.mw-parser-output .div-col ol,.mw-parser-output .div-col ul{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .div-col li,.mw-parser-output .div-col dd{page-break-inside:avoid;break-inside:avoid-column}</style></p><div class="div-col" style="column-width: 27em;">
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Olduvai_Gorge" title="Olduvai Gorge">Olduvai</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_technology" title="Stone technology">stone technology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oldowan" title="Oldowan">Oldowan</a>) 2.5 million years ago (scrapers; to butcher dead animals)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hut" title="Hut">Huts</a>, 2 million years ago.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acheulean" title="Acheulean">Acheulean</a> stone technology 1.6 million years ago (hand axe)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">Fire</a> creation and manipulation, used since the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a>, possibly by <a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a> as early as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E13_s" title="1 E13 s">1.5 Million years ago</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">Boats</a>, 900,000 years ago.</li>

</div><p>Several of the six classic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_machines" title="Simple machines">simple machines</a> were invented in Mesopotamia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Mesopotamians have been credited with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheel</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Wheel_and_axle" title="Wheel and axle">wheel and axle</a> mechanism first appeared with the <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel" title="Potter's wheel">potter's wheel</a>, invented in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (modern Iraq) during the 5th millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This led to the invention of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wheeled_vehicle" title="Wheeled vehicle">wheeled vehicle</a> in Mesopotamia during the early 4th millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">lever</a> was used in the <a href="/wiki/Shadoof" title="Shadoof">shadoof</a> water-lifting device, the first <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">crane</a> machine, which appeared in Mesopotamia circa 3000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and then in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_technology" title="Ancient Egyptian technology">ancient Egyptian technology</a> circa 2000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The earliest evidence of <a href="/wiki/Pulley" title="Pulley">pulleys</a> date back to Mesopotamia in the early 2nd millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenbrauns_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenbrauns-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Screw_(simple_machine)" title="Screw (simple machine)">screw</a>, the last of the simple machines to be invented,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods-21">[21]</a></sup> first appeared in Mesopotamia during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian" title="Neo-Assyrian">Neo-Assyrian</a> period (911-609) BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenbrauns_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenbrauns-20">[20]</a></sup> The Assyrian King <a href="/wiki/Sennacherib" title="Sennacherib">Sennacherib</a> (704–681 BC) claims to have invented automatic sluices and to have been the first to use water <a href="/wiki/Screw_pump" title="Screw pump">screw pumps</a>, of up to 30 tons weight, which were cast using two-part clay molds rather than by the '<a href="/wiki/Lost-wax_casting" title="Lost-wax casting">lost wax</a>' process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalley2013_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalley2013-22">[22]</a></sup> The Jerwan Aqueduct (c. 688 BC) is made with stone arches and lined with waterproof concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The earliest evidence of <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermills</a> date back to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">ancient Near East</a> in the 4th century BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> specifically in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian Empire</a> before 350 BC, in the regions of Mesopotamia (Iraq) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a> (Iran).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This pioneering use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_power" title="Water power">water power</a> constituted the first human-devised motive force not to rely on muscle power (besides the <a href="/wiki/Sail" title="Sail">sail</a>).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">Mechanical</a> devices experienced a major expansion in their use in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a> with the systematic employment of new energy sources, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterwheel" title="Waterwheel">waterwheels</a>. Their use expanded through the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a> with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmills</a>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Machine_tools">Machine tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Machine tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Machine_tool" title="Machine tool">Machine tools</a> occasioned a surge in producing new tools in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Pre-industrial machinery was built by various craftsmen—<a href="/wiki/Millwright" title="Millwright">millwrights</a> built water and windmills, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenter" title="Carpenter">carpenters</a> made wooden framing, and smiths and turners made metal parts. Wooden components had the disadvantage of changing dimensions with temperature and humidity, and the various joints tended to rack (work loose) over time.  As the Industrial Revolution progressed, machines with metal parts and frames became more common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other important uses of metal parts were in firearms and threaded fasteners, such as machine screws, bolts, and nuts. There was also the need for precision in making parts.  Precision would allow better working machinery, interchangeability of parts, and standardization of threaded fasteners. The demand for metal parts led to the development of several <a href="/wiki/Machine_tool" title="Machine tool">machine tools</a>. They have their origins in the tools developed in the 18th century by makers of clocks and watches and scientific instrument makers to enable them to batch-produce small mechanisms. Before the advent of machine tools, metal was worked manually using the basic hand tools of hammers, files, scrapers, saws, and chisels. Consequently, the use of metal machine parts was kept to a minimum.  Hand methods of production were very laborious and costly and precision was difficult to achieve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hounshell-1984_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hounshell-1984-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roe1916_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roe1916-29">[29]</a></sup> With their inherent precision, machine tools enabled the economical production of <a href="/wiki/Interchangeable_parts" title="Interchangeable parts">interchangeable parts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Examples of machine tools include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>

</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One can classify tools according to their basic functions:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cutting" title="Cutting">Cutting</a> and edge tools, such as the <a href="/wiki/Knife" title="Knife">knife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">sickle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scythe" title="Scythe">scythe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hatchet" title="Hatchet">hatchet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Axe" title="Axe">axe</a>, are wedge-shaped implements that produce a <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shearing force</a> along a narrow face. Ideally, the edge of the tool needs to be <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">harder</a> than the material being cut or the blade will become dulled with repeated use. But even resilient tools will require periodic <a href="/wiki/Sharpening" title="Sharpening">sharpening</a>, which is the process of removing deformation wear from the edge. Other examples of cutting tools include <a href="/wiki/Chisel#Gouge" title="Chisel">gouges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drill_bit" title="Drill bit">drill bits</a>.</li>
<li>Moving tools move large and tiny items. Many are <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">levers</a> which give the user a <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_advantage" title="Mechanical advantage">mechanical advantage</a>. Examples of force-concentrating tools include the <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a> which moves a nail or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post_maul" title="Post maul">maul</a> which moves a stake. These operate by applying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">physical compression</a> to a surface. In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Screwdriver" title="Screwdriver">screwdriver</a>, the force is rotational and called <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a>. By contrast, an <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a> concentrates force on an object being hammered by <i>preventing</i> it from moving away when struck. <a href="/wiki/Writing_implement" title="Writing implement">Writing implements</a> deliver a fluid to a surface via compression to activate the ink cartridge. Grabbing and twisting nuts and bolts with <a href="/wiki/Pliers" title="Pliers">pliers</a>, a glove, a <a href="/wiki/Wrench" title="Wrench">wrench</a>, etc. likewise move items by applying torque (rotational force).</li>
<li>Tools that enact chemical changes, including temperature and ignition, such as <a href="/wiki/Lighter" title="Lighter">lighters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blowtorch" title="Blowtorch">blowtorches</a>.</li>
<li>Guiding, measuring and perception tools include the <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasses" title="Glasses">glasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Square_(tool)" title="Square (tool)">square</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Straightedge" title="Straightedge">straightedge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">theodolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_monitor" title="Computer monitor">monitor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clock</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phone" title="Phone">phone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Printer_(computing)" title="Printer (computing)">printer</a></li>
<li>Shaping tools, such as <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">molds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jig_(tool)" title="Jig (tool)">jigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trowel" title="Trowel">trowels</a>.</li>
<li>Fastening tools, such as <a href="/wiki/Welder" title="Welder">welders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soldering_iron" title="Soldering iron">soldering irons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rivet_gun" title="Rivet gun">rivet guns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nail_gun" title="Nail gun">nail guns</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glue_gun" title="Glue gun">glue guns</a>.</li>
<li>Information and data manipulation tools, such as <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integrated_development_environment" title="Integrated development environment">IDE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spreadsheet" title="Spreadsheet">spreadsheets</a></li></ul>
<p>Some tools may be combinations of other tools. An alarm-clock is for example a combination of a measuring tool (the clock) and a perception tool (the alarm). This enables the alarm-clock to be a tool that falls outside of all the categories mentioned above.
</p><p>There is some debate on whether to consider protective gear items as tools, because they do not directly help perform work, just protect the worker like ordinary clothing. They do meet the general definition of tools and in many cases are necessary for the completion of the work. <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">Personal protective equipment</a> includes such items as <a href="/wiki/Glove" title="Glove">gloves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_glasses" title="Safety glasses">safety glasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earplug" title="Earplug">ear defenders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biohazard" title="Biohazard">biohazard</a> suits.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tool_substitution">Tool substitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tool substitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Often, by design or coincidence, a tool may share key functional attributes with one or more other tools. In this case, some tools can substitute for other tools, either as a makeshift solution or as a matter of practical efficiency. "One tool does it all" is a motto of some importance for workers who cannot practically carry every specialized tool to the location of every work task, such as a carpenter who does not necessarily work in a shop all day and needs to do jobs in a customer's house. Tool substitution may be divided broadly into two classes: substitution "by-design", or "multi-purpose", and substitution as makeshift. Substitution "by-design" would be tools that are designed specifically to accomplish multiple tasks using only that one tool.
</p><p>Substitution is "makeshift" when human ingenuity comes into play and a tool is used for an unintended purpose such as using a long screw driver to separate a cars control arm from a ball joint instead of using a tuning fork. In many cases, the designed secondary functions of tools are not widely known. For example, many wood-cutting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_saws" title="Hand saws">hand saws</a> integrate a <a href="/wiki/Square_(tool)" title="Square (tool)">square</a> by incorporating a specially shaped handle that allows 90° and 45° angles to be marked by aligning the appropriate part of the handle with an edge and scribing along the back edge of the saw. The latter is illustrated by the saying "All tools can be used as hammers." Nearly all tools can be used to function as a hammer, even though very few tools are intentionally designed for it and even fewer work as well as the original.
</p><p>
Tools are also often used to substitute for many mechanical apparatuses, especially in older mechanical devices. In many cases a cheap tool could be used to occupy the place of a missing mechanical part. A window roller in a car could easily be replaced with a pair of <a href="/wiki/Locking_pliers" title="Locking pliers">vise-grips</a> or regular <a href="/wiki/Pliers" title="Pliers">pliers</a>. A transmission shifter or ignition switch would be able to be replaced with a screwdriver. Again, these would be considered tools that are being used for their unintended purposes, substitution as makeshift. Tools such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotary_tool" title="Rotary tool">rotary tool</a> would be considered the substitution "by-design", or "multi-purpose". This class of tools allows the use of one tool that has at least two different capabilities. "Multi-purpose" tools are basically multiple tools in one device/tool. Tools such as this are often power tools that come with many different attachments like a rotary tool does, so you could say that a power drill is a "multi-purpose" tool because you can do more than just one thing with a power drill.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG/250px-Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG/375px-Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG/500px-Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bicycle_multi-tool.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bicycle multi-tool</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-use_tools">Multi-use tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Multi-use tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Multi-tool" title="Multi-tool">multi-tool</a> is a hand tool that incorporates several tools into a single, portable device; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_army_knife" title="Swiss army knife">Swiss army knife</a> represents one of the earliest examples. Other tools have a primary purpose but also incorporate other functionality – for example, <a href="/wiki/Lineman%27s_pliers" title="Lineman's pliers">lineman's pliers</a> incorporate a gripper and cutter and are often used as a hammer; and some <a href="/wiki/Hand_saw" title="Hand saw">hand saws</a> incorporate a <a href="/wiki/Square_(tool)" title="Square (tool)">square</a> in the right-angle between the blade's dull edge and the saw's handle. This would also be the category of "multi-purpose" tools, since they are also multiple tools in one (multi-use and multi-purpose can be used interchangeably – compare <a href="/wiki/Hand_axe" title="Hand axe">hand axe</a>). These types of tools were specifically made to catch the eye of many different craftsman who traveled to do their work. To these workers these types of tools were revolutionary because they were one tool or one device that could do several different things. With this new revolution of tools, the traveling craftsman would not have to carry so many tools with them to job sites, in that their space would be limited to the vehicle or to the beast of burden they were driving. Multi-use tools solve the problem of having to deal with many different tools.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_by_other_animals">Use by other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use by other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals" title="Tool use by animals">Tool use by animals</a></div><p>
Tool use by animals is a phenomenon in which an animal uses any kind of tool in order to achieve a goal such as acquiring food and water, <a href="/wiki/Personal_grooming" title="Personal grooming">grooming</a>, defense, <a href="/wiki/Animal_communication" title="Animal communication">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Play_(activity)" title="Play (activity)">recreation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_architecture" title="Animal architecture">construction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup> Originally thought to be a skill possessed only by <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, some tool use requires a sophisticated level of cognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-34">[34]</a></sup> There is considerable discussion about the definition of what constitutes a tool and therefore which behaviours can be considered true examples of tool use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-35">[35]</a></sup> Observation has confirmed that <a href="/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals" title="Tool use by animals">a number of species can use tools</a> including <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">apes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>, several birds, and <a href="/wiki/Sea_otter" title="Sea otter">sea otters</a>. Now the unique relationship of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> with tools is considered to be that we are the only species that uses tools to make <i>other</i> tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CooperRobertson1997_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-CooperRobertson1997-36">[36]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1952" data-file-width="2608" decoding="async" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg/250px-A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg/375px-A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg/500px-A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Bonobo_at_the_San_Diego_Zoo_%22fishing%22_for_termites.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Bonobo" title="Bonobo">Bonobo</a> at the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Zoo" title="San Diego Zoo">San Diego Zoo</a> "fishing" for <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a></div></div></div><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">Primates</a> are well known for using tools for hunting or gathering food and water, cover for rain, and self-defense. Chimpanzees have often been the object of study in regard to their usage of tools, most famously by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goodall" title="Jane Goodall">Jane Goodall</a>, since these animals are frequently kept in captivity and are closely related to humans. Wild tool-use in other primates, especially among <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">apes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monkeys" title="Monkeys">monkeys</a>, is considered relatively common, though its full extent remains poorly documented, as many primates in the wild are mainly only observed distantly or briefly when in their natural environments and living without human influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-35">[35]</a></sup> Some novel tool-use by primates may arise in a localized or isolated manner within certain unique <a href="/wiki/Animal_culture" title="Animal culture">primate cultures</a>, being transmitted and practiced among socially connected primates through <a href="/wiki/Cultural_learning#In_non-human_animals" title="Cultural learning">cultural learning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-34">[34]</a></sup> Many famous researchers, such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> in his book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Descent_of_Man" title="The Descent of Man">The Descent of Man</a></i>, mentioned tool-use in <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Baboon" title="Baboon">baboons</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, both wild and captive <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> are known to create tools using their trunks and feet, mainly for swatting flies, scratching, plugging up waterholes that they have dug (to close them up again so the water doesn't evaporate), and reaching food that is out of reach. Many other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_animals" title="Social animals">social mammals</a> particularly have been observed engaging in tool-use. A group of <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shark_Bay" title="Shark Bay">Shark Bay</a> uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" title="Sea sponge">sea sponges</a> to protect their beaks while foraging. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_otters" title="Sea otters">Sea otters</a> will use rocks or other hard objects to dislodge food (such as <a href="/wiki/Abalone" title="Abalone">abalone</a>) and break open <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>. Many or most mammals of the order <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">Carnivora</a> have been observed using tools, often to trap or break open the shells of prey, as well as for scratching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid">Corvids</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raven_(bird)" title="Raven (bird)">ravens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">rooks</a>) are well known for their large brains (among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds">birds</a>) and tool use. <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonian_crow" title="New Caledonian crow">New Caledonian crows</a> are among the only animals that create their own tools. They mainly manufacture probes out of twigs and wood (and sometimes metal wire) to catch or impale <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>. Tool use in some birds may be best exemplified in nest intricacy. <a href="/wiki/Tailorbird" title="Tailorbird">Tailorbirds</a> manufacture 'pouches' to make their nests in. Some birds, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weaver_bird" title="Weaver bird">weaver birds</a>, build complex nests utilizing a diverse array of objects and materials, many of which are specifically chosen by certain birds for their unique qualities. <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker_finch" title="Woodpecker finch">Woodpecker finches</a> insert twigs into trees in order to catch or impale larvae. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parrots" title="Parrots">Parrots</a> may use tools to wedge nuts so that they can crack open the outer shell of nuts without launching away the inner contents. Some birds take advantage of human activity, such as <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">carrion crows</a> in Japan, which drop nuts in front of cars to crack them open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_35-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several species of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> use tools to hunt and crack open shellfish, extract food that is out of reach, or clear an area for nesting. Among cephalopods (and perhaps uniquely or to an extent unobserved among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a>), <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopuses</a> are known to utilize tools relatively frequently, such as gathering coconut shells to create a shelter or using rocks to create barriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_35-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tool_metaphors">Tool metaphors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tool metaphors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A telephone is a communication tool that interfaces between two people engaged in conversation at one level. It also interfaces between each user and the communication network at another level. It is in the domain of media and communications technology that a counter-intuitive aspect of our relationships with our tools first began to gain popular recognition. <a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan" title="Marshall McLuhan">Marshall McLuhan</a> famously said "We shape our tools. And then our tools shape us."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Inline_citation#When_you_must_use_inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Inline citation"><span title="The text near this tag needs a citation. (June 2022)">quote citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> McLuhan was referring to the fact that our social practices co-evolve with our use of new tools and the refinements we make to existing tools.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
