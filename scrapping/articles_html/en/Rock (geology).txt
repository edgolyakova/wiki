<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <b>rock</b> (or <b>stone</b>) is any naturally occurring solid mass or aggregate of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mineraloid" title="Mineraloid">mineraloid</a> matter. It is categorized by the minerals included, its <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical composition</a>, and the way in which it is formed. Rocks form the Earth's outer solid layer, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">crust</a>, and most of its interior, except for the liquid <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core" title="Earth's outer core">outer core</a> and pockets of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a>. The study of rocks involves multiple subdisciplines of geology, including <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">petrology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a>. It may be limited to rocks found on Earth, or it may include <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">planetary geology</a> that studies the rocks of other celestial objects.
</p><p>Rocks are usually grouped into three main groups: <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rocks</a>. Igneous rocks are formed when <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> cools in the Earth's crust, or <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> cools on the ground surface or the seabed. Sedimentary rocks are formed by <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithification" title="Lithification">lithification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a>, which in turn are formed by the <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>, transport, and <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposition</a> of existing rocks. Metamorphic rocks are formed when existing rocks are subjected to such high pressures and temperatures that they are transformed without significant melting.
</p><p>Humanity has made use of rocks since the earliest humans. This early period, called the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>, saw the development of many stone tools. Stone was then used as a major component in the construction of buildings and early <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a> developed to extract rocks from the Earth and obtain the minerals within them, including <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>. Modern technology has allowed the development of new man-made rocks and rock-like substances, such as <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Study">Study</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">Petrology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">Mineralogy</a></div>
<p>Geology is the study of Earth and its components, including the study of rock formations. Petrology is the study of the character and origin of rocks. Mineralogy is the study of the mineral components that create rocks. The study of rocks and their components has contributed to the geological understanding of Earth's history, the <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> understanding of human history, and the development of <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> in human society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>While the <a href="/wiki/History_of_geology" title="History of geology">history of geology</a> includes many theories of rocks and their origins that have persisted throughout human history, the study of rocks was developed as a formal science during the 19th century. <a href="/wiki/Plutonism" title="Plutonism">Plutonism</a> was developed as a theory during this time, and the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> in 1896 allowed for the <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> of rocks. Understanding of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> developed in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Formation_of_rocks" title="Formation of rocks">Formation of rocks</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti,_Finland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2976" data-file-width="3681" decoding="async" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti%2C_Finland.jpg/220px-Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti%2C_Finland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti%2C_Finland.jpg/330px-Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti%2C_Finland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti%2C_Finland.jpg/440px-Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti%2C_Finland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kummakivi_balancing_rock_in_Ruokolahti,_Finland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Balancing_rock" title="Balancing rock">balancing rock</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Kummakivi" title="Kummakivi">Kummakivi</a></i> (literally <i>"strange stone"</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Rocks are composed primarily of grains of minerals, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline_solids" title="Crystalline solids">crystalline solids</a> formed from atoms <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemically bonded</a> into an orderly structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 3">:‚Ää3‚Ää</span></sup> Some rocks also contain <a href="/wiki/Mineraloid" title="Mineraloid">mineraloids</a>, which are rigid, mineral-like substances, such as <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_glass" title="Volcanic glass">volcanic glass</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 55, 79">:‚Ää55,‚Ää79‚Ää</span></sup> that lacks crystalline structure. The types and abundance of minerals in a rock are determined by the manner in which it was formed.
</p><p>Most rocks contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">silicate minerals</a>, compounds that include silica tetrahedra in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattice" title="Crystal lattice">crystal lattice</a>, and account for about one-third of all known mineral species and about 95% of the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">earth's crust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The proportion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> in rocks and minerals is a major factor in determining their names and properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson1995_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1995-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DirkvdM_rocks.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="852" data-file-width="1136" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/DirkvdM_rocks.jpg/220px-DirkvdM_rocks.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/DirkvdM_rocks.jpg/330px-DirkvdM_rocks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/DirkvdM_rocks.jpg/440px-DirkvdM_rocks.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DirkvdM_rocks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rock <a href="/wiki/Outcrop" title="Outcrop">outcrop</a> along a mountain creek near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oros%C3%AD" title="Oros√≠">Oros√≠</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Rocks are classified according to characteristics such as mineral and chemical composition, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texture_(geology)" title="Texture (geology)">texture</a> of the constituent particles, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">particle size</a>. These physical properties are the result of the processes that formed the rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt-5">[5]</a></sup> Over the course of time, rocks can be transformed from one type into another, as described by a geological model called the <a href="/wiki/Rock_cycle" title="Rock cycle">rock cycle</a>. This transformation produces three general classes of rock: <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a>.
</p><p>Those three classes are subdivided into many groups. There are, however, no hard-and-fast boundaries between allied rocks. By increase or decrease in the proportions of their minerals, they pass through gradations from one to the other; the distinctive structures of one kind of rock may thus be traced, gradually merging into those of another. Hence the definitions adopted in rock names simply correspond to selected points in a continuously graduated series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Igneous_rock">Igneous rock</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">Igneous rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GabbroRockCreek1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1436" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/GabbroRockCreek1.jpg/220px-GabbroRockCreek1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/GabbroRockCreek1.jpg/330px-GabbroRockCreek1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/GabbroRockCreek1.jpg/440px-GabbroRockCreek1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GabbroRockCreek1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sample of igneous <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a></div></div></div>
<p>Igneous rock (derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>igneus,</i> meaning <i>of fire,</i> from <i>ignis</i> meaning <i>fire)</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> is formed through the cooling and <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">solidification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>. This magma may be derived from partial melts of pre-existing rocks in either a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. Typically, the melting of rocks is caused by one or more of three processes: an increase in temperature, a decrease in pressure, or a change in composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 591‚Äì599">:‚Ää591‚Äì599‚Ää</span></sup>
</p><p>Igneous rocks are divided into two main categories:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonic" title="Plutonic">Plutonic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion" title="Intrusion">intrusive</a> rocks result when magma cools and <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallizes</a> slowly within the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>. A common example of this type is <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>.</li>
<li>Volcanic or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrusive_(geology)" title="Extrusive (geology)">extrusive</a> rocks result from magma reaching the surface either as lava or <i>fragmental ejecta</i>, forming minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Pumice" title="Pumice">pumice</a> or <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Magmas tend to become richer in silica as they rise towards the Earth's surface, a process called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magma_differentiation" title="Magma differentiation">magma differentiation</a></i>. This occurs both because minerals low in silica crystallize out of the magma as it begins to cool (<a href="/wiki/Bowen%27s_reaction_series" title="Bowen's reaction series">Bowen's reaction series</a>) and because the magma assimilates some of the crustal rock through which it ascends (<i><a href="/wiki/Country_rock_(geology)" title="Country rock (geology)">country rock</a></i>), and crustal rock tends to be high in silica. Silica content is thus the most important chemical criterion for classifying igneous rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson1995_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1995-7">[7]</a></sup> The content of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_metal_oxide" title="Alkali metal oxide">alkali metal oxides</a> is next in importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>About 65% of the Earth's crust by volume consists of igneous rocks. Of these, 66% are basalt and <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a>, 16% are granite, and 17% <a href="/wiki/Granodiorite" title="Granodiorite">granodiorite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diorite" title="Diorite">diorite</a>. Only 0.6% are <a href="/wiki/Syenite" title="Syenite">syenite</a> and 0.3% are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> is 99% basalt, which is an igneous rock of <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> composition. Granite and similar rocks, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granitoids" title="Granitoids">granitoids</a>, dominate the <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-condie_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-condie-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BucherGrapes2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BucherGrapes2011-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentary_rock">Sedimentary rock</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">Sedimentary rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_(Upper_Paleozoic;_quarry_near_Crossville,_Tennessee,_USA)_2_(40280403530).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2535" data-file-width="3993" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_%28Upper_Paleozoic%3B_quarry_near_Crossville%2C_Tennessee%2C_USA%29_2_%2840280403530%29.jpg/220px-%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_%28Upper_Paleozoic%3B_quarry_near_Crossville%2C_Tennessee%2C_USA%29_2_%2840280403530%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_%28Upper_Paleozoic%3B_quarry_near_Crossville%2C_Tennessee%2C_USA%29_2_%2840280403530%29.jpg/330px-%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_%28Upper_Paleozoic%3B_quarry_near_Crossville%2C_Tennessee%2C_USA%29_2_%2840280403530%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_%28Upper_Paleozoic%3B_quarry_near_Crossville%2C_Tennessee%2C_USA%29_2_%2840280403530%29.jpg/440px-%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_%28Upper_Paleozoic%3B_quarry_near_Crossville%2C_Tennessee%2C_USA%29_2_%2840280403530%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%22Liesegang_banding%22_in_quartzose_sandstone_(Upper_Paleozoic;_quarry_near_Crossville,_Tennessee,_USA)_2_(40280403530).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> with <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> bands</div></div></div>
<p>Sedimentary rocks are formed at the earth's surface by the accumulation and cementation of fragments of earlier rocks, minerals, and organisms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> or as chemical precipitates and organic growths in water (<a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>). This process causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clastic" title="Clastic">clastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> (pieces of rock) or <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic</a> particles (<a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>) to settle and accumulate or for minerals to chemically <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporite</a>) from a <a href="/wiki/Solution_(chemistry)" title="Solution (chemistry)">solution</a>. The particulate matter then undergoes compaction and <a href="/wiki/Cementation_(geology)" title="Cementation (geology)">cementation</a> at moderate temperatures and pressures (<a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenesis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 265‚Äì280">:‚Ää265‚Äì280‚Ää</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boggs-2006_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-boggs-2006-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 147‚Äì154">:‚Ää147‚Äì154‚Ää</span></sup>
</p><p>Before being deposited, sediments are formed by <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> of earlier rocks by <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> in a source area and then transported to the place of deposition by <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass_wasting" title="Mass wasting">mass movement</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> (agents of <a href="/wiki/Denudation" title="Denudation">denudation</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt-5">[5]</a></sup> About 7.9% of the crust by volume is composed of sedimentary rocks, with 82% of those being shales, while the remainder consists of 6% limestone and 12% sandstone and <a href="/wiki/Arkose" title="Arkose">arkoses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BucherGrapes2011_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-BucherGrapes2011-13">[13]</a></sup> Sedimentary rocks often contain <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>. Sedimentary rocks form under the influence of gravity and typically are deposited in horizontal or near horizontal layers or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strata" title="Strata">strata</a>, and may be referred to as stratified rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sediment and the particles of <a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">clastic</a> sedimentary rocks can be further classified by <a href="/wiki/Grain_size" title="Grain size">grain size</a>. The smallest sediments are <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>. Some systems include <a href="/wiki/Cobble_(geology)" title="Cobble (geology)">cobbles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boulder" title="Boulder">boulders</a> as measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metamorphic_rock">Metamorphic rock</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">Metamorphic rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:219px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skagit-gneiss-Cascades.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="217" decoding="async" height="324" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/69/Skagit-gneiss-Cascades.jpg" width="217"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Skagit-gneiss-Cascades.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Metamorphic banded <a href="/wiki/Gneiss" title="Gneiss">gneiss</a></div></div></div>
<p>Metamorphic rocks are formed by subjecting any rock type‚Äîsedimentary rock, igneous rock or another older metamorphic rock‚Äîto different <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> conditions than those in which the original rock was formed. This process is called <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphism</a>, meaning to "change in form". The result is a profound change in physical properties and chemistry of the stone. The original rock, known as the <a href="/wiki/Protolith" title="Protolith">protolith</a>, transforms into other mineral types or other forms of the same minerals, by <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(geology)" title="Recrystallization (geology)">recrystallization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt-5">[5]</a></sup> The temperatures and pressures required for this process are always higher than those found at the Earth's surface: temperatures greater than 150 to 200¬†¬∞C and pressures greater than 1500¬†bars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This occurs, for example, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_plates" title="Continental plates">continental plates</a> collide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 31‚Äì33, 134‚Äì139">:‚Ää31‚Äì33,‚Ää134‚Äì139‚Ää</span></sup> Metamorphic rocks compose 27.4% of the crust by volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BucherGrapes2011_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-BucherGrapes2011-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The three major classes of metamorphic rock are based upon the formation mechanism. An intrusion of magma that heats the surrounding rock causes contact metamorphism‚Äîa temperature-dominated transformation. Pressure metamorphism occurs when sediments are buried deep under the ground; pressure is dominant, and temperature plays a smaller role. This is termed burial metamorphism, and it can result in rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Jade" title="Jade">jade</a>. Where both heat and pressure play a role, the mechanism is termed regional metamorphism. This is typically found in mountain-building regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson1995_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1995-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Depending on the structure, metamorphic rocks are divided into two general categories. Those that possess a texture are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Foliation_(geology)" title="Foliation (geology)">foliated</a>; the remainders are termed non-foliated. The name of the rock is then determined based on the types of minerals present. <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">Schists</a> are foliated rocks that are primarily composed of <a href="/wiki/Lamella_(materials)" title="Lamella (materials)">lamellar minerals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">micas</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Gneiss" title="Gneiss">gneiss</a> has visible bands of differing <a href="/wiki/Lightness" title="Lightness">lightness</a>, with a common example being the granite gneiss. Other varieties of foliated rock include <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phyllite" title="Phyllite">phyllites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mylonite" title="Mylonite">mylonite</a>. Familiar examples of non-foliated metamorphic rocks include <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soapstone" title="Soapstone">soapstone</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine</a>. This branch contains <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a>‚Äîa metamorphosed form of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>‚Äîand <a href="/wiki/Hornfels" title="Hornfels">hornfels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson1995_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1995-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_rocks">Extraterrestrial rocks</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">Planetary geology</a></div>
<p>Though most understanding of rocks comes from those of Earth, rocks make up many of the universe's celestial bodies. In the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> are composed of rock, as are many <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">natural satellites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroids</a>. <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">Meteorites</a> that fall to Earth provide evidence of extraterrestrial rocks and their composition. They are typically heavier than rocks on Earth. Asteroid rocks can also be brought to Earth through space missions, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Hayabusa" title="Hayabusa">Hayabusa</a></i> mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">Lunar rocks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_rocks" title="Martian rocks">Martian rocks</a> have also been studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_use">Human use</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TallOvoo.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/TallOvoo.JPG/220px-TallOvoo.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/TallOvoo.JPG/330px-TallOvoo.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/TallOvoo.JPG/440px-TallOvoo.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TallOvoo.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ceremonial <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairn</a> of rocks, an <a href="/wiki/Ovoo" title="Ovoo">ovoo</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a></div></div></div>
<p>The use of rock has had a huge impact on the cultural and technological development of the human race. Rock has been used by humans and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominids" title="Hominids">hominids</a> for at least <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">2.5¬†million years</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lithic_technology" title="Lithic technology">Lithic technology</a> marks some of the oldest and continuously used technologies. The mining of rock for its metal content has been one of the most important factors of human advancement, and has progressed at different rates in different places, in part because of the kind of metals available from the rock of a region.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropic_rock">Anthropic rock</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anthropic_rock" title="Anthropic rock">Anthropic rock</a></div>
<p>Anthropic rock is synthetic or restructured rock formed by human activity. Concrete is recognized as a man-made rock constituted of natural and processed rock and having been developed since <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Rock can also be modified with other substances to develop new forms, such as <a href="/wiki/Epoxy_granite" title="Epoxy granite">epoxy granite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Artificial_stone" title="Artificial stone">Artificial stone</a> has also been developed, such as <a href="/wiki/Coade_stone" title="Coade stone">Coade stone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Geologist James R. Underwood has proposed anthropic rock as a fourth class of rocks alongside igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building">Building</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Building_material#Stone_or_rock" title="Building material">Building material ¬ß¬†Stone or rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3480" data-file-width="4640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg/220px-Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg/330px-Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg/440px-Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pirunvuoren_kivilinna.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A stonehouse on the hill in <a href="/wiki/Sastamala" title="Sastamala">Sastamala</a>, Finland</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg/220px-Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg/330px-Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg/440px-Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hochbeet_aus_Naturstein.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Raised-bed_gardening" title="Raised-bed gardening">Raised garden bed</a> with natural stones</div></div></div>
<p>Rock varies greatly in strength, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quartzites" title="Quartzites">quartzites</a> having a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> in excess of 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPa" title="MPa">MPa</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> to sedimentary rock so soft it can be crumbled with bare fingers (that is, it is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friable" title="Friable">friable</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> (For comparison, <a href="/wiki/Structural_steel" title="Structural steel">structural steel</a> has a tensile strength of around 350 MPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>) Relatively soft, easily worked sedimentary rock was quarried for construction as early as 4000 BCE in Egypt,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and stone was used to build fortifications in <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> as early as 2800 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The soft rock, <a href="/wiki/Tuff" title="Tuff">tuff</a>, is common in Italy, and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> used it for many buildings and bridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson-etal-2005_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-jackson-etal-2005-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">Limestone</a> was widely used in construction in the Middle Ages in Europe <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ashurst_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ashurst-33">[33]</a></sup> and remained popular into the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UraniumMineUtah.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UraniumMineUtah.JPG/220px-UraniumMineUtah.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UraniumMineUtah.JPG/330px-UraniumMineUtah.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UraniumMineUtah.JPG/440px-UraniumMineUtah.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UraniumMineUtah.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Steen#Mi_Vida" title="Charles Steen">Mi Vida uranium mine</a> near <a href="/wiki/Moab,_Utah" title="Moab, Utah">Moab, Utah</a></div></div></div>
<p>Mining is the extraction of <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">valuable</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> materials from the earth, from an <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> body, <a href="/wiki/Vein_(geology)" title="Vein (geology)">vein</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seam_(geology)" title="Seam (geology)">seam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The term also includes the removal of soil. Materials recovered by mining include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_metals" title="Base metals">base metals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precious_metals" title="Precious metals">precious metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">rock salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">construction aggregate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimension_stone" title="Dimension stone">dimension stone</a>. Mining is required to obtain any material that cannot be grown through <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> processes, or created <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">artificially</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratory</a> or <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factory</a>. Mining in a wider sense comprises extraction of any <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resource</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>) from the earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mining of rock and metals has been done since <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric</a> times. Modern mining processes involve <a href="/wiki/Prospecting" title="Prospecting">prospecting</a> for mineral deposits, analysis of the profit potential of a proposed mine, extraction of the desired materials, and finally reclamation of the land to prepare it for other uses once mining ceases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mining processes may create negative impacts on the environment both during the mining operations and for years after mining has ceased. These potential impacts have led to most of the world's nations adopting regulations to manage negative effects of mining operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">Tools</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">Stone tool</a></div>
<p>Stone tools have been used for millions of years by humans and earlier <a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">hominids</a>. The Stone Age was a period of widespread stone tool usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhm.ac.uk_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhm.ac.uk-39">[39]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Stone_Age" title="Early Stone Age">Early Stone Age</a> tools were simple implements, such as <a href="/wiki/Hammerstone" title="Hammerstone">hammerstones</a> and sharp flakes. <a href="/wiki/Middle_Stone_Age" title="Middle Stone Age">Middle Stone Age</a> tools featured sharpened points to be used as <a href="/wiki/Projectile_point" title="Projectile point">projectile points</a>, awls, or <a href="/wiki/Scraper_(archaeology)" title="Scraper (archaeology)">scrapers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Late_Stone_Age" title="Late Stone Age">Late Stone Age</a> tools were developed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Craftsmanship" title="Craftsmanship">craftsmanship</a> and distinct cultural identities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Stone tools were largely superseded by copper and bronze tools following the development of <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>.
</p>
