<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Bow and arrow</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Pre-gunpowder ranged weapon system</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Bows and Arrows" redirects here. For the album by The Walkmen, see <a href="/wiki/Bows_%2B_Arrows" title="Bows + Arrows">Bows + Arrows</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_(49109425873).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2805" data-file-width="4152" decoding="async" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_%2849109425873%29.jpg/330px-Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_%2849109425873%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_%2849109425873%29.jpg/495px-Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_%2849109425873%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_%2849109425873%29.jpg/660px-Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_%2849109425873%29.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guinea_Fowl_Hunter_(49109425873).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Karo_people_(East_Africa)" title="Karo people (East Africa)">Karo</a> boy holding a bow and arrow</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>bow and arrow</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Ranged_weapon" title="Ranged weapon">ranged weapon</a> system consisting of an <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elastic</a> launching device (bow) and long-shafted <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">arrows</a>). Humans used bows and arrows for <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aggression" title="Aggression">aggression</a> long before <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>, and the practice was common to many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> cultures. They were important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapon_of_war" title="Weapon of war">weapons of war</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient history</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, where they were rendered increasingly obsolete by the development of the more powerful and accurate <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a>. Today, bows and arrows are mostly used for <a href="/wiki/Bowhunting" title="Bowhunting">hunting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_competitive_archery" title="Modern competitive archery">sports</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">Archery</a> is the art, practice, or skill of using bows to <a href="/wiki/Shooting" title="Shooting">shoot</a> arrows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson17_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson17-1">[1]</a></sup> A person who shoots arrows with a bow is called a bowman or an archer. Someone who makes bows is known as a <a href="/wiki/Bowyer" title="Bowyer">bowyer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson31_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson31-2">[2]</a></sup> someone who makes arrows is a <a href="/wiki/Fletching" title="Fletching">fletcher</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson56_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson56-3">[3]</a></sup> and someone who manufactures metal <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead" title="Arrowhead">arrowheads</a> is an arrowsmith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson20_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson20-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_design_and_use">Basic design and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic design and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="676" data-file-width="487" decoding="async" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg/170px-19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg/255px-19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg/340px-19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:19th_century_knowledge_archery_drawing_the_bow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drawing a bow, from a 1908 archery manual</div></div></div>
<p>A bow consists of a semi-<a href="/wiki/Stiffness" title="Stiffness">rigid</a> but elastic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_(geometry)" title="Arc (geometry)">arc</a> with a high-tensile <a href="/wiki/Bowstring" title="Bowstring">bowstring</a> joining the ends of the two limbs of the bow. An arrow is a <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectile</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead" title="Arrowhead">pointed tip</a> and a long shaft with stabilizer <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fins</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fletching" title="Fletching">fletching</a>) towards the back, with a narrow notch (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nock_(arrow)" title="Nock (arrow)">nock</a></i>) at the very end to contact the bowstring.
</p><p>To load an arrow for shooting (<i>nocking</i> an arrow), the archer places an arrow across the middle of the bow with the bowstring in the arrow's nock. To shoot, the archer holds the bow at its center with one hand and pulls back (<a href="/wiki/Bow_draw" title="Bow draw">draws</a>) the arrow and the bowstring with the other (typically the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominant_hand" title="Dominant hand">dominant hand</a>). This flexes the two limbs of the bow rearwards, which perform the function of a pair of <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)#Types" title="Spring (device)">cantilever springs</a> to store <a href="/wiki/Elastic_energy" title="Elastic energy">elastic energy</a>.
</p><p>Typically while maintaining the draw, the archer aims the shot <a href="/wiki/Point_shooting" title="Point shooting">intuitively</a> or by sighting along the arrow. Then archer releases (looses) the draw, allowing the limbs' stored energy to convert into <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> transmitted via the bowstring to the arrow, propelling it to fly forward with high velocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson27_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson27-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A container or bag for additional arrows for quick reloading is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Quiver" title="Quiver">quiver</a></i>.
</p><p>When not in use, bows are generally kept <i>unstrung</i>, meaning one or both ends of the bowstring are detached from the bow. This removes all residual tension on the bow and can help prevent it from losing strength or elasticity over time. Many bow designs also let it straighten out more completely, reducing the space needed to store the bow. Returning the bowstring to its ready-to-use position is called <i>stringing</i> the bow.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_archery" title="History of archery">History of archery</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1949" data-file-width="2579" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg/220px-Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg/330px-Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg/440px-Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scythians_shooting_with_bows_Kertch_antique_Panticapeum_Ukrainia_4th_century_BCE.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> shooting with bows, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panticapeum" title="Panticapeum">Panticapeum</a> (modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kertch" title="Kertch">Kertch</a>), 4th century BCE.</div></div></div>
<p>The oldest known evidence of the bow and arrow comes from South African sites such as <a href="/wiki/Sibudu_Cave" title="Sibudu Cave">Sibudu Cave</a>, where likely arrowheads have been found, dating from approximately 72,000–60,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Backwell_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Backwell-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lombard_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lombard-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lombard_M_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lombard_M-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Backwell2018_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Backwell2018-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lombard2020_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lombard2020-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest probable arrowheads found outside of Africa were discovered in 2020 in <a href="/wiki/Fa_Hien_Cave" title="Fa Hien Cave">Fa Hien Cave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. They have been dated to 48,000 years ago. "Bow-and-arrow hunting at the Sri Lankan site likely focused on monkeys and smaller animals, such as squirrels, Langley says. Remains of these creatures were found in the same sediment as the bone points."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Elsewhere in Eurasia, the bow and arrow seems to reappear around the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a>. After the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, use of the bow seems to have spread to every inhabited region, except for Australasia and most of Oceania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest definite remains of bow and arrow from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> are possible fragments from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> found at Mannheim-Vogelstang dated 17,500–18,000 years ago, and at Stellmoor dated 11,000 years ago. <a href="/wiki/Azilian" title="Azilian">Azilian</a> points found in <a href="/wiki/Grotte_du_Bichon" title="Grotte du Bichon">Grotte du Bichon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, alongside the remains of both a <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bear</a> and a hunter, with <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> fragments found in the bear's third <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebra</a>, suggest the use of arrows at 13,500 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the site of <a href="/wiki/Nataruk" title="Nataruk">Nataruk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turkana_County" title="Turkana County">Turkana County</a>, Kenya, <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a> bladelets found embedded in a <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a> and within the <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_cavity" title="Thoracic cavity">thoracic cavity</a> of another <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeleton</a>, suggest the use of stone-tipped arrows as weapons about 10,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The oldest extant bows in one piece are the <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">elm</a> <a href="/wiki/Holmegaard_bow" title="Holmegaard bow">Holmegaard bows</a> from <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, which were dated to 9,000 BCE. Several bows from Holmegaard, Denmark, date 8,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> High-performance wooden bows are currently made following the Holmegaard design.
The Stellmoor bow fragments from northern Germany were dated to about 8,000 BCE, but they were destroyed in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_14_dating" title="Carbon 14 dating">carbon 14 dating</a> was available; their age is attributed by archaeological association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Savo.vaakuna.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Savo.vaakuna.svg/170px-Savo.vaakuna.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Savo.vaakuna.svg/255px-Savo.vaakuna.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Savo.vaakuna.svg/340px-Savo.vaakuna.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Savo.vaakuna.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bow and arrow pictured in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> of the historical province of <a href="/wiki/Savonia_(historical_province)" title="Savonia (historical province)">Savonia</a></div></div></div>
<p>The bow was an important weapon for both <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_warfare" title="Prehistoric warfare">warfare</a> from prehistoric times until the widespread use of <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> weapons in the 16th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was also common in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_warfare" title="Ancient warfare">ancient warfare</a>, although certain cultures would not favor them.  Greek poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archilocus" title="Archilocus">Archilocus</a> expressed scorn for fighting with bows and <a href="/wiki/Sling_(weapon)" title="Sling (weapon)">slings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kend_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kend-19">[19]</a></sup> Beginning with the reign of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>, the longbow was England's principal weapon of war until the end of the Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a> and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol_horde" title="Mongol horde">Mongol hordes</a> conquered much of the Eurasian steppe using short bows. Native Americans used archery to hunt and defend themselves during the days of English and later American colonization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organised warfare with bows ended in the early to mid-17th century in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, but it persisted into the 19th century in Eastern<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> cultures, including hunting and warfare in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Canada" title="Northern Canada">Canadian Arctic</a>, bows were made until the end of the 20th century for hunting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribou" title="Caribou">caribou</a>, for instance at <a href="/wiki/Igloolik" title="Igloolik">Igloolik</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The bow has more recently been used as a weapon of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribal_warfare" title="Tribal warfare">tribal warfare</a> in some parts of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>; an example was documented in 2009 in Kenya when <a href="/wiki/Kisii_people" title="Kisii people">Kisii people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalenjin_people" title="Kalenjin people">Kalenjin people</a> clashed, resulting in four deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The British upper class led a revival of archery as a sport in the late 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnes_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnes-25">[25]</a></sup> Sir <a href="/wiki/Ashton_Lever" title="Ashton Lever">Ashton Lever</a>, an antiquarian and collector, formed the Toxophilite Society in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1781, under the patronage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts_of_the_bow">Parts of the bow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parts of the bow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The basic elements of a bow are a pair of curved <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(engineering)" title="Deformation (engineering)">elastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Recurve_bow#terminology" title="Recurve bow">limbs</a>, traditionally made from <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, joined by a riser. However <a href="/wiki/Self_bow" title="Self bow">self bows</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/English_longbow" title="English longbow">English longbow</a> are made of a single piece of wood encompassing both limbs and the grip. Both ends of the limbs are connected by a string known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_string" title="Bow string">bow string</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson27_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson27-5">[5]</a></sup> By pulling the string backwards the <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archer</a> exerts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_strength" title="Compression strength">compression force</a> on the string-facing section, or <a href="/wiki/Recurve_bow#terminology" title="Recurve bow">belly</a>, of the limbs as well as placing the outer section, or <a href="/wiki/Recurve_bow#terminology" title="Recurve bow">back</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Tension_(physics)" title="Tension (physics)">tension</a>. While the string is held, this stores the energy later released in putting the arrow to flight. The force required to hold the string stationary at full draw is often used to express the power of a bow, and is known as its draw weight, or weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson111_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson111-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trad20_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trad20-27">[27]</a></sup> Other things being equal, a higher draw weight means a more powerful bow, which is able to project heavier arrows at the same velocity or the same arrow at a greater velocity.
</p><p>The various parts of the bow can be subdivided into further sections. The topmost limb is known as the upper limb, while the bottom limb is the lower limb. At the tip of each limb is a nock, which is used to attach the bowstring to the limbs. The riser is usually divided into the grip, which is held by the archer, as well as the arrow rest and the bow window. The arrow rest is a small ledge or extension above the grip which the arrow rests upon while being aimed. The bow window is that part of the riser above the grip, which contains the arrow rest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson27_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson27-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In bows drawn and held by hand, the maximum draw weight is determined by the strength of the archer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trad20_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trad20-27">[27]</a></sup> The maximum distance the string could be displaced and thus the longest arrow that could be loosed from it, a bow's draw length, is determined by the size of the archer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trad19_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trad19-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Composite_bow" title="Composite bow">composite bow</a> uses a combination of materials to create the limbs, allowing the use of materials specialized for the different functions of a bow limb. The classic composite bow uses wood for lightness and dimensional stability in the core, horn to store compression energy, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinew" title="Sinew">sinew</a> for its ability to store energy in tension. Such bows, typically Asian, would often use a stiff end on the limb end, having the effect of a recurve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson38_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson38-29">[29]</a></sup> In this type of bow, this is known by the Arabic name 'siyah'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern construction materials for bows include <a href="/wiki/Laminated_bow" title="Laminated bow">laminated</a> wood, <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath15_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heath15-31">[31]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> components.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrows">Arrows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arrows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">Arrow</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arrow.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="82" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Arrow.svg/300px-Arrow.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Arrow.svg/450px-Arrow.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Arrow.svg/600px-Arrow.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arrow.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic of an arrow showing its parts.</div></div></div>
<p>An arrow usually consists of a shaft with an arrowhead attached to the front end, with fletchings and a nock at the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson18_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson18-32">[32]</a></sup> Modern arrows are usually made from carbon fibre, aluminum, fiberglass, and wood shafts. Carbon shafts have the advantage that they do not bend or warp, but they can often be too light weight to shoot from some bows and are expensive. Aluminum shafts are less expensive than carbon shafts, but they can bend and warp from use. Wood shafts are the least expensive option but often will not be identical in weight and size to each other and break more often than the other types of shafts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trad21_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trad21-33">[33]</a></sup> Arrow sizes vary greatly across cultures and range from very short ones that require the use of special equipment to be shot to ones in use in the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> jungles that are 2.6 m (8.5 feet) long. Most modern arrows are 55 to 75 cm (22 to 30 inches) in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson18_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson18-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arrows come in many types, among which are breasted, bob-tailed, barreled, clout, and target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson18_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson18-32">[32]</a></sup> A breasted arrow is thickest at the area right behind the fletchings, and tapers towards the (nock) and head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson32_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson32-34">[34]</a></sup> A bob-tailed arrow is thickest right behind the head, and tapers to the nock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson25_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson25-35">[35]</a></sup> A barrelled arrow is thickest in the centre of the arrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson24_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson24-36">[36]</a></sup> Target arrows are those arrows used for target shooting rather than warfare or hunting, and usually have simple arrowheads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson103_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson103-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>For safety reasons, a bow should never be shot without an arrow nocked; without an arrow, the energy that is normally transferred into the projectile is instead directed back into the bow itself, which will cause damage to the bow's limbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrowheads">Arrowheads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arrowheads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arrowhead" title="Arrowhead">Arrowhead</a></div>
<p>The end of the arrow that is designed to hit the target is called the arrowhead. Usually, these are separate items that are attached to the arrow shaft by either tangs or sockets. Materials used in the past for arrowheads include flint, bone, horn, or metal. Most modern arrowheads are made of steel, but wood and other traditional materials are still used occasionally. A number of different types of arrowheads are known, with the most common being <a href="/wiki/Bodkin_point" title="Bodkin point">bodkins</a>, broadheads, and piles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson19_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson19-39">[39]</a></sup> Bodkin heads are simple spikes made of metal of various shapes, designed to pierce armour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson25_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson25-35">[35]</a></sup> A broadhead arrowhead is usually triangular or leaf-shaped and has a sharpened edge or edges. Broadheads are commonly used for hunting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson33_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson33-40">[40]</a></sup> A pile arrowhead is a simple metal cone, either sharpened to a point or somewhat blunt, that is used mainly for target shooting. A pile head is the same diameter as the arrow shaft and is usually just fitted over the tip of the arrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson85_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson85-41">[41]</a></sup> Other heads are known, including the blunt head, which is flat at the end and is used for hunting small game or birds, and is designed to not pierce the target nor embed itself in trees or other objects and make recovery difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson25_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson25-35">[35]</a></sup> Another type of arrowhead is a barbed head, usually used in warfare or hunting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson18_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson18-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowstrings">Bowstrings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bowstrings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bowstring" title="Bowstring">Bowstring</a></div>
<p>Bowstrings may have a nocking point marked on them, which serves to mark where the arrow is fitted to the bowstring before shooting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson80_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson80-42">[42]</a></sup> The area around the nocking point is usually bound with thread to protect the area around the nocking point from wear by the archer's hands. This section is called the serving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson93_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson93-43">[43]</a></sup> At one end of the bowstring a loop is formed, which is permanent. The other end of the bowstring also has a loop, but this is not permanently formed into the bowstring but is constructed by tying a knot into the string to form a loop. Traditionally this knot is known as the archer's knot, but is a form of the <a href="/wiki/Timber_hitch" title="Timber hitch">timber hitch</a>. The knot can be adjusted to lengthen or shorten the bowstring. The adjustable loop is known as the "tail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath27_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heath27-44">[44]</a></sup> The string is often twisted (this being called the "flemish twist").
</p><p>Bowstrings have been constructed of many materials throughout history, including fibres such as <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Other materials used were animal <a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">guts</a>, animal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinews" title="Sinews">sinews</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rawhide_(material)" title="Rawhide (material)">rawhide</a>. Modern fibres such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dacron" title="Dacron">Dacron</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a> are now used in commercial bowstring construction, as well as steel wires in some compound bows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson28_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson28-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Compound_bow" title="Compound bow">Compound bows</a> have a mechanical system of pulley cams over which the bowstring is wound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson93_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson93-43">[43]</a></sup> Nylon is useful only in emergency situations, as it stretches too much.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_bow">Types of bow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types of bow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no single accepted system of classification of bows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson37_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson37-48">[48]</a></sup> Bows may be described by various characteristics including the materials used, the length of the draw that they permit, the shape of the bow in sideways view, and the shape of the limb in cross-section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath14_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heath14-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commonly-used descriptors for bows include:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_side_profile">By side profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: By side profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Recurve_bow" title="Recurve bow">Recurve bow</a>: a bow with the tips curving away from the archer. The curves straighten out as the bow is drawn and the return of the tip to its curved state after release of the arrow adds extra velocity to the arrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson90_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson90-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflex_bow" title="Reflex bow">Reflex bow</a>: a bow whose entire limbs curve away from the archer when unstrung. The curves are opposite to the direction in which the bow flexes while drawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson90_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson90-51">[51]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_material">By material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: By material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Self_bow" title="Self bow">Self bow</a>: a bow made from one piece of wood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson93_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson93-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Composite_bow" title="Composite bow">Composite bow</a>: a bow made of more than one material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heath14_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heath14-49">[49]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_cross-section_of_limb">By cross-section of limb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: By cross-section of limb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Longbow" title="Longbow">Longbow</a>: a self bow with limbs rounded in cross-section, about the same height as the archer so as to allow a full draw, usually over 1.5 m (5 feet) long. The traditional <a href="/wiki/English_longbow" title="English longbow">English longbow</a> was usually made of <a href="/wiki/Yew" title="Yew">yew</a> wood, but other woods are also used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson73_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson73-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flatbow" title="Flatbow">Flatbow</a>: the limbs are approximately rectangular in cross-section. This was traditional in many <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> societies and was found to be the most efficient shape for bow limbs by American engineers in the 20th century<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_characteristics">Other characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_and_arrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Takedown_bow" title="Takedown bow">Takedown bow</a>: a bow that can be disassembled for transportation, usually consisting of three parts: two limbs and a riser, in addition to the string.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Compound_bow" title="Compound bow">Compound bow</a>: a bow with <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_amplifier" title="Mechanical amplifier">mechanical amplifiers</a> to aid with drawing the bowstring. Usually, these amplifiers are asymmetric <a href="/wiki/Pulley" title="Pulley">pulleys</a> called <i>cams</i> (though they are not actually <a href="/wiki/Cam" title="Cam">cams</a>) at the ends of the limbs, which provide a <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_advantage" title="Mechanical advantage">mechanical advantage</a> (known as the <i>let-off</i>) while holding the bow in full draw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson38-40_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson38-40-53">[53]</a></sup> Such bows typically have high draw weights and are usually drawn with a <a href="/wiki/Release_aid" title="Release aid">release aid</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Trigger_(firearms)" title="Trigger (firearms)">trigger</a> mechanism for a consistently clean release.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">Crossbow</a>: a bow mounted horizontally on a frame similar to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_(firearm)" title="Stock (firearm)">firearm stock</a>, which has a locking mechanism for holding the bowstring at full draw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson41_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson41-54">[54]</a></sup> Crossbows typically shoot arrow-like <a href="/wiki/Dart_(missile)" title="Dart (missile)">darts</a> called <a href="/wiki/Crossbow_bolt" title="Crossbow bolt">bolts</a> or "quarrels", rather than normal arrows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson26_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson26-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Footbow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Footbow (page does not exist)">Footbow</a>: a bow meant to be used with the legs and arms while lying down and used in the current distance record for the furthest arrow shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li></ul>
