<p><b>Particle physics</b> or <b>high energy physics</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">fundamental particles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">forces</a> that constitute <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>. The fundamental particles in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> are classified in the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> as <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a> (matter particles) and <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a> (force-carrying particles). There are three <a href="/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics)" title="Generation (particle physics)">generations</a> of fermions,  but ordinary matter is made only from the first fermion generation. The first generation consists of <a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark">up</a> and <a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark">down quarks</a> which form  <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">electron neutrinos</a>. The three fundamental interactions known to be mediated by bosons are <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>.
</p><p>Quarks cannot exist on their own but form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadrons" title="Hadrons">hadrons</a>.  Hadrons that contain an odd number of quarks are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryons" title="Baryons">baryons</a> and those that contain an even number are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesons" title="Mesons">mesons</a>. Two baryons, the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>, make up most of the mass of ordinary matter. Mesons are unstable and the longest-lived last for only a few hundredths of a <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">microsecond</a>. They occur after collisions between particles made of quarks, such as fast-moving protons and neutrons in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. Mesons are also produced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclotrons" title="Cyclotrons">cyclotrons</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>.
</p><p>Particles have corresponding  <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticles</a> with the same <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> but with opposite <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charges</a>. For example, the antiparticle of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> (also known as an antielectron). The electron has a negative electric charge, the positron has a positive charge. These antiparticles can theoretically form a corresponding form of matter called <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a>. Some particles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>, are their own antiparticle.
</p><p>These <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a> are excitations of the <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)#Quantum_fields" title="Field (physics)">quantum fields</a> that also govern their interactions. The dominant theory explaining these fundamental particles and fields, along with their dynamics, is called the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">reconciliation of gravity</a> to the current particle physics theory is not solved; many theories have addressed this problem, such as <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">loop quantum gravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry theory</a>.
</p><p>Practical particle physics is the study of these particles in <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive</a> processes and in particle accelerators such as the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a>. Theoretical particle physics is the study of these particles in the context of <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a>. The two are closely interrelated: the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a> was postulated by theoretical particle physicists and its presence confirmed by practical experiments.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_subatomic_physics" title="History of subatomic physics">History of subatomic physics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rutherford_Scattering.svg"><img alt="see caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="369" data-file-width="588" decoding="async" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Rutherford_Scattering.svg/220px-Rutherford_Scattering.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Rutherford_Scattering.svg/330px-Rutherford_Scattering.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Rutherford_Scattering.svg/440px-Rutherford_Scattering.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rutherford_Scattering.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93Marsden_experiments" title="Geiger–Marsden experiments">Geiger–Marsden experiments</a> observed that a small fraction of the alpha particles experienced strong deflection when being struck by the gold foil.</div></div></div>
<p>The idea that all <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> is fundamentally composed of <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a> dates from at least the 6th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a>, through his work on <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a>, concluded that each element of nature was composed of a single, unique type of particle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MARK_I._GROSSMAN_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MARK_I._GROSSMAN-2">[2]</a></sup> The word <i><a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a></i>, after the Greek word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CF%84%CE%BF%CE%BC%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:ἄτομος">atomos</a></i> meaning "indivisible", has since then denoted the smallest particle of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a>, but physicists soon discovered that atoms are not, in fact, the fundamental particles of nature, but are conglomerates of even smaller particles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. The early 20th century explorations of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a> led to proofs of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> in 1939 by <a href="/wiki/Lise_Meitner" title="Lise Meitner">Lise Meitner</a> (based on experiments by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bethe" title="Hans Bethe">Hans Bethe</a> in that same year; both discoveries also led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>.
</p><p>Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, a bewildering variety of particles were found in collisions of particles from beams of increasingly high energy. It was referred to informally as the "<a href="/wiki/Particle_zoo" title="Particle zoo">particle zoo</a>". Important discoveries such as the <a href="/wiki/CP_violation" title="CP violation">CP violation</a> by <a href="/wiki/James_Cronin" title="James Cronin">James Cronin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Val_Fitch" title="Val Fitch">Val Fitch</a> brought new questions to <a href="/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry" title="Baryon asymmetry">matter-antimatter imbalance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  After the formulation of the Standard Model during the 1970s, physicists clarified the origin of the particle zoo. The large number of particles was explained as combinations of a (relatively) small number of more fundamental particles and framed in the context of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theories</a>. This reclassification marked the beginning of modern particle physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Model">Standard Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standard Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a></div>
<p>The current state of the classification of all elementary particles is explained by the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>, which gained widespread acceptance in the mid-1970s after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental_confirmation" title="Experimental confirmation">experimental confirmation</a> of the existence of <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>. It describes the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interactions</a>, using mediating <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">gauge bosons</a>. The species of gauge bosons are eight <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a>, <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> bosons</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_p_120_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker_p_120-6">[6]</a></sup> The Standard Model also contains 24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_particle" title="Fundamental particle">fundamental</a> <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a> (12 particles and their associated anti-particles), which are the constituents of all <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdg_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdg-7">[7]</a></sup> Finally, the Standard Model also predicted the existence of a type of <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">boson</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>. On 4 July 2012, physicists with the Large Hadron Collider at CERN announced they had found a new particle that behaves similarly to what is expected from the Higgs boson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Standard Model, as currently formulated, has 61 elementary particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-braibant_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-braibant-9">[9]</a></sup> Those elementary particles can combine to form composite particles, accounting for the hundreds of other species of particles that have been discovered since the 1960s. The Standard Model has been found to agree with almost all the <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimental</a> tests conducted to date. However, most particle physicists believe that it is an incomplete description of nature and that a more fundamental theory awaits discovery (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Everything" title="Theory of Everything">Theory of Everything</a>). In recent years, measurements of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">mass</a> have provided the first experimental deviations from the Standard Model, since neutrinos do not have mass in the Standard Model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Modern particle physics research is focused on <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">subatomic particles</a>, including atomic constituents, such as <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> (protons and neutrons are composite particles called <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a>, made of <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>), that are produced by <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a> processes; such particles are <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muons</a>, as well as a wide range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exotic_particle" title="Exotic particle">exotic particles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> All particles and their interactions observed to date can be described almost entirely by the Standard Model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_p_120_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baker_p_120-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dynamics of particles are also governed by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>; they exhibit <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave–particle duality">wave–particle duality</a>, displaying particle-like behaviour under certain experimental conditions and <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a>-like behaviour in others. In more technical terms, they are described by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_state" title="Quantum state">quantum state</a> vectors in a <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert space</a>, which is also treated in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>. Following the convention of particle physicists, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a></i> is applied to those particles that are, according to current understanding, presumed to be indivisible and not composed of other particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-braibant_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-braibant-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quarks_and_leptons">Quarks and leptons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Quarks and leptons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">Quark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lepton" title="Lepton">Lepton</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beta_Negative_Decay.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Beta_Negative_Decay.svg/220px-Beta_Negative_Decay.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Beta_Negative_Decay.svg/330px-Beta_Negative_Decay.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Beta_Negative_Decay.svg/440px-Beta_Negative_Decay.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beta_Negative_Decay.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Feynman_diagram" title="Feynman diagram">Feynman diagram</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>β<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> decay</a>, showing a neutron (n, udd) converted into a proton (p, udu). "u" and "d" are the <a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark">up</a> and <a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark">down quarks</a>, "<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>" is the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>, and "<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">ν</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">e</sub></span></span>" is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_Antineutrino" title="Electron Antineutrino">electron antineutrino</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Ordinary <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> is made from first-<a href="/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics)" title="Generation (particle physics)">generation</a> quarks (<a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark">up</a>, <a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark">down</a>) and leptons (<a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">electron neutrino</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Povh02_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Povh02-12">[12]</a></sup> Collectively, quarks and leptons are called <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>, because they have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_spin" title="Quantum spin">quantum spin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Half-integer" title="Half-integer">half-integers</a> (-1/2, 1/2, 3/2, etc.). This cause the fermions to obey the <a href="/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle" title="Pauli exclusion principle">Pauli exclusion principle</a>, where no two particles may occupy the same <a href="/wiki/Quantum_state" title="Quantum state">quantum state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Quarks have fractional <a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">elementary electric charge</a> (-1/3 or 2/3)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and leptons have whole-numbered electric charge (0 or 1).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Quarks also have <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">color charge</a>, which is labeled arbitrarily with no correlation to actual light <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a> as red, green and blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R._Nave_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-R._Nave-15">[15]</a></sup> Because the interactions between the quarks stores energy which can convert to other particles when the quarks are far apart enough, quarks cannot be observed independently. This is called <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">color confinement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R._Nave_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-R._Nave-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are three known generations of quarks (up and down, <a href="/wiki/Strange_quark" title="Strange quark">strange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charm_quark" title="Charm quark">charm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Top_quark" title="Top quark">top</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bottom_quark" title="Bottom quark">bottom</a>) and leptons (electron and its neutrino, <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muon_neutrino" title="Muon neutrino">its neutrino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tau_(particle)" title="Tau (particle)">tau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tau_neutrino" title="Tau neutrino">its neutrino</a>), with strong indirect evidence that the fourth generation of fermions doesn't exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bosons">Bosons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bosons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">Boson</a></div>
<p>Bosons are the <a href="/wiki/Force_carrier" title="Force carrier">mediators or carriers</a> of fundamental interactions, such as <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DarkMatter_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-DarkMatter-17">[17]</a></sup> Electromagnetism is mediated by the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quanta</a> of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 29–30">: 29–30 </span></sup> The weak interaction is mediated by the <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons">W and Z bosons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The strong interaction is mediated by the <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluon</a>, which can link quarks together to form composite particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HyperPhysics_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-HyperPhysics-20">[20]</a></sup> Due to the aforementioned color confinement, gluons are never observed independently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a> gives mass to the W and Z bosons via the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDG_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDG-22">[22]</a></sup> – the gluon and photon are expected to be <a href="/wiki/Massless_particle" title="Massless particle">massless</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup> All bosons have an integer quantum spin (0 and 1) and can have the same <a href="/wiki/Quantum_state" title="Quantum state">quantum state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DarkMatter_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-DarkMatter-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiparticles_and_color_charge">Antiparticles and color charge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Antiparticles and color charge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">Antiparticle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">Color charge</a></div>
<p>Most aforementioned particles have corresponding <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticles</a>, which compose <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a>. Normal particles have positive <a href="/wiki/Lepton_number" title="Lepton number">lepton</a> or <a href="/wiki/Baryon_number" title="Baryon number">baryon number</a>, and antiparticles have these numbers negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Most properties of corresponding antiparticles and particles are the same, with a few gets reversed; the electron's antiparticle, positron, has an opposite charge. To differentiate between antiparticles and particles, a plus or negative sign is added in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superscript" title="Superscript">superscript</a>. For example, the electron and the positron are denoted <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raith_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-raith-24">[24]</a></sup> When a particle and an antiparticle interacts with each other, they are <a href="/wiki/Annihilation" title="Annihilation">annihilated</a> and convert to other particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Some particles have no antiparticles, such as the photon or gluon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Quarks and gluons additionally have color charges, which influences the strong interaction. Quark's color charges are called red, green and blue (though the particle itself have no physical color), and in antiquarks are called antired, antigreen and antiblue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R._Nave_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-R._Nave-15">[15]</a></sup> The gluon can have <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">eight color charges</a>, which are the result of quarks' interactions to form composite particles (gauge symmetry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SU(3)" title="SU(3)">SU(3)</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PeskinSchroeder_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-PeskinSchroeder-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composite">Composite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Composite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_particle" title="Composite particle">Composite particle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quark_structure_proton.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Quark_structure_proton.svg/220px-Quark_structure_proton.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Quark_structure_proton.svg/330px-Quark_structure_proton.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Quark_structure_proton.svg/440px-Quark_structure_proton.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quark_structure_proton.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> consists of two up quarks and one down quark, linked together by <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a>. The quarks' color charge are also visible.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nuclei</a> are <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a> – the neutron is composed of two down quarks and one up quark, and the proton is composed of two up quarks and one down quark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knowing2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knowing2-27">[27]</a></sup> A baryon is composed of three quarks, and a <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">meson</a> is composed of two quarks (one normal, one anti). Baryons and mesons are collectively called <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>. Quarks inside hadrons are governed by the strong interaction, thus are subjected to <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a> (color charges). The <a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bounded</a> quarks must have their color charge to be neutral, or "white" for analogy with <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">mixing the primary colors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> More <a href="/wiki/Exotic_hadron" title="Exotic hadron">exotic hadrons</a> can have other types, arrangement or number of quarks (<a href="/wiki/Tetraquark" title="Tetraquark">tetraquark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentaquark" title="Pentaquark">pentaquark</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>A normal atom is made from protons, neutrons and electrons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By modifying the particles inside a normal atom, <a href="/wiki/Exotic_atom" title="Exotic atom">exotic atoms</a> can be formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A simple example would be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-4.1" title="Hydrogen-4.1">hydrogen-4.1</a>, which has one of its electrons replaced with a muon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypothetical">Hypothetical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hypothetical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">Graviton</a> is a hypothetical particle that can mediate the gravitational interaction, but it has not been detected nor completely reconciled with current theories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_laboratories">Experimental laboratories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Experimental laboratories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg/220px-02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg/330px-02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg/440px-02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:02_Fermilab_-_Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory_-_American_particle_accelerator_Fermilab_near_Chicago_Illinois.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory, USA</div></div></div>
<p>The world's major particle physics laboratories are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). Its main facility is the <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_Heavy_Ion_Collider" title="Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider">Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider</a> (RHIC), which collides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_nuclear_collisions" title="Relativistic nuclear collisions">heavy ions</a> such as gold ions and polarized protons. It is the world's first heavy ion collider, and the world's only polarized proton collider.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Budker_Institute_of_Nuclear_Physics" title="Budker Institute of Nuclear Physics">Budker Institute of Nuclear Physics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Novosibirsk" title="Novosibirsk">Novosibirsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>). Its main projects are now the electron-positron <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">colliders</a> <a href="/wiki/VEPP-2000" title="VEPP-2000">VEPP-2000</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> operated since 2006, and VEPP-4,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> started experiments in 1994. Earlier facilities include the first electron–electron beam–beam <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">collider</a> VEP-1, which conducted experiments from 1964 to 1968; the electron-positron <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">colliders</a> VEPP-2, operated from 1965 to 1974; and, its successor VEPP-2M,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> performed experiments from 1974 to 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> (European Organization for Nuclear Research) (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">Franco</a>-<a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> border, near <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>). Its main project is now the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (LHC), which had its first beam circulation on 10 September 2008, and is now the world's most energetic collider of protons. It also became the most energetic collider of heavy ions after it began colliding lead ions. Earlier facilities include the <a href="/wiki/Large_Electron%E2%80%93Positron_Collider" title="Large Electron–Positron Collider">Large Electron–Positron Collider</a> (LEP), which was stopped on 2 November 2000 and then dismantled to give way for LHC; and the <a href="/wiki/Super_Proton_Synchrotron" title="Super Proton Synchrotron">Super Proton Synchrotron</a>, which is being reused as a pre-accelerator for the LHC and for fixed-target experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DESY" title="DESY">DESY</a> (Deutsches Elektronen-Synchrotron) (<a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>). Its main facility was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadron_Elektron_Ring_Anlage" title="Hadron Elektron Ring Anlage">Hadron Elektron Ring Anlage</a> (HERA), which collided electrons and positrons with protons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The accelerator complex is now focused on the production of synchrotron radiation with PETRA III, FLASH and the <a href="/wiki/European_XFEL" title="European XFEL">European XFEL</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab)</a> (<a href="/wiki/Batavia,_Illinois" title="Batavia, Illinois">Batavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). Its main facility until 2011 was the <a href="/wiki/Tevatron" title="Tevatron">Tevatron</a>, which collided protons and antiprotons and was the highest-energy particle collider on earth until the Large Hadron Collider surpassed it on 29 November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Institute_of_High_Energy_Physics" title="Institute of High Energy Physics">Institute of High Energy Physics</a> (IHEP) (<a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>). IHEP manages a number of China's major particle physics facilities, including the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Electron%E2%80%93Positron_Collider_II" title="Beijing Electron–Positron Collider II">Beijing Electron–Positron Collider II</a>(BEPC II), the Beijing Spectrometer (BES), the Beijing Synchrotron Radiation Facility (BSRF), the International Cosmic-Ray Observatory at Yangbajing in Tibet, the <a href="/wiki/Daya_Bay_Reactor_Neutrino_Experiment" title="Daya Bay Reactor Neutrino Experiment">Daya Bay Reactor Neutrino Experiment</a>, the <a href="/wiki/China_Spallation_Neutron_Source" title="China Spallation Neutron Source">China Spallation Neutron Source</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hard_X-ray_Modulation_Telescope" title="Hard X-ray Modulation Telescope">Hard X-ray Modulation Telescope</a> (HXMT), and the Accelerator-driven Sub-critical System (ADS) as well as the <a href="/wiki/Jiangmen_Underground_Neutrino_Observatory" title="Jiangmen Underground Neutrino Observatory">Jiangmen Underground Neutrino Observatory</a> (JUNO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/KEK" title="KEK">KEK</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsukuba,_Ibaraki" title="Tsukuba, Ibaraki">Tsukuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>). It is the home of a number of experiments such as the <a href="/wiki/K2K_experiment" title="K2K experiment">K2K experiment</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_oscillation" title="Neutrino oscillation">neutrino oscillation</a> experiment and <a href="/wiki/Belle_II_experiment" title="Belle II experiment">Belle II</a>, an experiment measuring the <a href="/wiki/CP_violation" title="CP violation">CP violation</a> of <a href="/wiki/B_meson" title="B meson">B mesons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory" title="SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory">SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California" title="Menlo Park, California">Menlo Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). Its 2-mile-long linear particle accelerator began operating in 1962 and was the basis for numerous <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> collision experiments until 2008. Since then the linear accelerator is being used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linac_Coherent_Light_Source" title="Linac Coherent Light Source">Linac Coherent Light Source</a> <a href="/wiki/X-ray_laser" title="X-ray laser">X-ray laser</a> as well as advanced accelerator design research. SLAC staff continue to participate in developing and building many <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">particle detectors</a> around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li></ul>

</tr><tr><td class="sidebar-navbar"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1063604349" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="navbar plainlinks hlist navbar-mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Quantum_field_theory" title="Template:Quantum field theory"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Quantum_field_theory" title="Template talk:Quantum field theory"><abbr title="Discuss this template">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Quantum_field_theory&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Theoretical particle physics attempts to develop the models, theoretical framework, and mathematical tools to understand current experiments and make predictions for future experiments (see also <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a>). There are several major interrelated efforts being made in theoretical particle physics today.
</p><p>One important branch attempts to better understand the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> and its tests. Theorists make quantitative predictions of observables at <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">collider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astroparticle_physics" title="Astroparticle physics">astronomical</a> experiments, which along with experimental measurements is used to extract the parameters of the Standard Model with less uncertainty. This work probes the limits of the Standard Model and therefore expands scientific understanding of nature's building blocks. Those efforts are made challenging by the difficulty of calculating high precision quantities in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a>. Some theorists working in this area use the tools of perturbative <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">effective field theory</a>, referring to themselves as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_physics_phenomenology" title="Particle physics phenomenology">phenomenologists</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Others make use of <a href="/wiki/Lattice_field_theory" title="Lattice field theory">lattice field theory</a> and call themselves <i>lattice theorists</i>.
</p><p>Another major effort is in model building where model builders develop ideas for what physics may lie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Beyond the Standard Model">beyond the Standard Model</a> (at higher energies or smaller distances). This work is often motivated by the <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_problem" title="Hierarchy problem">hierarchy problem</a> and is constrained by existing experimental data.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It may involve work on <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry</a>, alternatives to the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a>, extra spatial dimensions (such as the <a href="/wiki/Randall%E2%80%93Sundrum_model" title="Randall–Sundrum model">Randall–Sundrum models</a>), <a href="/wiki/Preon" title="Preon">Preon</a> theory, combinations of these, or other ideas.
</p><p>A third major effort in theoretical particle physics is <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a>. <i>String theorists</i> attempt to construct a unified description of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> by building a theory based on small strings, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branes" title="Branes">branes</a> rather than particles. If the theory is successful, it may be considered a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Everything" title="Theory of Everything">Theory of Everything</a>", or "TOE".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also other areas of work in theoretical particle physics ranging from <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics_in_cosmology" title="Particle physics in cosmology">particle cosmology</a> to <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">loop quantum gravity</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_applications">Practical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Practical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In principle, all physics (and practical applications developed therefrom) can be derived from the study of fundamental particles. In practice, even if "particle physics" is taken to mean only "high-energy atom smashers", many technologies have been developed during these pioneering investigations that later find wide uses in society. Particle accelerators are used to produce <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_in_medicine" title="Isotopes in medicine">medical isotopes</a> for research and treatment (for example, isotopes used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PET_imaging" title="PET imaging">PET imaging</a>), or used directly in <a href="/wiki/External_beam_radiotherapy" title="External beam radiotherapy">external beam radiotherapy</a>. The development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductors</a> has been pushed forward by their use in particle physics. The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a> technology were initially developed at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>. Additional applications are found in medicine, national security, industry, computing, science, and workforce development, illustrating a long and growing list of beneficial practical applications with contributions from particle physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary goal, which is pursued in several distinct ways, is to find and understand what physics may lie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyond_the_standard_model" title="Beyond the standard model">beyond the standard model</a>. There are several powerful experimental reasons to expect new physics, including <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrino_mass" title="Neutrino mass">neutrino mass</a>. There are also theoretical hints that this new physics should be found at accessible energy scales.
</p><p>Much of the effort to find this new physics are focused on new collider experiments. The <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (LHC) was completed in 2008 to help continue the search for the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersymmetric_particle" title="Supersymmetric particle">supersymmetric particles</a>, and other new physics. An intermediate goal is the construction of the <a href="/wiki/International_Linear_Collider" title="International Linear Collider">International Linear Collider</a> (ILC), which will complement the LHC by allowing more precise measurements of the properties of newly found particles. In August 2004, a decision for the technology of the ILC was taken but the site has still to be agreed upon.
</p><p>There are important non-collider experiments that attempt to find and understand <a href="/wiki/Physics_beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Physics beyond the Standard Model">physics beyond the Standard Model</a>. One is the determination of the <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> masses, since these masses may arise from neutrinos mixing with very heavy particles. Another is <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> observations that provide constraints on the dark matter, although it may be impossible to determine the exact nature of the dark matter without the colliders. Finally, lower bounds on the very long <a href="/wiki/Proton_decay" title="Proton decay">lifetime of the proton</a> put constraints on <a href="/wiki/Grand_Unified_Theory" title="Grand Unified Theory">Grand Unified Theories</a> at energy scales much higher than collider experiments will be able to probe any time soon.
</p><p>In May 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Particle_Physics_Project_Prioritization_Panel" title="Particle Physics Project Prioritization Panel">Particle Physics Project Prioritization Panel</a> released its report on particle physics funding priorities for the United States over the next decade. This report emphasized continued U.S. participation in the LHC and ILC, and expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_Underground_Neutrino_Experiment" title="Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment">Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment</a>, among other recommendations.
</p>
