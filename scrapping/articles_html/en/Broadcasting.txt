<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Broadcasting</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Distribution of audio or audiovisual content to dispersed audiences</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Broadcast" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Broadcast_(disambiguation)" title="Broadcast (disambiguation)">Broadcast (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Broadcasting1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Broadcasting1.JPG/220px-Broadcasting1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Broadcasting1.JPG/330px-Broadcasting1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Broadcasting1.JPG/440px-Broadcasting1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Broadcasting1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A broadcasting antenna in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Broadcasting</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distribution_(business)" title="Distribution (business)">distribution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">audio</a> or video content to a dispersed <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">audience</a> via any electronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium_(communication)" title="Medium (communication)">mass communications medium</a>, but typically one using the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a>), in a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/one-to-many" title="wikt:one-to-many">one-to-many</a> model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peters_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-peters-1">[1]</a></sup> Broadcasting began with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_radio" title="AM radio">AM radio</a>, which came into popular use around 1920 with the spread of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tube</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_transmitter" title="Radio transmitter">radio transmitters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">receivers</a>.  Before this, all forms of electronic communication (early <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a>) were <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/one-to-one" title="wikt:one-to-one">one-to-one</a>, with the message intended for a single recipient.  The term <i>broadcasting</i> evolved from its use as the agricultural method of sowing seeds in a field by casting them broadly about.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-douglas_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-douglas-2">[2]</a></sup> It was later adopted for describing the widespread distribution of information by printed materials<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or by telegraph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Examples applying it to "one-to-many" radio transmissions of an individual station to multiple listeners appeared as early as 1898.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the air broadcasting is usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">television</a>, though more recently, both radio and television transmissions have begun to be distributed by cable (<a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a>). The receiving parties may include the general public or a relatively small subset; the point is that anyone with the appropriate receiving technology and equipment (e.g., a radio or television set) can receive the signal. The field of broadcasting includes both government-managed services such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_radio" title="Public radio">public radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_radio" title="Community radio">community radio</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_television" title="Public television">public television</a>, and private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_radio" title="Commercial radio">commercial radio</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_television" title="Commercial television">commercial television</a>. The U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, title 47, part 97 defines "broadcasting" as "transmissions intended for reception by the general public, either direct or relayed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Private or two-way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a> transmissions do not qualify under this definition. For example, <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur</a> ("ham") and <a href="/wiki/Citizens_band_radio" title="Citizens band radio">citizens band</a> (CB) radio operators are not allowed to broadcast. As defined, "transmitting" and "broadcasting" are not the same.
</p><p>Transmission of radio and television programs from a radio or television station to home receivers by <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> is referred to as "over the air" (OTA) or <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">terrestrial</a> broadcasting and in most countries requires a <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_license" title="Broadcast license">broadcasting license</a>.  Transmissions using a wire or cable, like <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> (which also retransmits OTA stations with their <a href="/wiki/Retransmission_consent" title="Retransmission consent">consent</a>), are also considered broadcasts but do not necessarily require a license (though in some countries, a license is required).  In the 2000s, transmissions of television and radio programs via <a href="/wiki/Streaming_media" title="Streaming media">streaming</a> digital technology have increasingly been referred to as broadcasting as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadcasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_broadcasting" title="History of broadcasting">History of broadcasting</a></div>
<p>The earliest broadcasting consisted of sending telegraph signals over the airwaves, using <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a>, a system developed in the 1830s by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Morse" title="Samuel Morse">Samuel Morse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Henry" title="Joseph Henry">Joseph Henry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Vail" title="Alfred Vail">Alfred Vail</a>. They developed an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">electrical telegraph</a> system which sent pulses of <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> along wires which controlled an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnet</a> that was located at the receiving end of the telegraph system. A code was needed to transmit natural language using only these pulses, and the silence between them. Morse therefore developed the forerunner to modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Morse_code" title="International Morse code">International Morse code</a>. This was particularly important for ship-to-ship and ship-to-shore communication, but it became increasingly important for business and general news reporting, and as an arena for personal communication by radio amateurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-douglas_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-douglas-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1894, Italian inventor <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Marconi" title="Guglielmo Marconi">Guglielmo Marconi</a> began developing a wireless communication using the then-newly discovered phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a>, showing by 1901 that they could be transmitted across the Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This was the start of <a href="/wiki/Wireless_telegraphy" title="Wireless telegraphy">wireless telegraphy</a> by radio. Audio radio broadcasting began experimentally in the first decade of the 20th century. On 17 December 1902, a transmission from the Marconi station in <a href="/wiki/Glace_Bay" title="Glace Bay">Glace Bay</a>, Nova Scotia, Canada, became the world's first radio message to cross the Atlantic from North America. In 1904, a commercial service was established to transmit nightly news summaries to subscribing ships, which incorporated them into their onboard newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> accelerated the development of radio for <a href="/wiki/Military_communications" title="Military communications">military communications</a>. After the war, commercial radio <a href="/wiki/AM_broadcasting" title="AM broadcasting">AM broadcasting</a> began in the 1920s and became an important mass medium for entertainment and news. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> again accelerated the development of radio for the wartime purposes of aircraft and land communication, radio navigation, and radar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Development of stereo <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM broadcasting</a> of radio began in the 1930s in the United States and the 1970s in the United Kingdom, displacing AM as the dominant commercial standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 25 March 1925, <a href="/wiki/John_Logie_Baird" title="John Logie Baird">John Logie Baird</a> demonstrated the transmission of moving pictures at the London department store <a href="/wiki/Selfridges" title="Selfridges">Selfridges</a>. Baird's device relied upon the <a href="/wiki/Nipkow_disk" title="Nipkow disk">Nipkow disk</a> and thus became known as the <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_television" title="Mechanical television">mechanical television</a>. It formed the basis of experimental broadcasts done by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="British Broadcasting Corporation">British Broadcasting Corporation</a> beginning on 30 September 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, for most of the 20th century, televisions depended on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a> invented by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ferdinand_Braun" title="Karl Ferdinand Braun">Karl Braun</a>. The first version of such a television to show promise was produced by <a href="/wiki/Philo_Farnsworth" title="Philo Farnsworth">Philo Farnsworth</a> and demonstrated to his family on 7 September 1927.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, interrupted experiments resumed and television became an important home entertainment broadcast medium, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">VHF</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UHF" title="UHF">UHF</a> spectrum. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_broadcasting" title="Satellite broadcasting">Satellite broadcasting</a> was initiated in the 1960s and moved into general industry usage in the 1970s, with DBS (Direct Broadcast Satellites) emerging in the 1980s.
</p><p>Originally all broadcasting was composed of <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">analog signals</a> using <a href="/wiki/Analog_transmission" title="Analog transmission">analog transmission</a> techniques but in the 2000s, broadcasters <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_switchover" title="Digital switchover">switched</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_signal_(broadcasting)" title="Digital signal (broadcasting)">digital signals</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_transmission" title="Digital transmission">digital transmission</a>. An analog signal is any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_signal" title="Continuous signal">continuous signal</a> representing some other quantity, i.e., <i>analogous</i> to another quantity. For example, in an analog <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal" title="Audio signal">audio signal</a>, the instantaneous signal <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> varies continuously with the <a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure" title="Sound pressure">pressure of the sound waves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In contrast, a <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal" title="Digital signal">digital signal</a> represents the original time-varying quantity as a <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(signal_processing)" title="Sampling (signal processing)">sampled</a> sequence of <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(signal_processing)" title="Quantization (signal processing)">quantized</a> values which imposes some <a href="/wiki/Nyquist_frequency" title="Nyquist frequency">bandwidth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a> constraints on the representation. In general usage, broadcasting most frequently refers to the transmission of information and entertainment programming from various sources to the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_audio" title="Analog audio">Analog audio</a> radio (AM, FM) vs. <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_radio_service" title="Digital audio radio service">Digital audio radio</a> (<a href="/wiki/HD_Radio" title="HD Radio">HD Radio</a>), <a href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Broadcasting" title="Digital Audio Broadcasting">Digital Audio Broadcasting</a> (DAB), <a href="/wiki/Satellite_radio" title="Satellite radio">Satellite radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_Radio_Mondiale" title="Digital Radio Mondiale">Digital Radio Mondiale</a> (DRM)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Analog_television" title="Analog television">Analog television</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Digital_television" title="Digital television">Digital television</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wireless" title="Wireless">Wireless</a></li></ul>
<p>The world's technological capacity to receive information through one-way broadcast networks more than quadrupled during the two decades from 1986 to 2007, from 432 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exabytes" title="Exabytes">exabytes</a> of (optimally compressed) information, to 1.9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zettabytes" title="Zettabytes">zettabytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertLopez2011_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-16">[16]</a></sup> This is the information equivalent of 55 newspapers per person per day in 1986, and 175 newspapers per person per day by 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hilbertvideo2011_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hilbertvideo2011-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadcasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a broadcast system, the central high-powered <a href="/wiki/Radio_masts_and_towers" title="Radio masts and towers">broadcast tower</a> transmits a high-frequency <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_wave" title="Electromagnetic wave">electromagnetic wave</a> to numerous low-powered receivers. The high-frequency wave sent by the tower is modulated with a signal containing visual or audio information. The receiver is then <a href="/wiki/Antenna_tuner" title="Antenna tuner">tuned</a> so as to pick up the high-frequency wave and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demodulator" title="Demodulator">demodulator</a> is used to retrieve the signal containing the visual or audio information. The broadcast signal can be either analog (signal is varied continuously with respect to the information) or digital (information is encoded as a set of discrete values).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stallings-intro_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-stallings-intro-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, there have been several methods used for broadcasting <a href="/wiki/Electronic_media" title="Electronic media">electronic media</a> audio and video to the general public:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">Telephone</a> broadcasting (1881–1932): the earliest form of electronic broadcasting (not counting data services offered by stock <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">telegraph companies</a> from 1867, if <a href="/wiki/Ticker_tape" title="Ticker tape">ticker-tapes</a> are excluded from the definition). Telephone broadcasting began with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2trophone" title="Théâtrophone">Théâtrophone</a> ("Theatre Phone") systems, which were telephone-based distribution systems allowing subscribers to listen to live <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a> performances over telephone lines, created by French inventor <a href="/wiki/Cl%C3%A9ment_Ader" title="Clément Ader">Clément Ader</a> in 1881. Telephone broadcasting also grew to include <a href="/wiki/Telephone_newspaper" title="Telephone newspaper">telephone newspaper</a> services for news and entertainment <a href="/wiki/Radio_programming" title="Radio programming">programming</a> which were introduced in the 1890s, primarily located in large <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> cities. These telephone-based <a href="/wiki/Subscription_business_model" title="Subscription business model">subscription services</a> were the first examples of electrical/electronic broadcasting and offered a wide variety of programming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">Radio broadcasting</a> (experimentally from 1906, commercially from 1920); <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">audio</a> signals sent through the air as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a>, picked up by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_antenna" title="Radio antenna">antenna</a> and sent to a <a href="/wiki/Tuner_(radio)" title="Tuner (radio)">receiver</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_stations" title="Radio stations">Radio stations</a> can be linked in <a href="/wiki/Radio_network" title="Radio network">radio networks</a> to broadcast common <a href="/wiki/Radio_program" title="Radio program">radio programs</a>, either in <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_syndication" title="Broadcast syndication">broadcast syndication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simulcast" title="Simulcast">simulcast</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subchannel" title="Subchannel">subchannels</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">Television</a> broadcasting (telecast), experimentally from 1925, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_television" title="Commercial television">commercially</a> from the 1930s: an extension of radio to include <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> signals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cable_radio" title="Cable radio">Cable radio</a> (also called "cable FM", from 1928) and <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> (from 1932): both via <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a>,  originally serving principally as transmission media for programming produced at either radio or <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television stations</a>, but later expanding into a broad universe of cable-originated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cable_channel" title="Cable channel">channels</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite" title="Direct-broadcast satellite">Direct-broadcast satellite</a> (DBS) (from c. 1974) and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_radio" title="Satellite radio">satellite radio</a> (from c. 1990): meant for direct-to-home broadcast programming (as opposed to studio network uplinks and down-links), provides a mix of traditional radio or television broadcast programming, or both, with dedicated satellite radio programming. (See also: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">Satellite television</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Webcast#Origins" title="Webcast">Webcasting</a> of video/television (from c. 1993) and audio/radio (from c. 1994) streams: offers a mix of traditional radio and television station broadcast programming with dedicated <a href="/wiki/Internet_radio" title="Internet radio">Internet radio</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_television" title="Internet television">Internet television</a>.</li></ul>

<p>There are several means of providing financial support for continuous broadcasting:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Commercial_broadcasting" title="Commercial broadcasting">Commercial broadcasting</a>: for-profit, usually privately owned stations, channels, networks, or services providing programming to the public, supported by the sale of air time to advertisers for <a href="/wiki/Radio_advertisement" title="Radio advertisement">radio</a> or <a href="/wiki/Television_advertisement" title="Television advertisement">television advertisements</a> during or in breaks between programs, often in combination with cable or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pay_cable" title="Pay cable">pay cable</a> subscription fees.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Public_broadcasting" title="Public broadcasting">Public broadcasting</a>: usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit" title="Non-profit">non-profit</a>, publicly owned stations or networks supported by license fees, government funds, grants from foundations, corporate <a href="/wiki/Underwriting" title="Underwriting">underwriting</a>, audience memberships, contributions or a combination of these.</li>
<li>Community broadcasting: a form of <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a> in which a <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television station</a>, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio station</a>, is owned, operated or <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_programming" title="Broadcast programming">programmed</a>, by a community group to provide programs of local interest known as <a href="/wiki/Local_programming" title="Local programming">local programming</a>. Community stations are most commonly operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_group" title="Non-profit group">non-profit groups</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooperatives" title="Cooperatives">cooperatives</a>; however, in some cases they may be operated by a local <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> or <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cable_company" title="Cable company">cable company</a> or a municipal government.</li>
<li>Internet Webcast: the audience pays to recharge and buy virtual gifts for the anchor, and the platform converts the gifts into virtual currency. The anchor withdraws the virtual currency, which is drawn by the platform. If the anchor belongs to a trade union, it will be settled by the trade union and the live broadcasting platform, and the anchor will get the salary and part of the bonus. This is the most common profit model of live broadcast products.</li></ul>
<p>Broadcasters may rely on a combination of these <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">business models</a>. For example, in the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a> (NPR) and the <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">Public Broadcasting Service</a> (PBS, television)  supplement public membership subscriptions and grants with funding from the <a href="/wiki/Corporation_for_Public_Broadcasting" title="Corporation for Public Broadcasting">Corporation for Public Broadcasting</a> (CPB), which is allocated bi-annually by Congress. US public broadcasting corporate and charitable grants are generally given in consideration of <a href="/wiki/Underwriting_spot" title="Underwriting spot">underwriting spots</a> which differ from commercial advertisements in that they are governed by specific <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a> restrictions, which prohibit the advocacy of a product or a "call to action".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recorded_and_live_forms">Recorded and live forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadcasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recorded and live forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Broadcasting&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p><span class="anchor" id="Recorded_broadcasts_and_live_broadcasts"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG/220px-TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG/330px-TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG/440px-TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TV_Station_Control_Room.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Television_studio" title="Television studio">television studio</a> <a href="/wiki/Production_control_room" title="Production control room">production control room</a> in <a href="/wiki/Olympia,_Washington" title="Olympia, Washington">Olympia, Washington</a>, August 2008</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg/220px-On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg/330px-On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg/440px-On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:On_Air_DSC0004_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An "On Air" sign is illuminated, usually in red, while a broadcast or recording session is taking place.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="965" data-file-width="1448" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg/220px-Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg/330px-Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg/440px-Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radio_Maria_Studio_in_Adliswil.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Radio_Maria" title="Radio Maria">Radio Maria</a> studio in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The first regular television broadcasts started in 1937. Broadcasts can be classified as "recorded" or "live". The former allows correcting errors, and removing superfluous or undesired material, rearranging it, applying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slow-motion" title="Slow-motion">slow-motion</a> and repetitions, and other techniques to enhance the program. However, some live events like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports_television" title="Sports television">sports television</a> can include some of the aspects including slow-motion clips of important goals/hits, etc., in between the <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">live television</a> telecast. American radio-network broadcasters habitually forbade prerecorded broadcasts in the 1930s and 1940s, requiring radio programs played for the Eastern and Central <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">time zones</a> to be repeated three hours later for the Pacific time zone (See: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_time_on_North_American_broadcasting" title="Effects of time on North American broadcasting">Effects of time on North American broadcasting</a>). This restriction was dropped for special occasions, as in the case of the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirigible" title="Dirigible">dirigible</a> airship <i><a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">Hindenburg</a></i> disaster at <a href="/wiki/Lakehurst,_New_Jersey" title="Lakehurst, New Jersey">Lakehurst, New Jersey</a>, in 1937. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, prerecorded broadcasts from war correspondents were allowed on U.S. radio. In addition, American radio programs were recorded for playback by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Radio" title="Armed Forces Radio">Armed Forces Radio</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio stations</a> around the world.
</p><p>A disadvantage of recording first is that the public may learn the outcome of an event before the recording is broadcast, which may be a "<a href="/wiki/Spoiler_(media)" title="Spoiler (media)">spoiler</a>". Prerecording may be used to prevent <a href="/wiki/Announcer" title="Announcer">announcers</a> from deviating from an officially approved <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">script</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Live_radio" title="Live radio">live radio</a> broadcast, as occurred with <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> broadcasts from Germany in the 1940s and with <a href="/wiki/Voice_of_Russia" title="Voice of Russia">Radio Moscow</a> in the 1980s. Many events are advertised as being live, although they are often "recorded live" (sometimes called "<a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">live</a>-to-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_tape" title="Video tape">tape</a>"). This is particularly true of performances of musical artists on radio when they visit for an in-studio <a href="/wiki/Concert" title="Concert">concert</a> performance. Similar situations have occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_production" title="Television production">television production</a> ("<i><a href="/wiki/The_Cosby_Show" title="The Cosby Show">The Cosby Show</a></i> is recorded in front of a <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">live television</a> studio <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">audience</a>") and <a href="/wiki/News_broadcasting" title="News broadcasting">news broadcasting</a>.
</p><p>A broadcast may be distributed through several physical means. If coming directly from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_studio" title="Radio studio">radio studio</a> at a single station or <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television station</a>, it is simply sent through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio/transmitter_link" title="Studio/transmitter link">studio/transmitter link</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> and hence from the <a href="/wiki/Television_antenna" title="Television antenna">television antenna</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Radio_masts_and_towers" title="Radio masts and towers">radio masts and towers</a> out to the world. Programming may also come through a <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellite</a>, played either live or recorded for later transmission. Networks of stations may <a href="/wiki/Simulcast" title="Simulcast">simulcast</a> the same programming at the same time, originally via <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> link, now usually by satellite. Distribution to stations or networks may also be through physical media, such as <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> (CD), <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, and sometimes other formats. Usually these are included in another broadcast, such as when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_news_gathering" title="Electronic news gathering">electronic news gathering</a> (ENG) returns a story to the station for inclusion on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/News_program" title="News program">news programme</a>.
</p><p>The final leg of broadcast distribution is how the signal gets to the listener or viewer. It may come over the air as with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio station</a> or <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television station</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">radio receiver</a>, or may come through <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Cable_radio" title="Cable radio">cable radio</a> (or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_cable" title="Wireless cable">wireless cable</a>") via the station or directly from a network. The <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> may also bring either <a href="/wiki/Internet_radio" title="Internet radio">internet radio</a> or <a href="/wiki/Streaming_media" title="Streaming media">streaming media</a> television to the recipient, especially with <a href="/wiki/Multicast" title="Multicast">multicasting</a> allowing the signal and <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> to be shared. The term "<a href="/wiki/Broadcast_network" title="Broadcast network">broadcast network</a>" is often used to distinguish networks that broadcast an over-the-air television signals that can be received using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuner_(television)" title="Tuner (television)">tuner (television)</a> inside a <a href="/wiki/Television_set" title="Television set">television set</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Television_antenna" title="Television antenna">television antenna</a> from so-called networks that are broadcast only via <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cablecast" title="Cablecast">cablecast</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite television</a> that uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dish_antenna" title="Dish antenna">dish antenna</a>. The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_television" title="Broadcast television">broadcast television</a>" can refer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programs</a> of such networks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_impact">Social impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadcasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Social impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WTUL_Microphone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/WTUL_Microphone.jpg/220px-WTUL_Microphone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/WTUL_Microphone.jpg/330px-WTUL_Microphone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/WTUL_Microphone.jpg/440px-WTUL_Microphone.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WTUL_Microphone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">Radio station</a> <a href="/wiki/WTUL" title="WTUL">WTUL</a> studio, <a href="/wiki/Tulane_University" title="Tulane University">Tulane University</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a></div></div></div>
<p>The sequencing of content in a broadcast is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduling_(broadcasting)" title="Scheduling (broadcasting)">schedule</a>. As with all technological endeavors, a number of technical terms and <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a> have developed. A list of these terms can be found at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_broadcasting_terms" title="List of broadcasting terms">List of broadcasting terms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">Television</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> programs are distributed through radio broadcasting or <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a>, often both simultaneously. By coding signals and having a <a href="/wiki/Cable_converter_box" title="Cable converter box">cable converter box</a> with <a href="/wiki/Code_(cryptography)" title="Code (cryptography)">decoding</a> equipment in <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">homes</a>, the latter also enables <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscription" title="Subscription">subscription</a>-based channels, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pay-tv" title="Pay-tv">pay-tv</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pay-per-view" title="Pay-per-view">pay-per-view</a> services. In his essay, <a href="/wiki/John_Durham_Peters" title="John Durham Peters">John Durham Peters</a> wrote that <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a> is a tool used for dissemination. Peters stated, "<a href="/wiki/Dissemination" title="Dissemination">Dissemination</a> is a lens—sometimes a usefully distorting one—that helps us tackle basic issues such as interaction, presence, and space and time ... on the agenda of any future <a href="/wiki/Communication_theory" title="Communication theory">communication theory</a> in general".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpeters_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpeters-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 211">: 211 </span></sup> Dissemination focuses on the message being relayed from one main source to one large <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">audience</a> without the exchange of <a href="/wiki/Dialogue" title="Dialogue">dialogue</a> in between. It is possible for the message to be <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">changed or corrupted by government officials</a> once the main source releases it. There is no way to predetermine how the larger population or audience will absorb the message. They can choose to listen, analyze, or simply ignore it. Dissemination in communication is widely used in the world of broadcasting.
</p><p>Broadcasting focuses on getting a message out and it is up to the general public to do what they wish with it. Peters also states that broadcasting is used to address an open-ended destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpeters_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-jpeters-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 212">: 212 </span></sup> There are many forms of broadcasting, but they all aim to distribute a signal that will reach the target <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">audience</a>. Broadcasters typically arrange audiences into entire assemblies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpeters_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-jpeters-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 213">: 213 </span></sup> In terms of media broadcasting, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_show" title="Radio show">radio show</a> can gather a large number of followers who tune in every day to specifically listen to that specific <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a>. The disc jockey follows the script for his or her radio show and just talks into the <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpeters_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-jpeters-22">[22]</a></sup> He or she does not expect immediate feedback from any listeners. The message is broadcast across airwaves throughout the community, but there the listeners cannot always respond immediately, especially since many radio shows are recorded prior to the actual air time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcast_engineering">Broadcast engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadcasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Broadcast engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_engineering" title="Broadcast engineering">Broadcast engineering</a></div>
<p>Broadcast engineering is the field of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, and now to some extent <a href="/wiki/Computer_engineering" title="Computer engineering">computer engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, which deals with <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> broadcasting.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_engineering" title="Audio engineering">Audio engineering</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RF_engineering" title="RF engineering">RF engineering</a> are also essential parts of broadcast engineering, being their own <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subsets</a> of electrical engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Broadcast engineering involves both the <a href="/wiki/Television_studio" title="Television studio">studio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> aspects (the entire <a href="/wiki/Airchain" title="Airchain">airchain</a>), as well as <a href="/wiki/Remote_broadcast" title="Remote broadcast">remote broadcasts</a>.  Every <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_network" title="Broadcast network">station</a> has a broadcast <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>, though one may now serve an entire station group in a city. In small <a href="/wiki/Media_market" title="Media market">media markets</a> the engineer may work on a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> basis for one or more stations as needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
