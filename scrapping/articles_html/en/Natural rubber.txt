<p><b>Rubber</b>, also called <b>India rubber</b>, <b>latex</b>, <b>Amazonian rubber</b>, <i><b>caucho</b></i>, or <i><b>caoutchouc</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup> as initially produced, consists of <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> of the organic compound <a href="/wiki/Isoprene" title="Isoprene">isoprene</a>, with minor impurities of other organic compounds. <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> are three of the leading rubber producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Types of <a href="/wiki/Polyisoprene" title="Polyisoprene">polyisoprene</a> that are used as natural rubbers are classified as <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomers</a>.
</p><p>Currently, rubber is harvested mainly in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Hevea_brasiliensis" title="Hevea brasiliensis">rubber tree</a> (<i>Hevea brasiliensis</i>) or others. The latex is a sticky, milky and white colloid drawn off by making incisions in the bark and collecting the fluid in vessels in a process called "tapping". The latex then is refined into the rubber that is ready for commercial processing.  In major areas, latex is allowed to coagulate in the collection cup. The coagulated lumps are collected and processed into dry forms for sale.
</p><p>Natural rubber is used extensively in many applications and products, either alone or in combination with other materials. In most of its useful forms, it has a large stretch ratio and high resilience and also is water-proof.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Industrial demand for rubber-like materials began to outstrip natural rubber supplies by the end of the 19th century, leading to the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">synthetic rubber</a> in 1909 by chemical means.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="Amazonian_rubber_tree_.28Hevea_brasiliensis.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Amazonian_rubber_tree_(Hevea_brasiliensis)">Amazonian rubber tree (<i>Hevea brasiliensis</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amazonian rubber tree (Hevea brasiliensis)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The major commercial source of natural rubber latex is the Amazonian rubber tree (<i><a href="/wiki/Hevea_brasiliensis" title="Hevea brasiliensis">Hevea brasiliensis</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup> a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spurge" title="Spurge">spurge</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a></i>. This species is preferred because it grows well under cultivation. A properly managed tree responds to wounding by producing more latex for several years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Congo_rubber_.28Landolphia_owariensis_and_L._spp..29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Congo_rubber_(Landolphia_owariensis_and_L._spp.)">Congo rubber (<i>Landolphia owariensis</i> and <i>L. spp.</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Congo rubber (Landolphia owariensis and L. spp.)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo_rubber" title="Congo rubber">Congo rubber</a>, formerly a major source of rubber, came from vines in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Landolphia" title="Landolphia">Landolphia</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Landolphia_kirkii" title="Landolphia kirkii">L. kirkii</a></i>, <i>L. heudelotis</i>, and <i>L. owariensis</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dandelion">Dandelion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dandelion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dandelion" title="Dandelion">Dandelion</a> milk contains latex. The latex exhibits the same quality as the natural rubber from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber_trees" title="Rubber trees">rubber trees</a>. In the wild types of dandelion, latex content is low and varies greatly. In <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, research projects tried to use dandelions as a base for rubber production, but failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heim2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heim2002-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2013, by inhibiting one key enzyme and using modern cultivation methods and optimization techniques, scientists in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Institute_for_Molecular_Biology_and_Applied_Ecology" title="Fraunhofer Institute for Molecular Biology and Applied Ecology">Fraunhofer Institute for Molecular Biology and Applied Ecology</a> (IME) in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> developed a cultivar of the Russian dandelion (<i><a href="/wiki/Taraxacum_kok-saghyz" title="Taraxacum kok-saghyz">Taraxacum kok-saghyz</a></i>) that is suitable for commercial production of natural rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily-7">[7]</a></sup> In collaboration with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_Tire" title="Continental Tire">Continental Tires</a>, IME began a pilot facility.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many other plants produce forms of latex rich in isoprene polymers, though not all produce usable forms of polymer as easily as the Pará.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some of them require more elaborate processing to produce anything like usable rubber, and most are more difficult to tap. Some produce other desirable materials, for example <a href="/wiki/Gutta-percha" title="Gutta-percha">gutta-percha</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Palaquium_gutta" title="Palaquium gutta">Palaquium gutta</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Chicle" title="Chicle">chicle</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Manilkara" title="Manilkara">Manilkara</a></i> species. Others that have been commercially exploited, or at least showed promise as rubber sources, include the rubber fig (<i><a href="/wiki/Ficus_elastica" title="Ficus elastica">Ficus elastica</a></i>), Panama rubber tree (<i><a href="/wiki/Castilla_elastica" title="Castilla elastica">Castilla elastica</a></i>), various spurges (<i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbia" title="Euphorbia">Euphorbia</a></i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Lactuca" title="Lactuca">Lactuca</a></i> species), the related <i><a href="/wiki/Scorzonera_tau-saghyz" title="Scorzonera tau-saghyz">Scorzonera tau-saghyz</a></i>, various <i><a href="/wiki/Taraxacum" title="Taraxacum">Taraxacum</a></i> species, including common dandelion (<i><a href="/wiki/Taraxacum_officinale" title="Taraxacum officinale">Taraxacum officinale</a></i>) and Russian dandelion, and, perhaps most importantly for its hypoallergenic properties, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guayule" title="Guayule">guayule</a> (<i>Parthenium argentatum</i>). The term <b>gum rubber</b> is sometimes applied to the tree-obtained version of natural rubber in order to distinguish it from the synthetic version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber_boom" title="Rubber boom">Rubber boom</a></div>
<p>The first use of rubber was by the indigenous cultures of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>. The earliest archeological evidence of the use of natural latex from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hevea_tree" title="Hevea tree"><i>Hevea</i> tree</a> comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olmec" title="Olmec">Olmec</a> culture, in which rubber was first used for making balls for the <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_ballgame" title="Mesoamerican ballgame">Mesoamerican ballgame</a>. Rubber was later used by the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztec</a> cultures – in addition to making balls Aztecs used rubber for other purposes such as making containers and to make textiles waterproof by impregnating them with the latex sap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Marie_de_La_Condamine" title="Charles Marie de La Condamine">Charles Marie de La Condamine</a> is credited with introducing samples of rubber to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Royale_des_Sciences" title="Académie Royale des Sciences">Académie Royale des Sciences</a></i> of France in 1736.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-b-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1751, he presented a paper by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Fresneau" title="François Fresneau">François Fresneau</a> to the Académie (published in 1755) that described many of rubber's properties. This has been referred to as the first scientific paper on rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-b-13">[13]</a></sup> In England, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a>, in 1770, observed that a piece of the material was extremely good for rubbing off <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">pencil</a> marks on paper, hence the name "rubber". It slowly made its way around England. In 1764, François Fresnau discovered that <a href="/wiki/Turpentine" title="Turpentine">turpentine</a> was a rubber <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>. <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Fabbroni" title="Giovanni Fabbroni">Giovanni Fabbroni</a> is credited with the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a> as a rubber solvent in 1779.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Goodyear" title="Charles Goodyear">Charles Goodyear</a> redeveloped <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_vulcanization" title="Sulfur vulcanization">vulcanization</a> in 1839, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoamerican" title="Mesoamerican">Mesoamericans</a> had used stabilized rubber for balls and other objects as early as 1600 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slack2002_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slack2002-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>South America remained the main source of latex rubber used during much of the 19th century. The rubber trade was heavily controlled by business interests but no laws expressly prohibited the export of seeds or plants. In 1876, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wickham_(explorer)" title="Henry Wickham (explorer)">Henry Wickham</a> smuggled 70,000 Amazonian rubber tree seeds from Brazil and delivered them to <a href="/wiki/Kew_Gardens" title="Kew Gardens">Kew Gardens</a>, England. Only 2,400 of these germinated. Seedlings were then sent to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Ceylon" title="British Ceylon">British Ceylon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>), <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_Malaya" title="British Malaya">British Malaya</a>. Malaya (now <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Malaysia" title="Peninsular Malaysia">Peninsular Malaysia</a>) was later to become the biggest producer of rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1900s, the <a href="/wiki/Congo_Free_State" title="Congo Free State">Congo Free State</a> in Africa was also a significant source of natural rubber latex, mostly gathered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_labor" title="Forced labor">forced labor</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> King Leopold II's colonial state brutally enforced production quotas. Tactics to enforce the rubber quotas included removing the hands of victims to prove they had been killed. Soldiers often came back from raids with baskets full of chopped-off hands. Villages that resisted were razed to encourage better compliance locally.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033199720">.mw-parser-output div.crossreference{padding-left:0}</style><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable crossreference" role="note">(See <a href="/wiki/Atrocities_in_the_Congo_Free_State" title="Atrocities in the Congo Free State">Atrocities in the Congo Free State</a> for more information on the rubber trade in the Congo Free State in the late 1800s and early 1900s.)</span>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, commercial cultivation was introduced by British planters, although the experimental efforts to grow rubber on a commercial scale were initiated as early as 1873 at the <a href="/wiki/Acharya_Jagadish_Chandra_Bose_Indian_Botanic_Garden" title="Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Indian Botanic Garden">Calcutta Botanical Garden</a>. The first commercial <i>Hevea</i> plantations were established at Thattekadu in <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in 1902. In later years the plantation expanded to <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands" title="Andaman and Nicobar Islands">Andaman and Nicobar Islands</a> of India. Today, India is the world's 3rd largest producer and 4th largest consumer of rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Singapore and Malaya, commercial production was heavily promoted by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Nicholas_Ridley" title="Henry Nicholas Ridley">Sir Henry Nicholas Ridley</a>, who served as the first Scientific Director of the <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Botanic_Gardens" title="Singapore Botanic Gardens">Singapore Botanic Gardens</a> from 1888 to 1911. He distributed rubber seeds to many planters and developed the first technique for tapping trees for latex without causing serious harm to the tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Because of his fervent promotion of this crop, he is popularly remembered by the nickname "Mad Ridley".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Pre.E2.80.93World_War_II"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre–World_War_II">Pre–World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pre–World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before World War II significant uses included door and window profiles, hoses, belts, gaskets, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber_mat" title="Rubber mat">matting</a>, flooring, and dampeners (antivibration mounts) for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive" title="Automotive">automotive</a> industry. The use of rubber in car <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tires</a> (initially solid rather than pneumatic) in particular consumed a significant amount of rubber.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloves" title="Gloves">Gloves</a> (medical, household, and industrial) and toy <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloons</a> were large consumers of rubber, although the type of rubber used is concentrated latex. Significant tonnage of rubber was used as <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a> in many manufacturing industries and products, although the two most noticeable were the paper and the carpet industries. Rubber was commonly used to make <a href="/wiki/Rubber_band" title="Rubber band">rubber bands</a> and pencil <a href="/wiki/Eraser" title="Eraser">erasers</a>.
</p><p>Rubber produced as a fiber, sometimes called 'elastic', had significant value to the textile industry because of its excellent elongation and recovery properties. For these purposes, manufactured rubber fiber was made as either an extruded round fiber or rectangular fibers cut into strips from extruded film. Because of its low dye acceptance, feel and appearance, the rubber fiber was either covered by yarn of another fiber or directly woven with other yarns into the fabric. Rubber yarns were used in foundation garments. While rubber is still used in textile manufacturing, its low tenacity limits its use in lightweight garments because latex lacks resistance to oxidizing agents and is damaged by aging, sunlight, oil and perspiration. The textile industry turned to <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a> (polymer of <a href="/wiki/Chloroprene" title="Chloroprene">chloroprene</a>), a type of synthetic rubber, as well as another more commonly used elastomer fiber, <a href="/wiki/Spandex" title="Spandex">spandex</a> (also known as elastane), because of their superiority to rubber in both strength and durability.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="501" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg/220px-Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg/330px-Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg/440px-Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Latex_being_collected_from_a_tapped_rubber_tree.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rubber latex</div></div></div>
<p>Rubber exhibits unique physical and chemical properties. Rubber's stress–strain behavior exhibits the <a href="/wiki/Mullins_effect" title="Mullins effect">Mullins effect</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Payne_effect" title="Payne effect">Payne effect</a> and is often modeled as <a href="/wiki/Hyperelastic_material" title="Hyperelastic material">hyperelastic</a>. Rubber <a href="/wiki/Strain_crystallization" title="Strain crystallization">strain crystallizes</a>. Because there are weakened <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allyl" title="Allyl">allylic</a> C-H bonds in each <a href="/wiki/Repeat_unit" title="Repeat unit">repeat unit</a>, natural rubber is susceptible to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcanisation" title="Vulcanisation">vulcanisation</a> as well as being sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Ozone_cracking" title="Ozone cracking">ozone cracking</a>. The two main <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> for rubber are <a href="/wiki/Turpentine" title="Turpentine">turpentine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a> (petroleum). Because rubber does not dissolve easily, the material is finely divided by shredding prior to its immersion. An <a href="/wiki/Ammonia_solution" title="Ammonia solution">ammonia solution</a> can be used to prevent the <a href="/wiki/Flocculation" title="Flocculation">coagulation</a> of raw latex. Rubber begins to melt at approximately 180 °C (356 °F).
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Koh_Chang,_Thailand,_Rubber_tapping,_Latex.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2688" data-file-width="4032" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Koh_Chang%2C_Thailand%2C_Rubber_tapping%2C_Latex.jpg/220px-Koh_Chang%2C_Thailand%2C_Rubber_tapping%2C_Latex.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Koh_Chang%2C_Thailand%2C_Rubber_tapping%2C_Latex.jpg/330px-Koh_Chang%2C_Thailand%2C_Rubber_tapping%2C_Latex.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Koh_Chang%2C_Thailand%2C_Rubber_tapping%2C_Latex.jpg/440px-Koh_Chang%2C_Thailand%2C_Rubber_tapping%2C_Latex.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Koh_Chang,_Thailand,_Rubber_tapping,_Latex.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rubber latex elasticity</div></div></div>
<p>On a microscopic scale, relaxed rubber is a disorganized cluster of erratically changing wrinkled chains. In stretched rubber, the chains are almost linear. The restoring force is due to the preponderance of wrinkled conformations over more linear ones. For the quantitative treatment see <a href="/wiki/Ideal_chain" title="Ideal chain">ideal chain</a>, for more examples see <a href="/wiki/Entropic_force" title="Entropic force">entropic force</a>.
</p><p>Cooling below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_transition_temperature" title="Glass transition temperature">glass transition temperature</a> permits local conformational changes but a reordering is practically impossible because of the larger <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">energy barrier</a> for the concerted movement of longer chains. "Frozen" rubber's elasticity is low and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strain_(materials_science)" title="Strain (materials science)">strain</a> results from small changes of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bond</a> lengths and angles: this caused the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Challenger_disaster" title="Challenger disaster"><i>Challenger</i> disaster</a>, when the American <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>'s flattened <a href="/wiki/O-ring" title="O-ring">o-rings</a> failed to relax to fill a widening gap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The glass transition is fast and reversible: the force resumes on heating.
</p><p>The parallel chains of stretched rubber are susceptible to crystallization. This takes some time because turns of twisted chains have to move out of the way of the growing <a href="/wiki/Crystallite" title="Crystallite">crystallites</a>. Crystallization has occurred, for example, when, after days, an inflated toy balloon is found withered at a relatively large remaining volume. Where it is touched, it shrinks because the temperature of the hand is enough to melt the crystals.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">Vulcanization</a> of rubber creates <a href="/wiki/Disulfide" title="Disulfide">di-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polysulfide" title="Polysulfide">polysulfide</a> bonds between chains, which limits the <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics)" title="Degrees of freedom (mechanics)">degrees of freedom</a> and results in chains that tighten more quickly for a given strain, thereby increasing the elastic force constant and making the rubber harder and less extensible.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malodour">Malodour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Malodour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raw rubber storage depots and rubber processing can produce malodour that is serious enough to become a source of complaints and protest to those living in the vicinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Microbial impurities originate during the processing of block rubber. These impurities break down during storage or thermal degradation and produce volatile organic compounds. Examination of these compounds using <a href="/wiki/Gas_chromatography" title="Gas chromatography">gas chromatography</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> (GC/MS) and gas chromatography (GC) indicates that they contain sulfur, ammonia, <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, nitrogen, and low-molecular-weight fatty acids (C2–C5).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aidic.it_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-aidic.it-23">[23]</a></sup> When latex concentrate is produced from rubber, sulfuric acid is used for coagulation. This produces malodourous hydrogen sulfide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aidic.it_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-aidic.it-23">[23]</a></sup> The industry can mitigate these bad odours with <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubber systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aidic.it_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-aidic.it-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_makeup">Chemical makeup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chemical makeup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RubberSyn%26Natural.png"><img alt="(1)trans-1,4-polyisoprene is called gutta-percha. (2)in natural rubber various chains are held together by weak Van Der Waal's interactions and has a coiled structure.so it can be stretched like a spring and exhibits elastic properties" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="619" data-file-width="2064" decoding="async" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/RubberSyn%26Natural.png/330px-RubberSyn%26Natural.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/RubberSyn%26Natural.png/495px-RubberSyn%26Natural.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/RubberSyn%26Natural.png/660px-RubberSyn%26Natural.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RubberSyn%26Natural.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chemical structure of cis-polyisoprene, the main constituent of natural rubber. Synthetic cis-polyisoprene and natural cis-polyisoprene are derived from distinct precursors, <a href="/wiki/Isopentenyl_pyrophosphate" title="Isopentenyl pyrophosphate">isopentenyl pyrophosphate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isoprene" title="Isoprene">isoprene</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Rubber is the polymer cis-1,4-polyisoprene – with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight">molecular weight</a> of 100,000 to 1,000,000 <a href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">daltons</a>. Typically, a small percentage (up to 5% of dry mass) of other materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resins</a>, and inorganic materials (salts) are found in natural rubber. Polyisoprene can also be created synthetically, producing what is sometimes referred to as "synthetic natural rubber", but the synthetic and natural routes are distinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10">[10]</a></sup> Some natural rubber sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Gutta-percha" title="Gutta-percha">gutta-percha</a>, are composed of trans-1,4-polyisoprene, a <a href="/wiki/Structural_isomer" title="Structural isomer">structural isomer</a> that has similar properties. Natural rubber is an elastomer and a <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a>. Once the rubber is vulcanized, it is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoset" title="Thermoset">thermoset</a>. Most rubber in everyday use is vulcanized to a point where it shares properties of both; i.e., if it is heated and cooled, it is degraded but not destroyed. The final properties of a rubber item depend not just on the polymer, but also on modifiers and fillers, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Factice" title="Factice">factice</a>, whiting and others.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Biosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rubber particles are formed in the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> of specialized latex-producing cells called <a href="/wiki/Laticifer" title="Laticifer">laticifers</a> within rubber plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Rubber particles are surrounded by a single <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipid</a> membrane with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> tails pointed inward. The membrane allows biosynthetic proteins to be sequestered at the surface of the growing rubber particle, which allows new monomeric units to be added from outside the biomembrane, but within the lacticifer. The rubber particle is an enzymatically active entity that contains three layers of material, the rubber particle, a biomembrane and free monomeric units. The biomembrane is held tightly to the rubber core by the high negative charge along the double bonds of the rubber polymer backbone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Free monomeric units and conjugated proteins make up the outer layer. The rubber precursor is <a href="/wiki/Isopentenyl_pyrophosphate" title="Isopentenyl pyrophosphate">isopentenyl pyrophosphate</a> (an <a href="/wiki/Allyl_group" title="Allyl group">allylic</a> compound), which elongates by Mg<sup>2+</sup>-dependent condensation by the action of rubber transferase. The monomer adds to the pyrophosphate end of the growing polymer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Citation removed as it was attributed to a predatory publisher. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  The process displaces the terminal high-energy pyrophosphate. The reaction produces a cis polymer. The initiation step is catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Prenyltransferase" title="Prenyltransferase">prenyltransferase</a>, which converts three monomers of isopentenyl pyrophosphate into <a href="/wiki/Farnesyl_pyrophosphate" title="Farnesyl pyrophosphate">farnesyl pyrophosphate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The farnesyl pyrophosphate can bind to rubber transferase to elongate a new rubber polymer.
</p><p>The required isopentenyl pyrophosphate is obtained from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mevalonate" title="Mevalonate">mevalonate</a> pathway, which derives from <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl-CoA</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a>. In plants, isoprene pyrophosphate can also be obtained from the 1-deox-D-xyulose-5-phosphate/2-C-methyl-D-erythritol-4-phosphate pathway within plasmids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The relative ratio of the farnesyl pyrophosphate initiator unit and isoprenyl pyrophosphate elongation monomer determines the rate of new particle synthesis versus elongation of existing particles. Though rubber is known to be produced by only one enzyme, extracts of latex host numerous small molecular weight proteins with unknown function. The proteins possibly serve as cofactors, as the synthetic rate decreases with complete removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rubber_trees_in_Kerala,_India.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Rubber_trees_in_Kerala%2C_India.jpg/220px-Rubber_trees_in_Kerala%2C_India.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Rubber_trees_in_Kerala%2C_India.jpg/330px-Rubber_trees_in_Kerala%2C_India.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Rubber_trees_in_Kerala%2C_India.jpg/440px-Rubber_trees_in_Kerala%2C_India.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rubber_trees_in_Kerala,_India.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rubber is generally cultivated in large plantations. The image shows a coconut shell used in collecting latex, in plantations in <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, India.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natural_rubber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6000" data-file-width="8000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Natural_rubber.jpg/220px-Natural_rubber.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Natural_rubber.jpg/330px-Natural_rubber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Natural_rubber.jpg/440px-Natural_rubber.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Natural_rubber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sheets of natural rubber</div></div></div>
<p>More than 28 million tons of rubber were produced in 2017, of which approximately 47% was natural. Since the bulk is synthetic, which is derived from petroleum, the price of natural rubber is determined, to a large extent, by the prevailing global price of crude oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Asia was the main source of natural rubber, accounting for about 94% of output in 2005. The three largest producers, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, Indonesia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and Malaysia, together account for around 72% of all natural rubber production. Natural rubber is not cultivated widely in its native continent of South America because of the South American leaf <a href="/wiki/Blight" title="Blight">blight</a>, and other natural predators, there.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rubber latex is extracted from rubber trees. The economic life period of rubber trees in plantations is around 32 years, up to 7 years of immature phase and about 25 years of productive phase.
</p><p>The soil requirement is well-drained, weathered soil consisting of <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">laterite</a>, lateritic types, sedimentary types, nonlateritic red or <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvial</a> soils.
</p><p>The climatic conditions for optimum growth of rubber trees are:
</p>
<ul><li>Rainfall of around 250 centimetres (98 in) evenly distributed without any marked dry season and with at least 100 rainy days per year</li>
<li>Temperature range of about 20 to 34 °C (68 to 93 °F), with a monthly mean of 25 to 28 °C (77 to 82 °F)</li>
<li>Atmospheric humidity of around 80%</li>
<li>About 2,000 hours sunshine per year at the rate of six hours per day throughout the year</li>
<li>Absence of strong winds</li></ul>
<p>Many high-yielding clones have been developed for commercial planting. These clones yield more than 2,000 kilograms per hectare (1,800 lb/acre) of dry rubber per year, under ideal conditions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rubber_tapping" title="Rubber tapping">Rubber tapping</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vintage_tobacco_card,_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree,_India,_Products_of_the_World_series,_Player%27s_Cigarettes,_1909.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1769" decoding="async" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vintage_tobacco_card%2C_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree%2C_India%2C_Products_of_the_World_series%2C_Player%27s_Cigarettes%2C_1909.jpg/170px-Vintage_tobacco_card%2C_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree%2C_India%2C_Products_of_the_World_series%2C_Player%27s_Cigarettes%2C_1909.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vintage_tobacco_card%2C_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree%2C_India%2C_Products_of_the_World_series%2C_Player%27s_Cigarettes%2C_1909.jpg/255px-Vintage_tobacco_card%2C_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree%2C_India%2C_Products_of_the_World_series%2C_Player%27s_Cigarettes%2C_1909.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vintage_tobacco_card%2C_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree%2C_India%2C_Products_of_the_World_series%2C_Player%27s_Cigarettes%2C_1909.jpg/340px-Vintage_tobacco_card%2C_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree%2C_India%2C_Products_of_the_World_series%2C_Player%27s_Cigarettes%2C_1909.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vintage_tobacco_card,_Tapping_a_Rubber_Tree,_India,_Products_of_the_World_series,_Player%27s_Cigarettes,_1909.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vintage tobacco card, Tapping a Rubber Tree, India, Products of the World series, Player's Cigarettes, 1909</div></div></div>
<p>In places such as Kerala and Sri Lanka, where coconuts are in abundance, the half shell of coconut was used as the latex collection container. Glazed pottery or aluminium or plastic cups became more common in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerala_India" title="Kerala India">Kerala-India</a> and other countries. The cups are supported by a wire that encircles the tree. This wire incorporates a spring so it can stretch as the tree grows. The latex is led into the cup by a <a href="/wiki/Galvanization" title="Galvanization">galvanised</a> "spout" knocked into the bark. <a href="/wiki/Rubber_tapping" title="Rubber tapping">Rubber tapping</a> normally takes place early in the morning, when the internal pressure of the tree is highest. A good tapper can tap a tree every 20 seconds on a standard half-spiral system, and a common daily "task" size is between 450 and 650 trees. Trees are usually tapped on alternate or third days, although many variations in timing, length and number of cuts are used. "Tappers would make a slash in the bark with a small hatchet. These slanting cuts allowed latex to flow from ducts located on the exterior or the inner layer of bark (<a href="/wiki/Cambium" title="Cambium">cambium</a>) of the tree. Since the cambium controls the growth of the tree, growth stops if it is cut. Thus, rubber tapping demanded accuracy, so that the incisions would not be too many given the size of the tree, or too deep, which could stunt its growth or kill it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceylon_rubber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="281" decoding="async" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Ceylon_rubber.jpg/170px-Ceylon_rubber.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Ceylon_rubber.jpg/255px-Ceylon_rubber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2b/Ceylon_rubber.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceylon_rubber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A woman in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> harvesting rubber, c. 1920</div></div></div>
<p>It is usual to tap a pannel at least twice, sometimes three times, during the tree's life. The economic life of the tree depends on how well the tapping is carried out, as the critical factor is bark consumption. A standard in Malaysia for alternate daily tapping is 25 cm (vertical) bark consumption per year. The latex-containing tubes in the bark ascend in a spiral to the right. For this reason, tapping cuts usually ascend to the left to cut more tubes. The trees drip latex for about four hours, stopping as latex coagulates naturally on the tapping cut, thus blocking the latex tubes in the bark. Tappers usually rest and have a meal after finishing their tapping work and then start collecting the liquid "field latex" at about midday.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Field_coagula">Field coagula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Field coagula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MixedFieldCoagula.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="372" data-file-width="550" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/MixedFieldCoagula.jpg/220px-MixedFieldCoagula.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/MixedFieldCoagula.jpg/330px-MixedFieldCoagula.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/MixedFieldCoagula.jpg/440px-MixedFieldCoagula.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MixedFieldCoagula.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mixed field coagula.</div></div></div>
<p>The four types of field coagula are "cuplump", "treelace", "smallholders' lump", and "earth scrap". Each has significantly different properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Some trees continue to drip after the collection leading to a small amount of "cup lump" that is collected at the next tapping. The latex that coagulates on the cut is also collected as "tree lace". Tree lace and cup lump together account for 10%–20% of the dry rubber produced. Latex that drips onto the ground, "earth scrap", is also collected periodically for processing of low-grade product.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Cup_lump">Cup lump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cup lump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MyanmarRubber1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2481" data-file-width="4243" decoding="async" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/MyanmarRubber1.jpg/220px-MyanmarRubber1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/MyanmarRubber1.jpg/330px-MyanmarRubber1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/MyanmarRubber1.jpg/440px-MyanmarRubber1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MyanmarRubber1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cup lump rubber coagula in a <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> road stall.</div></div></div>
<p>Cup lump is the coagulated material found in the collection cup when the tapper next visits the tree to tap it again. It arises from latex clinging to the walls of the cup after the latex was last poured into the bucket, and from late-dripping latex exuded before the latex-carrying vessels of the tree become blocked. It is of higher purity and of greater value than the other three types.
</p><p>'Cup lumps' can also be used to describe a completely different type of coagulate that has collected in smallholder plantations over a period of 1–2 weeks. After tapping all of the trees, the tapper will return to each tree and stir in some type of acid, which allows the newly harvested latex to mix with the previously coagulated material. The rubber/acid mixture is what gives rubber plantations, markets, and factories a strong odor.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree_lace">Tree lace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Tree lace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Tree lace is the coagulum strip that the tapper peels off the previous cut before making a new cut. It usually has higher copper and manganese contents than cup lump. Both copper and manganese are pro-oxidants and can damage the physical properties of the dry rubber.
</p>
<h5><span id="Smallholders.27_lump"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Smallholders'_lump">Smallholders' lump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Smallholders' lump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Smallholders' lump is produced by smallholders, who collect rubber from trees far from the nearest factory. Many Indonesian smallholders, who farm paddies in remote areas, tap dispersed trees on their way to work in the paddy fields and collect the latex (or the coagulated latex) on their way home. As it is often impossible to preserve the latex sufficiently to get it to a factory that processes latex in time for it to be used to make high quality products, and as the latex would anyway have coagulated by the time it reached the factory, the smallholder will coagulate it by any means available, in any container available. Some smallholders use small containers, buckets etc., but often the latex is coagulated in holes in the ground, which are usually lined with plastic sheeting. Acidic materials and fermented fruit juices are used to coagulate the latex — a form of assisted biological coagulation. Little care is taken to exclude twigs, leaves, and even bark from the lumps that are formed, which may also include tree lace.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_scrap">Earth scrap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Earth scrap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Earth scrap is material that gathers around the base of the tree. It arises from latex overflowing from the cut and running down the bark, from rain flooding a collection cup containing latex, and from spillage from tappers' buckets during collection. It contains soil and other contaminants, and has variable rubber content, depending on the amount of contaminants. Earth scrap is collected by field workers two or three times a year and may be cleaned in a scrap-washer to recover the rubber, or sold to a contractor who cleans it and recovers the rubber. It is of low quality.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing">Processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="362" data-file-width="550" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg/220px-RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg/330px-RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg/440px-RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RemovingCoagulumFromCoagulatingTroughs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Removing coagulum from coagulating troughs.</div></div></div>
<p>Latex coagulates in the cups, if kept for long and must be collected before this happens. The collected latex, "field latex", is transferred into coagulation tanks for the preparation of dry rubber or transferred into air-tight containers with sieving for ammoniation. Ammoniation, invented by patent lawyer and vice-president of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Rubber_Company" title="United States Rubber Company">United States Rubber Company</a> Ernest Hopkinson around 1920, preserves the latex in a colloidal state for longer periods of time. Latex is generally processed into either latex concentrate for manufacture of dipped goods or coagulated under controlled, clean conditions using formic acid. The coagulated latex can then be processed into the higher-grade, technically specified block rubbers such as SVR 3L or SVR CV or used to produce Ribbed Smoke Sheet grades. Naturally coagulated rubber (cup lump) is used in the manufacture of TSR10 and TSR20 grade rubbers. Processing for these grades is a size reduction and cleaning process to remove contamination and prepare the material for the final stage of drying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The dried material is then baled and palletized for storage and shipment.
</p>

<p>Natural rubber is often vulcanized – a process by which the rubber is heated and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxide</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol" title="Bisphenol">bisphenol</a> are added to improve resistance and <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elasticity</a> and to prevent it from perishing. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">Carbon black</a> is often used as an additive to rubber to improve its strength, especially in vehicle tires, which account for about 70% (~9 million tons) of carbon black production.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Natural rubber latex is shipped from factories in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a> to destinations around the world. As the cost of natural rubber has risen significantly and rubber products are dense, the shipping methods offering the lowest cost per unit weight are preferred. Depending on destination, warehouse availability, and transportation conditions, some methods are preferred by certain buyers. In international trade, latex rubber is mostly shipped in 20-foot ocean containers. Inside the container, smaller containers are used to store the latex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rubber_shortage">Rubber shortage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Rubber shortage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is growing concern for the future supply of rubber because of various things, including
plant disease, climate change, and the falling commodity price of rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rubber_boots.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1142" data-file-width="857" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Rubber_boots.jpg/170px-Rubber_boots.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Rubber_boots.jpg/255px-Rubber_boots.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Rubber_boots.jpg/340px-Rubber_boots.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rubber_boots.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Compression molded (cured) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber_boot" title="Rubber boot">rubber boots</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Flash_(manufacturing)" title="Flash (manufacturing)">flashes</a> are removed</div></div></div>
<p>Uncured rubber is used for cements;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> for adhesive, insulating, and friction tapes; and for crepe rubber used in insulating blankets and footwear. <a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">Vulcanized rubber</a> has many more applications. Resistance to abrasion makes softer kinds of rubber valuable for the treads of vehicle tires and conveyor belts, and makes hard rubber valuable for pump housings and piping used in the handling of abrasive sludge.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexibility" title="Flexibility">flexibility</a> of rubber is appealing in hoses, tires and rollers for devices ranging from domestic clothes wringers to printing presses; its elasticity makes it suitable for various kinds of shock absorbers and for specialized machinery mountings designed to reduce vibration. Its relative gas <a href="/wiki/Permeation" title="Permeation">impermeability</a> makes it useful in the manufacture of articles such as air hoses, balloons, balls and cushions. The <a href="/wiki/Permeation" title="Permeation">resistance of rubber to water</a> and to the action of most fluid chemicals has led to its use in rainwear, diving gear, and chemical and medicinal tubing and as a lining for storage tanks, processing equipment and railroad tank cars. Because of their <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance_and_conductance" title="Electrical resistance and conductance">electrical resistance</a>, soft rubber goods are used as insulation and for protective gloves, shoes, and blankets; <a href="/wiki/Ebonite" title="Ebonite">hard rubber</a> is used for articles such as telephone housings and parts for radio sets, meters, and other electrical instruments. The coefficient of <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> of rubber, which is high on dry surfaces and low on wet surfaces, leads to its use for <a href="/wiki/Belt_(mechanical)" title="Belt (mechanical)">power-transmission belting</a>, highly flexible couplings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and for water-lubricated bearings in deep-well pumps. Indian rubber balls or <a href="/wiki/Lacrosse_ball" title="Lacrosse ball">lacrosse balls</a> are made of rubber.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1802" data-file-width="2400" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg/220px-Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg/330px-Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg/440px-Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compression_Molding_Machine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Compression molding machine for rubber parts</div></div></div>
<p>Around 25 million tonnes of rubber are produced each year, of which 30 percent is natural.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The remainder is synthetic rubber derived from petrochemical sources. The top end of latex production results in latex products such as surgeons' gloves, balloons, and other relatively high-value products. The mid-range which comes from the technically specified natural rubber materials ends up largely in tires but also in conveyor belts, marine products, windshield wipers, and miscellaneous goods. Natural rubber offers good elasticity, while synthetic materials tend to offer better resistance to environmental factors such as oils, temperature, chemicals, and ultraviolet light. "Cured rubber" is rubber that has been compounded and subjected to the vulcanisation process to create cross-links within the rubber matrix.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allergic_reactions">Allergic reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Allergic reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Latex_allergy" title="Latex allergy">Latex allergy</a></div>
<p>Some people have a serious <a href="/wiki/Latex_allergy" title="Latex allergy">latex allergy</a>, and exposure to natural latex rubber products such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latex_glove" title="Latex glove">latex gloves</a> can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaphylactic_shock" title="Anaphylactic shock">anaphylactic shock</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibody_generator" title="Antibody generator">antigenic proteins</a> found in <i>Hevea</i> latex may be deliberately reduced (though not eliminated)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-510k_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-510k-43">[43]</a></sup> through processing.
</p><p>Latex from non-<i><a href="/wiki/Hevea" title="Hevea">Hevea</a></i> sources, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guayule" title="Guayule">guayule</a>, can be used without allergic reaction by persons with an allergy to <i>Hevea</i> latex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some allergic reactions are not to the latex itself, but from residues of chemicals used to accelerate the cross-linking process. Although this may be confused with an allergy to latex, it is distinct from it, typically taking the form of <a href="/wiki/Type_IV_hypersensitivity" title="Type IV hypersensitivity">Type IV hypersensitivity</a> in the presence of traces of specific processing chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-510k_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-510k-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_degradation">Microbial degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_rubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Microbial degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural rubber is susceptible to degradation by a wide range of bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
The bacteria <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streptomyces_coelicolor" title="Streptomyces coelicolor">Streptomyces coelicolor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_citronellolis" title="Pseudomonas citronellolis">Pseudomonas citronellolis</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Nocardia" title="Nocardia">Nocardia</a></i> spp. are capable of degrading vulcanized natural rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
