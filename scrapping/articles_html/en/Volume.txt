<p><b>Volume</b> is a measure of occupied <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">three-dimensional space</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[1]</a></sup> It is often quantified numerically using <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived units</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">cubic metre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a>) or by various <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial units</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quart" title="Quart">quart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_inch" title="Cubic inch">cubic inch</a>). The definition of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a> (cubed) is interrelated with volume. The volume of a container is generally understood to be the capacity of the container; i.e., the amount of <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> (gas or liquid) that the container could hold, rather than the amount of space the container itself displaces.
</p><p>In ancient times, volume is measured using similar-shaped natural containers and later on, standardized containers. Some simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_dimensional" title="Three dimensional">three-dimensional</a> shapes can have its volume easily calculated using <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Formula" title="Formula">formulas</a>. Volumes of more complicated shapes can be calculated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_calculus" title="Integral calculus">integral calculus</a> if a formula exists for the shape's boundary. <a href="/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space" title="Zero-dimensional space">Zero-</a>, <a href="/wiki/One-dimensional_space" title="One-dimensional space">one-</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-dimensional" title="Two-dimensional">two-dimensional</a> objects have no volume; in <a href="/wiki/Four-dimensional_space" title="Four-dimensional space">fourth</a> and higher dimensions, an analogous concept to the normal volume is the hypervolume.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_history">Ancient history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2172" data-file-width="2680" decoding="async" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg/220px-Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg/330px-Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg/440px-Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pompeji_6_Hohlma%C3%9Fe_aus_Glas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>6 volumetric measures from the <i>mens ponderia</i> in <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, an ancient municipal institution for the control of weights and measures</div></div></div>
<p>The precision of volume measurements in the ancient period usually ranges between 10–50 mL (0.3–2 US fl oz; 0.4–2 imp fl oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 8">: 8 </span></sup> The earliest evidence of volume calculation came from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> as mathematical problems, approximating volume of simple shapes such as <a href="/wiki/Cuboid" title="Cuboid">cuboids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cylinder" title="Cylinder">cylinders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frustum" title="Frustum">frustum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cone" title="Cone">cones</a>. These math problems have been written in the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Mathematical_Papyrus" title="Moscow Mathematical Papyrus">Moscow Mathematical Papyrus</a> (c. 1820 BCE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 403">: 403 </span></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Reisner_Papyrus" title="Reisner Papyrus">Reisner Papyrus</a>, ancient Egyptians have written concrete units of volume for grain and liquids, as well as a table of length, width, depth, and volume for blocks of material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 116">: 116 </span></sup> The Egyptians use their units of length (the <a href="/wiki/Cubit" title="Cubit">cubit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_(unit)" title="Palm (unit)">palm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digit_(unit)" title="Digit (unit)">digit</a>) to devise their units of volume, such as the volume cubit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 117">: 117 </span></sup> or deny<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 396">: 396 </span></sup> (1 cubit × 1 cubit × 1 cubit), volume palm (1 cubit × 1 cubit × 1 palm), and volume digit (1 cubit × 1 cubit × 1 digit).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 117">: 117 </span></sup>
</p><p>The last three books of <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid's Elements">Euclid's <i>Elements</i></a>, written in around 300 BCE, detailed the exact formulas for calculating the volume of <a href="/wiki/Parallelepiped" title="Parallelepiped">parallelepipeds</a>, cones, <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramids</a>, cylinders, and <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">spheres</a>. The formula were determined by prior mathematicians by using a primitive form of <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integration</a>, by breaking the shapes into smaller and simpler pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 403">: 403 </span></sup> A century later, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 287 – 212 BCE</span>) devised approximate volume formula of several shapes used the <a href="/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion" title="Method of exhaustion">method of exhaustion</a> approach, meaning to derive solutions from previous known formulas from similar shapes. Primitive integration of shapes was also discovered independently by <a href="/wiki/Liu_Hui" title="Liu Hui">Liu Hui</a> in the 3rd century CE, <a href="/wiki/Zu_Chongzhi" title="Zu Chongzhi">Zu Chongzhi</a> in the 5th century CE, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 404">: 404 </span></sup>
</p><p>Archimedes also devised a way to calculate the volume of an irregular object, by submerging it underwater and measure the difference between the initial and final water volume. The water volume difference is the volume of the object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 404">: 404 </span></sup> Though highly popularized, Archimedes probably does not submerge the golden crown to find its volume, and thus its density and purity, due to the extreme precision involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inaccuracy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-inaccuracy-5">[4]</a></sup> Instead, he likely have devised a primitive form of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_Balance" title="Hydrostatic Balance">hydrostatic balance</a>. Here, the crown and a chunk of pure gold with a similar weight are put on both ends of a <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale" title="Weighing scale">weighing scale</a> submerged underwater, which will tip accordingly due to the <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_principle" title="Archimedes' principle">Archimedes' principle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-6">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculus_and_standardization_of_units">Calculus and standardization of units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Calculus and standardization of units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_calculus" title="History of calculus">History of calculus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apothecaries%27_system" title="Apothecaries' system">Apothecaries' system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram,_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams,_1926.jpg"><img alt="Pouring liquid to a marked flask" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1674" data-file-width="1648" decoding="async" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram%2C_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams%2C_1926.jpg/220px-%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram%2C_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams%2C_1926.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram%2C_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams%2C_1926.jpg/330px-%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram%2C_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams%2C_1926.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram%2C_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams%2C_1926.jpg/440px-%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram%2C_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams%2C_1926.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%22How_to_Measure%22_diagram,_with_graduated_cylinder_measuring_fluid_drams,_1926.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing how to measure volume using a graduated cylinder with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_dram" title="Fluid dram">fluid dram</a> markings, 1926</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, many units for measuring volume were made, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sester" title="Sester">sester</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Amber_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Amber (unit) (page does not exist)">amber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coomb_(unit)" title="Coomb (unit)">coomb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seam_(unit)" title="Seam (unit)">seam</a>. The sheer quantity of such units motivated British kings to standardize them, culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Assize_of_Bread_and_Ale" title="Assize of Bread and Ale">Assize of Bread and Ale</a> statute in 1258 by <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III of England</a>. The statute standardized weight, length and volume as well as introduced the peny, ounce, pound, gallon and bushel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-:3-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 73–74">: 73–74 </span></sup> In 1618, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Pharmacopoeia" title="London Pharmacopoeia">London Pharmacopoeia</a></i> (medicine compound catalog) adopted the Roman gallon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-7">[6]</a></sup> or <i><a href="/wiki/Congius" title="Congius">congius</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> as a basic unit of volume and gave a conversion table to the apothecaries' units of weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-7">[6]</a></sup> Around this time, volume measurements are becoming more precise and the uncertainty is narrowed to between 1–5 mL (0.03–0.2 US fl oz; 0.04–0.2 imp fl oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-:3-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 8">: 8 </span></sup>
</p><p>Around the early 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Bonaventura_Cavalieri" title="Bonaventura Cavalieri">Bonaventura Cavalieri</a> applied the philosophy of modern integral calculus to calculate the volume of any object. He devised the <a href="/wiki/Cavalieri%27s_principle" title="Cavalieri's principle">Cavalieri's principle</a>, which said that using thinner and thinner slices of the shape would make the resulting volume more and more accurate. This idea would then be later expanded by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat" title="Pierre de Fermat">Pierre de Fermat</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Wallis" title="John Wallis">John Wallis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Barrow" title="Isaac Barrow">Isaac Barrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Gregory_(mathematician)" title="James Gregory (mathematician)">James Gregory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maria_Gaetana_Agnesi" title="Maria Gaetana Agnesi">Maria Gaetana Agnesi</a> in the 17th and 18th centuries to form the modern integral calculus that is still being used in the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 404">: 404 </span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrication_and_redefinitions">Metrication and redefinitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metrication and redefinitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_metric_system" title="History of the metric system">History of the metric system</a></div>
<p>On 7 April 1795, the metric system was formally defined in French law using six units. Three of these are related to volume: the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St%C3%A8re" title="Stère">stère</a></i> (1 m<sup>3</sup>) for volume of firewood; the <i><a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a></i> (1 dm<sup>3</sup>) for volumes of liquid; and the <i><a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gramme</a></i>, for mass—defined as the mass of one cubic centimetre of water at maximum density, at about 4 °C (39 °F).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thirty years later in 1824, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_gallon" title="Imperial gallon">imperial gallon</a> was defined to be the volume occupied by ten <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pounds</a> of water at 17 °C (62 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 394">: 394 </span></sup> This definition was further refined until the United Kingdom's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weights_and_Measures_Act_1985" title="Weights and Measures Act 1985">Weights and Measures Act 1985</a>, which makes 1 imperial gallon precisely equal to 4.54609 litres with no use of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1960 redefinition of the metre from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Prototype_Metre" title="International Prototype Metre">International Prototype Metre</a> to the orange-red <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">emission line</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krypton-86" title="Krypton-86">krypton-86</a> atoms unbounded the metre, cubic metre, and litre from physical objects. This also make the metre and metre-derived units of volume resilient to changes to the International Prototype Metre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marion_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marion-10">[9]</a></sup> The definition of the metre was redefined again in 1983 to use the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a> (which is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Caesium_standard" title="Caesium standard">caesium standard</a>) and <a href="/wiki/2019_redefinition_of_the_SI_base_units" title="2019 redefinition of the SI base units">reworded for clarity in 2019</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg"><img alt="Glass cylinder with even markings" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2510" data-file-width="2008" decoding="async" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg/220px-Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg/330px-Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg/440px-Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graduated_Measuring_Cylinder_with_Stopper_.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Graduated_cylinder" title="Graduated cylinder">graduated cylinder</a> with a plastic <a href="/wiki/Stopper_(plug)" title="Stopper (plug)">stopper</a></div></div></div>
<p>The oldest way to roughly measure a volume of an object is using the human body, such as using hand size and <a href="/wiki/Pinch_(action)" title="Pinch (action)">pinches</a>. However, the human body's variations make it extremely unreliable. A better way to measure volume is to use roughly consistent and durable <a href="/wiki/Container" title="Container">containers</a> found in nature, such as <a href="/wiki/Gourd" title="Gourd">gourds</a>, sheep or pig <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomachs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urinary_bladder" title="Urinary bladder">bladders</a>. Later on, as <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass_production" title="Glass production">glass production</a> improved, small volumes nowadays are usually measured using standardized human-made containers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 393">: 393 </span></sup> This method is common for measuring small volume of fluids or <a href="/wiki/Granular_material" title="Granular material">granular materials</a>, by using a <a href="/wiki/Multiple_(mathematics)" title="Multiple (mathematics)">multiple</a> or fraction of the container. For granular materials, the container is shaken or leveled off to form a roughly flat surface. This method is not the most accurate way to measure volume but is often used to measure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooking_ingredient" title="Cooking ingredient">cooking ingredients</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 399">: 399 </span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Air_displacement_pipette" title="Air displacement pipette">Air displacement pipette</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> to measure volume of fluids at the microscopic scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-12">[11]</a></sup> Calibrated <a href="/wiki/Measuring_cup" title="Measuring cup">measuring cups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Measuring_spoon" title="Measuring spoon">spoons</a> are adequate for cooking and daily life applications, however, they are not precise enough for <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratories</a>. There, volume of liquids is measured using <a href="/wiki/Graduated_cylinder" title="Graduated cylinder">graduated cylinders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pipette" title="Pipette">pipettes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flask" title="Volumetric flask">volumetric flasks</a>. The largest of such calibrated containers are petroleum <a href="/wiki/Storage_tank" title="Storage tank">storage tanks</a>, some can hold up to 1,000,000 <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)#Oil_barrel" title="Barrel (unit)">bbl</a> (160,000,000 L) of fluids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 399">: 399 </span></sup> Even at this scale, by knowing petroleum's density and temperature, very precise volume measurement in these tanks can still be made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 403">: 403 </span></sup>
</p><p>For even larger volumes such as in a <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a>, the container's volume is modeled by shapes and calculated using mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 403">: 403 </span></sup> The task of numerically computing the volume of objects is studied in the field of <a href="/wiki/Computational_geometry" title="Computational geometry">computational geometry</a> in computer science, investigating efficient <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> to perform this computation, <a href="/wiki/Approximation_algorithm" title="Approximation algorithm">approximately</a> or <a href="/wiki/Exact_algorithm" title="Exact algorithm">exactly</a>, for various types of objects. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Convex_volume_approximation" title="Convex volume approximation">convex volume approximation</a> technique shows how to approximate the volume of any <a href="/wiki/Convex_body" title="Convex body">convex body</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Oracle_machine" title="Oracle machine">membership oracle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Units">Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_volume" title="Unit of volume">Unit of volume</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(volume)" title="Orders of magnitude (volume)">Orders of magnitude (volume)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Visualisation_litre_gram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="850" data-file-width="638" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Visualisation_litre_gram.svg/220px-Visualisation_litre_gram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Visualisation_litre_gram.svg/330px-Visualisation_litre_gram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Visualisation_litre_gram.svg/440px-Visualisation_litre_gram.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Visualisation_litre_gram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some SI units of volume to scale and approximate corresponding mass of water</div></div></div>
<p>The general form of a unit of volume is the <a href="/wiki/Cube_(algebra)" title="Cube (algebra)">cube</a> (<i>x</i><sup>3</sup>) of a unit of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a>. For instance, if the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> (m) is chosen as a unit of length, the corresponding unit of volume is the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">cubic metre</a> (m<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Thus, volume is a <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">unit dimension</a> is L<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> The metric units of volume uses <a href="/wiki/Metric_prefix" title="Metric prefix">metric prefixes</a>, strictly in <a href="/wiki/Power_of_10" title="Power of 10">powers of ten</a>. When applying prefixes to units of volume, which are expressed in units of length cubed, the cube operators are applied to the unit of length including the prefix. An example of converting cubic centimetre to cubic metre is: 2.3 cm<sup>3</sup> = 2.3 (cm)<sup>3</sup> = 2.3 (0.01 m)<sup>3</sup> = 0.0000023 m<sup>3</sup> (five zeros).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 143">: 143 </span></sup>
</p><p>Commonly used prefixes for cubed length units are the cubic millimetre (mm<sup>3</sup>), cubic centimetre (cm<sup>3</sup>), cubic decimetre (dm<sup>3</sup>), cubic metre (m<sup>3</sup>) and the cubic kilometre (km<sup>3</sup>). The conversion between the prefix units are as follows: 1000 mm<sup>3</sup> = 1 cm<sup>3</sup>, 1000 cm<sup>3</sup> = 1 dm<sup>3</sup>, and 1000 dm<sup>3</sup> = 1 m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> also includes the <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a> (L) as a unit of volume, where 1 L = 1 dm<sup>3</sup> = 1000 cm<sup>3</sup> = 0.001 m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 145">: 145 </span></sup> For the litre unit, the commonly used prefixes are the millilitre (mL), centilitre (cL), and the litre (L), with 1000 mL = 1 L, 10 mL = 1 cL, 10 cL = 1 dL, and 10 dL = 1 L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Litres are most commonly used for items (such as <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluids</a> and solids that can be poured) which are measured by the capacity or size of their container, whereas cubic metres (and derived units) are most commonly used for items measured either by their dimensions or their displacements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Various other <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">U.S. customary</a> units of volume are also in use, including:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 396–398">: 396–398 </span></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cubic_inch" title="Cubic inch">cubic inch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_foot" title="Cubic foot">cubic foot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_yard" title="Cubic yard">cubic yard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acre-foot" title="Acre-foot">acre-foot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_mile" title="Cubic mile">cubic mile</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Minim_(unit)" title="Minim (unit)">minim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dram_(unit)" title="Dram (unit)">drachm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluid_ounce" title="Fluid ounce">fluid ounce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pint" title="Pint">pint</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Teaspoon" title="Teaspoon">teaspoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tablespoon" title="Tablespoon">tablespoon</a>;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gill_(volume)" title="Gill (volume)">gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quart" title="Quart">quart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">barrel</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cord_(unit)" title="Cord (unit)">cord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peck" title="Peck">peck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bushel" title="Bushel">bushel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hogshead" title="Hogshead">hogshead</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The smallest volume known to be occupied by matter is probably the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>, with its radius is known to be smaller than 1 <a href="/wiki/Femtometre" title="Femtometre">femtometre</a>. This means its volume must be smaller than <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="6955419000000000000♠"></span>4.19<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−45</sup> m<sup>3</sup></span>, though the exact value is still under debate as of 2019 as the <a href="/wiki/Proton_radius_puzzle" title="Proton radius puzzle">proton radius puzzle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_volume" title="Van der Waals volume">van der Waals volume</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atom is far larger, which ranges from <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="6970419000000000000♠"></span>4.19<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−30</sup> m<sup>3</sup></span> to <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="6970724000000000000♠"></span>7.24<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−30</sup> m<sup>3</sup></span> as a sphere with a radius between 100 and 120 <a <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacity_and_volume">Capacity and volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Capacity and volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Capacity is the maximum amount of material that a container can hold, measured in volume or <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>. However, the contained volume does not need to fill towards the container's capacity, or vice versa. Containers can only hold a specific amount of physical volume, not weight (excluding practical concerns). For example, a 50,000 bbl (7,900,000 L) tank that can hold 7,200 t (15,900,000 lb) of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a> will not be able to contain the same 7,200 t (15,900,000 lb) of <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>, due to naphtha's lower density and thus larger volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 390–391">: 390–391 </span></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integral_calculus">Integral calculus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Integral calculus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg"><img alt="f(x) and g(x) rotated in the x-axis" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="431" data-file-width="437" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg/220px-Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg/330px-Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg/440px-Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Integral_apl_rot_objem3.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of a solid of revolution, which the volume of rotated g(x) subtracts the volume of rotated f(x).</div></div></div>
<p>The calculation of volume is a vital part of <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integral</a> calculus. <p>In <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>, a branch of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a volume form on a <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">differentiable manifold</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Differential_form" title="Differential form">differential form</a> of top degree (i.e., whose degree is equal to the dimension of the <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">manifold</a>) that is nowhere equal to zero. A manifold has a volume form if and only if it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orientable" title="Orientable">orientable</a>. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orientable_manifold" title="Orientable manifold">orientable manifold</a> has infinitely many volume forms, since multiplying a volume form by a non-vanishing function yields another volume form. On non-orientable manifolds, one may instead define the weaker notion of a <a href="/wiki/Density_on_a_manifold" title="Density on a manifold">density</a>. Integrating the volume form gives the volume of the manifold according to that form.
</p><p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orientation_(space)" title="Orientation (space)">oriented</a> <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold" title="Pseudo-Riemannian manifold">pseudo-Riemannian manifold</a> has a natural volume form. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derived_quantities">Derived quantities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Derived quantities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_physical_quantities" title="List of physical quantities">List of physical quantities</a></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">Density</a> is the substance's <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> per unit volume, or total mass divided by total volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Specific_volume" title="Specific volume">Specific volume</a> is total volume divided by mass, or the inverse of density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">volumetric flow rate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> is the volume of fluid which passes through a given surface per unit time.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_heat_capacity" title="Volumetric heat capacity">volumetric heat capacity</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> of the substance divided by its volume.</li></ul>
