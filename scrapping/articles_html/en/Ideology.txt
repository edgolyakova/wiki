<p>An <b>ideology</b> is a set of <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">beliefs</a> or philosophies attributed to a person or group of persons, especially as held for reasons that are not purely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epistemic" title="Epistemic">epistemic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in which "practical elements are as prominent as theoretical ones."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Formerly applied primarily to <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> theories and policies, in a tradition going back to <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>, more recent use treats the term as mainly condemnatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Destutt_de_Tracy" title="Antoine Destutt de Tracy">Antoine Destutt de Tracy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocrat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>, who conceived it in 1796 as the "science of ideas" to develop a rational system of ideas to oppose the irrational impulses of the mob. In <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, the term is used in a <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_description" title="Linguistic description">descriptive</a> sense to refer to <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_ideologies" title="List of political ideologies">political belief systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_history">Etymology and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Destutt_de_Tracy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="198" data-file-width="160" decoding="async" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Destutt_de_Tracy.jpg" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Destutt_de_Tracy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Antoine_Destutt_de_Tracy" title="Antoine Destutt de Tracy">Antoine Destutt de Tracy</a> (1754–1836)</div></div></div>
<p>The term <i>ideology</i> originates from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>idéologie</i>, itself deriving from combining <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <i lang="el">idéā</i> (<span title="Ancient Greek-language text"><span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%B0%CE%B4%CE%AD%CE%B1" title="wikt:ἰδέα">ἰδέα</a></span></span>, 'notion, pattern'; close to the <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">Lockean</a> sense of <a href="/wiki/Idea#John_Locke" title="Idea"><i>idea</i></a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/-logy" title="-logy">-logíā</a></i> (<span title="Ancient Greek-language text"><span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%CE%BB%CE%BF%CE%B3%E1%BF%90%CC%81%E1%BE%B1" title="wikt:-λογῐ́ᾱ">-λογῐ́ᾱ</a></span></span>, 'the study of').
</p><p>The term ideology, and the system of ideas associated with it, was coined in 1796 by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Destutt_de_Tracy" title="Antoine Destutt de Tracy">Antoine Destutt de Tracy</a> while in prison pending trial during the <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Reign of Terror</a>, where he read the works of <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">Locke</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Bonnot_de_Condillac" title="Étienne Bonnot de Condillac">Condillac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Hoping to form a secure foundation for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_Science" title="Moral Science">moral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political sciences</a>, Tracy devised the term for a "science of ideas," basing such upon two things:
</p>
<ol><li>the sensations that people experience as they interact with the material world; and</li>
<li>the ideas that form in their minds due to those sensations.</li></ol>
<p>He conceived <i>ideology</i> as a <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> philosophy that would defend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Individual_liberty" title="Individual liberty">individual liberty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_markets" title="Free markets">free markets</a>, and constitutional limits on state power. He argues that, among these aspects, ideology is the most generic term because the 'science of ideas' also contains the study of their expression and deduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennedy-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup</a> that overthrew <a href="/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre" title="Maximilien Robespierre">Maximilien Robespierre</a> allowed Tracy to pursue his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-kennedy-6">[6]</a></sup> Tracy reacted to the terroristic phase of the revolution (during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic regime</a>) by trying to work out a <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rational</a> system of ideas to oppose the <a href="/wiki/Irrationality" title="Irrationality">irrational</a> mob impulses that had nearly destroyed him.
</p><p>A subsequent early source for the near-original meaning of <i>ideology</i> is <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Taine" title="Hippolyte Taine">Hippolyte Taine</a>'s work on the <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">Ancien Régime</a>, <i>Origins of Contemporary France</i> I. He describes <i>ideology</i> as rather like teaching philosophy via the <a href="/wiki/Socratic_method" title="Socratic method">Socratic method</a>, though without extending the vocabulary beyond what the general reader already possessed, and without the examples from observation that practical science would require. Taine identifies it not just with Destutt De Tracy, but also with his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milieu" title="Milieu">milieu</a>, and includes <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Bonnot_de_Condillac" title="Étienne Bonnot de Condillac">Condillac</a> as one of its precursors.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> came to view <i>ideology</i> as a term of abuse, which he often hurled against his liberal foes in Tracy's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institutional" title="Institutional">Institutional</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Karl_Mannheim" title="Karl Mannheim">Karl Mannheim</a>'s historical reconstruction of the shifts in the meaning of <i>ideology</i>, the modern meaning of the word was born when Napoleon used it to describe his opponents as "the ideologues." Tracy's major book, <i>The Elements of Ideology</i>, was soon translated into the major languages of Europe.
</p><p>In the century following Tracy, the term <i>ideology</i> moved back and forth between positive and negative connotations. During this next generation, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Napoleonic_depression" title="Post-Napoleonic depression">post-Napoleonic</a> governments adopted a <a href="/wiki/Reactionary" title="Reactionary">reactionary</a> stance, influenced the Italian, Spanish and Russian thinkers who had begun to describe themselves as "liberals" and who attempted to reignite revolutionary activity in the early 1820s, including the <a href="/wiki/Carlism" title="Carlism">Carlist</a> rebels in Spain; the <a href="/wiki/Carbonari" title="Carbonari">Carbonari societies</a> in France and Italy; and the <a href="/wiki/Decembrist_revolt" title="Decembrist revolt">Decembrists</a> in Russia. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> adopted Napoleon's negative sense of the term, using it in his writings, in which he once described Tracy as a <span title="German-language text"><i lang="de">fischblütige Bourgeoisdoktrinär</i></span> (a 'fish-blooded bourgeois doctrinaire').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term has since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphemism_Treadmill" title="Euphemism Treadmill">dropped some of its pejorative sting</a>, and has become a neutral term in the analysis of differing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_opinions" title="Political opinions">political opinions</a> and views of <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">social groups</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> While Marx situated the term within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a> and domination,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> others believed it was a necessary part of <a href="/wiki/Structural_functionalism" title="Structural functionalism">institutional functioning</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_cohesion" title="Social cohesion">social integration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_and_analysis">Definitions and analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions and analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many different kinds of ideologies, including <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_issues" title="Social issues">social</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epistemological" title="Epistemological">epistemological</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethical</a>.
</p><p>Recent analysis tends to posit that <i>ideology</i> is a 'coherent system of ideas' that rely on a few basic assumptions about reality that may or may not have any factual basis. Through this system, ideas become coherent, repeated patterns through the <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjective</a> ongoing choices that people make. These ideas serve as the seed around which further <a href="/wiki/Thought" title="Thought">thought</a> grows. The belief in an ideology can range from passive acceptance up to fervent advocacy. According to most recent analysis, ideologies are neither necessarily right nor wrong.
</p><p>Definitions, such as by <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Steger" title="Manfred Steger">Manfred Steger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_James_(academic)" title="Paul James (academic)">Paul James</a> emphasize both the issue of patterning and <a href="/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy)" title="Contingency (philosophy)">contingent</a> claims to truth:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Ideologies are patterned clusters of normatively imbued ideas and concepts, including particular representations of power relations. These conceptual maps help people navigate the complexity of their political universe and carry claims to social truth.</p></blockquote>
<p>Studies of the concept of ideology itself (rather than specific ideologies) have been carried out under the name of <i><a href="/wiki/Systematic_ideology" title="Systematic ideology">systematic ideology</a></i> in the works of George Walford and Harold Walsby, who attempt to explore the relationships between ideology and <a href="/wiki/Social_system" title="Social system">social systems</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE" title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE"><span title="An editor has requested that an example be provided. (February 2019)"><span style="font-style:italic; padding-right:0.15em;">example  needed</span></span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>David W. Minar describes six different ways the word <i>ideology</i> has been used:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>As a collection of certain ideas with certain kinds of <i>content</i>, usually normative;</li>
<li>As the <i>form or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_logic" title="Internal logic">internal logical</a> structure</i> that ideas have within a set;</li>
<li>By the role ideas play in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_social_interaction" title="Human social interaction">human-social interaction</a></i>;</li>
<li>By the role ideas play in the <i><a href="/wiki/Organizational_structure" title="Organizational structure">structure of an organization</a></i>;</li>
<li>As meaning, whose purpose is <i><a href="/wiki/Persuasion" title="Persuasion">persuasion</a></i>; and</li>
<li>As the <i>locus</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_interaction" title="Social interaction">social interaction</a>.</li></ol>
<p>For Willard A. Mullins, an <i>ideology</i> should be contrasted with the related (but different) issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopia</a></i> and <i>historical myth</i>. An ideology is composed of four basic characteristics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>it must have power over <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a>;</li>
<li>it must be capable of guiding one's <a href="/wiki/Evaluation" title="Evaluation">evaluations</a>;</li>
<li>it must provide guidance towards action; and</li>
<li>it must be logically coherent.</li></ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Terry_Eagleton" title="Terry Eagleton">Terry Eagleton</a> outlines (more or less in no particular order) some definitions of ideology:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The process of production of meanings, signs and values in social life</li>
<li>A body of ideas characteristic of a particular social group or class</li>
<li>Ideas that help legitimate a dominant political power</li>
<li>False ideas that help legitimate a dominant political power</li>
<li>Systematically distorted communication</li>
<li>Ideas that offer a position for a subject</li>
<li>Forms of thought motivated by social interests</li>
<li>Identity thinking</li>
<li>Socially necessary illusion</li>
<li>The conjuncture of <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a> and power</li>
<li>The medium in which conscious social actors make sense of their world</li>
<li>Action-oriented sets of beliefs</li>
<li>The confusion of linguistic and phenomenal reality</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">Semiotic</a> closure<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 197">: 197 </span></sup></li>
<li>The indispensable medium in which individuals live out their relations to a <a href="/wiki/Social_structure" title="Social structure">social structure</a></li>
<li>The process that converts social life to a natural reality</li></ol>
<p>German philosopher Christian Duncker called for a "critical reflection of the ideology concept."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In his work, he strove to bring the concept of ideology into the foreground, as well as the closely connected concerns of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a> and history, defining <i>ideology</i> in terms of a system of presentations that explicitly or implicitly claim to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_truth" title="Absolute truth">absolute truth</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxist_interpretation">Marxist interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marxist interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Karl_Marx.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Karl_Marx.jpg/150px-Karl_Marx.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Karl_Marx.jpg/225px-Karl_Marx.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Karl_Marx.jpg/300px-Karl_Marx.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Karl_Marx.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> posits that a society's dominant ideology is integral to its superstructure.</div></div></div>
<p>Marx's analysis sees ideology as a system of falsehoods deliberately promulgated by the ruling class as a means of self-perpetuation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> <a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">base and superstructure</a> model of society, <i>base</i> denotes the <a href="/wiki/Relations_of_production" title="Relations of production">relations of production</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modes_of_production" title="Modes of production">modes of production</a>, and <i>superstructure</i> denotes the <a href="/wiki/Dominant_ideology" title="Dominant ideology">dominant ideology</a> (i.e. religious, legal, political systems). The economic base of production determines the political superstructure of a society. Ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_interest" title="Class interest">class-interests</a> determine the superstructure and the nature of the justifying ideology—actions feasible because the <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a> control the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>. For example, in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feudal" title="Feudal">feudal</a> <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_production" title="Mode of production">mode of production</a>, religious ideology is the most prominent aspect of the superstructure, while in capitalist formations, ideologies such as <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democracy</a> dominate. Hence the great importance of the ideology justifying a society; it politically confuses the alienated groups of society via <a href="/wiki/False_consciousness" title="False consciousness">false consciousness</a>.
</p><p>Some explanations have been presented. <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Antonio Gramsci</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural hegemony</a> to explain why the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working-class" title="Working-class">working-class</a> have a false ideological conception of what their best interests are. Marx argued that "The class which has the means of material production at its disposal has control at the same time over the means of mental production."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Marxist formulation of "ideology as an instrument of social reproduction" is conceptually important to the <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_knowledge" title="Sociology of knowledge">sociology of knowledge</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> viz. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Mannheim" title="Karl Mannheim">Karl Mannheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bell" title="Daniel Bell">Daniel Bell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jürgen Habermas">Jürgen Habermas</a> et al. Moreover, Mannheim has developed, and progressed, from the "total" but "special" Marxist conception of ideology to a "general" and "total" ideological conception acknowledging that all ideology (including <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>) resulted from social life, an idea developed by the <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bourdieu" title="Pierre Bourdieu">Pierre Bourdieu</a>. <a href="/wiki/Slavoj_%C5%BDi%C5%BEek" title="Slavoj Žižek">Slavoj Žižek</a> and the earlier <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_School" title="Frankfurt School">Frankfurt School</a> added to the "general theory" of ideology a psychoanalytic insight that ideologies do not include only conscious, but also <a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">unconscious</a> ideas.
</p>
<h3><span id="Ideological_state_apparatuses_.28Althusser.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideological_state_apparatuses_(Althusser)">Ideological state apparatuses (Althusser)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ideological state apparatuses (Althusser)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Original_research plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>. Statements consisting only of original research should be removed.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>French Marxist philosopher <a href="/wiki/Louis_Althusser" title="Louis Althusser">Louis Althusser</a> proposed that ideology is "the imagined existence (or idea) of things as it relates to the real conditions of existence" and makes use of a lacunar discourse. A number of propositions, which are never untrue, suggest a number of other propositions, which are. In this way, the essence of the lacunar discourse is what is <i>not</i> told (but is suggested).
</p><p>For example, the statement "All are <a href="/wiki/Equality_before_the_law" title="Equality before the law">equal before the law</a>," which is a theoretical groundwork of current legal systems, suggests that all people may be of equal worth or have equal opportunities. This is not true, for the concept of <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a> and power over the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a> results in some people being able to own more (<i>much</i> more) than others. This power disparity contradicts the claim that all share both practical worth and future opportunity equally; for example, the rich can afford better legal representation, which practically privileges them before the law.
</p><p>Althusser also proffered the concept of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideological_state_apparatus" title="Ideological state apparatus">ideological state apparatus</a> to explain his theory of ideology. His first thesis was "<i>ideology has no history</i>": while individual ideolog<i>ies</i> have histories, interleaved with the general class struggle of society, the general form of ideology is external to history.
</p><p>For Althusser, beliefs and ideas are the products of social practices, not the reverse. His thesis that "<i>ideas are material</i>" is illustrated by the "scandalous advice" of <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Pascal</a> toward unbelievers: "Kneel and pray, and then you will believe." What is ultimately ideological for Althusser are not the subjective beliefs held in the conscious "minds" of human individuals, but rather discourses that produce these beliefs, the material institutions and rituals that individuals take part in without submitting it to conscious examination and so much more <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">critical thinking</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Ideology_and_the_Commodity_.28Debord.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_and_the_Commodity_(Debord)">Ideology and the Commodity (Debord)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ideology and the Commodity (Debord)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> theorist <a href="/wiki/Guy_Debord" title="Guy Debord">Guy Debord</a>, founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Situationist_International" title="Situationist International">Situationist International</a>, argued that when the commodity becomes the "essential category" of society, i.e. when the process of <a href="/wiki/Commodification" title="Commodification">commodification</a> has been consummated to its fullest extent, the image of society propagated by the commodity (as it describes all of life as constituted by notions and objects deriving their value only as commodities tradeable in terms of <a href="/wiki/Exchange_value" title="Exchange value">exchange value</a>), colonizes all of life and reduces society to a mere representation, <a href="/wiki/The_Society_of_the_Spectacle" title="The Society of the Spectacle">The Society of the Spectacle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Unifying_agents_.28Hoffer.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Unifying_agents_(Hoffer)">Unifying agents (Hoffer)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unifying agents (Hoffer)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The American philosopher <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hoffer" title="Eric Hoffer">Eric Hoffer</a> identified several elements that unify followers of a particular ideology:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><b>Hatred</b>: "Mass movements can rise and spread without a <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>, but never without belief in a <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">devil</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup> The "ideal devil" is a foreigner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 93">: 93 </span></sup></li>
<li><b>Imitation</b>: "The less satisfaction we derive from being ourselves, the greater is our desire to be like others…the more we mistrust our judgment and luck, the more are we ready to follow the example of others."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 101–2">: 101–2 </span></sup></li>
<li><b>Persuasion</b>: The proselytizing zeal of propagandists derives from "a passionate search for something not yet found more than a desire to bestow something we already have."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 110">: 110 </span></sup></li>
<li><b>Coercion</b>: Hoffer asserts that violence and <a href="/wiki/Fanaticism" title="Fanaticism">fanaticism</a> are interdependent. People forcibly converted to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> beliefs become as fanatical as those who did the forcing. "It takes fanatical faith to rationalize our cowardice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 107–8">: 107–8 </span></sup></li>
<li><b>Leadership</b>: Without the leader, there is no movement. Often the leader must wait long in the wings until the time is ripe. He calls for sacrifices in the present, to justify his vision of a breathtaking future. The skills required include: audacity, brazenness, iron will, fanatical conviction; passionate hatred, cunning, a delight in symbols; ability to inspire blind faith in the masses; and a group of able <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">lieutenants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 112–4">: 112–4 </span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charlatan" title="Charlatan">Charlatanism</a> is indispensable, and the leader often imitates both friend and foe, "a single-minded fashioning after a model." He will not lead followers towards the "<a href="/wiki/Promised_Land" title="Promised Land">promised land</a>," but only "away from their unwanted selves."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 116–9">: 116–9 </span></sup></li>
<li><b>Action</b>: Original thoughts are suppressed, and unity encouraged, if the masses are kept occupied through great projects, marches, exploration and industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-8"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 120–1">: 120–1 </span></sup></li>
<li><b>Suspicion</b>: "There is prying and spying, tense watching and a tense awareness of being watched." This pathological mistrust goes unchallenged and encourages <a href="/wiki/Conformity" title="Conformity">conformity</a>, not <a href="/wiki/Dissent" title="Dissent">dissent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_21-9"><a href="#cite_note-:3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 124">: 124 </span></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ronald_Inglehart">Ronald Inglehart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ronald Inglehart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ronald_Inglehart" title="Ronald Inglehart">Ronald Inglehart</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> is author of the <a href="/wiki/World_Values_Survey" title="World Values Survey">World Values Survey</a>, which, since 1980, has mapped social attitudes in 100 countries representing 90% of global population. Results indicate that where people live is likely to closely correlate with their ideological beliefs. In much of Africa, South Asia and the Middle East, people prefer traditional beliefs and are less tolerant of liberal values. <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Europe" title="Christianity in Europe">Protestant Europe</a>, at the other extreme, adheres more to secular beliefs and liberal values. Alone among high-income countries, the United States is exceptional in its adherence to traditional beliefs, in this case Christianity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_ideologies">Political ideologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Political ideologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:European-political-spectrum.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="818" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/European-political-spectrum.png/220px-European-political-spectrum.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/European-political-spectrum.png/330px-European-political-spectrum.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/European-political-spectrum.png/440px-European-political-spectrum.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:European-political-spectrum.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Political spectrum</div></div></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_ideologies" title="List of political ideologies">List of political ideologies</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Social_studies" title="Social studies">social studies</a>, a political ideology is a certain ethical set of <a href="/wiki/Ideal_(ethics)" title="Ideal (ethics)">ideals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Principle" title="Principle">principles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctrine" title="Doctrine">doctrines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">myths</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a>, or large group that explains how <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> should work, offering some political and cultural blueprint for a certain <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a>. Political ideologies are concerned with many different aspects of a society, including (for example): the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_law" title="Labor law">labor law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justice_system" title="Justice system">justice system</a>, the provision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_welfare" title="Social welfare">social welfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">minors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a>, use of the <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotism</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Established_religion" title="Established religion">established religion</a>.
</p><p>Political ideologies have two dimensions:
</p>
<ol><li>Goals: how society should work; and</li>
<li>Methods: the most appropriate ways to achieve the ideal arrangement.</li></ol>
<p>There are many proposed methods for the classification of political ideologies, each of these different methods generate a specific <a href="/wiki/Political_spectrum" title="Political spectrum">political spectrum</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ideologies also identify themselves by their position on the spectrum (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_(politics)" title="Center (politics)">center</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">right</a>), though precision in this respect can often become controversial. Finally, ideologies can be distinguished from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_strategy" title="Political strategy">political strategies</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populism</a>) and from <a href="/wiki/Single-issue_politics" title="Single-issue politics">single issues</a> that a party may be built around (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana_political_parties" title="Marijuana political parties">legalization of marijuana</a>). Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Michael_Oakeshott" title="Michael Oakeshott">Michael Oakeshott</a> defines such ideology as "the formalized abridgment of the supposed sub-stratum of the rational truth contained in the tradition." Moreover, Charles Blattberg offers an account that distinguishes political <i>ideologies</i> from <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political <i>philosophies</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>A political ideology largely concerns itself with how to allocate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">power</a> and to what ends power should be used. Some parties follow a certain ideology very closely, while others may take broad inspiration from a group of related ideologies without specifically embracing any one of them. Each political ideology contains certain ideas on what it considers the best <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Form_of_government" title="Form of government">form of government</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demagogy" title="Demagogy">demagogy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theocracy" title="Theocracy">theocracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">caliphate</a> etc.), scope of government (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federalism</a>, etc) and the best <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic system</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>, etc.). Sometimes the same word is used to identify both an ideology and one of its main ideas. For instance, <i>socialism</i> may refer to an economic system, or it may refer to an ideology that supports that economic system.
</p><p>Post 1991, many commentators claim that we are living in a post-ideological age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> in which redemptive, all-encompassing ideologies have failed. This view is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fukuyama" title="Francis Fukuyama">Francis Fukuyama</a>'s writings on the <i><a href="/wiki/End_of_history" title="End of history">end of history</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Contrastly, Nienhueser (2011) sees research (in the field of <a href="/wiki/Human_resource_management" title="Human resource management">human resource management</a>) as ongoingly "generating ideology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavoj_Zizek" title="Slavoj Zizek">Slavoj Zizek</a> has pointed out how the very notion of post-ideology can enable the deepest, blindest form of ideology. A sort of false consciousness or false cynicism, engaged in for the purpose of lending one's point of view the respect of being objective, pretending neutral cynicism, without truly being so. Rather than help avoiding ideology, this lapse only deepens the commitment to an existing one. Zizek calls this "a <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">post-modernist</a> trap."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sloterdijk" title="Peter Sloterdijk">Peter Sloterdijk</a> advanced the same idea already in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies have shown that political ideology is somewhat genetically <a href="/wiki/Heritability" title="Heritability">heritable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideocracy">Ideocracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ideocracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ideocracy" title="Ideocracy">Ideocracy</a></div>
<p>When a political ideology becomes a dominantly pervasive component within a government, one can speak of an <i>ideocracy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Different forms of government utilize ideology in various ways, not always restricted to politics and society. Certain ideas and schools of thought become favored, or rejected, over others, depending on their compatibility with or use for the reigning social order.
</p><p>As <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> expresses, "Madmen in authority, who hear voices in the air, are distilling their frenzy from some academic scribbler of a few years back."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Anatomy_of_Revolution" title="The Anatomy of Revolution">The Anatomy of Revolution</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_Brinton" title="Crane Brinton">Crane Brinton</a> said that new ideology spreads when there is discontent with an old regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The may be repeated during revolutions itself; extremists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lenin" title="Lenin">Lenin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robespierre" title="Robespierre">Robespierre</a> may thus overcome more moderate revolutionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This stage is soon followed by <a href="/wiki/Thermidor" title="Thermidor">Thermidor</a>, a reining back of <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">revolutionary</a> enthusiasm under <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatists</a> like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalin" title="Stalin">Stalin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, who bring "<a href="/wiki/Return_to_normalcy" title="Return to normalcy">normalcy</a> and equilibrium."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Brinton's sequence ("men of ideas&gt;<a href="/wiki/Fanaticism" title="Fanaticism">fanatics</a>&gt;practical men of action") is reiterated by <a href="/wiki/J._William_Fulbright" title="J. William Fulbright">J. William Fulbright</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> while a similar form occurs in <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hoffer" title="Eric Hoffer">Eric Hoffer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_True_Believer" title="The True Believer">The True Believer</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The revolution thus becomes established as an <i>ideocracy</i>, though its rise is likely to be checked by a 'political midlife crisis.'
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epistemological_ideologies">Epistemological ideologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Epistemological ideologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Even when the challenging of existing beliefs is encouraged, as in scientific theories, the dominant <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mindset" title="Mindset">mindset</a> can prevent certain challenges, theories, or experiments from being advanced.
</p><p>A special case of science that has inspired ideology is ecology, which studies the relationships among living things on Earth. <a href="/wiki/Perceptual_psychology" title="Perceptual psychology">Perceptual psychologist</a> <a href="/wiki/James_J._Gibson" title="James J. Gibson">James J. Gibson</a> believed that human perception of ecological relationships was the basis of <a href="/wiki/Self-awareness" title="Self-awareness">self-awareness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a> itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguist</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Lakoff" title="George Lakoff">George Lakoff</a> has proposed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_science_of_mathematics" title="Cognitive science of mathematics">cognitive science of mathematics</a> wherein even the most fundamental ideas of arithmetic would be seen as consequences or products of human perception—which is itself necessarily evolved within an ecology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">Deep ecology</a> and the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecology_movement" title="Ecology movement">ecology movement</a> (and, to a lesser degree, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_parties" title="Green parties">Green parties</a>) appear to have adopted ecological sciences as a positive ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some notable economically based ideologies include <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monetarism" title="Monetarism">monetarism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mixed_economy" title="Mixed economy">mixed economy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire economics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a>. There are also current theories of <a href="/wiki/Safe_trade" title="Safe trade">safe trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a> that can be seen as ideologies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_and_the_social_sciences">Ideology and the social sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ideology and the social sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_research">Psychological research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Psychological research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A large amount of research in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> is concerned with the causes, consequences and content of ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jost2009_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jost2009-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlenker2012_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schlenker2012-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saucier2000_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saucier2000-47">[47]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/System_justification" title="System justification"><i>system justification theory</i></a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jost2008_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-jost2008-48">[48]</a></sup> ideologies reflect (<a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">unconscious</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motivational_processes" title="Motivational processes">motivational processes</a>, as opposed to the view that political convictions always reflect independent and unbiased thinking. Jost, Ledgerwood and Hardin (2008) propose that ideologies may function as prepackaged units of <a href="/wiki/Interpretation_(logic)" title="Interpretation (logic)">interpretation</a> that spread because of basic human motives to understand the world, avoid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existential_threat" title="Existential threat">existential threat</a>, and maintain valued <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">interpersonal relationships</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jost2008_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-jost2008-48">[48]</a></sup> The authors conclude that such motives may lead disproportionately to the adoption of system-justifying <a href="/wiki/Worldview" title="Worldview">worldviews</a>. Psychologists generally agree that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personality_traits" title="Personality traits">personality traits</a>, individual difference variables, needs, and ideological beliefs seem to have something in common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiotic_theory">Semiotic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Semiotic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
According to <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotician</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hodge_(linguist)" title="Bob Hodge (linguist)">Bob Hodge</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><blockquote><p>[Ideology] identifies a unitary object that incorporates complex sets of meanings with the social agents and processes that produced them. No other term captures this object as well as 'ideology'. <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Foucault</a>'s '<a href="/wiki/Episteme" title="Episteme">episteme</a>' is too narrow and abstract, not social enough. His 'discourse', popular because it covers some of ideology's terrain with less baggage, is too confined to verbal systems. '<a href="/wiki/Worldview" title="Worldview">Worldview</a>' is too <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a>, '<a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a>' too loaded. Despite or because of its contradictions, 'ideology' still plays a key role in semiotics oriented to social, political life.</p></blockquote><p>Authors such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_Freeden" title="Michael Freeden">Michael Freeden</a> have also recently incorporated a <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> analysis to the study of ideologies.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">Sociologists</a> define <i>ideology</i> as "cultural beliefs that justify particular social arrangements, including patterns of inequality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-51">[51]</a></sup> Dominant groups use these sets of cultural beliefs and practices to justify the systems of inequality that maintain their group's social power over non-dominant groups. Ideologies use a society's symbol system to organize social relations in a <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>, with some social identities being superior to other social identities, which are considered inferior. The dominant ideology in a society is passed along through the society's major social institutions, such as the media, the family, education, and religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> As societies changed throughout history, so did the ideologies that justified systems of inequality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sociological examples of ideologies include: <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>; <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">sexism</a>; <a href="/wiki/Heterosexism" title="Heterosexism">heterosexism</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ableism" title="Ableism">ableism</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">ethnocentrism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotations">Quotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Quotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>"We do not need…to believe in an ideology. All that is necessary is for each of us to develop our good human qualities. The need for a sense of universal responsibility affects every aspect of modern life." — <a href="/wiki/Dalai_Lama" title="Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>"The function of ideology is to stabilize and perpetuate dominance through masking or illusion." — <a href="/wiki/Sally_Haslanger" title="Sally Haslanger">Sally Haslanger</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>"[A]n ideology differs from a simple opinion in that it claims to possess either the key to history, or the solution for all the ‘riddles of the universe,’ or the intimate knowledge of the hidden universal laws, which are supposed to rule nature and man." — <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Arendt" title="Hannah Arendt">Hannah Arendt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li></ul>
