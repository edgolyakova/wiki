<p><b>Katsushika Hokusai</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">葛飾 北斎</span></span>, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 31 October 1760 – 10 May 1849)</span>, known simply as <b>Hokusai</b>, was a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a> artist of the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, active as a painter and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printmaker" title="Printmaker">printmaker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is best known for the <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing_in_Japan" title="Woodblock printing in Japan">woodblock print</a> series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty-Six_Views_of_Mount_Fuji" title="Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji">Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji</a></i>, which includes the iconic print <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Wave_off_Kanagawa" title="The Great Wave off Kanagawa">The Great Wave off Kanagawa</a></i>. Hokusai was instrumental in developing <i>ukiyo-e</i> from a style of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portraiture" title="Portraiture">portraiture</a> largely focused on <a href="/wiki/Courtesan" title="Courtesan">courtesans</a> and actors into a much broader style of art that focused on landscapes, plants, and animals.
</p><p>Hokusai created the monumental <i>Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji</i> as a response to a domestic travel boom in Japan and as part of a personal interest in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji" title="Mount Fuji">Mount Fuji</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith-2">[2]</a></sup> It was this series, specifically, <i>The Great Wave off Kanagawa</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fine_Wind,_Clear_Morning" title="Fine Wind, Clear Morning">Fine Wind, Clear Morning</a></i>, that secured his fame both in Japan and overseas..<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hokusai was best known for his woodblock ukiyo-e prints, but he worked in a variety of mediums including painting and book illustration.  Starting as a young child, he continued working and improving his style until his death, aged 88. In a long and successful career, Hokusai produced over 30,000 paintings, sketches, woodblock prints, and images for picture books in total. Innovative in his compositions and exceptional in his drawing technique, Hokusai is considered one of the greatest masters in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_art" title="History of art">history of art</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hokusai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Hokusai's date of birth is unclear, but is often stated as the 23rd day of the 9th month of the 10th year of the <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dreki" title="Hōreki">Hōreki</a> era (in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_calendar" title="Japanese calendar">old calendar</a>, or 31 October 1760) to an <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisan</a> family, in the <a href="/wiki/Katsushika" title="Katsushika">Katsushika</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a>, the capital of the ruling <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston116_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston116-4">[4]</a></sup> His childhood name was Tokitarō.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagata_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nagata-5">[5]</a></sup> It is believed his father was Nakajima Ise, a <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirror</a>-maker for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Shōgun">shōgun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagata_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nagata-5">[5]</a></sup> His father never made Hokusai an heir, so it is possible that his mother was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston116_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-weston116-4">[4]</a></sup> Hokusai began painting around the age of six, perhaps learning from his father, whose work included the painting of designs around mirrors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston116_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-weston116-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hokusai was known by at least thirty names during his lifetime. While the use of multiple names was a common practice of Japanese artists of the time, his number of <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonyms</a> exceeds that of any other major Japanese artist. His name changes are so frequent, and so often related to changes in his artistic production and style, that they are used for breaking his life up into periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston116_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-weston116-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the age of 12, his father sent him to work in a bookshop and <a href="/wiki/Lending_library" title="Lending library">lending library</a>, a popular institution in Japanese cities, where reading books made from woodcut blocks was a popular entertainment of the middle and upper classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston-6">[6]</a></sup> At 14, he worked as an apprentice to a woodcarver, until the age of 18, when he entered the studio of <a href="/wiki/Katsukawa_Shunsh%C5%8D" title="Katsukawa Shunshō">Katsukawa Shunshō</a>. Shunshō was an artist of <i><a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a></i>, a style of woodblock prints and paintings that Hokusai would master, and head of the so-called Katsukawa school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagata_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-nagata-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Ukiyo-e</i>, as practised by artists like Shunshō, focused on images of the <a href="/wiki/Courtesan" title="Courtesan">courtesans</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Bijin-ga" title="Bijin-ga">bijin-ga</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> actors (<i><a href="/wiki/Yakusha-e" title="Yakusha-e">yakusha-e</a></i>) who were popular in Japan's cities at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston117_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston117-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>After a year, Hokusai's name changed for the first time, when he was dubbed Shunrō by his master. It was under this name that he published his first prints, a series of pictures of kabuki actors published in 1779. During the decade he worked in Shunshō's studio, Hokusai was married to his first wife, about whom very little is known except that she died in the early 1790s. He married again in 1797, although this second wife also died after a short time. He fathered two sons and three daughters with these two wives, and his youngest daughter Ei, also known as <a href="/wiki/Katsushika_%C5%8Ci" title="Katsushika Ōi">Ōi</a>, eventually became an artist and his assistant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston117_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-weston117-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Fireworks in the Cool of Evening at Ryogoku Bridge in Edo</i> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1788–89</span>) dates from this period of Hokusai's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon the death of Shunshō in 1793, Hokusai began exploring other styles of art, including European styles he was exposed to through French and Dutch copper engravings he was able to acquire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston117_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-weston117-7">[7]</a></sup> He was soon expelled from the Katsukawa school by Shunkō, the chief disciple of Shunshō, possibly due to his studies at the rival <a href="/wiki/Kan%C5%8D_school" title="Kanō school">Kanō school</a>. This event was, in his own words, inspirational: "What really motivated the development of my artistic style was the embarrassment I suffered at Shunkō's hands."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lane_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-lane-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hokusai also changed the subjects of his works, moving away from the images of courtesans and actors that were the traditional subjects of ukiyo-e. Instead, his work became focused on landscapes and images of the daily life of Japanese people from a variety of social levels. This change of subject was a breakthrough in ukiyo-e and in Hokusai's career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston117_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-weston117-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_period">Middle period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hokusai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The next period saw Hokusai's association with the Tawaraya School and the adoption of the name "Tawaraya Sōri". He produced many privately commissioned prints for special occasions (<i><a href="/wiki/Surimono" title="Surimono">surimono</a></i>), and illustrations for books of humorous poems (<i>kyōka ehon</i>) during this time. In 1798, Hokusai passed his name on to a pupil and set out as an independent artist, free from ties to a school for the first time, adopting the name Hokusai Tomisa.
</p><p>By 1800, Hokusai was further developing his use of ukiyo-e for purposes other than portraiture. He had also adopted the name he would most widely be known by, Katsushika Hokusai, the former name referring to the part of Edo where he was born, the latter meaning 'north studio', in honour of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Star" title="North Star">North Star</a>, symbol of a deity important in his religion of <a href="/wiki/Nichiren_Buddhism" title="Nichiren Buddhism">Nichiren Buddhism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> That year, he published two collections of landscapes, <i>Famous Sights of the Eastern Capital</i> and <i>Eight Views of Edo</i> (modern Tokyo). He also began to attract students of his own, eventually teaching 50 pupils over the course of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston117_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-weston117-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>He became increasingly famous over the next decade, both due to his artwork and his talent for self-promotion. During an Edo festival in 1804, he created an enormous portrait of the Buddhist prelate <a href="/wiki/Bodhidharma" title="Bodhidharma">Daruma</a>, said to be 200 square meters, using a broom and buckets full of ink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Another story places him in the court of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Shōgun">shōgun</a> <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ienari" title="Tokugawa Ienari">Tokugawa Ienari</a>, invited there to compete with another artist who practised more traditional brushstroke painting. Hokusai painted a blue curve on paper, then chased a chicken whose feet had been dipped in red paint across the image. He described the painting to the shōgun as a landscape showing the Tatsuta River with red maple leaves floating in it, winning the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston1-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1804 and 1815 saw Hokusai collaborate with the popular novelist <a href="/wiki/Takizawa_Bakin" title="Takizawa Bakin">Takizawa Bakin</a> on a series of illustrated books. Especially popular was the fantasy novel <i>Chinsetsu Yumiharizuki</i> (<i>Strange Tales of the Crescent Moon</i>, 1807–1811) with <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Tametomo" title="Minamoto no Tametomo">Minamoto no Tametomo</a> as the main character, and Hokusai gained fame with his creative and powerful illustrations, but the collaboration ended after 
thirteen works. There are various theories as to why they dissolved their cooperation, such as discordant personalities and conflicting opinions on how to draw illustrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Hokusai also created several albums of <a href="/wiki/Erotic_art" title="Erotic art">erotic art</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shunga_(art)" title="Shunga (art)">shunga</a></i>). His most famous image in this genre is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dream_of_the_Fisherman%27s_Wife" title="The Dream of the Fisherman's Wife">The Dream of the Fisherman's Wife</a></i>, which depicts a young woman entwined sexually with a pair of octopuses, from <i><a href="/wiki/Kinoe_no_Komatsu" title="Kinoe no Komatsu">Kinoe no Komatsu</a></i>, a three-volume book of shunga from 1814.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hokusai paid close attention to the production of his work. In letters during his involvement with <i>Toshisen Ehon</i>, a Japanese edition of an anthology of Chinese poetry, Hokusai wrote to the publisher that the blockcutter <a href="/wiki/Egawa_Tomekichi" title="Egawa Tomekichi">Egawa Tomekichi</a>, with whom Hokusai had previously worked and whom he respected, had strayed from Hokusai's style in the cutting of certain heads. He also wrote directly to another blockcutter involved in the project, Sugita Kinsuke, stating that he disliked the <a href="/wiki/Utagawa_school" title="Utagawa school">Utagawa school</a> style in which Kinsuke had cut the figure's eyes and noses and that amendments were needed for the final prints to be true to his style. In his letter, Hokusai included examples of both his style of illustrating eyes and noses and the Utagawa school style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1811, at the age of 51, Hokusai changed his name to <i>Taito</i> and entered the period in which he created the <i><a href="/wiki/Hokusai_Manga" title="Hokusai Manga">Hokusai Manga</a></i> and various <i>etehon</i>, or art manuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagata_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-nagata-5">[5]</a></sup> These manuals beginning in 1812 with <i>Quick Lessons in Simplified Drawing</i>, were intended as a convenient way to make money and attract more students. The first volume of <i>Manga</i> (meaning random drawings) was published in 1814 and was an immediate success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> By 1820, he had produced twelve volumes (with three more published posthumously) which include thousands of drawings of objects, plants, animals, religious figures, and everyday people, often with humorous overtones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston118_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston118-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 5 October 1817, he painted the <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Daruma" title="Great Daruma">Great Daruma</a></i> outside the <a href="/wiki/Hongan-ji_Nagoya_Betsuin" title="Hongan-ji Nagoya Betsuin">Hongan-ji Nagoya Betsuin</a> in Nagoya. This portrait in ink on paper measured 18 × 10.8 metres, and the event drew huge crowds.  The feat was recounted in a popular song and he received the name "<i>Darusen</i><span style="padding-left:.15em;">"</span> or "Daruma Master"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Although the original was destroyed in 1945, Hokusai's promotional handbills from that time survived and are preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Nagoya_City_Museum" title="Nagoya City Museum">Nagoya City Museum</a>.
</p><p>In 1820, Hokusai changed his name yet again, this time to "Iitsu," a change which marked the start of a period in which he secured fame as an artist throughout Japan. His most celebrated work, <i><a href="/wiki/Thirty-six_Views_of_Mount_Fuji" title="Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji">Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji</a></i>, including the famous <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Wave_off_Kanagawa" title="Great Wave off Kanagawa">Great Wave off Kanagawa</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Fuji" title="Red Fuji">Red Fuji</a></i> was produced in the early 1830s. The results of Hokusai's perspectival studies in <i>Manga</i> can be seen here in <i>The Great Wave</i> where he uses what would have been seen as a western perspective to represent depth and volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> It proved so popular that ten more prints were later added to the series. Among the other popular series of prints he made during this time are <i><a href="/wiki/A_Tour_of_the_Waterfalls_of_the_Provinces" title="A Tour of the Waterfalls of the Provinces">A Tour of the Waterfalls of the Provinces</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Oceans_of_Wisdom" title="Oceans of Wisdom">Oceans of Wisdom</a></i> and <i>Unusual Views of Celebrated Bridges in the Provinces</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston2-23">[23]</a></sup> He also began producing a number of detailed individual images of flowers and birds (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kach%C5%8D-e" title="Kachō-e">kachō-e</a></i>), including the extraordinarily detailed <i>Poppies</i> and <i>Flock of Chickens</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weston119_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-weston119-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hokusai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1888" data-file-width="1257" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png/220px-Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png/330px-Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png/440px-Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Katsushika_Hokusai_by_disciple_Keisai_Eisen.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of Hokusai by disciple Keisai Eisen</div></div></div>
<p>The next period, beginning in 1834, saw Hokusai working under the name "Gakyō Rōjin" (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">画狂老人</span></span>; "The Old Man Mad About Art").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gvjh.org_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-gvjh.org-25">[25]</a></sup> It was at this time that he produced <i><a href="/wiki/One_Hundred_Views_of_Mount_Fuji" title="One Hundred Views of Mount Fuji">One Hundred Views of Mount Fuji</a></i>, another significant series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> generally considered "the masterpiece among his landscape picture books".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lane_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-lane-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Colophon_(publishing)" title="Colophon (publishing)">colophon</a> to this work, Hokusai writes:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>From the age of six, I had a passion for copying the form of things and since the age of fifty I have published many drawings, yet of all I drew by my seventieth year there is nothing worth taking into account. At seventy-three years I partly understood the structure of animals, birds, insects and fishes, and the life of grasses and plants. And so, at eighty-six I shall progress further; at ninety I shall even further penetrate their secret meaning, and by one hundred I shall perhaps truly have reached the level of the marvellous and divine. When I am one hundred and ten, each dot, each line will possess a life of its own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In 1839, a fire destroyed Hokusai's studio and much of his work. By this time, his career was beginning to fade as younger artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/And%C5%8D_Hiroshige" title="Andō Hiroshige">Andō Hiroshige</a> became increasingly popular. At the age of 83, Hokusai traveled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obuse,_Nagano" title="Obuse, Nagano">Obuse</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shinano_Province" title="Shinano Province">Shinano Province</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Nagano_Prefecture" title="Nagano Prefecture">Nagano Prefecture</a>) at the invitation of a wealthy farmer, Takai Kozan where he stayed for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hokusai_Museum_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hokusai_Museum-28">[28]</a></sup> During his time in Obuse, he created several masterpieces, included the <i>Masculine Wave</i> and the <i>Feminine Wave</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hokusai_Museum_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hokusai_Museum-28">[28]</a></sup> Between 1842 and 1843, in what he described as "daily exorcisms" (<i>nisshin joma</i>), Hokusai painted Chinese lions (<i><a href="/wiki/Chinese_guardian_lions" title="Chinese guardian lions">shishi</a></i>) every morning in ink on paper as a <a href="/wiki/Talisman" title="Talisman">talisman</a> against misfortune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hokusai continued working almost until the end, painting <i>The Dragon of Smoke Escaping from Mt Fuji</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tiger_in_the_Snow" title="Tiger in the Snow">Tiger in the Snow</a></i> in early 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsuji_Nobou_in_Calza_2003,_p._72_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tsuji_Nobou_in_Calza_2003,_p._72-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Constantly seeking to produce better work, he apparently exclaimed on his deathbed, "If only Heaven will give me just another ten years ... Just another five more years, then I could become a real painter." He died on 10 May 1849<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and was buried at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seiky%C5%8D-ji&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seikyō-ji (page does not exist)">Seikyō-ji</a> in Tokyo (Taito Ward).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagata_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-nagata-5">[5]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a> he composed shortly before his death reads: "Though as a ghost, I shall lightly tread, the summer fields."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsuji_Nobou_in_Calza_2003,_p._72_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tsuji_Nobou_in_Calza_2003,_p._72-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="786" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg/220px-Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg/330px-Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg/440px-Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Debussy_-_La_Mer_-_The_great_wave_of_Kanaga_from_Hokusai.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cover of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debussy" title="Debussy">Debussy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/La_mer_(Debussy)" title="La mer (Debussy)">La Mer</a></i>, 1905</div></div></div>
<p>Hokusai had achievements in various fields as an artist. He made designs for book illustrations and woodblock prints, sketches, and painting for over 70 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Hokusai was an early experimenter with western linear perspective among Japanese artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Hokusai himself was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Sessh%C5%AB_T%C5%8Dy%C5%8D" title="Sesshū Tōyō">Sesshū Tōyō</a> and other styles of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">Chinese painting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csuchico_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-csuchico-36">[36]</a></sup> His influences stretched across the globe to his western contemporaries in nineteenth-century Europe with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japonism" title="Japonism">Japonism</a>, which started with a craze for collecting Japanese art, particularly ukiyo-e, of which some of the first samples were to be seen in Paris, when in about 1856, the French artist <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Bracquemond" title="Félix Bracquemond">Félix Bracquemond</a> first came across a copy of the sketchbook <i>Hokusai Manga</i> at the workshop of his printer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>He influenced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impressionist" title="Impressionist">Impressionism</a> movement, with themes echoing his work appearing in the work of <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Pierre-Auguste Renoir</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Jugendstil" title="Jugendstil">Jugendstil</a> in Germany.  His woodcuts were collected by many European artists, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degas" title="Degas">Degas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauguin" title="Gauguin">Gauguin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klimt" title="Klimt">Klimt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Marc" title="Franz Marc">Franz Marc</a>, <a href="/wiki/August_Macke" title="August Macke">August Macke</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manet" title="Manet">Manet</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Gogh" title="Van Gogh">van Gogh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hokusai_Retrospective_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hokusai_Retrospective-37">[37]</a></sup> Degas said of him, "Hokusai is not just one artist among others in the Floating World. He is an island, a continent, a whole world in himself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Obrist" title="Hermann Obrist">Hermann Obrist</a>'s whiplash motif, or <i>Peitschenhieb</i>, which came to exemplify the new movement, is visibly influenced by Hokusai's work.
</p><p>Even after his death, exhibitions of his artworks continue to grow. In 2005, Tokyo National Museum held a Hokusai exhibition which had the largest number of visitors of any exhibit there that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-39">[39]</a></sup> Several paintings from the Tokyo exhibition were also exhibited in the United Kingdom. The British Museum held the first exhibition of Hokusai's later year artworks including <i>'The Great Wave'</i> in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hokusai inspired the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">Hugo Award</a>–winning short story by science fiction author <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">Roger Zelazny</a>, "<a href="/wiki/24_Views_of_Mt._Fuji,_by_Hokusai" title="24 Views of Mt. Fuji, by Hokusai">24 Views of Mt. Fuji, by Hokusai</a>", in which the protagonist tours the area surrounding Mount Fuji, stopping at locations painted by Hokusai. A 2011 book on <a href="/wiki/Mindfulness" title="Mindfulness">mindfulness</a> closes with the poem "Hokusai Says" by Roger Keyes, preceded with the explanation that "[s]ometimes poetry captures the soul of an idea better than anything else."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1985 <i>Encyclopaedia Britannica</i>, Richard Lane characterizes Hokusai as "since the later 19th century [having] impressed Western artists, critics and art lovers alike, more, possibly, than any other single Asian artist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>'Store Selling Picture Books and Ukiyo-e'</i> by Hokusai shows how <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a> during the time was actually sold; it shows how these prints were sold at local shops, and ordinary people could buy ukiyo-e. Unusually in this image, Hokusai used a hand-colored approach instead of using several separated woodblocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>His youngest daughter Ei has her own manga and film called <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Hokusai" title="Miss Hokusai">Miss Hokusai</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>A biographical film about the painter was released in Japan on May 28, 2021.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOKUSAI_IN_CINEMAS_MAY_28_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-HOKUSAI_IN_CINEMAS_MAY_28-45">[45]</a></sup> It was premiered at the 33rd <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_International_Film_Festival" title="Tokyo International Film Festival">Tokyo International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
