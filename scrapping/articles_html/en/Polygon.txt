<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Polygon</span></h1>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Plane figure bounded by line segments</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Polygon_(disambiguation)" title="Polygon (disambiguation)">Polygon (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Assorted_polygons.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="1190" decoding="async" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Assorted_polygons.svg/400px-Assorted_polygons.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Assorted_polygons.svg/600px-Assorted_polygons.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Assorted_polygons.svg/800px-Assorted_polygons.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Assorted_polygons.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some polygons of different kinds: open (excluding its boundary), boundary only (excluding interior), closed (including both boundary and interior), and self-intersecting.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, a <b>polygon</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> <a href="/wiki/Shape" title="Shape">figure</a> that is described by a finite number of straight <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segments</a> connected to form a closed <i><a href="/wiki/Polygonal_chain" title="Polygonal chain">polygonal chain</a></i> (or <i>polygonal circuit</i>). The bounded plane <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Region_(mathematics)" title="Region (mathematics)">region</a>, the bounding circuit, or the two together, may be called a polygon.
</p><p>The segments of a polygonal circuit are called its <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a></i> or <i>sides</i>. The points where two edges meet are the polygon's <i><a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a></i> (singular: vertex) or <i>corners</i>. The interior of a solid polygon is sometimes called its <i>body</i>. An <b><i>n</i>-gon</b> is a polygon with <i>n</i> sides; for example, a <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangle</a> is a 3-gon.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">simple polygon</a> is one which does not intersect itself. Mathematicians are often concerned only with the bounding polygonal chains of simple polygons and they often define a polygon accordingly. A polygonal boundary may be allowed to cross over itself, creating <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygons</a> and other <a href="/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons" title="List of self-intersecting polygons">self-intersecting polygons</a>.
</p><p>A polygon is a 2-dimensional example of the more general <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytope</a> in any number of dimensions. There are many more <a href="#Generalizations">generalizations of polygons</a> defined for different purposes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>polygon</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> adjective πολύς (<i>polús</i>) 'much', 'many' and γωνία (<i>gōnía</i>) 'corner' or 'angle'. It has been suggested that γόνυ (<i>gónu</i>) 'knee' may be the origin of <i>gon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polygon_types.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="630" data-file-width="750" decoding="async" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polygon_types.svg/300px-Polygon_types.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polygon_types.svg/450px-Polygon_types.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polygon_types.svg/600px-Polygon_types.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polygon_types.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some different types of polygon</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_sides">Number of sides</span></h3>
<p>Polygons are primarily classified by the number of sides. See the <a href="#Naming">table below</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Convexity_and_intersection">Convexity and intersection</span></h3>
<p>Polygons may be characterized by their convexity or type of non-convexity:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Convex_polygon" title="Convex polygon">Convex</a>: any line drawn through the polygon (and not tangent to an edge or corner) meets its boundary exactly twice. As a consequence, all its interior angles are less than 180°. Equivalently, any line segment with endpoints on the boundary passes through only interior points between its endpoints. This condition is true for polygons in any geometry, not just Euclidean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Non-convex: a line may be found which meets its boundary more than twice. Equivalently, there exists a line segment between two boundary points that passes outside the polygon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">Simple</a>: the boundary of the polygon does not cross itself. All convex polygons are simple.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Concave_polygon" title="Concave polygon">Concave</a>: Non-convex and simple. There is at least one interior angle greater than 180°.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Star-shaped_polygon" title="Star-shaped polygon">Star-shaped</a>: the whole interior is visible from at least one point, without crossing any edge. The polygon must be simple, and may be convex or concave. All convex polygons are star-shaped.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons" title="List of self-intersecting polygons">Self-intersecting</a>: the boundary of the polygon crosses itself. The term <i>complex</i> is sometimes used in contrast to <i>simple</i>, but this usage risks confusion with the idea of a <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_polytope" title="Complex polytope">complex polygon</a></i> as one which exists in the complex <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert</a> plane consisting of two <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex</a> dimensions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">Star polygon</a>: a polygon which self-intersects in a regular way. A polygon cannot be both a star and star-shaped.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equality_and_symmetry">Equality and symmetry</span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Equiangular_polygon" title="Equiangular polygon">Equiangular</a>: all corner angles are equal.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Equilateral_polygon" title="Equilateral polygon">Equilateral</a>: all edges are of the same length.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">Regular</a>: both equilateral and equiangular.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclic_polygon" title="Cyclic polygon">Cyclic</a>: all corners lie on a single <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a>, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumcircle" title="Circumcircle">circumcircle</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tangential_polygon" title="Tangential polygon">Tangential</a>: all sides are tangent to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inscribed_circle" title="Inscribed circle">inscribed circle</a>.</li>
<li>Isogonal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertex-transitive" title="Vertex-transitive">vertex-transitive</a>: all corners lie within the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_orbit" title="Symmetry orbit">symmetry orbit</a>. The polygon is also cyclic and equiangular.</li>
<li>Isotoxal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge-transitive" title="Edge-transitive">edge-transitive</a>: all sides lie within the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_orbit" title="Symmetry orbit">symmetry orbit</a>. The polygon is also equilateral and tangential.</li></ul>
<p>The property of regularity may be defined in other ways: a polygon is regular if and only if it is both isogonal and isotoxal, or equivalently it is both cyclic and equilateral. A non-convex regular polygon is called a <i>regular <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygon</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous">Miscellaneous</span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon" title="Rectilinear polygon">Rectilinear</a>: the polygon's sides meet at right angles, i.e. all its interior angles are 90 or 270 degrees.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monotone_polygon" title="Monotone polygon">Monotone</a> with respect to a given line <i>L</i>: every line <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_(geometry)" title="Orthogonal (geometry)">orthogonal</a> to L intersects the polygon not more than twice.</li></ul>

<p><a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a> is assumed throughout.
</p>

Tracing around a convex <i>n</i>-gon, the angle "turned" at a corner is the exterior or external angle. Tracing all the way around the polygon makes one full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">turn</a>, so the sum of the exterior angles must be 360°. This argument can be generalized to concave simple polygons, if external angles that turn in the opposite direction are subtracted from the total turned. Tracing around an <i>n</i>-gon in general, the sum of the exterior angles (the total amount one rotates at the vertices) can be any integer multiple <i>d</i> of 360°, e.g. 720° for a <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a> and 0° for an angular "eight" or <a href="/wiki/Antiparallelogram" title="Antiparallelogram">antiparallelogram</a>, where <i>d</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Density_(polytope)#Polygons" title="Density (polytope)">density</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turning_number" title="Turning number">turning number</a> of the polygon. In either case, the area formula is correct in <a href="/wiki/Absolute_value" title="Absolute value">absolute value</a>. This is commonly called the <i><a href="/wiki/Shoelace_formula" title="Shoelace formula">shoelace formula</a></i> or <i>surveyor's formula</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-intersecting">Self-intersecting</span></h4>
<p>The area of a <a href="/wiki/Complex_polygon" title="Complex polygon">self-intersecting polygon</a> can be defined in two different ways, giving different answers:
</p>
<ul><li>Using the formulas for simple polygons, we allow that particular regions within the polygon may have their area multiplied by a factor which we call the <i>density</i> of the region. For example, the central convex pentagon in the center of a pentagram has density 2. The two triangular regions of a cross-quadrilateral (like a figure 8) have opposite-signed densities, and adding their areas together can give a total area of zero for the whole figure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Considering the enclosed regions as point sets, we can find the area of the enclosed point set. This corresponds to the area of the plane covered by the polygon or to the area of one or more simple polygons having the same outline as the self-intersecting one. In the case of the cross-quadrilateral, it is treated as two simple triangles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span></h2>
<p>The idea of a polygon has been generalized in various ways. Some of the more important include:
</p>
<ul><li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical_polygon" title="Spherical polygon">spherical polygon</a> is a circuit of arcs of great circles (sides) and vertices on the surface of a sphere. It allows the <a href="/wiki/Digon" title="Digon">digon</a>, a polygon having only two sides and two corners, which is impossible in a flat plane. Spherical polygons play an important role in <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a> (map making) and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wythoff%27s_construction" title="Wythoff's construction">Wythoff's construction</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uniform_polyhedra" title="Uniform polyhedra">uniform polyhedra</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Skew_polygon" title="Skew polygon">skew polygon</a> does not lie in a flat plane, but zigzags in three (or more) dimensions. The <a href="/wiki/Petrie_polygon" title="Petrie polygon">Petrie polygons</a> of the regular polytopes are well known examples.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Apeirogon" title="Apeirogon">apeirogon</a> is an infinite sequence of sides and angles, which is not closed but has no ends because it extends indefinitely in both directions.</li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skew_apeirogon" title="Skew apeirogon">skew apeirogon</a> is an infinite sequence of sides and angles that do not lie in a flat plane.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Complex_polytope" title="Complex polytope">complex polygon</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Configuration_(polytope)" title="Configuration (polytope)">configuration</a> analogous to an ordinary polygon, which exists in the <a href="/wiki/Complex_plane" title="Complex plane">complex plane</a> of two <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number">imaginary</a> dimensions.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Abstract_polytope" title="Abstract polytope">abstract polygon</a> is an algebraic <a href="/wiki/Partially_ordered_set" title="Partially ordered set">partially ordered set</a> representing the various elements (sides, vertices, etc.) and their connectivity. A real geometric polygon is said to be a <i>realization</i> of the associated abstract polygon. Depending on the mapping, all the generalizations described here can be realized.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> is a three-dimensional solid bounded by flat polygonal faces, analogous to a polygon in two dimensions. The corresponding shapes in four or higher dimensions are called <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytopes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> (In other conventions, the words <i>polyhedron</i> and <i>polytope</i> are used in any dimension, with the distinction between the two that a polytope is necessarily bounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>polygon</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>polygōnum</i> (a noun), from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> πολύγωνον (<i>polygōnon/polugōnon</i>), noun use of neuter of πολύγωνος (<i>polygōnos/polugōnos</i>, the masculine adjective), meaning "many-angled". Individual polygons are named (and sometimes classified) according to the number of sides, combining a <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>-derived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_prefix" title="Numerical prefix">numerical prefix</a> with the suffix <i>-gon</i>, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dodecagon" title="Dodecagon">dodecagon</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quadrilateral" title="Quadrilateral">quadrilateral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonagon" title="Nonagon">nonagon</a> are exceptions.
</p><p>Beyond decagons (10-sided) and dodecagons (12-sided), mathematicians generally use numerical notation, for example 17-gon and 257-gon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathworld_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-mathworld-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Exceptions exist for side counts that are easily expressed in verbal form (e.g. 20 and 30), or are used by non-mathematicians. Some special polygons also have their own names; for example the <a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular</a> <a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star</a> <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a> is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="564" decoding="async" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/220px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/330px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/440px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical image of polygons (1699)</div></div></div>
<p>Polygons have been known since ancient times. The <a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular polygons</a> were known to the ancient Greeks, with the <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a>, a non-convex regular polygon (<a href="/wiki/Star_polygon" title="Star polygon">star polygon</a>), appearing as early as the 7th century B.C. on a <a href="/wiki/Krater" title="Krater">krater</a> by <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes_(vase_painter)" title="Aristophanes (vase painter)">Aristophanes</a>, found at <a href="/wiki/Caere" title="Caere">Caere</a> and now in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitoline_Museum" title="Capitoline Museum">Capitoline Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first known systematic study of non-convex polygons in general was made by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bradwardine" title="Thomas Bradwardine">Thomas Bradwardine</a> in the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1952, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Colin_Shephard" title="Geoffrey Colin Shephard">Geoffrey Colin Shephard</a> generalized the idea of polygons to the complex plane, where each <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real</a> dimension is accompanied by an <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number">imaginary</a> one, to create <a href="/wiki/Complex_polytope" title="Complex polytope">complex polygons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_nature">In nature</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="192" data-file-width="256" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg/220px-Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7f/Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant's Causeway">Giant's Causeway</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a></div></div></div>
<p>Polygons appear in rock formations, most commonly as the flat facets of <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>, where the angles between the sides depend on the type of mineral from which the crystal is made.
</p><p>Regular hexagons can occur when the cooling of <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> forms areas of tightly packed columns of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>, which may be seen at the <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant's Causeway">Giant's Causeway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, or at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Postpile" title="Devil's Postpile">Devil's Postpile</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, the surface of the wax <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb" title="Honeycomb">honeycomb</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a> is an array of <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagons</a>, and the sides and base of each cell are also polygons.
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">computer graphics</a>, a polygon is a <a href="/wiki/Geometric_primitive" title="Geometric primitive">primitive</a> used in modelling and rendering. They are defined in a database, containing arrays of <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(computer_graphics)" title="Vertex (computer graphics)">vertices</a> (the coordinates of the <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">geometrical vertices</a>, as well as other attributes of the polygon, such as color, shading and texture), connectivity information, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Material_(computer_graphics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Material (computer graphics) (page does not exist)">materials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Any surface is modelled as a tessellation called <a href="/wiki/Polygon_mesh" title="Polygon mesh">polygon mesh</a>. If a square mesh has <span class="nowrap"><i>n</i> + 1</span> points (vertices) per side, there are <i>n</i> squared squares in the mesh, or 2<i>n</i> squared triangles since there are two triangles in a square. There are <span class="nowrap">(<i>n</i> + 1)<sup>2</sup> / 2(<i>n</i><sup>2</sup>)</span> vertices per triangle. Where <i>n</i> is large, this approaches one half. Or, each vertex inside the square mesh connects four edges (lines).
</p><p>The imaging system calls up the structure of polygons needed for the scene to be created from the database. This is transferred to active memory and finally, to the display system (screen, TV monitors etc.) so that the scene can be viewed. During this process, the imaging system renders polygons in correct perspective ready for transmission of the processed data to the display system. Although polygons are two-dimensional, through the system computer they are placed in a visual scene in the correct three-dimensional orientation.
