<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Lens</span></h1>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Optical device which transmits and refracts light</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Lens_(disambiguation)" title="Lens (disambiguation)">Lens (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BiconvexLens.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="396" data-file-width="484" decoding="async" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/BiconvexLens.jpg/250px-BiconvexLens.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/BiconvexLens.jpg/375px-BiconvexLens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/BiconvexLens.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BiconvexLens.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A biconvex lens</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:185px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lens_and_wavefronts.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="356" data-file-width="183" decoding="async" height="356" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Lens_and_wavefronts.gif" width="183"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Lenses can be used to focus light</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>lens</b> is a transmissive <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optical</a> device which focuses or disperses a <a href="/wiki/Light_beam" title="Light beam">light beam</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Simple_lens" title="Simple lens">simple lens</a> consists of a single piece of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparent_material" title="Transparent material">transparent material</a>, while a <a href="#Compound_lenses">compound lens</a> consists of several simple lenses (<i>elements</i>), usually arranged along a common <a href="/wiki/Optical_axis" title="Optical axis">axis</a>. Lenses are made from materials such as <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>, and are <a href="/wiki/Grinding_(abrasive_cutting)" title="Grinding (abrasive cutting)">ground</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polishing" title="Polishing">polished</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">molded</a> to a desired shape. A lens can focus light to form an <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">image</a>, unlike a <a href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prism</a>, which refracts light without focusing. Devices that similarly focus or disperse waves and radiation other than visible light are also called lenses, such as <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> lenses, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_lens" title="Electron lens">electron lenses</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acoustic_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Acoustic lens (page does not exist)">acoustic lenses</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Explosive_lens" title="Explosive lens">explosive lenses</a>.
</p><p>Lenses are used in various imaging devices like <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">cameras</a>. They are also used as visual aids in <a href="/wiki/Glasses" title="Glasses">glasses</a> to correct defects of vision such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-sightedness" title="Near-sightedness">myopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Far-sightedness" title="Far-sightedness">hypermetropia</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_optics" title="History of optics">History of optics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">Camera lens</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="624" data-file-width="1328" decoding="async" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg/220px-Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg/330px-Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg/440px-Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Optics_from_Roger_Bacon%27s_De_multiplicatone_specierum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Light being refracted by a spherical glass container full of water. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>, 13th century</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2786" data-file-width="4266" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg/220px-LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg/330px-LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg/440px-LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LSST_Telescope_-_L1_Lens_of_the_camera.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lens for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_Synoptic_Survey_Telescope" title="Large Synoptic Survey Telescope">LSST</a>, a planned sky surveying telescope</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lens" title="wikt:lens">lens</a></i> comes from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lens_(genus)" title="Lens (genus)">lēns</a></i>, the Latin name of the <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentil</a> (a seed of a lentil plant), because a double-convex lens is lentil-shaped. The lentil also gives its name to a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(geometry)" title="Lens (geometry)">geometric figure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scholars argue that the archeological evidence indicates that there was widespread use of lenses in antiquity, spanning several millennia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The so-called <a href="/wiki/Nimrud_lens" title="Nimrud lens">Nimrud lens</a> is a rock crystal artifact dated to the 7th century BC which may or may not have been used as a magnifying glass, or a burning glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nimrud_lens_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nimrud_lens-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Others have suggested that certain <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a>  depict "simple glass meniscal lenses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kriss_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kriss-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (July 2013)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The oldest certain reference to the use of lenses is from <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>' play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Clouds" title="The Clouds">The Clouds</a></i> (424 BC) mentioning a burning-glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Clouds_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Clouds-7">[7]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> (1st century) confirms that burning-glasses were known in the Roman period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
Pliny also has the earliest known reference to the use of a <a href="/wiki/Corrective_lens" title="Corrective lens">corrective lens</a> when he mentions that <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a> was said to watch the <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiatorial</a> games using an <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a> (presumably <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concave" title="wikt:concave">concave</a> to correct for <a href="/wiki/Myopia" title="Myopia">nearsightedness</a>, though the reference is vague).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Both Pliny and <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca the Younger</a> (3 BC–65 AD) described the magnifying effect of a glass globe filled with water.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> (2nd century) wrote a book on <i><a href="/wiki/Optics_(Ptolemy)" title="Optics (Ptolemy)">Optics</a></i>, which however survives only in the Latin translation of an incomplete and very poor Arabic translation.
The book was, however, received by medieval scholars in the Islamic world, and commented upon by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Sahl_(mathematician)" title="Ibn Sahl (mathematician)">Ibn Sahl</a> (10th century), who was in turn improved upon by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alhazen" title="Alhazen">Alhazen</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a></i>, 11th century). The Arabic translation of Ptolemy's <i>Optics</i> became available in Latin translation in the 12th century (<a href="/wiki/Eugenius_of_Palermo" title="Eugenius of Palermo">Eugenius of Palermo</a> 1154). Between the 11th and 13th century "<a href="/wiki/Reading_stone" title="Reading stone">reading stones</a>" were invented. These were primitive plano-convex lenses initially made by cutting a glass sphere in half. The medieval (11th or 12th century) rock crystal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visby_lens" title="Visby lens">Visby lenses</a> may or may not have been intended for use as burning glasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectacles" title="Spectacles">Spectacles</a> were invented as an improvement of the "reading stones" of the high medieval period in Northern Italy in the second half of the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This was the start of the optical industry of grinding and polishing lenses for spectacles, first in Venice and Florence in the late 13th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and later in the spectacle-making centres in both the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
Spectacle makers created improved types of lenses for the correction of vision based more on empirical knowledge gained from observing the effects of the lenses (probably without the knowledge of the rudimentary optical theory of the day).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The practical development and experimentation with lenses led to the invention of the compound <a href="/wiki/Optical_microscope" title="Optical microscope">optical microscope</a> around 1595, and the <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescope</a> in 1608, both of which appeared in the spectacle-making centres in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LZZginzib4C_page_55_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-LZZginzib4C_page_55-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_telescope" title="History of the telescope">History of the telescope</a></div>
<p>With the invention of the telescope and microscope there was a great deal of experimentation with lens shapes in the 17th and early 18th centuries by those trying to correct chromatic errors seen in lenses. Opticians tried to construct lenses of varying forms of curvature, wrongly assuming errors arose from defects in the spherical figure of their surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Optical theory on <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> and experimentation was showing no single-element lens could bring all colours to a focus. This led to the invention of the compound <a href="/wiki/Achromatic_lens" title="Achromatic lens">achromatic lens</a> by <a href="/wiki/Chester_Moore_Hall" title="Chester Moore Hall">Chester Moore Hall</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1733, an invention also claimed by fellow Englishman <a href="/wiki/John_Dollond" title="John Dollond">John Dollond</a> in a 1758 patent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_of_simple_lenses">Construction of simple lenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction of simple lenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most lenses are <i>spherical lenses</i>: their two surfaces are parts of the surfaces of spheres. Each surface can be <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convex" title="wikt:convex"><i>convex</i></a> (bulging outwards from the lens), <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concave" title="wikt:concave"><i>concave</i></a> (depressed into the lens), or <i>planar</i> (flat). The line joining the centres of the spheres making up the lens surfaces is called the <i>axis</i> of the lens. Typically the lens axis passes through the physical centre of the lens, because of the way they are manufactured. Lenses may be cut or ground after manufacturing to give them a different shape or size. The lens axis may then not pass through the physical centre of the lens.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Toric_lens" title="Toric lens">Toric</a> or sphero-cylindrical lenses have surfaces with two different radii of curvature in two orthogonal planes. They have a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Focal_power" title="Focal power">focal power</a> in different meridians. This forms an <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism_(optical_systems)" title="Astigmatism (optical systems)">astigmatic</a> lens. An example is eyeglass lenses that are used to correct <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism" title="Astigmatism">astigmatism</a> in someone's eye.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_simple_lenses">Types of simple lenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types of simple lenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lenses_en.svg" title="Types of lenses"><img alt="Types of lenses" data-file-height="248" data-file-width="531" decoding="async" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Lenses_en.svg/450px-Lenses_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Lenses_en.svg/675px-Lenses_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Lenses_en.svg/900px-Lenses_en.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a></div>
<p>Lenses are classified by the curvature of the two optical surfaces. A lens is <i>biconvex</i> (or <i>double convex</i>, or just <i>convex</i>) if both surfaces are <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convex" title="wikt:convex">convex</a>. If both surfaces have the same radius of curvature, the lens is <i>equiconvex</i>. A lens with two <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concave" title="wikt:concave">concave</a> surfaces is <i>biconcave</i> (or just <i>concave</i>). If one of the surfaces is flat, the lens is <i>plano-convex</i> or <i>plano-concave</i> depending on the curvature of the other surface. A lens with one convex and one concave side is <i>convex-concave</i> or <i>meniscus</i>. It is this type of lens that is most commonly used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrective_lenses#Lens_shape" title="Corrective lenses">corrective lenses</a>.
</p><p>If the lens is biconvex or plano-convex, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collimated_light" title="Collimated light">collimated</a> beam of light passing through the lens converges to a spot (a <i>focus</i>) behind the lens. In this case, the lens is called a <i>positive</i> or <i>converging</i> lens. For a <a href="/wiki/Thin_lens" title="Thin lens">thin lens</a> in air, the distance from the lens to the spot is the <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a> of the lens, which is commonly represented by <i>f</i> in diagrams and equations. An <a href="/wiki/Extended_hemispherical_lens" title="Extended hemispherical lens">extended hemispherical lens</a> is a special type of plano-convex lens, in which the lens's curved surface is a full hemisphere and the lens is much thicker than the radius of curvature.
</p>

<tbody><tr>

<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>If the lens is biconcave or plano-concave, a collimated beam of light passing through the lens is diverged (spread); the lens is thus called a <i>negative</i> or <i>diverging</i> lens. The beam, after passing through the lens, appears to emanate from a particular point on the axis in front of the lens. For a thin lens in air, the distance from this point to the lens is the focal length, though it is negative with respect to the focal length of a converging lens.
</p>
<table>
<tbody><tr>
<td><div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lens1b.svg" title="Biconcave lens"><img alt="Biconcave lens" data-file-height="345" data-file-width="522" decoding="async" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lens1b.svg/390px-Lens1b.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lens1b.svg/585px-Lens1b.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lens1b.svg/780px-Lens1b.svg.png 2x" width="390"/></a></div>

<td><div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Concave_lens.jpg"><img alt="Concave lens.jpg" data-file-height="378" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Concave_lens.jpg/250px-Concave_lens.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Concave_lens.jpg/375px-Concave_lens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Concave_lens.jpg/500px-Concave_lens.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a></div>

<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Convex-concave (meniscus) lenses can be either positive or negative, depending on the relative curvatures of the two surfaces. A <i>negative meniscus</i> lens has a steeper concave surface and is thinner at the centre than at the periphery. Conversely, a <i>positive meniscus</i> lens has a steeper convex surface and is thicker at the centre than at the periphery. An ideal <a href="/wiki/Thin_lens" title="Thin lens">thin lens</a> with two surfaces of equal curvature would have zero <a href="/wiki/Optical_power" title="Optical power">optical power</a>, meaning that it would neither converge nor diverge light. All real lenses have nonzero thickness, however, which makes a real lens with identical curved surfaces slightly positive. To obtain exactly zero optical power, a meniscus lens must have slightly unequal curvatures to account for the effect of the lens' thickness.
</p>

<p>The focal length <i>f</i> is positive for converging lenses, and negative for diverging lenses. The <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">reciprocal</a> of the focal length, 1/<i>f</i>, is the <a href="/wiki/Optical_power" title="Optical power">optical power</a> of the lens. If the focal length is in metres, this gives the optical power in <a href="/wiki/Dioptre" title="Dioptre">dioptres</a> (inverse metres).
</p><p>Lenses have the same focal length when light travels from the back to the front as when light goes from the front to the back. Other properties of the lens, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aberration_in_optical_systems" title="Aberration in optical systems">aberrations</a> are not the same in both directions.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sign_convention_for_radii_of_curvature_R1_and_R2">Sign convention for radii of curvature <i>R</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>R</i><sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sign convention for radii of curvature R1 and R2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_curvature_(optics)" title="Radius of curvature (optics)">Radius of curvature (optics)</a></div>
<p>The signs of the lens' radii of curvature indicate whether the corresponding surfaces are convex or concave. The <a href="/wiki/Sign_convention" title="Sign convention">sign convention</a> used to represent this varies, but in this article a <i>positive</i> <i>R</i> indicates a surface's center of curvature is further along in the direction of the ray travel (right, in the accompanying diagrams), while <i>negative</i> <i>R</i> means that rays reaching the surface have already passed the center of curvature. Consequently, for external lens surfaces as diagrammed above, <span class="nowrap"><i>R</i><sub>1</sub> &gt; 0</span> and  <span class="nowrap"><i>R</i><sub>2</sub> &lt; 0</span> indicate <i>convex</i> surfaces (used to converge light in a positive lens), while <span class="nowrap"><i>R</i><sub>1</sub> &lt; 0</span> and  <span class="nowrap"><i>R</i><sub>2</sub> &gt; 0</span> indicate <i>concave</i> surfaces. The reciprocal of the radius of curvature is called the <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a>. A flat surface has zero curvature, and its radius of curvature  is <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinite</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imaging_properties">Imaging properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Imaging properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As mentioned above, a positive or converging lens in air focuses a collimated beam travelling along the lens axis to a spot (known as the <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focal point</a>) at a distance <i>f</i> from the lens. Conversely, a <a href="/wiki/Point_source" title="Point source">point source</a> of light placed at the focal point is converted into a collimated beam by the lens. These two cases are examples of <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">image</a> formation in lenses. In the former case, an object at an infinite distance (as represented by a collimated beam of waves) is focused to an image at the focal point of the lens. In the latter, an object at the focal length distance from the lens is imaged at infinity. The plane perpendicular to the lens axis situated at a distance <i>f</i> from the lens is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Cardinal_point_(optics)#Focal_planes" title="Cardinal point (optics)">focal plane</a></i>.
</p><p>If the distances from the object to the lens and from the lens to the image are <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> respectively, for a lens of negligible thickness (<a href="/wiki/Thin_lens" title="Thin lens">thin lens</a>), in air, the distances are related by the <b>thin lens formula</b>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>

<p>Therefore, if an object is placed at a distance <span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub> &gt; <i>f</i></span> from a positive lens of focal length <i>f</i>, we will find an image distance <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> according to this formula. If a screen is placed at a distance <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> on the opposite side of the lens, an image is formed on it. This sort of image, which can be projected onto a screen or <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">image sensor</a>, is known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Real_image" title="Real image">real image</a></i>. This is the principle of the <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a>, and also of the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> serves as the image sensor.
</p><p>The focusing adjustment of a camera adjusts <i>S</i><sub>2</sub>, as using an image distance different from that required by this formula produces a <a href="/wiki/Defocus_aberration" title="Defocus aberration">defocused</a> (fuzzy) image for an object at a distance of <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> from the camera. Put another way, modifying <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> causes objects at a different <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> to come into perfect focus.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lens3b.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="362" decoding="async" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lens3b.svg/350px-Lens3b.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lens3b.svg/525px-Lens3b.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lens3b.svg/700px-Lens3b.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lens3b.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Virtual image formation using a positive lens as a magnifying glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In some cases <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> is negative, indicating that the image is formed on the opposite side of the lens from where those rays are being considered. Since the diverging light rays emanating from the lens never come into focus, and those rays are not physically present at the point where they <i>appear</i> to form an image, this is called a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_image" title="Virtual image">virtual image</a>. Unlike real images, a virtual image cannot be projected on a screen, but appears to an observer looking through the lens as if it were a real object at the location of that virtual image. Likewise, it appears to a subsequent lens as if it were an object at that location, so that second lens could again focus that light into a real image, <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> then being measured from the virtual image location behind the first lens to the second lens. This is exactly what the eye does when looking through a <a href="/wiki/Magnifying_glass" title="Magnifying glass">magnifying glass</a>. The magnifying glass creates a (magnified) virtual image behind the magnifying glass, but those rays are then re-imaged by the <a href="/wiki/Lens_(anatomy)" title="Lens (anatomy)">lens of the eye</a> to create a <i>real image</i> on the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a>.
</p>

<p>Using a positive lens of focal length <i>f</i>, a virtual image results when <span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub> &lt; <i>f</i></span>, the lens thus being used as a magnifying glass (rather than if <span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub> &gt;&gt; <i>f</i></span> as for a camera). Using a negative lens (<span class="nowrap"><i>f</i> &lt; 0</span>) with a <i>real object</i> (<span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub> &gt; 0</span>) can only produce a virtual image (<span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>2</sub> &lt; 0</span>), according to the above formula. It is also possible for the object distance <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> to be negative, in which case the lens sees a so-called <i>virtual object</i>. This happens when the lens is inserted into a converging beam (being focused by a previous lens) <i>before</i> the location of its real image. In that case even a negative lens can project a real image, as is done by a <a href="/wiki/Barlow_lens" title="Barlow lens">Barlow lens</a>.
</p>

<p>For a <a href="/wiki/Thin_lens" title="Thin lens">thin lens</a>, the distances <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> are measured from the object and image to the position of the lens, as described above. When the thickness of the lens is not much smaller than <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> or there are multiple lens elements (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_lens" title="Compound lens">compound lens</a>), one must instead measure from the object and image to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_plane" title="Principal plane">principal planes</a> of the lens. If distances <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> or <i>S</i><sub>2</sub> pass through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium_(optics)" title="Medium (optics)">medium</a> other than air or vacuum a more complicated analysis is required.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnification">Magnification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Magnification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The linear <i><a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">magnification</a></i> of an imaging system using a single lens is given by
</p>

<p>where <i>M</i> is the magnification factor defined as the ratio of the size of an image compared to the size of the object. The sign convention here dictates that if <i>M</i> is negative, as it is for real images, the image is upside-down with respect to the object. For virtual images <i>M</i> is positive, so the image is upright.
</p><p>This magnification formula provides two easy ways to distinguish converging (<i>f &gt; 0</i>) and diverging (<i>f &lt; 0</i>) lenses: For an object very close to the lens (<span class="nowrap">0 &lt; <i>S</i><sub>1</sub> &lt; |f|</span>), a converging lens would form a magnified (bigger) virtual image, whereas a diverging lens would form a demagnified (smaller) image; For an object very far from the lens (<span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub> &gt; |f| &gt; 0</span>), a converging lens would form an inverted image, whereas a diverging lens would form an upright image.
</p><p>Linear magnification <i>M</i> is not always the most useful measure of magnifying power. For instance, when characterizing a visual telescope or binoculars that produce only a virtual image, one would be more concerned with the <a href="/wiki/Magnification#Magnification_as_a_number_(optical_magnification)" title="Magnification">angular magnification</a>—which expresses how much larger a distant object appears through the telescope compared to the naked eye. In the case of a camera one would quote the <a href="/wiki/Plate_scale" title="Plate scale">plate scale</a>, which compares the apparent (angular) size of a distant object to the size of the real image produced at the focus. The plate scale is the reciprocal of the focal length of the camera lens; lenses are categorized as <a href="/wiki/Long-focus_lens" title="Long-focus lens">long-focus lenses</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wide-angle_lens" title="Wide-angle lens">wide-angle lenses</a> according to their focal lengths.
</p><p>Using an inappropriate measurement of magnification can be formally correct but yield a meaningless number. For instance, using a magnifying glass of 5 cm focal length, held 20 cm from the eye and 5 cm from the object, produces a virtual image at infinity of infinite linear size: <span class="nowrap"><i>M</i> = ∞</span>. But the <i>angular magnification</i> is 5, meaning that the object appears 5 times larger to the eye than without the lens. When taking a picture of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a> using a camera with a 50 mm lens, one is not concerned with the linear magnification <span class="nowrap"><i>M</i> ≈ <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="3001500000000000000♠"></span>−50 mm</span> / <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7008380000000000000♠"></span>380<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> km</span> = <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="3009870000000000000♠"></span>−1.3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−10</sup></span>.</span> Rather, the plate scale of the camera is about 1°/mm, from which one can conclude that the 0.5 mm image on the film corresponds to an angular size of the moon seen from earth of about 0.5°.
</p><p>In the extreme case where an object is an infinite distance away, <span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>1</sub> = ∞</span>, <span class="nowrap"><i>S</i><sub>2</sub> = <i>f</i></span> and <span class="nowrap"><i>M</i> = −<i>f</i>/∞= 0</span>, indicating that the object would be imaged to a single point in the focal plane. In fact, the diameter of the projected spot is not actually zero, since <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> places a lower limit on the size of the <a href="/wiki/Point_spread_function" title="Point spread function">point spread function</a>. This is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffraction_limit" title="Diffraction limit">diffraction limit</a>.
</p>

<p>Lenses do not form perfect images, and a lens always introduces some degree of distortion or <i>aberration</i> that makes the image an imperfect replica of the object. Careful design of the lens system for a particular application minimizes the aberration. Several types of aberration affect image quality, including spherical aberration, coma, and chromatic aberration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spherical_aberration">Spherical aberration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Spherical aberration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">Spherical aberration</a></div>
<p><i>Spherical aberration</i> occurs because spherical surfaces are not the ideal shape for a lens, but are by far the simplest shape to which glass can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrication_and_testing_of_optical_components" title="Fabrication and testing of optical components">ground and polished</a>, and so are often used. Spherical aberration causes beams parallel to, but distant from, the lens axis to be focused in a slightly different place than beams close to the axis. This manifests itself as a blurring of the image. Spherical aberration can be minimised with normal lens shapes by carefully choosing the surface curvatures for a particular application. For instance, a plano-convex lens, which is used to focus a collimated beam, produces a sharper focal spot when used with the convex side towards the beam source.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lens5.svg"><img alt="Lens5.svg" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Lens5.svg/400px-Lens5.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Lens5.svg/600px-Lens5.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Lens5.svg/800px-Lens5.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coma">Coma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Coma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coma_(optics)" title="Coma (optics)">Coma (optics)</a></div>
<p><i>Coma</i>, or <i>comatic aberration</i>, derives its name from the <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>-like appearance of the aberrated image. Coma occurs when an object off the optical axis of the lens is imaged, where rays pass through the lens at an angle to the axis θ. Rays that pass through the centre of a lens of focal length <i>f</i> are focused at a point with distance <span class="nowrap"><i>f</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangent_function" title="Tangent function">tan</a> θ</span> from the axis. Rays passing through the outer margins of the lens are focused at different points, either further from the axis (positive coma) or closer to the axis (negative coma). In general, a bundle of parallel rays passing through the lens at a fixed distance from the centre of the lens are focused to a ring-shaped image in the focal plane, known as a <i>comatic circle</i>. The sum of all these circles results in a V-shaped or comet-like flare. As with spherical aberration, coma can be minimised (and in some cases eliminated) by choosing the curvature of the two lens surfaces to match the application. Lenses in which both spherical aberration and coma are minimised are called <i>bestform</i> lenses.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lens-coma.svg"><img alt="Lens-coma.svg" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Lens-coma.svg/400px-Lens-coma.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Lens-coma.svg/600px-Lens-coma.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Lens-coma.svg/800px-Lens-coma.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chromatic_aberration">Chromatic aberration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chromatic aberration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">Chromatic aberration</a></div>
<p><i>Chromatic aberration</i> is caused by the <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">dispersion</a> of the lens material—the variation of its <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>, <i>n</i>, with the wavelength of light. Since, from the formulae above, <i>f</i> is dependent upon <i>n</i>, it follows that light of different wavelengths is focused to different positions. Chromatic aberration of a lens is seen as fringes of colour around the image. It can be minimised by using an <a href="/wiki/Achromatic_lens" title="Achromatic lens">achromatic doublet</a> (or <i>achromat</i>) in which two materials with differing dispersion are bonded together to form a single lens. This reduces the amount of chromatic aberration over a certain range of wavelengths, though it does not produce perfect correction. The use of achromats was an important step in the development of the optical microscope. An <a href="/wiki/Apochromat" title="Apochromat">apochromat</a> is a lens or lens system with even better chromatic aberration correction, combined with improved spherical aberration correction. Apochromats are much more expensive than achromats.
</p><p>Different lens materials may also be used to minimise chromatic aberration, such as specialised coatings or lenses made from the crystal <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a>. This naturally occurring substance has the highest known <a href="/wiki/Abbe_number" title="Abbe number">Abbe number</a>, indicating that the material has low dispersion.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg"><img alt="Chromatic aberration lens diagram.svg" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg/400px-Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg/600px-Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg/800px-Chromatic_aberration_lens_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_aberration">Other types of aberration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other types of aberration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other kinds of aberration include <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_curvature" title="Field curvature">field curvature</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrel_distortion" title="Barrel distortion">barrel</a> </i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pincushion_distortion" title="Pincushion distortion">pincushion distortion</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Astigmatism_(optical_systems)" title="Astigmatism (optical systems)">astigmatism</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aperture_diffraction">Aperture diffraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Aperture diffraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even if a lens is designed to minimize or eliminate the aberrations described above, the image quality is still limited by the <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> of light passing through the lens' finite <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffraction-limited" title="Diffraction-limited">diffraction-limited</a> lens is one in which aberrations have been reduced to the point where the image quality is primarily limited by diffraction under the design conditions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_lenses">Compound lenses <span class="anchor" id="compound_lens_anchor"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Compound lenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_lens" title="Photographic lens">Photographic lens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(lens)" title="Doublet (lens)">Doublet (lens)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triplet_lens" title="Triplet lens">Triplet lens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Achromatic_lens" title="Achromatic lens">Achromatic lens</a></div>
<p>Simple lenses are subject to the <a href="#Aberrations">optical aberrations</a> discussed above. In many cases these aberrations can be compensated for to a great extent by using a combination of simple lenses with complementary aberrations. A <i>compound lens</i> is a collection of simple lenses of different shapes and made of materials of different refractive indices, arranged one after the other with a common axis.
</p><p>The simplest case is where lenses are placed in contact: if the lenses of focal lengths <i>f</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>f</i><sub>2</sub> are "<a href="/wiki/Thin_lens" title="Thin lens">thin</a>", the combined focal length <i>f</i> of the lenses is given by

This type of system is called an <i><a href="/wiki/Afocal_system" title="Afocal system">afocal system</a></i>, since it produces no net convergence or divergence of the beam. Two lenses at this separation form the simplest type of <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">optical telescope</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non_spherical_types">Non spherical types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Non spherical types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="680" data-file-width="397" decoding="async" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg/170px-Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg/255px-Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg/340px-Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pfeilh%C3%B6he.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An aspheric biconvex lens.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cylindrical_lens" title="Cylindrical lens">Cylindrical lenses</a> have curvature along only one axis. They are used to focus light into a line, or to convert the elliptical light from a <a href="/wiki/Laser_diode" title="Laser diode">laser diode</a> into a round beam. They are also used in motion picture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anamorphic_lens" title="Anamorphic lens">anamorphic lenses</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aspheric_lens" title="Aspheric lens">Aspheric lenses</a> have at least one surface that is neither spherical nor cylindrical. The more complicated shapes allow such lenses to form images with less <a href="/wiki/Optical_aberration" title="Optical aberration">aberration</a> than standard simple lenses, but they are more difficult and expensive to produce. These were formerly complex to make and often extremely expensive, but advances in technology have greatly reduced the manufacturing cost for such lenses. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1441" data-file-width="2292" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG/240px-Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG/360px-Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG/480px-Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flat_flexible_plastic_sheet_lens.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close-up view of a flat <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a> has its optical surface broken up into narrow rings, allowing the lens to be much thinner and lighter than conventional lenses. Durable Fresnel lenses can be molded from plastic and are inexpensive.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lenticular_lens" title="Lenticular lens">Lenticular lenses</a> are arrays of <a href="/wiki/Microlens" title="Microlens">microlenses</a> that are used in <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_printing" title="Lenticular printing">lenticular printing</a> to make images that have an illusion of depth or that change when viewed from different angles.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bifocal_lens" title="Bifocal lens">Bifocal lens</a> has two or more, or a graduated, focal lengths ground into the lens.
</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gradient_index_lens" title="Gradient index lens">gradient index lens</a> has flat optical surfaces, but has a radial or axial variation in index of refraction that causes light passing through the lens to be focused.
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Axicon" title="Axicon">axicon</a> has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cone_(geometry)" title="Cone (geometry)">conical</a> optical surface. It images a <a href="/wiki/Point_source" title="Point source">point source</a> into a line <i>along</i> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optic_axis" title="Optic axis">optic axis</a>, or transforms a laser beam into a ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proteep_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proteep-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffractive_optical_element" title="Diffractive optical element">Diffractive optical elements</a> can function as lenses.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Superlens" title="Superlens">Superlenses</a> are made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_index_metamaterials" title="Negative index metamaterials">negative index metamaterials</a> and claim to produce images at spatial resolutions exceeding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffraction_limit" title="Diffraction limit">diffraction limit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grbic_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grbic-28">[28]</a></sup> The first superlenses were made in 2004 using such a <a href="/wiki/Metamaterial" title="Metamaterial">metamaterial</a> for microwaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grbic_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grbic-28">[28]</a></sup> Improved versions have been made by other researchers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valenitne-J._29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Valenitne-J.-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the superlens has not yet been demonstrated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_frequency" title="Visible frequency">visible</a> or near-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mielsen10_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-mielsen10-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>A prototype flat ultrathin lens, with no curvature has been developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A single convex lens mounted in a frame with a handle or stand is a <a href="/wiki/Magnifying_glass" title="Magnifying glass">magnifying glass</a>.
</p><p>Lenses are used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosthetic" title="Prosthetic">prosthetics</a> for the correction of <a href="/wiki/Refractive_error" title="Refractive error">refractive errors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Myopia" title="Myopia">myopia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypermetropia" title="Hypermetropia">hypermetropia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Presbyopia" title="Presbyopia">presbyopia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism_(optical_systems)" title="Astigmatism (optical systems)">astigmatism</a>. (See <a href="/wiki/Corrective_lens" title="Corrective lens">corrective lens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contact_lens" title="Contact lens">contact lens</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyeglasses" title="Eyeglasses">eyeglasses</a>.) Most lenses used for other purposes have strict <a href="/wiki/Axial_symmetry" title="Axial symmetry">axial symmetry</a>; eyeglass lenses are only approximately symmetric. They are usually shaped to fit in a roughly oval, not circular, frame; the optical centres are placed over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_eyeball" title="Human eyeball">eyeballs</a>; their curvature may not be axially symmetric to correct for <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism_(optical_systems)" title="Astigmatism (optical systems)">astigmatism</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunglass_lens" title="Sunglass lens">Sunglasses' lenses</a> are designed to attenuate light; sunglass lenses that also correct visual impairments can be custom made.
</p><p>Other uses are in imaging systems such as <a href="/wiki/Monocular" title="Monocular">monoculars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">telescopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">cameras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Movie_projector" title="Movie projector">projectors</a>. Some of these instruments produce a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_image" title="Virtual image">virtual image</a> when applied to the human eye; others produce a <a href="/wiki/Real_image" title="Real image">real image</a> that can be captured on <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a> or an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_sensor" title="Optical sensor">optical sensor</a>, or can be viewed on a screen. In these devices lenses are sometimes paired up with <a href="/wiki/Curved_mirror" title="Curved mirror">curved mirrors</a> to make a <a href="/wiki/Catadioptric_system" title="Catadioptric system">catadioptric system</a> where the lens's spherical aberration corrects the opposite aberration in the mirror (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schmidt_corrector_plate" title="Schmidt corrector plate">Schmidt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meniscus_corrector" title="Meniscus corrector">meniscus</a> correctors).
</p><p>Convex lenses produce an image of an object at infinity at their focus; if the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> is imaged, much of the visible and infrared light incident on the lens is concentrated into the small image. A large lens creates enough intensity to burn a flammable object at the focal point. Since ignition can be achieved even with a poorly made lens, lenses have been used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burning-glass" title="Burning-glass">burning-glasses</a> for at least 2400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Clouds_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Clouds-7">[7]</a></sup> A modern application is the use of relatively large lenses to <a href="/wiki/Concentrator_photovoltaics" title="Concentrator photovoltaics">concentrate solar energy</a> on relatively small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_cell" title="Photovoltaic cell">photovoltaic cells</a>, harvesting more energy without the need to use larger and more expensive cells.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">Radio astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> systems often use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_lens" title="Dielectric lens">dielectric lenses</a>, commonly called a <a href="/wiki/Lens_antenna" title="Lens antenna">lens antenna</a> to refract <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> into a collector antenna.
</p><p>Lenses can become scratched and abraded. <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(mechanical)" title="Abrasion (mechanical)">Abrasion</a>-resistant coatings are available to help control this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
