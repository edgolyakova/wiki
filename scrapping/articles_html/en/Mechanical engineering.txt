<p><b>Mechanical engineering</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_branch" title="Engineering branch">engineering branch</a> that combines <a href="/wiki/Engineering_physics" title="Engineering physics">engineering physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering_mathematics" title="Engineering mathematics">mathematics</a> principles with <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, analyze, manufacture, and maintain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_system" title="Mechanical system">mechanical systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is one of the oldest and broadest of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_engineering_branches" title="List of engineering branches">engineering branches</a>.
</p><p>The mechanical engineering field requires an understanding of core areas including <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analytical_dynamics" title="Analytical dynamics">dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_analysis" title="Structural analysis">structural analysis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. In addition to these core principles, mechanical engineers use tools such as <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> (CAD), <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">computer-aided manufacturing</a> (CAM), and <a href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle" title="Product lifecycle">product lifecycle</a> management to design and analyze <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacturing_plants" title="Manufacturing plants">manufacturing plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_equipment" title="Industrial equipment">industrial equipment</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_machinery" title="Industrial machinery">machinery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">heating and cooling systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">watercraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_devices" title="Medical devices">medical devices</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons" title="Weapons">weapons</a>, and others. It is the branch of engineering that involves the design, production, and operation of <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machinery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mechanical engineering emerged as a field during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> in Europe in the 18th century; however, its development can be traced back several thousand years around the world. In the 19th century, developments in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> led to the development of mechanical engineering science. The field has continually evolved to incorporate advancements; today mechanical engineers are pursuing developments in such areas as <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">mechatronics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>. It also overlaps with <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallurgical_engineering" title="Metallurgical engineering">metallurgical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">structural engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing_engineering" title="Manufacturing engineering">manufacturing engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">industrial engineering</a>, and other engineering disciplines to varying amounts. Mechanical engineers may also work in the field of <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a>, specifically with <a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">biomechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transport_phenomena" title="Transport phenomena">transport phenomena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomechatronics" title="Biomechatronics">biomechatronics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bionanotechnology" title="Bionanotechnology">bionanotechnology</a>, and modelling of biological systems.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_mechanical_engineering" title="History of mechanical engineering">History of mechanical engineering</a></div>
<p>The application of mechanical engineering can be seen in the archives of various ancient and medieval societies. The six classic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_machines" title="Simple machines">simple machines</a> were known in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">ancient Near East</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wedge_(mechanical_device)" title="Wedge (mechanical device)">wedge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inclined_plane" title="Inclined plane">inclined plane</a> (ramp) were known since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheel</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Wheel_and_axle" title="Wheel and axle">wheel and axle</a> mechanism, was invented in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (modern Iraq) during the 5th millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">lever</a> mechanism first appeared around 5,000 years ago in the Near East, where it was used in a simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balance_scale" title="Balance scale">balance scale</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paipetis_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paipetis-6">[6]</a></sup> and to move large objects in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_technology" title="Ancient Egyptian technology">ancient Egyptian technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The lever was also used in the <a href="/wiki/Shadoof" title="Shadoof">shadoof</a> water-lifting device, the first <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">crane</a> machine, which appeared in Mesopotamia circa 3000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paipetis_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paipetis-6">[6]</a></sup> The earliest evidence of <a href="/wiki/Pulley" title="Pulley">pulleys</a> date back to Mesopotamia in the early 2nd millennium BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">Sakia</a> was developed in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kingdom of Kush</a> during the 4th century BC. It relied on animal power reducing the tow on the requirement of human energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">Reservoirs</a> in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafirs" title="Hafirs">Hafirs</a> were developed in Kush to store water and boost irrigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hinkel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hinkel-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">Bloomeries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> were developed during the seventh century BC in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meroe" title="Meroe">Meroe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphris_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humphris-14">[14]</a></sup> Kushite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sundials" title="Sundials">sundials</a> applied mathematics in the form of advanced trigonometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest practical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water-power" title="Water-power">water-powered</a> machines, the <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermill</a>, first appeared in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian Empire</a>, in what are now Iraq and Iran, by the early 4th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>, the works of <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> (287–212 BC) influenced mechanics in the Western tradition. In <a href="/wiki/Roman_Egypt" title="Roman Egypt">Roman Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Heron of Alexandria</a> (c. 10–70 AD) created the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam-powered" title="Steam-powered">steam-powered</a> device (<a href="/wiki/Aeolipile" title="Aeolipile">Aeolipile</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/History_of_China#Xin_dynasty" title="History of China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">Zhang Heng</a> (78–139 AD) improved a <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">water clock</a> and invented a <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismometer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ma_Jun_(mechanical_engineer)" title="Ma Jun (mechanical engineer)">Ma Jun</a> (200–265 AD) invented a chariot with <a href="/wiki/Differential_(mechanical_device)" title="Differential (mechanical device)">differential</a> gears. The medieval Chinese horologist and engineer <a href="/wiki/Su_Song" title="Su Song">Su Song</a> (1020–1101 AD) incorporated an <a href="/wiki/Escapement" title="Escapement">escapement</a> mechanism into his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical" title="Astronomical">astronomical</a> <a href="/wiki/Clock_tower" title="Clock tower">clock tower</a> two centuries before escapement devices were found in medieval European clocks. He also invented the world's first known endless power-transmitting <a href="/wiki/Chain_drive" title="Chain drive">chain drive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a> (7th to 15th century), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inventions_in_medieval_Islam" title="Inventions in medieval Islam">Muslim inventors</a> made remarkable contributions in the field of mechanical technology. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Jazari" title="Al-Jazari">Al-Jazari</a>, who was one of them, wrote his famous <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ingenious_Devices" title="Book of Ingenious Devices">Book of Ingenious Devices</a></i> in 1206 and presented many mechanical designs. 
</p><p>In the 17th century, important breakthroughs in the foundations of mechanical engineering occurred in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Continent</a>. The Dutch mathematician and physicist <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Pendulum_clock" title="Pendulum clock">pendulum clock</a> in 1657, which was the first reliable timekeeper for almost 300 years, and <a href="/wiki/Horologium_Oscillatorium" title="Horologium Oscillatorium">published a work</a> dedicated to clock designs and the theory behind them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In England, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> formulated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_Laws_of_Motion" title="Newton's Laws of Motion">Newton's Laws of Motion</a> and developed the <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>, which would become the mathematical basis of physics. Newton was reluctant to publish his works for years, but he was finally persuaded to do so by his colleagues, such as <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">Edmond Halley</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a>, who earlier designed a <a href="/wiki/Stepped_reckoner" title="Stepped reckoner">mechanical calculator</a>, is also credited with developing the calculus during the same time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the early 19th century Industrial Revolution, <a href="/wiki/Machine_tool" title="Machine tool">machine tools</a> were developed in England, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. This allowed mechanical engineering to develop as a separate field within engineering. They brought with them manufacturing machines and the engines to power them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The first British professional society of mechanical engineers was formed in 1847 <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="Institution of Mechanical Engineers">Institution of Mechanical Engineers</a>, thirty years after the civil engineers formed the first such professional society <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Civil_Engineers" title="Institution of Civil Engineers">Institution of Civil Engineers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_continent" title="European continent">European continent</a>, Johann von Zimmermann (1820–1901) founded the first factory for grinding machines in <a href="/wiki/Chemnitz" title="Chemnitz">Chemnitz</a>, Germany in 1848.
</p><p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="American Society of Mechanical Engineers">American Society of Mechanical Engineers</a> (ASME) was formed in 1880, becoming the third such professional engineering society, after the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a> (1852) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Mining_Engineers" title="American Institute of Mining Engineers">American Institute of Mining Engineers</a> (1871).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The first schools in the United States to offer an engineering education were the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Academy" title="United States Military Academy">United States Military Academy</a> in 1817, an institution now known as <a href="/wiki/Norwich_University" title="Norwich University">Norwich University</a> in 1819, and <a href="/wiki/Rensselaer_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute">Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute</a> in 1825. Education in mechanical engineering has historically been based on a strong foundation in mathematics and science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="365" data-file-width="505" decoding="async" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif/220px-Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif/330px-Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif/440px-Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Archimedes-screw_one-screw-threads_with-ball_3D-view_animated_small.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_screw" title="Archimedes' screw">Archimedes' screw</a> was operated by hand and could efficiently raise water, as the animated red ball demonstrates.</div></div></div>
<p>Degrees in mechanical engineering are offered at various universities worldwide. Mechanical engineering programs typically take four to five years of study depending on the place and university and result in a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Engineering" title="Bachelor of Engineering">Bachelor of Engineering</a> (B.Eng. or B.E.), <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> (B.Sc. or B.S.), Bachelor of Science Engineering (B.Sc.Eng.), <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Technology" title="Bachelor of Technology">Bachelor of Technology</a> (B.Tech.), Bachelor of Mechanical Engineering (B.M.E.), or <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Applied_Science" title="Bachelor of Applied Science">Bachelor of Applied Science</a> (B.A.Sc.) degree, in or with emphasis in mechanical engineering. In Spain, Portugal and most of South America, where neither B.S. nor B.Tech. programs have been adopted, the formal name for the degree is "Mechanical Engineer", and the course work is based on five or six years of training. In Italy the course work is based on five years of education, and training, but in order to qualify as an Engineer one has to pass a state exam at the end of the course. In Greece, the coursework is based on a five-year curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, most <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate</a> mechanical engineering programs are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_accreditation" title="School accreditation">accredited</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accreditation_Board_for_Engineering_and_Technology" title="Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology">Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology</a> (ABET) to ensure similar course requirements and standards among universities. The ABET web site lists 302 accredited mechanical engineering programs as of 11 March 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Mechanical engineering programs in Canada are accredited by the Canadian Engineering Accreditation Board (CEAB),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and most other countries offering engineering degrees have similar accreditation societies.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, mechanical engineering degrees are awarded as Bachelor of Engineering (Mechanical) or similar nomenclature, although there are an increasing number of specialisations. The degree takes four years of full-time study to achieve. To ensure quality in engineering degrees, <a href="/wiki/Engineers_Australia" title="Engineers Australia">Engineers Australia</a> accredits engineering degrees awarded by Australian universities in accordance with the global <a href="/wiki/Washington_Accord_(credentials)" title="Washington Accord (credentials)">Washington Accord</a>. Before the degree can be awarded, the student must complete at least 3 months of on the job work experience in an engineering firm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Similar systems are also present in South Africa and are overseen by the Engineering Council of South Africa (ECSA).
</p><p>In India, to become an engineer, one needs to have an engineering degree like a B.Tech or B.E, have a diploma in engineering, or by completing a course in an engineering trade like fitter from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_Training_Institute" title="Industrial Training Institute">Industrial Training Institute</a> (ITIs) to receive a "ITI Trade Certificate" and also pass the All India Trade Test (AITT) with an engineering trade conducted by the National Council of Vocational Training (NCVT) by which one is awarded a "National Trade Certificate". A similar system is used in Nepal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some mechanical engineers go on to pursue a postgraduate degree such as a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Engineering" title="Master of Engineering">Master of Engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_of_Technology" title="Master of Technology">Master of Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Engineering_Management" title="Master of Engineering Management">Master of Engineering Management</a> (M.Eng.Mgt. or M.E.M.), a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> in engineering (Eng.D. or Ph.D.) or an <a href="/wiki/Engineer%27s_degree" title="Engineer's degree">engineer's degree</a>. The master's and engineer's degrees may or may not include <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>. The Doctor of Philosophy includes a significant research component and is often viewed as the entry point to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">academia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The Engineer's degree exists at a few institutions at an intermediate level between the master's degree and the doctorate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coursework">Coursework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coursework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Standards set by each country's accreditation society are intended to provide uniformity in fundamental subject material, promote competence among graduating engineers, and to maintain confidence in the engineering profession as a whole. Engineering programs in the U.S., for example, are required by ABET to show that their students can "work professionally in both thermal and mechanical systems areas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The specific courses required to graduate, however, may differ from program to program. Universities and <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_technology" title="Institute of technology">institutes of technology</a> will often combine multiple subjects into a single class or split a subject into multiple classes, depending on the faculty available and the university's major area(s) of research.
</p><p>The fundamental subjects required for mechanical engineering usually include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematics</a> (in particular, <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_equations" title="Differential equations">differential equations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>)</li>
<li>Basic physical sciences (including <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Statics" title="Statics">Statics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">Strength of materials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solid_mechanics" title="Solid mechanics">solid mechanics</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Materials_engineering" title="Materials engineering">Materials engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composites</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" title="Energy conversion">energy conversion</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuels" title="Fuels">Fuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">Fluid mechanics</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_statics" title="Fluid statics">fluid statics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering)" title="Mechanism (engineering)">Mechanism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machine_(mechanical)" title="Machine (mechanical)">Machine</a> design (including <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">kinematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Instrumentation" title="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Manufacturing_engineering" title="Manufacturing engineering">Manufacturing engineering</a>, technology, or processes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">Vibration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Control_engineering" title="Control engineering">control engineering</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydraulics" title="Hydraulics">Hydraulics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pneumatics" title="Pneumatics">Pneumatics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">Mechatronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_design" title="Engineering design">Engineering design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Product_design" title="Product design">product design</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Engineering_drawing" title="Engineering drawing">Drafting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> (CAD) and <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">computer-aided manufacturing</a> (CAM)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Mechanical engineers are also expected to understand and be able to apply basic concepts from chemistry, physics, <a href="/wiki/Tribology" title="Tribology">tribology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>. All mechanical engineering programs include multiple semesters of mathematical classes including calculus, and advanced mathematical concepts including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_equations" title="Differential equations">differential equations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equations" title="Partial differential equations">partial differential equations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>, among others.
</p><p>In addition to the core mechanical engineering curriculum, many mechanical engineering programs offer more specialized programs and classes, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_systems" title="Control systems">control systems</a>, robotics, <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> technology, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_engineering" title="Automotive engineering">automotive engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">biomechanics</a>, vibration, <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a> and others, if a separate department does not exist for these subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most mechanical engineering programs also require varying amounts of research or community projects to gain practical problem-solving experience. In the United States it is common for mechanical engineering students to complete one or more <a href="/wiki/Internship" title="Internship">internships</a> while studying, though this is not typically mandated by the university. <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_education" title="Cooperative education">Cooperative education</a> is another option. Future work skills<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> research puts demand on study components that feed student's creativity and innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Job_duties">Job duties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Job duties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mechanical engineers research, design, develop, build, and test mechanical and thermal devices, including tools, engines, and machines.
</p><p>Mechanical engineers typically do the following:
</p>
<ul><li>Analyze problems to see how mechanical and thermal devices might help solve the problem.</li>
<li>Design or redesign mechanical and thermal devices using analysis and computer-aided design.</li>
<li>Develop and test prototypes of devices they design.</li>
<li>Analyze the test results and change the design as needed.</li>
<li>Oversee the manufacturing process for the device.</li>
<li>Manage a team of professionals in specialized fields like mechanical drafting and designing, prototyping, 3D printing or/and CNC Machines specialists.</li></ul>
<p>Mechanical engineers design and oversee the manufacturing of many products ranging from medical devices to new batteries. They also design power-producing machines such as electric generators, internal combustion engines, and steam and gas turbines as well as power-using machines, such as refrigeration and air-conditioning systems.
</p><p>Like other engineers, mechanical engineers use computers to help create and analyze designs, run simulations and test how a machine is likely to work.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="License_and_regulation">License and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: License and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Engineers may seek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Licensing" title="Licensing">license</a> by a state, provincial, or national government. The purpose of this process is to ensure that engineers possess the necessary technical knowledge, real-world experience, and knowledge of the local legal system to practice engineering at a professional level. Once certified, the engineer is given the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_Engineer" title="Professional Engineer">Professional Engineer</a> (United States, Canada, Japan, South Korea, Bangladesh and South Africa), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer" title="Chartered Engineer">Chartered Engineer</a> (in the United Kingdom, Ireland, India and Zimbabwe), <i>Chartered Professional Engineer</i> (in Australia and New Zealand) or <i>European Engineer</i> (much of the European Union).
</p><p>In the U.S., to become a licensed Professional Engineer (PE), an engineer must pass the comprehensive FE (Fundamentals of Engineering) exam, work a minimum of 4 years as an <i>Engineering Intern (EI)</i> or <i>Engineer-in-Training (EIT)</i>, and pass the "Principles and Practice" or PE (Practicing Engineer or Professional Engineer) exams. The requirements and steps of this process are set forth by the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Examiners_for_Engineering_and_Surveying" title="National Council of Examiners for Engineering and Surveying">National Council of Examiners for Engineering and Surveying</a> (NCEES), composed of engineering and land surveying licensing boards representing all U.S. states and territories.
</p><p>In the UK, current graduates require a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BEng" title="BEng">BEng</a> plus an appropriate master's degree or an integrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MEng" title="MEng">MEng</a> degree, a minimum of 4 years post graduate on the job competency development and a peer reviewed project report to become a Chartered Mechanical Engineer (CEng, MIMechE) through the <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="Institution of Mechanical Engineers">Institution of Mechanical Engineers</a>. CEng MIMechE can also be obtained via an examination route administered by the <a href="/wiki/City_and_Guilds_of_London_Institute" title="City and Guilds of London Institute">City and Guilds of London Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>In most developed countries, certain engineering tasks, such as the design of bridges, electric power plants, and chemical plants, must be approved by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_engineer" title="Professional engineer">professional engineer</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chartered_engineer" title="Chartered engineer">chartered engineer</a>. "Only a licensed engineer, for instance, may prepare, sign, seal and submit engineering plans and drawings to a public authority for approval, or to seal engineering work for public and private clients."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This requirement can be written into state and provincial legislation, such as in the Canadian provinces, for example the Ontario or Quebec's Engineer Act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In other countries, such as Australia, and the UK, no such legislation exists; however, practically all certifying bodies maintain a <a href="/wiki/Ethical_code" title="Ethical code">code of ethics</a> independent of legislation, that they expect all members to abide by or risk expulsion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FE_Exam" title="FE Exam">FE Exam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_Engineer" title="Professional Engineer">Professional Engineer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incorporated_Engineer" title="Incorporated Engineer">Incorporated Engineer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Washington_Accord" title="Washington Accord">Washington Accord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Regulation_and_licensure_in_engineering" title="Regulation and licensure in engineering">Regulation and licensure in engineering</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salaries_and_workforce_statistics">Salaries and workforce statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Salaries and workforce statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The total number of engineers employed in the U.S. in 2015 was roughly 1.6 million. Of these, 278,340 were mechanical engineers (17.28%), the largest discipline by size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 2012, the median annual income of mechanical engineers in the U.S. workforce was $80,580. The median income was highest when working for the government ($92,030), and lowest in education ($57,090).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In 2014, the total number of mechanical engineering jobs was projected to grow 5% over the next decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> As of 2009, the average starting salary was $58,800 with a bachelor's degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdisciplines">Subdisciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Subdisciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The field of mechanical engineering can be thought of as a collection of many mechanical engineering science disciplines. Several of these subdisciplines which are typically taught at the undergraduate level are listed below, with a brief explanation and the most common application of each. Some of these subdisciplines are unique to mechanical engineering, while others are a combination of mechanical engineering and one or more other disciplines. Most work that a mechanical engineer does uses skills and techniques from several of these subdisciplines, as well as specialized subdisciplines. Specialized subdisciplines, as used in this article, are more likely to be the subject of graduate studies or on-the-job training than undergraduate research. Several specialized subdisciplines are discussed in this section.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanics">Mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mohrs_circle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mohrs_circle.svg/220px-Mohrs_circle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mohrs_circle.svg/330px-Mohrs_circle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mohrs_circle.svg/440px-Mohrs_circle.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mohrs_circle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mohr%27s_circle" title="Mohr's circle">Mohr's circle</a>, a common tool to study <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stresses</a> in a mechanical element</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">Mechanics</a></div>
<p>Mechanics is, in the most general sense, the study of <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> and their effect upon <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>. Typically, engineering mechanics is used to analyze and predict the acceleration and deformation (both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_Deformation" title="Elastic Deformation">elastic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_Deformation" title="Plastic Deformation">plastic</a>) of objects under known forces (also called loads) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stresses</a>. Subdisciplines of mechanics include
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Statics" title="Statics">Statics</a>, the study of non-moving bodies under known loads, how forces affect static bodies</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">Dynamics</a> the study of how forces affect moving bodies. Dynamics includes kinematics (about movement, velocity, and acceleration) and kinetics (about forces and resulting accelerations).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanics_of_materials" title="Mechanics of materials">Mechanics of materials</a>, the study of how different materials deform under various types of stress</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">Fluid mechanics</a>, the study of how fluids react to forces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">Kinematics</a>, the study of the motion of bodies (objects) and systems (groups of objects), while ignoring the forces that cause the motion. Kinematics is often used in the design and analysis of <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering)" title="Mechanism (engineering)">mechanisms</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">Continuum mechanics</a>, a method of applying mechanics that assumes that objects are continuous (rather than <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/discrete" title="wikt:discrete">discrete</a>)</li></ul>
<p>Mechanical engineers typically use mechanics in the design or analysis phases of engineering. If the engineering project were the design of a vehicle, statics might be employed to design the frame of the vehicle, in order to evaluate where the stresses will be most intense. Dynamics might be used when designing the car's engine, to evaluate the forces in the <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">pistons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cam" title="Cam">cams</a> as the engine cycles. Mechanics of materials might be used to choose appropriate materials for the frame and engine. Fluid mechanics might be used to design a ventilation system for the vehicle (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a>), or to design the <a href="/wiki/Intake" title="Intake">intake</a> system for the engine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechatronics_and_robotics">Mechatronics and robotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mechatronics and robotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FMS1_small.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="448" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/FMS1_small.JPG/220px-FMS1_small.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/FMS1_small.JPG/330px-FMS1_small.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/FMS1_small.JPG/440px-FMS1_small.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FMS1_small.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Training FMS with learning robot <a href="/wiki/Intelitek" title="Intelitek">SCORBOT-ER 4u</a>, workbench CNC Mill and CNC Lathe</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">Mechatronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">Robotics</a></div>
<p>Mechatronics is a combination of mechanics and electronics. It is an interdisciplinary branch of mechanical engineering, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> that is concerned with integrating electrical and mechanical engineering to create hybrid automation systems. In this way, machines can be automated through the use of <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Servomechanism" title="Servomechanism">servo-mechanisms</a>, and other electrical systems in conjunction with special software. A common example of a mechatronics system is a CD-ROM drive. Mechanical systems open and close the drive, spin the CD and move the laser, while an optical system reads the data on the CD and converts it to <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bits</a>. Integrated software controls the process and communicates the contents of the CD to the computer.
</p><p>Robotics is the application of mechatronics to create robots, which are often used in industry to perform tasks that are dangerous, unpleasant, or repetitive. These robots may be of any shape and size, but all are preprogrammed and interact physically with the world. To create a robot, an engineer typically employs kinematics (to determine the robot's range of motion) and mechanics (to determine the stresses within the robot).
</p><p>Robots are used extensively in industrial automation engineering. They allow businesses to save money on labor, perform tasks that are either too dangerous or too precise for humans to perform them economically, and to ensure better quality. Many companies employ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assembly_lines" title="Assembly lines">assembly lines</a> of robots, especially in Automotive Industries and some factories are so robotized that they can run <a href="/wiki/Lights_out_(manufacturing)" title="Lights out (manufacturing)">by themselves</a>. Outside the factory, robots have been employed in bomb disposal, <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, and many other fields. Robots are also sold for various residential applications, from recreation to domestic applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_analysis">Structural analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Structural analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Structural_analysis" title="Structural analysis">Structural analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Failure_analysis" title="Failure analysis">Failure analysis</a></div>
<p>Structural analysis is the branch of mechanical engineering (and also civil engineering) devoted to examining why and how objects fail and to fix the objects and their performance. Structural failures occur in two general modes: static failure, and fatigue failure. <i>Static structural failure</i> occurs when, upon being loaded (having a force applied) the object being analyzed either breaks or is deformed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_deformation" title="Plastic deformation">plastically</a>, depending on the criterion for failure. <i>Fatigue failure</i> occurs when an object fails after a number of repeated loading and unloading cycles. Fatigue failure occurs because of imperfections in the object: a microscopic crack on the surface of the object, for instance, will grow slightly with each cycle (propagation) until the crack is large enough to cause <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_failure" title="Ultimate failure">ultimate failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Failure is not simply defined as when a part breaks, however; it is defined as when a part does not operate as intended. Some systems, such as the perforated top sections of some plastic bags, are designed to break. If these systems do not break, failure analysis might be employed to determine the cause.
</p><p>Structural analysis is often used by mechanical engineers after a failure has occurred, or when designing to prevent failure. Engineers often use online documents and books such as those published by ASM<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> to aid them in determining the type of failure and possible causes.
</p><p>Once theory is applied to a mechanical design, physical testing is often performed to verify calculated results. Structural analysis may be used in an office when designing parts, in the field to analyze failed parts, or in laboratories where parts might undergo controlled failure tests.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics_and_thermo-science">Thermodynamics and thermo-science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics and thermo-science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</a> is an applied science used in several branches of engineering, including mechanical and chemical engineering. At its simplest, thermodynamics is the study of energy, its use and transformation through a <a href="/wiki/Physical_system" title="Physical system">system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Typically, engineering thermodynamics is concerned with changing energy from one form to another. As an example, automotive engines convert chemical energy (<a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>) from the fuel into heat, and then into mechanical work that eventually turns the wheels.
</p><p>Thermodynamics principles are used by mechanical engineers in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermofluids" title="Thermofluids">thermofluids</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" title="Energy conversion">energy conversion</a>. Mechanical engineers use thermo-science to design <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a>, heating, ventilation, and air-conditioning (HVAC) systems, <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_sink" title="Heat sink">heat sinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiator" title="Radiator">radiators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_drafting">Design and drafting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Design and drafting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mech_2_3D.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="418" data-file-width="471" decoding="async" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mech_2_3D.png/220px-Mech_2_3D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mech_2_3D.png/330px-Mech_2_3D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mech_2_3D.png/440px-Mech_2_3D.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mech_2_3D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A CAD model of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_seal" title="Mechanical seal">mechanical double seal</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">Technical drawing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNC" title="CNC">CNC</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">Drafting</a> or technical drawing is the means by which mechanical engineers design products and create instructions for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacture" title="Manufacture">manufacturing</a> parts. A technical drawing can be a computer model or hand-drawn schematic showing all the dimensions necessary to manufacture a part, as well as assembly notes, a list of required materials, and other pertinent information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> A U.S. mechanical engineer or skilled worker who creates technical drawings may be referred to as a drafter or draftsman. Drafting has historically been a two-dimensional process, but <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> (CAD) programs now allow the designer to create in three dimensions.
</p><p>Instructions for manufacturing a part must be fed to the necessary machinery, either manually, through programmed instructions, or through the use of a <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">computer-aided manufacturing</a> (CAM) or combined CAD/CAM program. Optionally, an engineer may also manually manufacture a part using the technical drawings. However, with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Numerical_control#CNC_arrives" title="Numerical control">computer numerically controlled</a> (CNC) manufacturing, parts can now be fabricated without the need for constant technician input. Manually manufactured parts generally consist of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_spray" title="Thermal spray">spray coatings</a>, surface finishes, and other processes that cannot economically or practically be done by a machine.
</p><p>Drafting is used in nearly every subdiscipline of mechanical engineering, and by many other branches of engineering and architecture. Three-dimensional models created using CAD software are also commonly used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> (FEA) and <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">computational fluid dynamics</a> (CFD).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_tools">Modern tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cad_crank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="460" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Cad_crank.jpg/220px-Cad_crank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Cad_crank.jpg/330px-Cad_crank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Cad_crank.jpg/440px-Cad_crank.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cad_crank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An oblique view of a four-cylinder inline crankshaft with pistons</div></div></div>
<p>Many mechanical engineering companies, especially those in industrialized nations, have begun to incorporate <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_engineering" title="Computer-aided engineering">computer-aided engineering</a> (CAE) programs into their existing design and analysis processes, including 2D and 3D <a href="/wiki/Solid_modeling" title="Solid modeling">solid modeling</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> (CAD). This method has many benefits, including easier and more exhaustive visualization of products, the ability to create virtual assemblies of parts, and the ease of use in designing mating interfaces and tolerances.
</p><p>Other CAE programs commonly used by mechanical engineers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle_management" title="Product lifecycle management">product lifecycle management</a> (PLM) tools and analysis tools used to perform complex simulations. Analysis tools may be used to predict product response to expected loads, including fatigue life and manufacturability. These tools include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> (FEA), <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">computational fluid dynamics</a> (CFD), and <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">computer-aided manufacturing</a> (CAM).
</p><p>Using CAE programs, a mechanical design team can quickly and cheaply iterate the design process to develop a product that better meets cost, performance, and other constraints. No physical prototype need be created until the design nears completion, allowing hundreds or thousands of designs to be evaluated, instead of a relative few. In addition, CAE analysis programs can model complicated physical phenomena which cannot be solved by hand, such as <a href="/wiki/Viscoelasticity" title="Viscoelasticity">viscoelasticity</a>, complex contact between mating parts, or <a href="/wiki/Non-Newtonian_fluid" title="Non-Newtonian fluid">non-Newtonian flows</a>.
</p><p>As mechanical engineering begins to merge with other disciplines, as seen in <a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">mechatronics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary_design_optimization" title="Multidisciplinary design optimization">multidisciplinary design optimization</a> (MDO) is being used with other CAE programs to automate and improve the iterative design process. MDO tools wrap around existing CAE processes, allowing product evaluation to continue even after the analyst goes home for the day. They also utilize sophisticated optimization algorithms to more intelligently explore possible designs, often finding better, innovative solutions to difficult multidisciplinary design problems.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_research">Areas of research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Areas of research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mechanical engineers are constantly pushing the boundaries of what is physically possible in order to produce safer, cheaper, and more efficient machines and mechanical systems. Some technologies at the cutting edge of mechanical engineering are listed below (see also <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_engineering" title="Exploratory engineering">exploratory engineering</a>).
</p>
<h3><span id="Micro_electro-mechanical_systems_.28MEMS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Micro_electro-mechanical_systems_(MEMS)">Micro electro-mechanical systems (MEMS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Micro electro-mechanical systems (MEMS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Micron-scale mechanical components such as springs, gears, fluidic and heat transfer devices are fabricated from a variety of substrate materials such as silicon, glass and polymers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SU8" title="SU8">SU8</a>. Examples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MEMS" title="MEMS">MEMS</a> components are the accelerometers that are used as car airbag sensors, modern cell phones, gyroscopes for precise positioning and microfluidic devices used in biomedical applications.
</p>
<h3><span id="Friction_stir_welding_.28FSW.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Friction_stir_welding_(FSW)">Friction stir welding (FSW)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Friction stir welding (FSW)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Friction_stir_welding" title="Friction stir welding">Friction stir welding</a></div>
<p>Friction stir welding, a new type of <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>, was discovered in 1991 by <a href="/wiki/The_Welding_Institute" title="The Welding Institute">The Welding Institute</a> (TWI). The innovative steady state (non-fusion) welding technique joins materials previously un-weldable, including several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>. It plays an important role in the future construction of airplanes, potentially replacing rivets. Current uses of this technology to date include welding the seams of the aluminum main Space Shuttle external tank, Orion Crew Vehicle, Boeing Delta II and Delta IV Expendable Launch Vehicles and the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> Falcon 1 rocket, armor plating for amphibious assault ships, and welding the wings and fuselage panels of the new Eclipse 500 aircraft from Eclipse Aviation among an increasingly growing pool of uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composites">Composites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Composites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg/220px-Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg/330px-Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg/440px-Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kohlenstofffasermatte.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Composite cloth consisting of woven carbon fiber</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">Composite material</a></div>
<p>Composites or composite materials are a combination of materials which provide different physical characteristics than either material separately. Composite material research within mechanical engineering typically focuses on designing (and, subsequently, finding applications for) stronger or more rigid materials while attempting to reduce <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>, susceptibility to corrosion, and other undesirable factors. Carbon fiber reinforced composites, for instance, have been used in such diverse applications as spacecraft and fishing rods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechatronics">Mechatronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mechatronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">Mechatronics</a> is the synergistic combination of mechanical engineering, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_engineering" title="Electronic engineering">electronic engineering</a>, and software engineering. The discipline of mechatronics began as a way to combine mechanical principles with electrical engineering. Mechatronic concepts are used in the majority of electro-mechanical systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Typical electro-mechanical sensors used in mechatronics are strain gauges, thermocouples, and pressure transducers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Nanotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</a></div>
<p>At the smallest scales, mechanical engineering becomes nanotechnology—one speculative goal of which is to create a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_assembler" title="Molecular assembler">molecular assembler</a> to build molecules and materials via <a href="/wiki/Mechanosynthesis" title="Mechanosynthesis">mechanosynthesis</a>. For now that goal remains within <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_engineering" title="Exploratory engineering">exploratory engineering</a>. Areas of current mechanical engineering research in nanotechnology include nanofilters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> nanofilms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and nanostructures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> among others.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Picotechnology" title="Picotechnology">Picotechnology</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finite_element_analysis">Finite element analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Finite element analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" title="Finite element analysis">Finite element analysis</a></div>
<p>Finite Element Analysis is a computational tool used to estimate stress, strain, and deflection of solid bodies. It uses a mesh setup with user-defined sizes to measure physical quantities at a node. The more nodes there are, the higher the precision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> This field is not new, as the basis of Finite Element Analysis (FEA) or Finite Element Method (FEM) dates back to 1941. But the evolution of computers has made FEA/FEM a viable option for analysis of structural problems. Many commercial codes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASTRAN" title="NASTRAN">NASTRAN</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANSYS" title="ANSYS">ANSYS</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABAQUS" title="ABAQUS">ABAQUS</a> are widely used in industry for research and the design of components. Some 3D modeling and CAD software packages have added FEA modules. In the recent times, cloud simulation platforms like <a href="/wiki/SimScale" title="SimScale">SimScale</a> are becoming more common.
</p><p>Other techniques such as finite difference method (FDM) and finite-volume method (FVM) are employed to solve problems relating heat and mass transfer, fluid flows, fluid surface interaction, etc.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomechanics">Biomechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Biomechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">Biomechanics</a></div>
<p>Biomechanics is the application of mechanical principles to biological systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Biomechanics also aids in creating prosthetic limbs and artificial organs for humans. Biomechanics is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, because it often uses traditional engineering sciences to analyze biological systems. Some simple applications of <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials sciences</a> can supply correct approximations to the mechanics of many biological systems.
</p><p>In the past decade, reverse engineering of materials found in nature such as bone matter has gained funding in academia. The structure of bone matter is optimized for its purpose of bearing a large amount of compressive stress per unit weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The goal is to replace crude steel with bio-material for structural design.
</p><p>Over the past decade the <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_method" title="Finite element method">Finite element method</a> (FEM) has also entered the Biomedical sector highlighting further engineering aspects of Biomechanics. FEM has since then established itself as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/In_vivo" title="In vivo">in vivo</a> surgical assessment and gained the wide acceptance of academia. The main advantage of Computational Biomechanics lies in its ability to determine the endo-anatomical response of an anatomy, without being subject to ethical restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> This has led FE modelling to the point of becoming ubiquitous in several fields of Biomechanics while several projects have even adopted an open source philosophy (e.g. BioSpine).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_fluid_dynamics">Computational fluid dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Computational fluid dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">Computational fluid dynamics</a></div>
<p>Computational fluid dynamics, usually abbreviated as CFD, is a branch of fluid mechanics that uses numerical methods and algorithms to solve and analyze problems that involve fluid flows. Computers are used to perform the calculations required to simulate the interaction of liquids and gases with surfaces defined by boundary conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> With high-speed supercomputers, better solutions can be achieved. Ongoing research yields software that improves the accuracy and speed of complex simulation scenarios such as turbulent flows. Initial validation of such software is performed using a wind tunnel with the final validation coming in full-scale testing, e.g. flight tests.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustical_engineering">Acoustical engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Acoustical engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acoustical_engineering" title="Acoustical engineering">Acoustical engineering</a></div>
<p>Acoustical engineering is one of many other sub-disciplines of mechanical engineering and is the application of acoustics. Acoustical engineering is the study of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">Sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">Vibration</a>. These engineers work effectively to reduce <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> in mechanical devices and in buildings by soundproofing or removing sources of unwanted noise. The study of acoustics can range from designing a more efficient hearing aid, microphone, headphone, or recording studio to enhancing the sound quality of an orchestra hall. Acoustical engineering also deals with the vibration of different mechanical systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
