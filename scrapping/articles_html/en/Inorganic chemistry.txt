<p><b>Inorganic chemistry</b> deals with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">synthesis</a> and behavior of <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic</a> compounds. This field covers <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> that are not carbon-based, which are the subjects of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>. The distinction between the two disciplines is far from absolute, as there is much overlap in the subdiscipline of <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a>. It has applications in every aspect of the chemical industry, including <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coating" title="Coating">coatings</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">medications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_concepts">Key concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Key concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compounds</a> are <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a>, consisting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> joined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Examples of salts (which are ionic compounds) are <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_chloride" title="Magnesium chloride">magnesium chloride</a> MgCl<sub>2</sub>, which consists of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> cations Mg<sup>2+</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> anions Cl<sup>−</sup>; or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">sodium oxide</a> Na<sub>2</sub>O, which consists of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> cations Na<sup>+</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> anions O<sup>2−</sup>.  In any salt, the proportions of the ions are such that the electric charges cancel out, so that the bulk compound is electrically neutral. The ions are described by their <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> and their ease of formation can be inferred from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionization_potential" title="Ionization potential">ionization potential</a> (for cations) or from the <a href="/wiki/Electron_affinity" title="Electron affinity">electron affinity</a> (anions) of the parent elements.
</p><p>The strength of a bond in ionic compounds is known as <a href="/wiki/Lattice_energy" title="Lattice energy">lattice energy</a>. It is not possible to determine these values experimentally due to the number of conditions that could influence the reaction but it can be estimated using the <a href="/wiki/Born%E2%80%93Haber_cycle" title="Born–Haber cycle">Born-Haber cycle</a>
</p><p>Important classes of inorganic compounds are the <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a>.  Many inorganic compounds are characterized by high <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a>.  Inorganic salts typically are poor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">conductors</a> in the solid state but rise slightly while molten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Other important features include their high melting point and ease of <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallization</a>.  Where some salts (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">NaCl</a>) are very soluble in water, others (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Iron_sulfide" title="Iron sulfide">FeS</a>) are not.
</p><p>The simplest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemical_reaction" title="Inorganic chemical reaction">inorganic reaction</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_displacement_reaction" title="Double displacement reaction">double displacement</a> when in mixing of two salts the ions are swapped without a change in oxidation state. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redox_reaction" title="Redox reaction">redox reactions</a> one reactant, the <i>oxidant</i>, lowers its oxidation state and another reactant, the <i>reductant</i>, has its oxidation state increased. The net result is an exchange of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>. Electron exchange can occur indirectly as well, e.g., in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>, a key concept in <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>.
</p><p>When one reactant contains hydrogen atoms, a reaction can take place by exchanging protons in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction_theories" title="Acid-base reaction theories">acid-base chemistry</a>. In a more general definition, any chemical species capable of binding to electron pairs is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a>; conversely any molecule that tends to donate an electron pair is referred to as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_base" title="Lewis base">Lewis base</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As a refinement of acid-base interactions, the <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">HSAB theory</a> takes into account polarizability and size of ions.
</p><p>Inorganic compounds are found in nature as <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Soil may contain iron sulfide as <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> or calcium sulfate as <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Inorganic compounds are also found multitasking as <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a>: as electrolytes (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>), in energy storage (<a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>) or in construction (the <a href="/wiki/Polyphosphate" title="Polyphosphate">polyphosphate</a> backbone in <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>).
</p><p>The first important man-made inorganic compound was <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> for soil fertilization through the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Inorganic compounds are synthesized for use as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium(V) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titanium(III)_chloride" title="Titanium(III) chloride">titanium(III) chloride</a>, or as <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagents</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride" title="Lithium aluminium hydride">lithium aluminium hydride</a>.
</p><p>Subdivisions of inorganic chemistry are <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_chemistry" title="Cluster chemistry">cluster chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioinorganic_chemistry" title="Bioinorganic chemistry">bioinorganic chemistry</a>. These fields are active areas of research in inorganic chemistry, aimed toward new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">therapies</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_inorganic_chemistry">Industrial inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Industrial inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Inorganic chemistry is a highly practical area of science.  Traditionally, the scale of a nation's economy could be evaluated by their productivity of sulfuric acid. 
The manufacturing of fertilizers, which often begins with the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber-Bosch process</a>, is another practical application of industrial inorganic chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptive_inorganic_chemistry">Descriptive inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Descriptive inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Descriptive inorganic chemistry focuses on the classification of compounds based on their properties.  Partly the classification focuses on the position in the periodic table of the heaviest element (the element with the highest atomic weight) in the compound, partly by grouping compounds by their structural similarities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_compounds">Coordination compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coordination compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="636" decoding="async" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png/200px-CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png/300px-CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png/400px-CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CoEDTA-anion-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a> <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelates</a> an octahedrally coordinated <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">Co<sup>3+</sup></a> ion in <span class="nowrap">[Co(EDTA)]<sup>−</sup></span></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">Coordination chemistry</a></div>
<p>Classical coordination compounds feature metals bound to "<a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pairs</a>" of electrons residing on the main group atoms of ligands such as H<sub>2</sub>O, NH<sub>3</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">Cl<sup>−</sup></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">CN<sup>−</sup></a>.  In modern coordination compounds almost all organic and inorganic compounds can be used as ligands.  The "metal" usually is a metal from the groups 3–13, as well as the <i>trans</i>-<a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> and <i>trans</i>-<a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinides</a>, but from a certain perspective, all chemical compounds can be described as coordination complexes.
</p><p>The stereochemistry of coordination complexes can be quite rich, as hinted at by Werner's separation of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enantiomers" title="Enantiomers">enantiomers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hexol" title="Hexol">[Co((OH)<sub>2</sub>Co(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>]<sup>6+</sup></a>, an early demonstration that chirality is not inherent to organic compounds.  A topical theme within this specialization is supramolecular coordination chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: [Co(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a>)]<sup>−</sup>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_hexammine_chloride" title="Cobalt(III) hexammine chloride">[Co(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup></a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_tetrachloride" title="Titanium tetrachloride">TiCl<sub>4</sub></a>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/THF" title="THF">THF</a>)<sub>2</sub>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_group_compounds">Main group compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Main group compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1043" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png/200px-Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png/300px-Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png/400px-Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tetrasulfur-tetranitride-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tetrasulfur_tetranitride" title="Tetrasulfur tetranitride">Tetrasulfur tetranitride</a>, S<sub>4</sub>N<sub>4</sub>, is a main group compound that continues to intrigue chemists</div></div></div>
<p>These species feature elements from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_table_group" title="Periodic table group">groups</a> I, II, III, IV, V,VI, VII, 0 (excluding hydrogen) of the periodic table.  Due to their often similar reactivity, the elements in group 3 (<a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">Sc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Y</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">La</a>) and group 12 (<a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">Zn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">Cd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Hg</a>) are also generally included, and the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinides</a> are sometimes included as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Main group compounds have been known since the beginnings of chemistry, e.g., elemental <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and the distillable white <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>.  Experiments on oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>, by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Lavoisier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Priestley</a> not only identified an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a> gas, but opened the way for describing compounds and reactions according to <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometric</a> ratios.  The discovery of a practical synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> using iron catalysts by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bosch" title="Carl Bosch">Carl Bosch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Haber" title="Fritz Haber">Fritz Haber</a> in the early 1900s deeply impacted mankind, demonstrating the significance of inorganic chemical synthesis.
Typical main group compounds are SiO<sub>2</sub>, SnCl<sub>4</sub>, and N<sub>2</sub>O.  Many main group compounds can also be classed as "organometallic", as they contain organic groups, e.g., B(<a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">CH<sub>3</sub></a>)<sub>3</sub>).  Main group compounds also occur in nature, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> in <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, and therefore may be classed as bioinorganic.  Conversely, organic compounds lacking (many) hydrogen ligands can be classed as "inorganic", such as the fullerenes, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">buckytubes</a> and binary carbon oxides.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Tetrasulfur_tetranitride" title="Tetrasulfur tetranitride">tetrasulfur tetranitride</a> S<sub>4</sub>N<sub>4</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Diborane" title="Diborane">diborane</a> B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">buckminsterfullerene</a> C<sub>60</sub>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noble_gas_compounds">Noble gas compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Noble gas compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_gas_compounds" title="Noble gas compounds">Noble gas compounds</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">Noble gases</a> are elements which have filled <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electron</a> shells in their neutral state, and are thus stable as lone atoms.  Historically known as being inert, methods were discovered to react with them.  The trend within the group is for the larger elements to be more reactive.  <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">Xenon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a> are more easily ionized, and can combine with extremely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronegative" title="Electronegative">electronegative</a> elements to make fluorides and oxides and form solid ionic compounds.  <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">Argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> are much less reactive, though in <a href="/wiki/Cosmochemistry" title="Cosmochemistry">cosmochemistry</a> ArH<sup>+</sup> has been observed spectroscopically in interstellar gas.  Noble gases can also be trapped in solids while not being directly coordinated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrates" title="Clathrates">clathrates</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Endohedral_fullerene" title="Endohedral fullerene">endohedral fullerenes</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Xenon_hexafluoride" title="Xenon hexafluoride">xenon hexafluoride</a> XeF<sub>6</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Xenon_trioxide" title="Xenon trioxide">xenon trioxide</a> XeO<sub>3</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Krypton_difluoride" title="Krypton difluoride">krypton difluoride</a> KrF<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Argon_fluorohydride" title="Argon fluorohydride">argon fluorohydride</a> HArF</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_metal_compounds">Transition metal compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transition metal compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Compounds containing metals from group 4 to 11 are considered transition metal compounds.  Compounds with a metal from group 3 or 12 are sometimes also incorporated into this group, but also often classified as main group compounds.
</p><p>Transition metal compounds show a rich coordination chemistry, varying from tetrahedral for titanium (e.g., TiCl<sub>4</sub>) to square planar for some nickel complexes to octahedral for coordination complexes of cobalt.  A range of transition metals can be found in biologically important compounds, such as iron in hemoglobin.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Iron_pentacarbonyl" title="Iron pentacarbonyl">iron pentacarbonyl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_tetrachloride" title="Titanium tetrachloride">titanium tetrachloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cisplatin" title="Cisplatin">cisplatin</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organometallic_compounds">Organometallic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organometallic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1001" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png/200px-N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png/300px-N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png/400px-N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N-butyllithium-tetramer-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Organolithium_reagent" title="Organolithium reagent">Organolithium reagents</a> are most often found in polymeric form, such as <a href="/wiki/N-Butyllithium" title="N-Butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> shown here</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">Organometallic chemistry</a></div>
<p>Usually, organometallic compounds are considered to contain the M-C-H group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The metal (M) in these species can either be a main group element or a transition metal.  Operationally, the definition of an organometallic compound is more relaxed to include also highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophilic" title="Lipophilic">lipophilic</a> complexes such as <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">metal carbonyls</a> and even metal <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a>.
</p><p>Organometallic compounds are mainly considered a special category because organic ligands are often sensitive to hydrolysis or oxidation, necessitating that organometallic chemistry employs more specialized preparative methods than was traditional in Werner-type complexes.  Synthetic methodology, especially the ability to manipulate complexes in solvents of low coordinating power, enabled the exploration of very weakly coordinating ligands such as hydrocarbons, H<sub>2</sub>, and N<sub>2</sub>.  Because the ligands are petrochemicals in some sense, the area of organometallic chemistry has greatly benefited from its relevance to industry.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyliron_dicarbonyl_dimer" title="Cyclopentadienyliron dicarbonyl dimer">Cyclopentadienyliron dicarbonyl dimer</a> (C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)Fe(CO)<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>3</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrocene" title="Ferrocene">ferrocene</a> Fe(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_hexacarbonyl" title="Molybdenum hexacarbonyl">molybdenum hexacarbonyl</a> Mo(CO)<sub>6</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Triethylborane" title="Triethylborane">triethylborane</a> Et<sub>3</sub>B, <a href="/wiki/Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)" title="Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)">Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)</a> Pd<sub>2</sub>(dba)<sub>3</sub>)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cluster_compounds">Cluster compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cluster compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Decaborane-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="865" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Decaborane-3D-balls.png/200px-Decaborane-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Decaborane-3D-balls.png/300px-Decaborane-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Decaborane-3D-balls.png/400px-Decaborane-3D-balls.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Decaborane-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Decaborane" title="Decaborane">Decaborane</a> is a powerfully toxic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_compound" title="Cluster compound">cluster compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png/200px-Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png/300px-Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png/400px-Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fe4S4-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Iron%E2%80%93sulfur_cluster" title="Iron–sulfur cluster">Iron–sulfur clusters</a> are central components of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron%E2%80%93sulfur_protein" title="Iron–sulfur protein">iron–sulfur proteins</a>, essential for human <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_compound" title="Cluster compound">Cluster compound</a></div>
<p>Clusters can be found in all classes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a>.  According to the commonly accepted definition, a cluster consists minimally of a triangular set of atoms that are directly bonded to each other.  But metal-metal bonded dimetallic complexes are highly relevant to the area.  Clusters occur in "pure" inorganic systems, organometallic chemistry, main group chemistry, and bioinorganic chemistry.  The distinction between very large clusters and bulk solids is increasingly blurred.  This interface is the chemical basis of nanoscience or <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a> and specifically arise from the study of <a href="/wiki/Mesoscopic_physics" title="Mesoscopic physics">quantum size effects</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_selenide" title="Cadmium selenide">cadmium selenide</a> clusters. Thus, large clusters can be described as an array of bound atoms intermediate in character between a molecule and a solid.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Triiron_dodecacarbonyl" title="Triiron dodecacarbonyl">Fe<sub>3</sub>(CO)<sub>12</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Decaborane" title="Decaborane">B<sub>10</sub>H<sub>14</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum(II)_chloride" title="Molybdenum(II) chloride">[Mo<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>14</sub>]<sup>2−</sup></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron%E2%80%93sulfur_protein" title="Iron–sulfur protein">4Fe-4S</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioinorganic_compounds">Bioinorganic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bioinorganic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png/200px-Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png/300px-Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png/400px-Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vitamin-B12-Co-centre-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The octahedral <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> centre of <a href="/wiki/Cyanocobalamin" title="Cyanocobalamin">Vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioinorganic_chemistry" title="Bioinorganic chemistry">Bioinorganic chemistry</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bioorganometallic_chemistry" title="Bioorganometallic chemistry">Bioorganometallic chemistry</a></div>
<p>By definition, these compounds occur in nature, but the subfield includes anthropogenic species, such as pollutants (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a>) and drugs (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cisplatin" title="Cisplatin">Cisplatin</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  The field, which incorporates many aspects of biochemistry, includes many kinds of compounds, e.g., the phosphates in DNA, and also metal complexes containing ligands that range from biological macromolecules, commonly <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a>, to ill-defined species such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humic_acid" title="Humic acid">humic acid</a>, and to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (e.g., coordinated to <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> complexes employed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">MRI</a>).  Traditionally bioinorganic chemistry focuses on electron- and energy-transfer in proteins relevant to respiration.  Medicinal inorganic chemistry includes the study of both non-essential and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral">essential elements</a> with applications to diagnosis and therapies.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carboxypeptidase" title="Carboxypeptidase">carboxypeptidase</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_state_compounds">Solid state compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Solid state compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_chemistry" title="Solid-state chemistry">solid-state chemistry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:YBa2Cu3O7.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1514" data-file-width="1272" decoding="async" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/YBa2Cu3O7.png/150px-YBa2Cu3O7.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/YBa2Cu3O7.png/225px-YBa2Cu3O7.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/YBa2Cu3O7.png/300px-YBa2Cu3O7.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:YBa2Cu3O7.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Yttrium_barium_copper_oxide" title="Yttrium barium copper oxide">YBa<sub>2</sub>Cu<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a>, or YBCO, is a high temperature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductor</a> able to <a href="/wiki/Meissner_effect" title="Meissner effect">levitate</a> above a magnet when colder than its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_temperature" title="Critical temperature">critical temperature</a> of about 90 K (−183 °C)</div></div></div>
<p>This important area focuses on <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">structure</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> bonding, and the physical properties of materials.  In practice, solid state inorganic chemistry uses techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a> to gain an understanding of the properties that result from collective interactions between the subunits of the solid.  Included in solid state chemistry are metals and their <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> or intermetallic derivatives.  Related fields are <a href="/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics" title="Condensed matter physics">condensed matter physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>Examples: <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">silicon chips</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeolites" title="Zeolites">zeolites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_barium_copper_oxide" title="Yttrium barium copper oxide">YBa<sub>2</sub>Cu<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_inorganic_chemistry">Theoretical inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Theoretical inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternative perspective on the area of inorganic chemistry begins with the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">Bohr model</a> of the atom and, using the tools and models of <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_chemistry" title="Theoretical chemistry">theoretical chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computational_chemistry" title="Computational chemistry">computational chemistry</a>, expands into bonding in simple and then more complicated molecules.  Precise quantum mechanical descriptions for multielectron species, the province of inorganic chemistry, is difficult.  This challenge has spawned many semi-quantitative or semi-empirical approaches including <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital_theory" title="Molecular orbital theory">molecular orbital theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ligand_field_theory" title="Ligand field theory">ligand field theory</a>, In parallel with these theoretical descriptions, approximate methodologies are employed, including <a href="/wiki/Density_functional_theory" title="Density functional theory">density functional theory</a>.
</p><p>Exceptions to theories, qualitative and quantitative, are extremely important in the development of the field.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_acetate" title="Copper(II) acetate">Cu<sup>II</sup><sub>2</sub>(OAc)<sub>4</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub></a> is almost diamagnetic below room temperature whereas <a href="/wiki/Crystal_field_theory" title="Crystal field theory">crystal field theory</a> predicts that the molecule would have two unpaired electrons.  The disagreement between qualitative theory (paramagnetic) and observation (diamagnetic) led to the development of models for magnetic coupling, such as the exchange interaction.  These improved models led to the development of new magnetic materials and new technologies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualitative_theories">Qualitative theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Qualitative theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ferricyanide-3D.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="682" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ferricyanide-3D.png/200px-Ferricyanide-3D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ferricyanide-3D.png/300px-Ferricyanide-3D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ferricyanide-3D.png/400px-Ferricyanide-3D.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ferricyanide-3D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Crystal_field_theory" title="Crystal field theory">Crystal field theory</a> explains why <a href="/wiki/Ferricyanide" title="Ferricyanide">[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup></a> has only one unpaired electron</div></div></div>
<p>Inorganic chemistry has greatly benefited from qualitative theories.  Such theories are easier to learn as they require little background in quantum theory.  Within main group compounds, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VSEPR" title="VSEPR">VSEPR</a> theory powerfully predicts, or at least rationalizes, the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">structures</a> of main group compounds, such as an explanation for why <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<sub>3</sub></a> is pyramidal whereas <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_trifluoride" title="Chlorine trifluoride">ClF<sub>3</sub></a> is T-shaped.  For the transition metals, <a href="/wiki/Crystal_field_theory" title="Crystal field theory">crystal field theory</a> allows one to understand the magnetism of many simple complexes, such as why <a href="/wiki/Ferricyanide" title="Ferricyanide">[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup></a> has only one unpaired electron, whereas [Fe<sup>III</sup>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> has five.  A particularly powerful qualitative approach to assessing the structure and reactivity begins with classifying molecules according to <a href="/wiki/Electron_counting" title="Electron counting">electron counting</a>, focusing on the numbers of <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a>, usually at the central atom in a molecule.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_symmetry_group_theory">Molecular symmetry group theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Molecular symmetry group theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="819" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png/200px-Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png/300px-Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png/400px-Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nitrogen-dioxide-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">Nitrogen dioxide</a>, NO<sub>2</sub>, exhibits <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group"><i>C<sub>2v</sub></i> symmetry</a></div></div></div>
<p>A central construct in inorganic chemistry is the theory of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Mathematical <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a> provides the language to describe the shapes of molecules according to their <a href="/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions" title="Point groups in three dimensions">point group symmetry</a>.  Group theory also enables factoring and simplification of theoretical calculations.
</p><p>Spectroscopic features are analyzed and described with respect to the symmetry properties of the, <i>inter alia</i>, vibrational or electronic states. Knowledge of the symmetry properties of the ground and excited states allows one to predict the numbers and intensities of absorptions in vibrational and electronic spectra.  A classic application of group theory is the prediction of the number of C-O vibrations in substituted metal carbonyl complexes.  The most common applications of symmetry to spectroscopy involve vibrational and electronic spectra.
</p><p>Group theory highlights commonalities and differences in the bonding of otherwise disparate species.  For example, the metal-based orbitals transform identically for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten(VI)_fluoride" title="Tungsten(VI) fluoride">WF<sub>6</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_hexacarbonyl" title="Tungsten hexacarbonyl">W(CO)<sub>6</sub></a>, but the energies and populations of these orbitals differ significantly.  A similar relationship exists <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> and molecular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beryllium_difluoride" title="Beryllium difluoride">beryllium difluoride</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics_and_inorganic_chemistry">Thermodynamics and inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics and inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternative quantitative approach to inorganic chemistry focuses on energies of reactions.  This approach is highly traditional and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">empirical</a>, but it is also useful.  Broad concepts that are couched in thermodynamic terms include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redox_potential" title="Redox potential">redox potential</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> changes.  A classic concept in inorganic thermodynamics is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Born-Haber_cycle" title="Born-Haber cycle">Born-Haber cycle</a>, which is used for assessing the energies of elementary processes such as <a href="/wiki/Electron_affinity" title="Electron affinity">electron affinity</a>, some of which cannot be observed directly.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanistic_inorganic_chemistry">Mechanistic inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mechanistic inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An important aspect of inorganic chemistry focuses on reaction pathways, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Reaction_mechanism" title="Reaction mechanism">reaction mechanisms</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_group_elements_and_lanthanides">Main group elements and lanthanides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Main group elements and lanthanides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mechanisms of main group compounds of groups 13-18 are usually discussed in the context of organic chemistry (organic compounds are main group compounds, after all).  Elements heavier than C, N, O, and F often form compounds with more electrons than predicted by the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a>, as explained in the article on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypervalent" title="Hypervalent">hypervalent</a> molecules.  The mechanisms of their reactions differ from organic compounds for this reason.  Elements lighter than <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">Be</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">Li</a>) as well as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Al</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a> often form electron-deficient structures that are electronically akin to <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a>.  Such electron-deficient species tend to react via associative pathways.  The chemistry of the lanthanides mirrors many aspects of chemistry seen for aluminium.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_metal_complexes">Transition metal complexes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transition metal complexes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transition metal and main group compounds often react differently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The important role of d-orbitals in bonding strongly influences the pathways and rates of ligand substitution and dissociation.  These themes are covered in articles on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">coordination chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a>.  Both associative and dissociative pathways are observed.
</p><p>An overarching aspect of mechanistic transition metal chemistry is the kinetic lability of the complex illustrated by the exchange of free and bound water in the prototypical complexes [M(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>n+</sup>:
</p>
<dl><dd>[M(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>n+</sup>  + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O*  →  [M(H<sub>2</sub>O*)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>n+</sup>  +  6 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>where H<sub>2</sub>O* denotes <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopically</a> enriched water, e.g., H<sub>2</sub><sup>17</sup>O</dd></dl>
<p>The rates of water exchange varies by 20 orders of magnitude across the periodic table, with lanthanide complexes at one extreme and Ir(III) species being the slowest.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_reactions">Redox reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Redox reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Redox reactions are prevalent for the transition elements.  Two classes of redox reaction are considered: atom-transfer reactions, such as oxidative addition/reductive elimination, and <a href="/wiki/Electron_transfer" title="Electron transfer">electron-transfer</a>.  A fundamental redox reaction is "self-exchange", which involves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degenerate_energy_level" title="Degenerate energy level">degenerate</a> reaction between an oxidant and a reductant.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a> and its one-electron reduced relative <a href="/wiki/Potassium_manganate" title="Potassium manganate">manganate</a> exchange one electron:
</p>
<dl><dd>[MnO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>−</sup> +  [Mn*O<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>  →  [MnO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> +  [Mn*O<sub>4</sub>]<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_at_ligands">Reactions at ligands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Reactions at ligands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Coordinated ligands display reactivity distinct from the free ligands.  For example, the acidity of the ammonia ligands in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_hexammine_chloride" title="Cobalt(III) hexammine chloride">[Co(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup></a> is elevated relative to NH<sub>3</sub> itself.  Alkenes bound to metal cations are reactive toward nucleophiles whereas alkenes normally are not.  The large and industrially important area of <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> hinges on the ability of metals to modify the reactivity of organic ligands.  <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_catalysis" title="Homogeneous catalysis">Homogeneous catalysis</a> occurs in solution and <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous catalysis</a> occurs when <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solution_(chemistry)" title="Solution (chemistry)">dissolved</a> substrates interact with surfaces of solids.  Traditionally <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_catalysis" title="Homogeneous catalysis">homogeneous catalysis</a> is considered part of organometallic chemistry and <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous catalysis</a> is discussed in the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_chemistry" title="Surface chemistry">surface science</a>, a subfield of solid state chemistry.  But the basic inorganic chemical principles are the same.  Transition metals, almost uniquely, react with small molecules such as CO, H<sub>2</sub>, O<sub>2</sub>, and C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>.  The industrial significance of these feedstocks drives the active area of catalysis.  Ligands can also undergo ligand transfer reactions such as <a href="/wiki/Transmetalation" title="Transmetalation">transmetalation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization_of_inorganic_compounds">Characterization of inorganic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Characterization of inorganic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the diverse range of elements and the correspondingly diverse properties of the resulting derivatives, inorganic chemistry is closely associated with many methods of analysis.  Older methods tended to examine bulk properties such as the electrical conductivity of solutions, <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a>.  With the advent of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a> and the corresponding expansion of electronic apparatus, new tools have been introduced to probe the electronic properties of inorganic molecules and solids.  Often these measurements provide insights relevant to theoretical models. Commonly encountered techniques are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>: This technique allows for the 3D determination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_structure" title="Molecular structure">molecular structures</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_polarisation_interferometer" title="Dual polarisation interferometer">Dual polarisation interferometer</a>: This technique measures the <a href="/wiki/Conformational_isomerism" title="Conformational isomerism">conformation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conformational_change" title="Conformational change">conformational change</a> of molecules.</li>
<li>Various forms of <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>:
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet-visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy">Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy</a>: Historically, this has been an important tool, since many inorganic compounds are strongly colored</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>: Besides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-1" title="Hydrogen-1"><sup>1</sup>H</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13"><sup>13</sup>C</a> many other NMR-active nuclei (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron-11" title="Boron-11"><sup>11</sup>B</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorine-19" title="Fluorine-19"><sup>19</sup>F</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphorus-31" title="Phosphorus-31"><sup>31</sup>P</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum-195" title="Platinum-195"><sup>195</sup>Pt</a>) can give important information on compound properties and structure.  The NMR of paramagnetic species can provide important structural information. Proton (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-1" title="Hydrogen-1"><sup>1</sup>H</a>) NMR is also important because the light hydrogen nucleus is not easily detected by X-ray crystallography.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">Infrared spectroscopy</a>: Mostly for absorptions from <a href="/wiki/Category:Carbonyl_complexes" title="Category:Carbonyl complexes">carbonyl ligands</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electron_nuclear_double_resonance" title="Electron nuclear double resonance">Electron nuclear double resonance</a> (ENDOR) spectroscopy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_spectroscopy" title="Mössbauer spectroscopy">Mössbauer spectroscopy</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron-spin_resonance" title="Electron-spin resonance">Electron-spin resonance</a>: ESR (or EPR) allows for the measurement of the environment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> metal centres.</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical" title="Electrochemical">Electrochemistry</a>: <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_voltammetry" title="Cyclic voltammetry">Cyclic voltammetry</a> and related techniques probe the redox characteristics of compounds.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_inorganic_chemistry">Synthetic inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inorganic_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Synthetic inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although some inorganic species can be obtained in pure form from nature, most are synthesized in chemical plants and in the laboratory.
</p><p>Inorganic synthetic methods can be classified roughly according to the volatility or solubility of the component reactants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  Soluble inorganic compounds are prepared using methods of <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>.  For metal-containing compounds that are reactive toward air, <a href="/wiki/Schlenk_line" title="Schlenk line">Schlenk line</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glove_box" title="Glove box">glove box</a> techniques are followed. Volatile compounds and gases are manipulated in "vacuum manifolds" consisting of glass piping interconnected through valves, the entirety of which can be evacuated to 0.001 mm Hg or less. Compounds are condensed using <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> (b.p. 78K) or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogen" title="Cryogen">cryogens</a>. Solids are typically prepared using tube furnaces, the reactants and products being sealed in containers, often made of fused silica (amorphous SiO<sub>2</sub>) but sometimes more specialized materials such as welded Ta tubes or Pt "boats". Products and reactants are transported between temperature zones to drive reactions.
</p>
