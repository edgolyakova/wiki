<p><b>Fishing</b> is the activity of trying to catch <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>. Fish are often caught as <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environments</a>, but may also be caught from <a href="/wiki/Fish_stocking" title="Fish stocking">stocked</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodies_of_water" title="Bodies of water">bodies of water</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fish_pond" title="Fish pond">ponds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Park" title="Park">park</a> <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_techniques" title="Fishing techniques">Fishing techniques</a> include <a href="/wiki/Gathering_seafood_by_hand" title="Gathering seafood by hand">hand-gathering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spearfishing" title="Spearfishing">spearing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_net" title="Fish net">netting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">angling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowfishing" title="Bowfishing">shooting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_trap" title="Fish trap">trapping</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Destructive_fishing_practices" title="Destructive fishing practices">more destructive</a> and often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illegal_fishing" title="Illegal fishing">illegal</a> techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Electrofishing" title="Electrofishing">electrocution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blast_fishing" title="Blast fishing">blasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_fishing" title="Cyanide fishing">poisoning</a>.
</p><p>The term fishing broadly includes catching <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic animals</a> other than fish, such as <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>/<a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>/<a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> (<a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopus</a>/<a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Starfish" title="Starfish">starfish</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>). The term is not normally applied to harvesting fish raised in <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">controlled cultivations</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">fish farming</a>). Nor is it normally applied to hunting <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_mammal" title="Aquatic mammal">aquatic mammals</a>, where terms like <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">sealing</a> are used instead.
</p><p>Fishing has been an important part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_culture" title="Human culture">human culture</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> times, and is one of the few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_production" title="Food production">food production</a> activities that have persisted from <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> into <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_history" title="Contemporary history">modern age</a>, surviving both the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technological_revolution#History" title="Technological revolution">successive Industrial Revolutions</a>. In addition to being caught to be <a href="/wiki/Fish_as_food" title="Fish as food">eaten for food</a>, fish are caught as <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational pastimes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tournament" title="Fishing tournament">Fishing tournaments</a> are held, and caught fish are sometimes kept long-term as <a href="/wiki/Taxidermy" title="Taxidermy">preserved</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fishkeeping" title="Fishkeeping">living</a> <a href="/wiki/Trophy_hunting" title="Trophy hunting">trophies</a>. When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioblitz" title="Bioblitz">bioblitzes</a> occur, fish are typically <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">caught, identified, and then released</a>.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">FAO</a> statistics, the total number of <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farmers</a> is estimated to be 38 million. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">Fishing industries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> provide direct and indirect <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a> to over 500 million people in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2005, the worldwide <i>per capita</i> consumption of fish captured from <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">wild fisheries</a> was 14.4 kilograms (32 lb), with an additional 7.4 kilograms (16 lb) harvested from fish farms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Fishing is an ancient practise that dates back to at least the beginning of the Upper <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> period about 40,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Isotope_analysis" title="Isotope analysis">Isotopic analysis</a> of the remains of <a href="/wiki/Tianyuan_man" title="Tianyuan man">Tianyuan man</a>, a 40,000-year-old modern human from eastern Asia, has shown that he regularly consumed freshwater fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">Archaeology</a> features such as <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">shell middens</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> discarded fish bones, and <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave paintings</a> show that sea foods were important for survival and consumed in significant quantities. Fishing in Africa is evident very early on in human history. <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a> were fishing by about 200,000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> People could have developed basketry for fish traps, and spinning and early forms of knitting in order to make fishing nets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> to be able to catch more fish in larger quantities.
</p><p>During this period, most people lived a <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> lifestyle and were, of necessity, constantly on the move. However, where there are early examples of permanent settlements (though not necessarily permanently occupied) such as those at <a href="/wiki/Lepenski_Vir" title="Lepenski Vir">Lepenski Vir</a>, they are almost always associated with fishing as a major source of food.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trawling">Trawling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trawling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British <a href="/wiki/Dogger_(boat)" title="Dogger (boat)">dogger</a> was a very early type of sailing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawler_(fishing)" title="Trawler (fishing)">trawler</a> from the 17th century, but the modern fishing trawler was developed in the 19th century, at the English fishing port of <a href="/wiki/Brixham" title="Brixham">Brixham</a>. By the early 19th century, the fishers at Brixham needed to expand their fishing area further than ever before due to the ongoing depletion of stocks that was occurring in the <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfished</a> waters of <a href="/wiki/South_Devon" title="South Devon">South Devon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Brixham_trawler" title="Brixham trawler">Brixham trawler</a> that evolved there was of a sleek build and had a tall <a href="/wiki/Gaff_rig" title="Gaff rig">gaff rig</a>, which gave the vessel sufficient speed to make long-distance trips out to the fishing grounds in the ocean. They were also sufficiently robust to be able to tow large trawls in deep water. The great trawling fleet that built up at Brixham earned the village the title of 'Mother of Deep-Sea Fisheries'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjappeal_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-kjappeal-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Brixham_trawler.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="832" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/A_Brixham_trawler.jpg/220px-A_Brixham_trawler.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/A_Brixham_trawler.jpg/330px-A_Brixham_trawler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/A_Brixham_trawler.jpg/440px-A_Brixham_trawler.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Brixham_trawler.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting of <i>A Brixham trawler</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Adolphus_Knell" title="William Adolphus Knell">William Adolphus Knell</a>. The painting is now in the <a href="/wiki/National_Maritime_Museum" title="National Maritime Museum">National Maritime Museum</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>This revolutionary design made large scale trawling in the ocean possible for the first time, resulting in a massive migration of fishers from the ports in the South of England, to villages further north, such as <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_North_Yorkshire" title="Scarborough, North Yorkshire">Scarborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grimsby" title="Grimsby">Grimsby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harwich" title="Harwich">Harwich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Yarmouth" title="Great Yarmouth">Yarmouth</a>, that were points of access to the large fishing grounds in the Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjappeal_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-kjappeal-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The small village of <a href="/wiki/Grimsby" title="Grimsby">Grimsby</a> grew to become the largest fishing port in the world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> by the mid 19th century. An <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> was first obtained in 1796, which authorised the construction of new quays and dredging of the Haven to make it deeper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lochist_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-lochist-11">[11]</a></sup> It was only in 1846, with the tremendous expansion in the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">fishing industry</a>, that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimsby_Docks" title="Grimsby Docks">Grimsby Dock Company</a> was formed. The foundation stone for the Royal Dock was laid by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Albert</a> the <a href="/wiki/Prince_consort" title="Prince consort">Prince consort</a> in 1849. The dock covered 25 acres (10 ha) and was formally opened by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> in 1854 as the first modern fishing port.
</p><p>The elegant <a href="/wiki/Brixham_trawler" title="Brixham trawler">Brixham trawler</a> spread across the world, influencing fishing fleets everywhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By the end of the 19th century, there were over 3,000 fishing trawlers in commission in Britain, with almost 1,000 at Grimsby. These trawlers were sold to fishers around Europe, including from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. Twelve trawlers went on to form the nucleus of the German fishing fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest steam-powered fishing boats first appeared in the 1870s and used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawl</a> system of fishing as well as lines and drift nets. These were large boats, usually 80–90 feet (24–27 m) in length with a beam of around 20 feet (6.1 m). They weighed 40–50 tons and travelled at 9–11 knots (17–20 km/h; 10–13 mph). The earliest purpose-built fishing vessels were designed and made by David Allan in <a href="/wiki/Leith" title="Leith">Leith</a>, Scotland in March 1875, when he converted a drifter to steam power. In 1877, he built the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screw_propeller" title="Screw propeller">screw propelled</a> <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steam trawler</a> in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grantontrawlers_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-grantontrawlers-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Steam trawlers were introduced at <a href="/wiki/Grimsby" title="Grimsby">Grimsby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a> in the 1880s. In 1890 it was estimated that there were 20,000 men on the North Sea. The steam drifter was not used in the herring fishery until 1897. The last sailing fishing trawler was built in 1925 in Grimsby. Trawler designs adapted as the way they were powered changed from sail to coal-fired steam by <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> to <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbines" title="Turbines">turbines</a> by the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.
</p><p>In 1931, the first powered drum was created by Laurie Jarelainen. The drum was a circular device that was set to the side of the boat and would draw in the nets. Since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_navigation_aid" title="Radio navigation aid">radio navigation aids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishfinder" title="Fishfinder">fish finders</a> have been widely used. The first trawlers fished over the side, rather than over the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>. The first purpose-built stern trawler was <i>Fairtry</i> built-in 1953 at <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, Scotland. The ship was much larger than any other trawlers then in operation and inaugurated the era of the 'super trawler'. As the ship pulled its nets over the stern, it could lift out a much greater haul of up to 60 tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hulltrawler_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hulltrawler-15">[15]</a></sup> The ship served as a basis for the expansion of 'super trawlers' around the world in the following decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hulltrawler_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hulltrawler-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreational_fishing">Recreational fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recreational fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">Recreational fishing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="591" data-file-width="367" decoding="async" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg/170px-Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg/255px-Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg/340px-Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louis_John_Rhead_-_Izaak_Wolton_and_his_scholar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Izaak_Walton" title="Izaak Walton">Izaak Walton</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compleat_Angler" title="The Compleat Angler">Compleat Angler</a></i>, published in 1653 helped popularise fly fishing as a sport.<br/>Woodcut by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Rhead" title="Louis Rhead">Louis Rhead</a></div></div></div>
<p>The early evolution of fishing as recreation is not clear. For example, there is anecdotal evidence for <a href="/wiki/Fly_fishing" title="Fly fishing">fly fishing</a> in Japan, however, fly fishing was likely to have been a means of survival, rather than recreation. The earliest English essay on recreational fishing was published in 1496, by <a href="/wiki/Juliana_Berners" title="Juliana Berners">Dame Juliana Berners</a>, the prioress of the Benedictine <a href="/wiki/Sopwell_Priory" title="Sopwell Priory">Sopwell Nunnery</a>. The essay was titled <i>Treatyse of Fysshynge wyth an Angle</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and included detailed information on fishing waters, the construction of <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">rods</a> and lines, and the use of natural baits and artificial flies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recreational fishing took a great leap forward after the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, where a newly found interest in the activity left its mark on the many books and treatises that were written on the subject at the time. <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Mascall" title="Leonard Mascall">Leonard Mascall</a> in 1589 wrote <i>A booke of Fishing with Hooke and Line</i> along with many others he produced in his life on game and wildlife in England at the time. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Compleat_Angler" title="The Compleat Angler">The Compleat Angler</a></i> was written by <a href="/wiki/Izaak_Walton" title="Izaak Walton">Izaak Walton</a> in 1653 (although Walton continued to add to it for a quarter of a century) and described the fishing in the <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> <a href="/wiki/River_Wye,_Derbyshire" title="River Wye, Derbyshire">Wye</a>. It was a celebration of the art and spirit of fishing in prose and verse. A second part to the book was added by Walton's friend <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cotton" title="Charles Cotton">Charles Cotton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herdfly_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herdfly-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Charles Kirby designed an improved fishing hook in 1655 that remains relatively unchanged to this day. He went on to invent the Kirby bend, a distinctive hook with an offset point, still commonly used today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:220px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ustonson_advert.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="408" data-file-width="218" decoding="async" height="408" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e8/Ustonson_advert.jpg" width="218"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ustonson_advert.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trading card of the Ustonson company, an early firm specializing in fishing equipment, and holder of a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Warrant_of_Appointment_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Warrant of Appointment (United Kingdom)">Royal Warrant</a> from the 1760s.</div></div></div>
<p>The 18th century was mainly an era of consolidation of the techniques developed in the previous century. Running rings began to appear along the fishing rods, which gave anglers greater control over the cast line. The rods themselves were also becoming increasingly sophisticated and specialised for different roles. Jointed rods became common from the middle of the century and <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> came to be used for the top section of the rod, giving it a much greater strength and flexibility.
</p><p>The industry also became commercialised – rods and tackle were sold at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haberdashers" title="Haberdashers">haberdashers</a> store. After the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a> in 1666, artisans moved to <a href="/wiki/Redditch" title="Redditch">Redditch</a> which became a centre of production of fishing related products from the 1730s. Onesimus Ustonson established his shop in 1761, and his establishment remained as a market leader for the next century. He received a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Warrant_of_Appointment_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Warrant of Appointment (United Kingdom)">Royal Warrant</a> from three successive monarchs starting with King <a href="/wiki/George_IV" title="George IV">George IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He also invented the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_reel" title="Fishing reel">multiplying winch</a>. The commercialization of the industry came at a time of expanded interest in fishing as a recreational hobby for members of the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The impact of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> was first felt in the manufacture of fly lines. Instead of anglers twisting their lines – a laborious and time-consuming process – the new textile spinning machines allowed for a variety of tapered lines to be easily manufactured and marketed.
</p><p>British fly-fishing continued to develop in the 19th Century, with the emergence of fly fishing clubs, along with the appearance of several books on the subject of fly tying and fly fishing techniques.
</p><p>By the mid to late 19th century, expanding <a href="/wiki/Leisure" title="Leisure">leisure</a> opportunities for the middle and lower classes began to have its effect on fly fishing, which steadily grew in mass appeal. The expansion of the railway network in Britain allowed the less affluent for the first time to take weekend trips to the seaside or rivers for fishing. Richer <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobbyists</a> ventured further abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herdy1800-1850_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herdy1800-1850-22">[22]</a></sup> The large rivers of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> replete with large stocks of <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> began to attract fishers from England in large numbers in the middle of the century – <i>Jones's guide to Norway, and salmon-fisher's pocket companion</i>, published in 1848, was written by Frederic Tolfrey and was a popular guide to the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herdy1800-1850_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herdy1800-1850-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nottingham_reel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="750" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/Nottingham_reel.jpg/230px-Nottingham_reel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/Nottingham_reel.jpg/345px-Nottingham_reel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/Nottingham_reel.jpg/460px-Nottingham_reel.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nottingham_reel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>'Nottingham' and 'Scarborough' reel designs.</div></div></div>
<p>Modern reel design had begun in England during the latter part of the 18th century, and the predominant model in use was known as the '<a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> reel'. The reel was a wide drum that spooled out freely and was ideal for allowing the bait to drift a long way out with the current. Geared multiplying reels never successfully caught on in Britain, but had more success in the United States, where similar models were modified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Snyder_(fly_reels)" title="George Snyder (fly reels)">George Snyder</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> into his bait-casting reel, the first American-made design in 1810.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The material used for the rod itself changed from the heavy woods native to England to lighter and more elastic varieties imported from abroad, especially from South America and the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">Bamboo</a> rods became the generally favoured option from the mid 19th century, and several strips of the material were cut from the cane, milled into shape, and then glued together to form the light, strong, hexagonal rods with a solid core that were superior to anything that preceded them. <a href="/wiki/George_Cotton" title="George Cotton">George Cotton</a> and his predecessors fished their flies with long rods, and light lines allowing the wind to do most of the work of getting the fly to the fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brit_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trout_fishing_1860s.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="239" data-file-width="320" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e9/Trout_fishing_1860s.jpg/220px-Trout_fishing_1860s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e9/Trout_fishing_1860s.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trout_fishing_1860s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fishing became a popular recreational activity in the 19th century. Print from <a href="/wiki/Currier_and_Ives" title="Currier and Ives">Currier and Ives</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Tackle design began to improve from the 1880s. The introduction of new woods to the manufacture of fly rods made it possible to cast flies into the wind on silk lines, instead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse_hair" title="Horse hair">horse hair</a>. These lines allowed for a much greater casting distance. However, these early fly lines proved troublesome as they had to be coated with various dressings to make them float and needed to be taken off the reel and dried every four hours or so to prevent them from becoming waterlogged. Another negative consequence was that it became easy for the much longer line to get into a tangle – this was called a 'tangle' in Britain, and a 'backlash' in the US. This problem spurred the invention of the regulator to evenly spool the line out and prevent tangling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brit_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The American, Charles F. Orvis, designed and distributed a novel reel and fly design in 1874, described by reel historian Jim Brown as the "benchmark of American reel design," and the first fully modern fly reel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_reel_treasury_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown_reel_treasury-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schullery_orvis_story_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-schullery_orvis_story-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Illingworth,_1st_Baron_Illingworth" title="Albert Illingworth, 1st Baron Illingworth">Albert Illingworth, 1st Baron Illingworth</a> a textiles magnate, patented the modern form of fixed-spool spinning reel in 1905. When casting Illingworth's reel design, the line was drawn off the leading edge of the spool but was restrained and rewound by a line pickup, a device which orbits around the stationary spool. Because the line did not have to pull against a rotating spool, much lighter lures could be cast than with conventional reels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brit_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The development of inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> rods, synthetic fly lines, and monofilament leaders in the early 1950s, that revived the popularity of fly fishing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg/220px-%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg/330px-%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg/440px-%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%C4%90%C3%B3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fishermen with traditional fish traps, Vietnam</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fishing_techniques" title="Fishing techniques">Fishing techniques</a></div>
<p>There are many fishing techniques and tactics for catching fish. The term can also be applied to methods for catching other <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic animals</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molluscs" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, octopus) and edible marine <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>.
</p><p>Fishing techniques include <a href="/wiki/Gathering_seafood_by_hand" title="Gathering seafood by hand">hand gathering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spearfishing" title="Spearfishing">spearfishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_net" title="Fish net">netting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">angling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_trap" title="Fish trap">trapping</a>. <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">Recreational</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">artisanal</a> fishers use different techniques, and also, sometimes, the same techniques. Recreational fishers fish for pleasure, sport, or to provide food for themselves, while commercial fishers fish for profit. Artisanal fishers use traditional, low-tech methods, for survival in third-world countries, and as a cultural heritage in other countries. Usually, recreational fishers use angling methods and commercial fishers use netting methods. A modern development is to fish with the assistance of a drone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Why a fish bites a baited hook or lure involves several factors related to the sensory physiology, behaviour, feeding ecology, and biology of the fish as well as the environment and characteristics of the bait/hook/lure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> There is an intricate link between various fishing techniques and knowledge about the fish and their behaviour including <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">foraging</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_habitats" title="Oceanic habitats">habitat</a>. The effective use of fishing techniques often depends on this additional knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Some fishers follow <a href="/wiki/Solunar_theory" title="Solunar theory">fishing folklores</a> which claim that fish feeding patterns are influenced by the position of the sun and the moon.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tackle">Tackle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tackle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg"><img alt="Man seated at the side of the water surrounded by fishing rods and tackle." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1480" data-file-width="2077" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg/220px-Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg/330px-Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg/440px-Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Angler_at_devizes_england_arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">angler</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Kennet_and_Avon_Canal" title="Kennet and Avon Canal">Kennet and Avon Canal</a>, England, with his tackle</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tackle" title="Fishing tackle">Fishing tackle</a></div>
<p>Fishing tackle is the equipment used by <a href="/wiki/Fisherman" title="Fisherman">fishers</a> when fishing. Almost any equipment or gear used for fishing can be called fishing tackle. Some examples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_hook" title="Fishing hook">hooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_line" title="Fishing line">lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_sinker" title="Fishing sinker">sinkers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_float" title="Fishing float">floats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">rods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_reel" title="Fishing reel">reels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_bait" title="Fishing bait">baits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_lure" title="Fishing lure">lures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spearfishing" title="Spearfishing">spears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">nets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_gaff" title="Fishing gaff">gaffs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_trap" title="Fishing trap">traps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waders_(footwear)" title="Waders (footwear)">waders</a> and tackle boxes.
</p><p>Tackle that is attached to the end of a <a href="/wiki/Fishing_line" title="Fishing line">fishing line</a> is called <b>terminal tackle</b>. This includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_hook" title="Fishing hook">hooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_sinker" title="Fishing sinker">sinkers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_float" title="Fishing float">floats</a>, leaders, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_swivel" title="Fishing swivel">swivels</a>, split rings and wire, snaps, beads, spoons, blades, spinners and clevises to attach spinner blades to fishing lures. People also tend to use dead or live fish as another form of bait.
</p><p>Fishing tackle refers to the physical equipment that is used when fishing, whereas <a href="/wiki/Fishing_techniques" title="Fishing techniques">fishing techniques</a> refers to the ways the tackle is used when fishing.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing_vessels">Fishing vessels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fishing vessels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1633" data-file-width="2183" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG/220px-Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG/330px-Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG/440px-Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Krabbenkutter_Ivonne_Pellworm_P5242390jm.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Commercial crab boat working in the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_in_the_North_Sea" title="Fishing in the North Sea">North Sea</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg/220px-Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg/330px-Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg/440px-Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Small_sport_fishing_boat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Small <a href="/wiki/Sport_fishing_boat" title="Sport fishing boat">sport fishing boat</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_vessels" title="Fishing vessels">Fishing vessels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_fishing_boats" title="Traditional fishing boats">Traditional fishing boats</a></div>
<p>A fishing vessel is a boat or ship used to catch fish in the sea, or on a lake or river. Many different kinds of vessels are used in <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">artisanal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational fishing</a>.
</p><p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a>, in 2004 there were four million commercial fishing vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2007_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO_2007-30">[30]</a></sup> About 1.3 million of these are decked vessels with enclosed areas. Nearly all of these decked vessels are mechanised, and 40,000 of them are over 100 tons. At the other extreme, two-thirds (1.8 million) of the <a href="/wiki/Deck_(ship)" title="Deck (ship)">undecked</a> boats are traditional craft of various types, powered only by sail and oars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2007_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO_2007-30">[30]</a></sup> These boats are used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">artisan fishers</a>.
</p><p>It is difficult to estimate how many <a href="/wiki/Recreational_boat_fishing" title="Recreational boat fishing">recreational fishing boats</a> there are, although the number is high. The term is fluid since some recreational boats may also be used for fishing from time to time. Unlike most commercial fishing vessels, recreational fishing boats are often not dedicated just to fishing. Just about anything that will stay afloat can be called a recreational fishing boat, so long as a <a href="/wiki/Fisherman" title="Fisherman">fisher</a> periodically climbs aboard with the intent to catch a fish. Fish are caught for recreational purposes from boats which range from <a href="/wiki/Dugout_canoe" title="Dugout canoe">dugout canoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Float_tube" title="Float tube">float tubes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kayaks" title="Kayaks">kayaks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raft" title="Raft">rafts</a>, stand up paddleboards, <a href="/wiki/Pontoon_boat" title="Pontoon boat">pontoon boats</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Dinghy" title="Dinghy">dinghies</a> to <a href="/wiki/Runabout_(boat)" title="Runabout (boat)">runabouts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabin_cruiser" title="Cabin cruiser">cabin cruisers</a> and cruising yachts to large, hi-tech and luxurious <a href="/wiki/Big-game_fishing" title="Big-game fishing">big game</a> rigs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Larger boats, purpose-built with recreational fishing in mind, usually have large, open <a href="/wiki/Cockpit_(sailing)" title="Cockpit (sailing)">cockpits</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>, designed for convenient fishing.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_fishing">Traditional fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Traditional fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Artisanal_fishing" title="Artisanal fishing">Artisanal fishing</a></div>
<p>Traditional fishing is any kind of small scale, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_economy" title="Subsistence economy">subsistence</a> fishing practices using traditional techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">rod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tackle" title="Fishing tackle">tackle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">arrows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harpoon" title="Harpoon">harpoons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Throw_net" title="Throw net">throw nets</a> and drag nets, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreational_fishing_2">Recreational fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recreational fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">Recreational fishing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1097" data-file-width="1627" decoding="async" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg/220px-An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg/330px-An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg/440px-An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:An_evening_fishing_-_by_Francis_Hannaway.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;"><a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">Angling</a></div></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="4800" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg/220px-Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg/330px-Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg/440px-Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanda_%C3%A5_Nukariforsen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A fisherman on the rapids in <a href="/wiki/Nukari" title="Nukari">Nukari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nurmij%C3%A4rvi" title="Nurmijärvi">Nurmijärvi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">Recreational</a> and sport fishing are fishing primarily for <a href="/wiki/Pleasure" title="Pleasure">pleasure</a> or competition. Recreational fishing has conventions, rules, licensing restrictions and laws that limit how fish may be caught; typically, these prohibit the use of nets and the catching of fish with hooks not in the mouth. The most common form of recreational fishing is done with a <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">rod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_reel" title="Fishing reel">reel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_line" title="Fishing line">line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_hook" title="Fish hook">hooks</a> and any one of a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Bait_(luring_substance)" title="Bait (luring substance)">baits</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fishing_lure" title="Fishing lure">lures</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Artificial_fly" title="Artificial fly">artificial flies</a>. The practice of catching or attempting to catch fish with a hook is generally known as <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">angling</a>. In angling, it is sometimes expected or required that fish be returned to the water (<a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">catch and release</a>). Recreational or sport fishermen may log their catches or participate in fishing competitions.
</p><p>The estimated global number of recreational fishers varies from 220 million to a maximum number of 700 million fishers globally,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> which is thought to be double the amount of individuals working as commercial fishers. In the United States alone it was estimated that 50.1 million people engaged in fishing activities in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltwater" title="Saltwater">saltwater</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Big-game_fishing" title="Big-game fishing">Big-game fishing</a> is fishing from boats to catch large open-water species such as <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marlin" title="Marlin">marlin</a>. Sportfishing (sometimes game fishing) is recreational fishing where the primary reward is the challenge of finding and catching the fish rather than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culinary" title="Culinary">culinary</a> or financial value of the fish's flesh. Fish sought after include <a href="/wiki/Tarpon" title="Tarpon">tarpon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sailfish" title="Sailfish">sailfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">mackerel</a> and many others.
</p>

<p>The fishing industry includes any industry or activity concerned with taking, culturing, processing, preserving, storing, transporting, marketing or selling fish or fish products. It is defined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> as including <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">subsistence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishing</a>, and the harvesting, <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">processing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fish_marketing" title="Fish marketing">marketing</a> sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The commercial activity is aimed at the delivery of fish and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seafood_products" title="Seafood products">seafood products</a> for human consumption or use as <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> in other industrial processes.
</p><p>There are three principal industry sectors:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[note 1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry#Commercial_sector" title="Fishing industry">commercial sector</a> comprises enterprises and individuals associated with wild-catch or aquaculture resources and the various transformations of those resources into products for sale.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry#Traditional_sector" title="Fishing industry">traditional sector</a> comprises enterprises and individuals associated with fisheries resources from which aboriginal people derive products following their traditions.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry#Recreational_sector" title="Fishing industry">recreational sector</a> comprises enterprises and individuals associated with the purpose of recreation, sport or sustenance with fisheries resources from which products are derived that are not for sale.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_fishing">Commercial fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">Commercial fishing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1750" data-file-width="2650" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg/220px-French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg/330px-French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg/440px-French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:French_Fishing_Vessel_%27Alf%27_in_the_Irish_Sea_MOD_45155246.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;">Fishing boat in heavy sea</div></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1111" data-file-width="1681" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg/220px-Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg/330px-Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg/440px-Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Push-up_f%C3%A4lla_-_Ystad-2021.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Push-up trap developed by <a href="/wiki/Swedish_University_of_Agricultural_Sciences" title="Swedish University of Agricultural Sciences">Swedish University of Agricultural Sciences</a> for more accurate and less harmful fishing</div></div></div>
<p>Commercial fishing is the capture of fish for commercial purposes. Those who practice it must often pursue fish far from the land under adverse conditions. Commercial fishermen harvest almost all aquatic species, from <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clam" title="Clam">clams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crab</a>, in various <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a> for these species. Commercial fishing methods have become very efficient using large nets and sea-going processing factories. <a href="/wiki/Individual_fishing_quota" title="Individual fishing quota">Individual fishing quotas</a> and international treaties seek to control the species and quantities caught.
</p><p>A commercial fishing enterprise may vary from one man with a small boat with hand-casting nets or a few pot traps, to a huge fleet of <a href="/wiki/Fishing_trawler" title="Fishing trawler">trawlers</a> processing tons of fish every day.
</p><p>Commercial fishing gear includes weights, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">nets</a> (e.g. purse <a href="/wiki/Seine_fishing" title="Seine fishing">seine</a>), seine nets (e.g. beach seine), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trawl" title="Trawl">trawls</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">bottom trawl</a>), dredges, <a href="/wiki/Fish_hook" title="Fish hook">hooks</a> and line (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-line_fishing" title="Long-line fishing">long line</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand-line_fishing" title="Hand-line fishing">handline</a>), lift nets, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a>, entangling nets and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_trap" title="Fishing trap">traps</a>.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> of the United Nations, the total world <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">capture fisheries</a> production in 2000 was 86 million tons (FAO 2002). The top producing countries were, in order, the People's Republic of China (excluding Hong Kong and Taiwan), Peru, Japan, the United States, Chile, Indonesia, Russia, India, Thailand, Norway, and Iceland. Those countries accounted for more than half of the world's production; China alone accounted for a third of the world's production. Of that production, over 90% was marine and less than 10% was inland.
</p><p>A small number of species support the majority of the world's fisheries. Some of these species are <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flounder" title="Flounder">flounder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">mullet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, crab, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">scallops</a>. All except these last four provided a worldwide catch of well over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million" title="Million">million</a> <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> in 1999, with <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a> together providing a catch of over 22 million metric tons in 1999. Many other species as well are fished in smaller numbers. They catch fish by the tonnes
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_farms">Fish farms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fish farms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">Fish farm</a></div>
<p>Fish farming is the principal form of <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, while other methods may fall under <a href="/wiki/Mariculture" title="Mariculture">mariculture</a>. It involves raising fish commercially in tanks or enclosures, usually for food. A facility that releases <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_fish" title="Juvenile fish">juvenile fish</a> into the wild for recreational fishing or to supplement a species' natural population is generally referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Fish_hatchery" title="Fish hatchery">fish hatchery</a>. Fish species raised by fish farms include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_salmon" title="Aquaculture of salmon">salmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_tilapia" title="Aquaculture of tilapia">tilapia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_catfish" title="Aquaculture of catfish">catfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a>.
</p><p>Increased demands on <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">wild fisheries</a> by <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishing</a> has caused widespread <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>. Fish farming offers an alternative solution to the increasing market demand for fish.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_products">Fish products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fish products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">Fish processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_products" title="Fish products">Fish products</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_(food)" title="Fish (food)">Fish (food)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">Seafood</a></div>
<p>Fish and fish products are <a href="/wiki/Fish_as_food" title="Fish as food">consumed as food</a> all over the world. With other <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafoods</a>, it provides the world's prime source of high-quality <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>: 14–16 percent of the animal protein consumed worldwide. Over one billion people rely on fish as their primary source of animal protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fish and other aquatic organisms are also processed into various food and non-food products, such as sharkskin leather, pigments made from the inky secretions of <a href="/wiki/Cuttlefish" title="Cuttlefish">cuttlefish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isinglass" title="Isinglass">isinglass</a> used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarification_(wine)" title="Clarification (wine)">clarification</a> of wine and beer, <a href="/wiki/Fish_emulsion" title="Fish emulsion">fish emulsion</a> used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertiliser" title="Fertiliser">fertiliser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_glue" title="Animal glue">fish glue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_oil" title="Fish oil">fish oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_meal" title="Fish meal">fish meal</a>.
</p><p>Fish are also collected live for research and the <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquarium</a> trade.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries_management">Fisheries management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fisheries management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3675" data-file-width="5433" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/220px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/330px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/440px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;"><a href="/wiki/Fishing_down_the_food_web" title="Fishing down the food web">Fishing down the food web</a></div></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">Fisheries management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">Fisheries science</a></div>
<p>Fisheries management draws on <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries science</a> to find ways to protect <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> resources so sustainable exploitation is possible. Modern fisheries management is often referred to as a governmental system of (hopefully appropriate) management rules based on defined objectives and a mix of management means to implement the rules, which are put in place by a system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monitoring_control_and_surveillance" title="Monitoring control and surveillance">monitoring control and surveillance</a>.
</p><p>Fisheries science is the academic discipline of managing and understanding fisheries. It is a multidisciplinary science, which draws on the disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_conservation" title="Marine conservation">marine conservation</a>, ecology, <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">population dynamics</a>, economics and management in an attempt to provide an integrated picture of fisheries. In some cases new disciplines have emerged, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioeconomics_(biology)" title="Bioeconomics (biology)">bioeconomics</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishing" title="Sustainable fishing">Sustainable fishing</a></div>
<p>Issues involved in the long term sustainability of fishing include <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/By-catch" title="By-catch">by-catch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">marine pollution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_fishing" title="Environmental effects of fishing">environmental effects of fishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries_and_climate_change" title="Fisheries and climate change">climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farming</a>.
</p><p>Conservation issues are part of <a href="/wiki/Marine_conservation" title="Marine conservation">marine conservation</a>, and are addressed in <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries science</a> programs. There is a growing gap between how many fish are available to be caught and humanity's desire to catch them, a problem that gets worse as the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> grows.
</p><p>Similar to other <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues" title="Environmental issues">environmental issues</a>, there can be conflict between the fishermen who depend on fishing for their livelihoods and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries_scientists" title="Fisheries scientists">fishery scientists</a> who realise that if future fish populations are to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishing" title="Sustainable fishing">sustainable</a> then some fisheries must limit fishing or cease operations.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_welfare_concerns">Animal welfare concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Animal welfare concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">Animal welfare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pain_in_fish" title="Pain in fish">Pain in fish</a></div>
<p>Historically, some doubted that fish could experience pain. Laboratory experiments have shown that fish do react to painful stimuli (e.g., injections of bee <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a>) in a similar way to mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> This is controversial and has been disputed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=" (November 2019)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> The expansion of <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">fish farming</a> as well as animal welfare concerns in society has led to research into more humane and faster ways of killing fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In large-scale operations like fish farms, stunning fish with electricity or putting them into water saturated with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> so that they cannot breathe, results in death more rapidly than just taking them out of the water. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_fishing" title="Sport fishing">sport fishing</a>, it is recommended that fish be killed soon after catching them by hitting them on the head followed by <a href="/wiki/Exsanguination" title="Exsanguination">bleeding out</a> or by stabbing the brain with a sharp object<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> (called <a href="/wiki/Pithing" title="Pithing">pithing</a> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ike_jime" title="Ike jime">ike jime</a></i> in Japanese). Some believe it is not cruel if you release the catch back to where it was caught however a study in 2018 states that the hook damages an important part of the feeding mechanism by which the fish sucks in food, ignoring the issue of pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>When fishing there are high chances of catching other marine wildlife in a fishing net. There are over 100 different fishing regulations on paper for reducing this <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_pollution">Plastic pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Plastic pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abandoned, lost, or otherwise discarded fishing gear includes netting, mono/multifilament lines, hooks, ropes, floats, buoys, sinkers, anchors, metallic materials and fish aggregating devices (FADs) made of non-biodegradable materials such as <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>. It has been estimated that global fishing gear losses each year include 5.7% of all fishing nets, 8.6% of all traps and 29% of all lines used. Abandoned, lost, or otherwise discarded fishing gear (ALDFG) can have serious impacts on marine organisms through entanglement and ingestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">[44]</a></sup> The potential for fishing gear to become ALDFG depends on a number of factors including:
</p>
<ul><li>Environmental factors are mostly related to seafloor topography and obstructions, although tides, currents, waves, winds, and interaction with wildlife are also important.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Operational losses and operator errors can occur even during normal fishing operations.</li>
<li>Problems such as inadequate fisheries management and regulations that do not include adequate controls can hamper collection of ALDFG (e.g. there may be poor access to collection facilities).</li></ul>
<ul><li>Gear loss resulting from conflicts primarily occurs (intentionally or unintentionally) in areas with high concentrations of fishing activities, leading to gear being towed away, fouled, sabotaged or vandalized. Passive and unattended gear such as pots, set gillnets and traps are particularly prone to conflict damage. In the Arctic, conflicts are the most common reason for lost gear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">[44]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact">Cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ona1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Ona1.jpg/220px-Ona1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Ona1.jpg/330px-Ona1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Ona1.jpg/440px-Ona1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ona1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ona,_Sand%C3%B8y" title="Ona, Sandøy">Ona</a>, a traditional fishing village in Norway</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fishing_Woman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Fishing_Woman.jpg/220px-Fishing_Woman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Fishing_Woman.jpg/330px-Fishing_Woman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Fishing_Woman.jpg/440px-Fishing_Woman.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fishing_Woman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jalia_Kaibarta" title="Jalia Kaibarta">Kaibarta</a> woman with traditional fish catching device made from bamboo in Assam</div></div></div>
<dl><dt>Community</dt>
<dd>For communities like <a href="/wiki/Fishing_village" title="Fishing village">fishing villages</a>, fisheries provide not only a source of food and work but also a community and cultural identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Economic</dt>
<dd>Some locations may be regarded as fishing destinations, which anglers visit on vacation or for competitions. The economic impact of fishing by visitors may be a significant, or even primary driver of tourism revenue for some destinations.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Semantic</dt>
<dd>A "<a href="/wiki/Fishing_expedition" title="Fishing expedition">fishing expedition</a>" is a situation where an interviewer implies they know more than they do to trick their target into divulging more information than they wish to reveal. Other examples of fishing terms that carry a negative connotation are: "fishing for compliments", "to be fooled <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tackle#Hook,_line_and_sinker" title="Fishing tackle">hook, line and sinker</a>" (to be fooled beyond merely "taking the bait"), and the internet scam of <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a>, in which a third party will duplicate a website where the user would put sensitive information (such as bank codes).</dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Religious</dt>
<dd>Fishing has had an effect on major religions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> including Christianity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, and the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_age" title="New age">new age</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> religions. Jesus was said to participate in fishing excursions, and a number of the <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a> and many parables and stories reported in the Bible involve fish or fishing. Since the <a href="/wiki/Apostles_in_the_New_Testament" title="Apostles in the New Testament">Apostle</a> <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> was a fisherman, the Catholic Church has adopted the use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishermans_ring" title="Fishermans ring">fishermans ring</a> into the Pope's traditional <a href="/wiki/Papal_regalia_and_insignia" title="Papal regalia and insignia">vestments</a>.</dd></dl>
