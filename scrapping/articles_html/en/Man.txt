<p>A <b>man</b> is an adult <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Prior to adulthood, a male human is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Boy" title="Boy">boy</a> (a male <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">child</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adolescent" title="Adolescent">adolescent</a>). Like most other male <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, a man's <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> usually inherits an <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosome</a> from the mother and a <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> from the father. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_differentiation" title="Sex differentiation">Sex differentiation</a> of the male fetus is governed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SRY" title="SRY">SRY</a> gene on the Y chromosome. During puberty, hormones which stimulate androgen production result in the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_sexual_characteristic" title="Secondary sexual characteristic">secondary sexual characteristics</a>, thus exhibiting greater differences between the sexes. These include greater muscle mass, the growth of facial hair and a lower body fat composition.
</p><p>Male anatomy is distinguished from female anatomy by the <a href="/wiki/Male_reproductive_system" title="Male reproductive system">male reproductive system</a>, which includes the <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testicles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sperm_duct" title="Sperm duct">sperm duct</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostate" title="Prostate">prostate gland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Epididymis" title="Epididymis">epididymis</a>, as well as secondary sex characteristics.
</p><p>Throughout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_world" title="History of the world">human history</a>, traditional <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> have often defined and limited men's activities and opportunities. Men often face  <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> into military service or are directed into professions with high <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rates</a>, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy#Sex_differences" title="Life expectancy">shorter life expectancy than women</a>. Many religious doctrines stipulate certain rules for men, such as <a href="/wiki/Forced_circumcision" title="Forced circumcision">forced circumcision</a>. Men are over-represented as both perpetrators and <a href="/wiki/Violence_against_men" title="Violence against men">victims of violence</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Trans_man" title="Trans man">Trans men</a> have a <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a> that does not align with their female <a href="/wiki/Sex_assignment" title="Sex assignment">sex assignment</a> at birth, while <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a> men may have sex characteristics that do not fit typical notions of male biology.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_terminology">Etymology and terminology</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Man_(word)" title="Man (word)">Man (word)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boy" title="Boy">boy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a>, <a href="/wiki/Husband" title="Husband">husband</a>, <a href="/wiki/Son" title="Son">son</a>, <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godparent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gentleman" title="Gentleman">gentleman</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Widower" title="Widower">widower</a></div>
<p>The English term "man" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> root <i>*man-</i> (see <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>/<a href="/wiki/Avestan" title="Avestan">Avestan</a> <i>manu-</i>, <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> <i>mǫž</i> "man, male").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> More directly, the word derives from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mann#Old_English" title="wikt:mann">mann</a></i>. The Old English form primarily meant "person" or "human being" and referred to men, women, and children alike. The Old English word for "man" as distinct from "woman" or "child" was <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wer#Old_English" title="wikt:wer">wer</a></i>. <i>Mann</i> only came to mean "man" in Middle English, replacing <i>wer</i>, which survives today only in the compounds "<a href="/wiki/Werewolf" title="Werewolf">werewolf</a>" (from Old English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/werwulf#Old_English" title="wikt:werwulf">werwulf</a></i>, literally "man-wolf"), and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wergild" title="Wergild">wergild</a>", literally "man-payment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_sexual_characteristics" title="Secondary sexual characteristics">Secondary sexual characteristics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sex_differences_in_humans" title="Sex differences in humans">Sex differences in humans</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png"><img alt="Karyotype of a human male." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2417" data-file-width="3084" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png/220px-NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png/330px-NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png/440px-NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NHGRI_human_male_karyotype.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karyogram" title="Karyogram">Karyogram</a> of a human male using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giemsa" title="Giemsa">Giemsa</a> staining. Human males typically possess an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_determination_and_differentiation_(human)" title="Sex determination and differentiation (human)">XY combination</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In humans, sperm cells normally carry either an <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y</a> sex chromosome. If a sperm cell carrying a <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> fertilizes the female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ova" title="Ova">ova</a>, the offspring will be male (XY). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SRY" title="SRY">SRY</a> gene is normally found on the Y chromosome and is the testis determining factor that governs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_differentiation" title="Sex differentiation">male sex differentiation</a>. Sex differentiation in males proceeds in a testes dependent way while female differentiation is not gonad dependent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans exhibit <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in many characteristics, many of which have no direct link to reproductive ability, although most of these characteristics do have a role in sexual attraction. Most expressions of sexual dimorphism in humans are found in height, weight, and body structure, though there are always examples that do not follow the overall pattern. For example, men tend to be taller than women, but there are many people of both sexes who are in the mid-height range for the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male,_with_labels_2.png"><img alt="Photograph of an adult male human, with an adult female for comparison. Note that the pubic hair of both models is removed." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="867" data-file-width="818" decoding="async" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male%2C_with_labels_2.png/220px-Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male%2C_with_labels_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male%2C_with_labels_2.png/330px-Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male%2C_with_labels_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male%2C_with_labels_2.png/440px-Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male%2C_with_labels_2.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anterior_view_of_human_female_and_male,_with_labels_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photograph of an adult male human (right side of image), with an adult female for comparison.(left side of image)  Note that the <a href="/wiki/Pubic_hair" title="Pubic hair">pubic hair</a> of both models is removed.</div></div></div>
<p>Primary sex characteristics (or sex organs) are characteristics that are present at birth and are integral to the reproductive process. For men, primary sex characteristics include the <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testicles</a>. Secondary sex characteristics are features that appear during <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a> in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melmed_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Melmed-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pack_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pack-10">[10]</a></sup> Such features are especially evident in the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_trait" title="Phenotypic trait">phenotypic traits</a> that distinguish between the sexes, but—unlike the primary sex characteristics—are not directly part of the <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_system" title="Reproductive system">reproductive system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bjorklund_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bjorklund-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Secondary sexual characteristics that are specific to men include:
</p>
<ul><li>Broadened shoulders;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>An enlarged larynx (also known as an <a href="/wiki/Adam%27s_apple" title="Adam's apple">Adam's apple</a>);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-14">[14]</a></sup> and</li>
<li>A voice that is significantly deeper than the voice of a child or a woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive_system">Reproductive system</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Male_reproductive_system" title="Male reproductive system">Male reproductive system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Male_pelvic_structures.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="257" data-file-width="270" decoding="async" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Male_pelvic_structures.svg/200px-Male_pelvic_structures.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Male_pelvic_structures.svg/300px-Male_pelvic_structures.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Male_pelvic_structures.svg/400px-Male_pelvic_structures.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Male_pelvic_structures.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A lateral cutaway of the human male lower abdomen, showing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_male_reproductive_system" title="Human male reproductive system">human male reproductive system</a> anatomy</div></div></div>
<p>The male reproductive system includes external and internal genitalia. The male external genitalia consist of the <a href="/wiki/Human_penis" title="Human penis">penis</a>, the male urethra, and the scrotum, while the male internal genitalia consist of the testes, the prostate, the epididymis, the seminal vesicle, the vas deferens, the ejaculatory duct, and the bulbourethral gland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The male reproductive system's function is to produce <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>, which carries <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoon" title="Spermatozoon">sperm</a> and thus <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">genetic information</a> that can unite with an egg within a woman. Since sperm that enters a woman's <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Fallopian_tube" title="Fallopian tube">fallopian tubes</a> goes on to <a href="/wiki/Fertilisation" title="Fertilisation">fertilize</a> an egg which develops into a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> or child, the male reproductive system plays no necessary role during the <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a>. The study of male reproduction and associated organs is called <a href="/wiki/Andrology" title="Andrology">andrology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_hormones">Sex hormones</span></h3>
<p>Testosterone stimulates the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolffian_duct" title="Wolffian duct">Wolffian ducts</a>, the penis, and closure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labioscrotal_folds" title="Labioscrotal folds">labioscrotal folds</a> into the scrotum. Another significant hormone in sexual differentiation is the <a href="/wiki/Anti-M%C3%BCllerian_hormone" title="Anti-Müllerian hormone">anti-Müllerian hormone</a>, which inhibits the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%BCllerian_duct" title="Müllerian duct">Müllerian ducts</a>. For males during puberty, testosterone, along with <a href="/wiki/Gonadotropin" title="Gonadotropin">gonadotropins</a> released by the <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a>, stimulates <a href="/wiki/Spermatogenesis" title="Spermatogenesis">spermatogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gender_disparities_in_health" title="Gender disparities in health">Gender disparities in health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_health" title="Men's health">Men's health</a></div>
<p>While a majority of the global health gender disparities is weighted against women, there are situations in which men tend to fare poorer. One such instance is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_conflicts" title="Armed conflicts">armed conflicts</a>, where men are often the immediate victims. A study of conflicts in 13 countries from 1955 to 2002 found that 81% of all violent <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a> deaths were male.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Development_Report_2012:_Gender_Equality_and_Development_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_Development_Report_2012:_Gender_Equality_and_Development-18">[18]</a></sup> Apart from armed conflicts, areas with high incidence of violence, such as regions controlled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_cartels" title="Drug cartels">drug cartels</a>, also see men experiencing higher mortality rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This stems from social beliefs that associate ideals of <a href="/wiki/Masculinity" title="Masculinity">masculinity</a> with aggressive, confrontational behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Street_Is_My_Home:_Youth_and_Violence_in_Caracas_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Street_Is_My_Home:_Youth_and_Violence_in_Caracas-20">[20]</a></sup> Lastly, sudden and drastic changes in economic environments and the loss of <a href="/wiki/Social_safety_net" title="Social safety net">social safety nets</a>, in particular social subsidies and food stamps, have also been linked to higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a> consumption and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_stress" title="Psychological stress">psychological stress</a> among men, leading to a spike in male mortality rates. This is because such situations often makes it harder for men to provide for their family, a task that has been long regarded as the "essence of masculinity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Autopsy_on_an_Empire:_Understanding_Mortality_in_Russia_and_the_Former_Soviet_Union_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Autopsy_on_an_Empire:_Understanding_Mortality_in_Russia_and_the_Former_Soviet_Union-21">[21]</a></sup> A retrospective analyses of people infected with the common cold found that doctors underrate the symptoms of men, and are more willing to attribute symptoms and illness to women than men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Women live longer than men in all countries, and across all age groups, for which reliable records exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In the United States, men are less healthy than women across all social classes. Non-white men are especially unhealthy. Men are over-represented in dangerous occupations and represent a majority of on the job deaths. Further, medical doctors provide men with less service, less advice, and spend less time with men than they do with women per medical encounter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexuality_and_gender">Sexuality and gender</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Human_male_sexuality" title="Human male sexuality">Human male sexuality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trans_man" title="Trans man">Trans man</a></div>
<p>Male sexuality and attraction vary from person to person, and a man's sexual behavior can be affected by many factors, including <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolved predispositions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personality" title="Personality">personality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">upbringing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>. While the majority of men are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterosexual" title="Heterosexual">heterosexual</a>, significant minorities are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosexual" title="Homosexual">homosexual</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bisexual" title="Bisexual">bisexual</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans_men" title="Trans men">Trans men</a> have a male <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a> that does not align with their <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> <a href="/wiki/Sex_assignment" title="Sex assignment">sex assignment</a> at birth and may undergo masculinizing <a href="/wiki/Hormone_replacement_therapy" title="Hormone replacement therapy">hormone replacement therapy</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">sex reassignment surgery</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a> men may have sex characteristics that do not fit typical notions of male biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatare_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-whatare-26">[26]</a></sup> A 2016 systemic review estimated that 0.256% of people self-identify as female-to-male transgender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collin2016_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collin2016-27">[27]</a></sup> A 2017 survey of 80,929 Minnesota students found that roughly twice as many female-assigned adolescents self-identified as transgender, compared to adolescents with a male sex assignment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_role">Social role</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Masculinity">Masculinity</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Masculinity" title="Masculinity">Masculinity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5472" data-file-width="3648" decoding="async" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg/180px-%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg/270px-%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg/360px-%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%27David%27_by_Michelangelo_Fir_JBU005_denoised.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/David_(Michelangelo)" title="David (Michelangelo)">David</a></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_art" title="Renaissance art">classical</a> image of youthful male beauty in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_art" title="Western art">Western art</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Masculinity (also sometimes called <i>manhood</i> or <i>manliness</i>) is the set of personality traits and attributes associated with boys and men. Although masculinity is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_constructs" title="Gender constructs">socially constructed</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shehan_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-shehan-29">[29]</a></sup> some research indicates that some behaviors considered masculine are biologically influenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-30">[30]</a></sup> To what extent masculinity is biologically or socially influenced is subject to debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-30">[30]</a></sup> It is <a href="/wiki/Sex_and_gender_distinction" title="Sex and gender distinction">distinct</a> from the definition of the <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">biological male sex</a>, as both males and females can exhibit masculine traits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Men generally face social stigma for embodying <a href="/wiki/Femininity" title="Femininity">feminine</a> traits, more so than women do for embodying masculine traits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson201733–34_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson201733–34-32">[32]</a></sup> This can also manifest as <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017146–149_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017146–149-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Standards of manliness or masculinity vary across different cultures and historical periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> While the outward signs of masculinity look different in different cultures, there are some common aspects to its definition across cultures. In all cultures in the past, and still among traditional and non-Western cultures, getting married is the most common and definitive distinction between boyhood and manhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> In the late 20th century, some qualities traditionally associated with marriage (such as the "triple Ps" of <i>protecting, providing, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procreating" title="Procreating">procreating</a></i>) were still considered signs of having achieved manhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span></h3>
<p>Platonic relationships are not significantly different between men and women, though some differences do exist. Friendships involving men tend to be based more on shared activities than self-disclosure and personal connection. Perceptions of friendship involving men varies among cultures and time periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017494–499_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017494–499-37">[37]</a></sup> In heterosexual romantic relationships, men are typically expected to take a proactive role, initiate the relationship, plan dates, and propose marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017571–574_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017571–574-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h3>
<p>Anthropology has shown that masculinity itself has <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">social status</a>, just like wealth, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a> and social class. In <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>, for example, greater masculinity usually brings greater social status. Many English words such as <i>virtue</i> and <i>virile</i> (from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_root" title="Indo-European root">Indo-European root</a> <i>vir</i> meaning <i>man</i>) reflect this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In most cultures, <a href="/wiki/Male_privilege" title="Male privilege">male privilege</a> allows men more rights and privileges than women. In societies where men are not given special legal privileges, they typically hold more positions of power, and men are seen as being taken more seriously in society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson201745–48_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson201745–48-41">[41]</a></sup> This is associated with a "gender-role strain" in which men face increased societal pressure to conform to gender roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017119–121_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson2017119–121-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entertainment_and_media">Entertainment and media</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Man&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2022</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Media portrayals of men often replicate traditional understanding of masculinity. Men are portrayed more frequently in television than women and most commonly appear as leads in action and drama programming. Men are typically more active in television programming than women and typically hold more power and status. Due to their prominence, men are more likely to be both the objects and instigators of humorous or disparaging content. Fathers are often portrayed in television as either idealized and caring or clumsy and inept. In advertising, men are disproportionately featured in advertisements for alcohol, vehicles, and business products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rights">Rights</span></h2>
<p>In most societies, men have more legal and cultural rights than women,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelgeson201745–48_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelgeson201745–48-41">[41]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogyny</a> is far more prevalent than <a href="/wiki/Misandry" title="Misandry">misandry</a> in society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ouellette_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ouellette-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Men typically receive less support after being victims of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_assault" title="Sexual assault">sexual assault</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rape_of_males" title="Rape of males">rape of males</a> is stigmatized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rabin_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rabin-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence_against_men" title="Domestic violence against men">Domestic violence against men</a> is similarly stigmatized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marginalizing_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marginalizing-47">[47]</a></sup> Opponents of <a href="/wiki/Circumcision" title="Circumcision">circumcision</a> describe it as a human rights violation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fathers%27_rights_movement" title="Fathers' rights movement">fathers' rights movement</a> seeks to support separated fathers that do not receive equal rights to care for their children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_movement" title="Men's movement">men's movement</a> is the response to issues faced by men in Western countries. It includes <a href="/wiki/Pro-feminism" title="Pro-feminism">pro-feminist</a> groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_liberation_movement" title="Men's liberation movement">men's liberation movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antifeminism" title="Antifeminism">anti-feminist</a> groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Manosphere" title="Manosphere">manosphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_rights_movement" title="Men's rights movement">men's rights movement</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_symbol">Sex symbol</span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_symbol" title="Mars symbol">Mars symbol</a> (♂) is a common symbol that represents the male sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The symbol is identical to the planetary symbol of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> It was first used to denote sex by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1751.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The symbol is sometimes seen as a stylized representation of the shield and spear of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman god</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>. According to Stearn, however, this derivation is "fanciful" and all the historical evidence favours "the conclusion of the French classical scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claude_de_Saumaise" title="Claude de Saumaise">Claude de Saumaise</a>" that it is derived from <i>θρ</i>, the contraction of a Greek name for the planet Mars, which is <i>Thouros</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
