<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Visual arts</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Art forms that create works that are primarily visual in nature</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the video game publisher, see <a href="/wiki/Visual_Arts_(company)" title="Visual Arts (company)">Visual Arts (company)</a>.</div>

</p>

<p>The <b>visual arts</b> are <a href="/wiki/Art#Forms,_genres,_media,_and_styles" title="Art">art forms</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramics_(art)" title="Ceramics (art)">ceramics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">filmmaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">crafts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>. Many artistic disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">conceptual art</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Textile_arts" title="Textile arts">textile arts</a> also involve aspects of visual arts as well as arts of other types. Also included within the visual arts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> are the <a href="/wiki/Applied_arts" title="Applied arts">applied arts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">fashion design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">interior design</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decorative_art" title="Decorative art">decorative art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Current usage of the term "visual arts" includes <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">fine art</a> as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Applied_art" title="Applied art">applied</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decorative_art" title="Decorative art">decorative arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">crafts</a>, but this was not always the case. Before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_Movement" title="Arts and Crafts Movement">Arts and Crafts Movement</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> and elsewhere at the turn of the 20th century, the term 'artist' had for some centuries often been restricted to a person working in the fine arts (such as painting, sculpture, or printmaking) and not the decorative arts, craft, or applied Visual arts media. The distinction was emphasized by artists of the Arts and Crafts Movement, who valued vernacular art forms as much as high forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">Art schools</a> made a distinction between the fine arts and the crafts, maintaining that a craftsperson could not be considered a practitioner of the arts.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metropolitan_Museum_1_(4675714481).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3084" data-file-width="4615" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Metropolitan_Museum_1_%284675714481%29.jpg/220px-Metropolitan_Museum_1_%284675714481%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Metropolitan_Museum_1_%284675714481%29.jpg/330px-Metropolitan_Museum_1_%284675714481%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Metropolitan_Museum_1_%284675714481%29.jpg/440px-Metropolitan_Museum_1_%284675714481%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metropolitan_Museum_1_(4675714481).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. Museums constitute a primary forum for the display of visual arts.</div></div></div>
<p>The increasing tendency to prefer the painting styles, and to a lesser degree sculpture, of technique or style over another has been a feature of artist throughout the ages. In many instances painting has been seen as relying to the highest degree on the imagination of the artist, and the furthest removed from manual labour – in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">Chinese painting</a> the most highly valued styles were those of "scholar-painting", at least in theory practiced by gentleman amateurs. The Western <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_of_genres" title="Hierarchy of genres">hierarchy of genres</a> reflected similar attitudes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_training">Education and training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts_education" title="Visual arts education">Visual arts education</a></div>
<p>Training in the visual arts has generally been through variations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apprentice" title="Apprentice">apprentice</a> and workshop systems. In Europe the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> movement to increase the prestige of the artist led to the <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">academy</a> system for training artists, and today most of the people who are pursuing a career in arts train in <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">art schools</a> at tertiary levels. Visual arts have now become an elective subject in most education systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawing">Drawing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drawing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">Drawing</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">Drawing</a> is a means of making an <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">image</a>, illustration or graphic using any of a wide variety of tools and techniques available online and offline. It generally involves making marks on a surface by applying pressure from a tool, or moving a tool across a surface using dry media such as <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">pencils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pen_and_ink" title="Pen and ink">pen and ink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">inked</a> <a href="/wiki/Brush" title="Brush">brushes</a>, wax <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_pencil" title="Color pencil">color pencils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayon" title="Crayon">crayons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">markers</a>. Digital tools, including pens, <a href="/wiki/Stylus" title="Stylus">stylus</a>, that simulate the effects of these are also used. The main techniques used in drawing are: line drawing, <a href="/wiki/Hatching" title="Hatching">hatching</a>, crosshatching, random hatching, shading, scribbling, <a href="/wiki/Stippling" title="Stippling">stippling</a>, and blending. An artist who excels in drawing is referred to as a <i>draftsman</i> or <i>draughtsman</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Drawing and painting goes back tens of thousands of years. <a href="/wiki/Art_of_the_Upper_Paleolithic" title="Art of the Upper Paleolithic">Art of the Upper Paleolithic</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Figurative_art" title="Figurative art">figurative art</a> beginning between about 40,000 to 35,000 years ago. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-figurative" title="Non-figurative">Non-figurative</a> <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave paintings</a> consisting of hand stencils and simple geometric shapes are even older. Paleolithic <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave representations</a> of animals are found in areas such as <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux, France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Altamira" title="Cave of Altamira">Altamira, Spain</a> in Europe, <a href="/wiki/Caves_in_the_Maros-Pangkep_karst" title="Caves in the Maros-Pangkep karst">Maros, Sulawesi</a> in Asia, and <a href="/wiki/Gabarnmung" title="Gabarnmung">Gabarnmung</a>, Australia.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Art_of_ancient_Egypt" title="Art of ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>, ink drawings on <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, often depicting people, were used as models for painting or sculpture. Drawings on <a href="/wiki/Pottery_of_ancient_Greece" title="Pottery of ancient Greece">Greek vases</a>, initially geometric, later developed to the human form with <a href="/wiki/Black-figure_pottery" title="Black-figure pottery">black-figure pottery</a> during the 7th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/History_of_paper" title="History of paper">paper</a> becoming common in Europe by the 15th century, drawing was adopted by masters such as <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Sandro Botticelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> who sometimes treated drawing as an art in its own right rather than a preparatory stage for painting or sculpture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting">Painting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Painting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg"><img alt="Mosaic of Battle of Issus Alexander against Darius" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2133" data-file-width="3562" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg/250px-Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg/375px-Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg/500px-Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Issus_mosaic_-_Museo_Archeologico_Nazionale_-_Naples_2013-05-16_16-25-06_BW.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mosaic of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Issus" title="Battle of Issus">Battle of Issus</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">Painting</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg"><img alt="drawing of Nefertari with Isis" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="368" data-file-width="237" decoding="async" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg/150px-Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg/225px-Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ankh_isis_nefertari.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Nefertari" title="Nefertari">Nefertari</a> with Isis</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">Painting</a> taken literally is the practice of applying <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> suspended in a carrier (or <a href="/wiki/Paint#Components" title="Paint">medium</a>) and a binding agent (a <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">glue</a>) to a surface (support) such as <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a> or a wall. However, when used in an artistic sense it means the use of this activity in combination with <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">composition</a>, or other aesthetic considerations in order to manifest the expressive and conceptual intention of the practitioner. Painting is also used to express spiritual motifs and ideas; sites of this kind of painting range from artwork depicting mythological figures on pottery to <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">The Sistine Chapel</a> to the human body itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_painting" title="History of painting">History of painting</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_early_history">Origins and early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origins and early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Like drawing, painting has its documented origins in caves and on rock faces. The finest examples, believed by some to be 32,000 years old, are in the <a href="/wiki/Chauvet_Cave" title="Chauvet Cave">Chauvet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux</a> caves in southern France. In shades of red, brown, yellow and black, the paintings on the walls and ceilings are of bison, cattle, horses and deer.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raphael_Spasimo.jpg"><img alt="Raphael painting of Christ Falling on the Way to Calvary from 1514–1516" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2952" data-file-width="2110" decoding="async" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Raphael_Spasimo.jpg/150px-Raphael_Spasimo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Raphael_Spasimo.jpg/225px-Raphael_Spasimo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Raphael_Spasimo.jpg/300px-Raphael_Spasimo.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raphael_Spasimo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Raphael: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ_Falling_on_the_Way_to_Calvary_(Raphael)" title="Christ Falling on the Way to Calvary (Raphael)">Spasimo</a></i> (1514–1516)</div></div></div>
<p>Paintings of human figures can be found in the tombs of ancient Egypt. In the great temple of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramses_II" title="Ramses II">Ramses II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nefertari" title="Nefertari">Nefertari</a>, his queen, is depicted being led by <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Greeks contributed to painting but much of their work has been lost. One of the best remaining representations are the Hellenistic <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">Fayum mummy portraits</a>. Another example is mosaic of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Issus" title="Battle of Issus">Battle of Issus</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, which was probably based on a Greek painting. Greek and Roman art contributed to <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine art</a> in the 4th century BC, which initiated a tradition in icon painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Renaissance">The Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">Italian Renaissance painting</a></div>
<p>Apart from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscripts" title="Illuminated manuscripts">illuminated manuscripts</a> produced by monks during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the next significant contribution to European art was from <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">Italy's renaissance painters</a>. From <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a> in the 13th century to <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a> at the beginning of the 16th century, this was the richest period in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Italy" title="Art of Italy">Italian art</a> as the <i>chiaroscuro</i> techniques were used to create the illusion of 3-D space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_ronda_de_noche,_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg"><img alt="Rembrandt painting Night Watch two men striding forward with a crowd" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="11528" data-file-width="14168" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/La_ronda_de_noche%2C_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg/200px-La_ronda_de_noche%2C_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/La_ronda_de_noche%2C_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg/300px-La_ronda_de_noche%2C_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/La_ronda_de_noche%2C_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg/400px-La_ronda_de_noche%2C_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_ronda_de_noche,_por_Rembrandt_van_Rijn.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rembrandt: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night_Watch_(painting)" title="Night Watch (painting)">The Night Watch</a></i>, 1642</div></div></div>
<p>Painters in northern Europe too were influenced by the Italian school. <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a> from Belgium, <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder" title="Pieter Bruegel the Elder">Pieter Bruegel the Elder</a> from the Netherlands and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Younger" title="Hans Holbein the Younger">Hans Holbein the Younger</a> from Germany are among the most successful painters of the times. They used the <a href="/wiki/Glaze_(painting_technique)" title="Glaze (painting technique)">glazing technique</a> with oils to achieve depth and luminosity.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg"><img alt="Claude Monet painting Déjeuner sur l'herbe from 1866 artists sitting on picnic blanket" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="894" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg/150px-Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg/225px-Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg/300px-Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monet_dejeunersurlherbe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Claude Monet: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_D%C3%A9jeuner_sur_l%E2%80%99herbe_(Monet)" title="Le Déjeuner sur l’herbe (Monet)">Le Déjeuner sur l’herbe</a></i> (1866)</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_masters">Dutch masters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dutch masters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age_painting" title="Dutch Golden Age painting">Dutch Golden Age painting</a></div>
<p>The 17th century witnessed the emergence of the great Dutch masters such as the versatile <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a> who was especially remembered for his portraits and Bible scenes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vermeer" title="Vermeer">Vermeer</a> who specialized in interior scenes of Dutch life.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Baroque">Baroque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Baroque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> started after the Renaissance, from the late 16th century to the late 17th century. Main artists of the Baroque included <a href="/wiki/Caravaggio" title="Caravaggio">Caravaggio</a>, who made heavy use of <a href="/wiki/Tenebrism" title="Tenebrism">tenebrism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a> painter who studied in Italy, worked for local churches in <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and also painted a series for <a href="/wiki/Marie_de%27_Medici" title="Marie de' Medici">Marie de' Medici</a>. <a href="/wiki/Annibale_Carracci" title="Annibale Carracci">Annibale Carracci</a> took influences from the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a> and created the genre of <a href="/wiki/Illusionistic_ceiling_painting" title="Illusionistic ceiling painting">illusionistic ceiling painting</a>. Much of the development that happened in the Baroque was because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> and the resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_Reformation" title="Counter Reformation">Counter Reformation</a>. Much of what defines the Baroque is dramatic lighting and overall visuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impressionism">Impressionism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impressionism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a> began in France in the 19th century with a loose association of artists including <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Pierre-Auguste Renoir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a> who brought a new freely brushed style to painting, often choosing to paint realistic scenes of modern life outside rather than in the studio. This was achieved through a new expression of aesthetic features demonstrated by brush strokes and the impression of reality. They achieved intense color vibration by using pure, unmixed colors and short brush strokes. The movement influenced art as a dynamic, moving through time and adjusting to newfound techniques and perception of art. Attention to detail became less of a priority in achieving, whilst exploring a biased view of landscapes and nature to the artist's eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg"><img alt="Paul Gauguin painting The Vision After the Sermon from 1888 nuns gathering around a small angel" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2001" data-file-width="2536" decoding="async" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg/150px-Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg/225px-Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg/300px-Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Gauguin_137.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paul Gauguin: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Vision_After_the_Sermon" title="The Vision After the Sermon">The Vision After the Sermon</a></i> (1888)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Scream.jpg"><img alt="Edvard Munch painting The Scream from 1893 man at bridge with hands to ears and mouth open" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3822" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/The_Scream.jpg/150px-The_Scream.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/The_Scream.jpg/225px-The_Scream.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/The_Scream.jpg/300px-The_Scream.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Scream.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Edvard Munch: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scream" title="The Scream">The Scream</a></i> (1893)</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-impressionism">Post-impressionism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post-impressionism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">Post-Impressionism</a></div>
<p>Towards the end of the 19th century, several young painters took impressionism a stage further, using geometric forms and unnatural color to depict emotions while striving for deeper symbolism. Of particular note are <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a>, who was strongly influenced by Asian, African and Japanese art, <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>, a Dutchman who moved to France where he drew on the strong sunlight of the south, and <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec">Toulouse-Lautrec</a>, remembered for his vivid paintings of night life in the Paris district of <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">Montmartre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Symbolism.2C_expressionism_and_cubism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism,_expressionism_and_cubism">Symbolism, expressionism and cubism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Symbolism, expressionism and cubism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">Modern art</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">Edvard Munch</a>, a Norwegian artist, developed his symbolistic approach at the end of the 19th century, inspired by the French impressionist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manet" title="Manet">Manet</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scream" title="The Scream">The Scream</a></i> (1893), his most famous work, is widely interpreted as representing the universal anxiety of modern man. Partly as a result of Munch's influence, the German <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionist</a> movement originated in Germany at the beginning of the 20th century as artists such as <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner" title="Ernst Ludwig Kirchner">Ernst Kirschner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erich_Heckel" title="Erich Heckel">Erich Heckel</a> began to distort reality for an emotional effect.
</p><p>In parallel, the style known as <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">cubism</a> developed in France as artists focused on the volume and space of sharp structures within a composition. <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">Georges Braque</a> were the leading proponents of the movement. Objects are broken up, analyzed, and re-assembled in an abstracted form. By the 1920s, the style had developed into surrealism with <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Dali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Magritte" title="René Magritte">Magritte</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printmaking">Printmaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Printmaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg"><img alt="Ancient Chinese engraving of female instrumentalists" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1895" data-file-width="1713" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg/150px-Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg/225px-Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg/300px-Ancient_Chinese_instrumentalists.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ancientchineseinstrumentalists.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ancient Chinese engraving of female instrumentalists</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">Printmaking</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">Printmaking</a> is creating, for artistic purposes, an image on a <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(printing)" title="Matrix (printing)">matrix</a> that is then transferred to a two-dimensional (flat) surface by means of ink (or another form of pigmentation). Except in the case of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotype" title="Monotype">monotype</a>, the same matrix can be used to produce many examples of the print.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg"><img alt="Albrecht Dürer engraving Melancholia I from 1541 seated angel contemplating figure" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="3146" decoding="async" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg/170px-D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg/255px-D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg/340px-D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Albrecht Dürer: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melancholia_I" title="Melancholia I">Melancholia I</a></i> (1541)</div></div></div>
<p>Historically, the major techniques (also called media) involved are <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Line_engraving" title="Line engraving">line engraving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">etching</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithography" title="Lithography">lithography</a>, and screen printing (serigraphy, silk screening) but there are many others, including modern digital techniques. Normally, the print is printed on <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, but other mediums range from cloth and <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a> to more modern materials.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_history">European history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: European history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">Old master print</a></div>
<p>Prints in the Western tradition produced before about 1830 are known as <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">old master prints</a>. In Europe, from around 1400 AD <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a>, was used for master prints on paper by using printing techniques developed in the Byzantine and Islamic worlds. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Wolgemut" title="Michael Wolgemut">Michael Wolgemut</a> improved German woodcut from about 1475, and <a href="/wiki/Erhard_Reuwich" title="Erhard Reuwich">Erhard Reuwich</a>, a Dutchman, was the first to use cross-hatching.  At the end of the century <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> brought the Western woodcut to a stage that has never been surpassed, increasing the status of the single-leaf woodcut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_origin_and_practice">Chinese origin and practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chinese origin and practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jingangjing.jpg"><img alt="The Chinese Diamond Sutra, the world's oldest Woodblock printing book from 868 CE" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3438" data-file-width="4917" decoding="async" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Jingangjing.jpg/220px-Jingangjing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Jingangjing.jpg/330px-Jingangjing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Jingangjing.jpg/440px-Jingangjing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jingangjing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Chinese <i><a href="/wiki/Diamond_Sutra" title="Diamond Sutra">Diamond Sutra</a></i>, the world's oldest <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">printed</a> book (868 CE)</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">Woodblock printing</a></div>
<p>In China, the art of printmaking developed some 1,100 years ago as illustrations alongside text cut in woodblocks for printing on paper. Initially images were mainly religious but in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>, artists began to cut landscapes. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming</a> (1368–1644) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing</a> (1616–1911) dynasties, the technique was perfected for both religious and artistic engravings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Development_in_Japan_1603.E2.80.931867"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_in_Japan_1603–1867">Development in Japan 1603–1867</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Development in Japan 1603–1867">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg"><img alt='Hokusai color print "Red Fuji southern wind clear morning" from Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji' class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2937" data-file-width="4400" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg/170px-Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg/255px-Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg/340px-Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_Fuji_southern_wind_clear_morning.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hokusai" title="Hokusai">Hokusai</a>: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Fuji" title="Red Fuji">Red Fuji</a></i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Thirty-six_Views_of_Mount_Fuji" title="Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji">Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji</a></i> (1830–1832)</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing_in_Japan" title="Woodblock printing in Japan">Woodblock printing in Japan</a></div>
<p>Woodblock printing in Japan (Japanese: 木版画, moku hanga) is a technique best known for its use in the <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a> artistic genre; however, it was also used very widely for printing <a href="/wiki/E-hon" title="E-hon">illustrated books</a> in the same period. Woodblock printing had been used in China for centuries to print books, long before the advent of movable type, but was only widely adopted in Japan during the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (1603–1867). Although similar to woodcut in western printmaking in some regards, moku hanga differs greatly in that water-based inks are used (as opposed to western woodcut, which uses oil-based inks), allowing for a wide range of vivid color, glazes and color transparency.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">Photography</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">Photography</a> is the process of making pictures by means of the action of light. The light patterns reflected or emitted from objects are recorded onto a sensitive medium or storage chip through a timed <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">exposure</a>. The process is done through mechanical <a href="/wiki/Shutter_(photography)" title="Shutter (photography)">shutters</a> or electronically timed exposure of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> processing or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digitizing" title="Digitizing">digitizing</a> devices known as <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">cameras</a>.
</p><p>The word comes from the Greek φως <i>phos</i> ("light"), and γραφις <i>graphis</i> ("stylus", "paintbrush") or γραφη <i>graphê</i>, together meaning "drawing with light" or "representation by means of lines" or "drawing." Traditionally, the product of photography has been called a <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">photograph</a>. The term <i>photo</i> is an abbreviation; many people also call them <i>pictures.</i> In digital photography, the term <i>image</i> has begun to replace <i>photograph.</i> (The term <i>image</i> is traditional in geometric <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">Architecture</a></div>

<p>Architecture is the process and the product of <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">designing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">constructing</a> <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a> or any other structures. Architectural works, in the material form of buildings, are often perceived as cultural symbols and as works of art. Historical civilizations are often identified with their surviving architectural achievements.
</p><p>The earliest surviving written work on the subject of architecture is <i><a href="/wiki/De_architectura" title="De architectura">De architectura</a></i>, by the Roman architect <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> in the early 1st century AD. According to Vitruvius, a good building should satisfy the three principles of firmitas, utilitas, venustas, commonly known by the original translation – firmness, commodity and delight. An equivalent in modern English would be:
</p>
<ol><li>Durability – a building should stand up robustly and remain in good condition.</li>
<li>Utility – it should be suitable for the purposes for which it is used.</li>
<li>Beauty – it should be aesthetically pleasing.</li></ol>
<p>Building first evolved out of the dynamics between needs (shelter, security, worship, etc.) and means (available <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building materials</a> and attendant skills). As human cultures developed and knowledge began to be formalized through oral traditions and practices, building became a <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a>, and "architecture" is the name given to the most highly formalized and respected versions of that craft.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmmaking">Filmmaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Filmmaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">Filmmaking</a></div>
<p>Filmmaking is the process of making a motion-picture, from an initial conception and research, through scriptwriting, shooting and recording, animation or other special effects, editing, sound and music work and finally distribution to an audience; it refers broadly to the creation of all types of films, embracing documentary, strains of theatre and literature in film, and poetic or experimental practices, and is often used to refer to video-based processes as well.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_art">Computer art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Computer art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computer_art" title="Computer art">Computer art</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="715" data-file-width="590" decoding="async" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg/220px-Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg/330px-Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg/440px-Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wiki.picture_by_drawing_machine_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Desmond_Paul_Henry" title="Desmond Paul Henry">Desmond Paul Henry</a>, Picture by Drawing Machine 1, c. 1960</div></div></div>
<p>Visual artists are no longer limited to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_(arts)" title="Media (arts)">traditional Visual arts media</a>. Computers have been used as an ever more common tool in the visual arts since the 1960s. Uses include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_capture" title="Image capture">capturing</a> or creating of images and forms, the editing of those images and forms (including exploring multiple <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">compositions</a>) and the final <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artistic_rendering" title="Artistic rendering">rendering</a> or <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> (including <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a>).
<b>Computer art</b> is any in which <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> played a role in production or display. Such art can be an image, sound, <a href="/wiki/Computer_animation" title="Computer animation">animation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a>, <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a>, <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a>, <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">website</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Performance" title="Performance">performance</a> or gallery installation. 
</p><p>Many traditional disciplines are now integrating <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">digital</a> technologies and, as a result, the lines between traditional works of art and <a href="/wiki/New_media" title="New media">new media</a> works created using computers have been blurred. For instance, an artist may combine traditional <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a> with <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_art" title="Algorithmic art">algorithmic art</a> and other digital techniques. As a result, defining computer art by its end product can be difficult. Nevertheless, this type of art is beginning to appear in art museum exhibits, though it has yet to prove its legitimacy as a form unto itself and this technology is widely seen in contemporary art more as a tool rather than a form as with painting. On the other hand, there are computer-based artworks which belong to a new <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">conceptual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Postdigital" title="Postdigital">postdigital</a> strand, assuming the same technologies, and their social impact, as an object of inquiry.
</p><p>Computer usage has blurred the distinctions between <a href="/wiki/Illustrator" title="Illustrator">illustrators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Image_editing" title="Image editing">photo editors</a>, <a href="/wiki/3D_modeling" title="3D modeling">3-D modelers</a>, and handicraft artists. Sophisticated rendering and editing software has led to multi-skilled image developers. <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">Photographers</a> may become <a href="/wiki/Digital_art" title="Digital art">digital artists</a>. Illustrators may become <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">animators</a>. Handicraft may be <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided" title="Computer-aided">computer-aided</a> or use <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated imagery</a> as a template. Computer <a href="/wiki/Clip_art" title="Clip art">clip art</a> usage has also made the clear distinction between visual arts and <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a> less obvious due to the easy access and editing of clip art in the process of <a href="/wiki/Pagination" title="Pagination">paginating</a> a document, especially to the unskilled observer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_arts">Plastic arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Plastic arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">Plastic arts</a></div>
<p><b>Plastic arts</b> is a term for art forms that involve physical manipulation of a plastic medium by moulding or modeling such as sculpture or ceramics. The term has also been applied to <i>all the visual (non-literary, non-musical) arts</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Materials that can be carved or shaped, such as stone or wood, concrete or steel, have also been included in the narrower definition, since, with appropriate tools, such materials are also capable of modulation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This use of the term "plastic" in the arts should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>'s use, nor with the movement he termed, in French and English, "<a href="/wiki/Neoplasticism" title="Neoplasticism">Neoplasticism</a>."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">Sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sculpture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">Sculpture</a></div>
<p>Sculpture is <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">three-dimensional</a> <a href="/wiki/Work_of_art" title="Work of art">artwork</a> created by shaping or combining hard or <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> material, sound, or text and or light, commonly <a href="/wiki/Stone_sculpture" title="Stone sculpture">stone</a> (either <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>), <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>. Some sculptures are created directly by finding or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/carving" title="wikt:carving">carving</a>; others are assembled, built together and <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">fired</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welded</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">molded</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">cast</a>. Sculptures are often <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">painted</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
A person who creates sculptures is called a sculptor.
</p><p>Because sculpture involves the use of materials that can be moulded or modulated, it is considered one of the <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">plastic arts</a>. The majority of <a href="/wiki/Public_art" title="Public art">public art</a> is sculpture. Many sculptures together in a <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">garden</a> setting may be referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Sculpture_garden" title="Sculpture garden">sculpture garden</a>. Sculptors do not always make sculptures by hand. With increasing technology in the 20th century and the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">conceptual art</a> over technical mastery, more sculptors turned to <a href="/wiki/Art_fabrication" title="Art fabrication">art fabricators</a> to produce their artworks. With fabrication, the artist creates a design and pays a fabricator to produce it. This allows sculptors to create larger and more complex sculptures out of material like cement, metal and plastic, that they would not be able to create by hand. Sculptures can also be made with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-d_printing" title="3-d printing">3-d printing</a> technology.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="US_copyright_definition_of_visual_art">US copyright definition of visual art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: US copyright definition of visual art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, the law protecting the copyright over a piece of visual art gives a more restrictive definition of "visual art".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A "work of visual art" is —
<br/>(1) a painting, drawing, print or sculpture, existing in a single copy, in a limited edition of 200 copies or fewer that are signed and consecutively numbered by the author, or, in the case of a sculpture, in multiple cast, carved, or fabricated sculptures of 200 or fewer that are consecutively numbered by the author and bear the signature or other identifying mark of the author; or
<br/>(2) a still photographic image produced for exhibition purposes only, existing in a single copy that is signed by the author, or in a limited edition of 200 copies or fewer that are signed and consecutively numbered by the author.<br/><br/>
A work of visual art does not include —
<br/>(A)(i) any poster, map, globe, chart, <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">technical drawing</a>, diagram, model, applied art, motion picture or other audiovisual work, book, magazine, newspaper, periodical, data base, electronic information service, electronic publication, or similar publication;
<br/>  (ii) any merchandising item or advertising, promotional, descriptive, covering, or packaging material or container;
<br/>  (iii) any portion or part of any item described in clause (i) or (ii);
<br/>(B) any <a href="/wiki/Work_for_hire" title="Work for hire">work made for hire</a>; or
<br/>(C) any work not subject to copyright protection under this title.
</p>
</blockquote>
