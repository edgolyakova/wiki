<p>An <b>aircraft</b> is a vehicle or machine that is able to <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">fly</a> by gaining support from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">air</a>. It counters the force of gravity by using either <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">static lift</a> or by using the <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">dynamic lift</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or in a few cases the <a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">downward thrust</a> from <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a>. Common examples of aircraft include <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Blimp" title="Blimp">blimps</a>), <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powered_paragliding" title="Powered paragliding">paramotors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wingsoverkansas_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wingsoverkansas-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The human activity that surrounds aircraft is called <i><a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a></i>. The science of aviation, including designing and building aircraft, is called <i><a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>.</i> <a href="/wiki/Aircrew" title="Aircrew">Crewed</a> aircraft are flown by an onboard <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_pilot" title="Aircraft pilot">pilot</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> may be remotely controlled or self-controlled by onboard <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a>. Aircraft may be classified by different criteria, such as lift type, <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft#Methods_of_propulsion" title="Powered aircraft">aircraft propulsion</a>, usage and others.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_aviation" title="History of aviation">History of aviation</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_aviation" title="Timeline of aviation">Timeline of aviation</a></div>
<p>Flying model craft and stories of manned <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> go back many centuries; however, the first manned ascent — and safe descent — in modern times took place by larger hot-air balloons developed in the 18th century. Each of the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wars" title="World Wars">World Wars</a> led to great technical advances. Consequently, the history of aircraft can be divided into five eras:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Early_flying_machines" title="Early flying machines">Pioneers of flight</a>, from the earliest experiments to 1914.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aviation_in_World_War_I" title="Aviation in World War I">First World War</a>, 1914 to 1918.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aviation_between_the_World_Wars" title="Aviation between the World Wars">Aviation between the World Wars</a>, 1918 to 1939.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_warfare_of_World_War_II" title="Air warfare of World War II">Second World War</a>, 1939 to 1945.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">Postwar era</a>, also called the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Age" title="Jet Age">Jet Age</a>, 1945 to the present day.</li></ul>

<h3><span id="Lighter_than_air_.E2.80.93_aerostats"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighter_than_air_–_aerostats">Lighter than air – aerostats<span class="anchor" id="Lighter_than_air"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lighter than air – aerostats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostat</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg/220px-Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg/330px-Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg/440px-Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hot air <a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">balloons</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostats</a> use <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> to float in the air in much the same way that ships float on the water. They are characterized by one or more large cells or canopies, filled with a relatively low-density gas such as <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air</a>, which is less dense than the surrounding air. When the weight of this is added to the weight of the aircraft structure, it adds up to the same weight as the air that the craft displaces.
</p><p>Small hot-air balloons, called <a href="/wiki/Sky_lantern" title="Sky lantern">sky lanterns</a>, were first invented in ancient China prior to the 3rd century BC and used primarily in cultural celebrations, and were only the second type of aircraft to fly, the first being <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kites</a>, which were first invented in ancient China over two thousand years ago. (See <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology_in_China#Han_Dynasty" title="History of science and technology in China">Han Dynasty</a>)
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USS_Akron_(ZRS-4)_in_flight_over_Manhattan,_circa_1931-1933.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4241" data-file-width="5516" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg/220px-USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg/330px-USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg/440px-USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USS_Akron_(ZRS-4)_in_flight_over_Manhattan,_circa_1931-1933.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airship <a href="/wiki/USS_Akron" title="USS Akron">USS <i>Akron</i></a> over Manhattan in the 1930s</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">balloon</a> was originally any aerostat, while the term <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airship</a> was used for large, powered aircraft designs — usually fixed-wing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1919, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Handley_Page" title="Frederick Handley Page">Frederick Handley Page</a> was reported as referring to "ships of the air," with smaller passenger types as "Air yachts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In the 1930s, large intercontinental flying boats were also sometimes referred to as "ships of the air" or "flying-ships".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> — though none had yet been built. The advent of powered balloons, called dirigible balloons, and later of rigid hulls allowing a great increase in size, began to change the way these words were used. Huge powered aerostats, characterized by a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_airship" title="Rigid airship">rigid</a> outer framework and separate aerodynamic skin surrounding the gas bags, were produced, the <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelins</a> being the largest and most famous. There were still no fixed-wing aircraft or non-rigid balloons large enough to be called airships, so "airship" came to be synonymous with these aircraft. Then several accidents, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">Hindenburg disaster</a> in 1937, led to the demise of these airships. Nowadays a "balloon" is an unpowered aerostat and an "airship" is a powered one.
</p><p>A powered, steerable aerostat is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">dirigible</a></i>. Sometimes this term is applied only to non-rigid balloons, and sometimes <i>dirigible balloon</i> is regarded as the definition of an airship (which may then be rigid or non-rigid). Non-rigid dirigibles are characterized by a moderately <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamic</a> gasbag with stabilizing fins at the back. These soon became known as <i><a href="/wiki/Blimp" title="Blimp">blimps</a></i>. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, this shape was widely adopted for <a href="/wiki/Barrage_balloon" title="Barrage balloon">tethered balloons</a>; in windy weather, this both reduces the strain on the tether and stabilizes the balloon. The nickname <i>blimp</i> was adopted along with the shape. In modern times, any small dirigible or airship is called a blimp, though a blimp may be unpowered as well as powered.
</p>
<h3><span id="Heavier-than-air_.E2.80.93_aerodynes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavier-than-air_–_aerodynes">Heavier-than-air – aerodynes<span class="anchor" id="Heavier_than_air"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heavier-than-air – aerodynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Heavier-than-air aircraft, such as <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, must find some way to push air or gas downwards so that a reaction occurs (by Newton's laws of motion) to push the aircraft upwards. This dynamic movement through the air is the origin of the term. There are two ways to produce dynamic upthrust — <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamic lift</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">powered lift</a> in the form of engine thrust.
</p><p>Aerodynamic lift involving <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> is the most common, with <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> being kept in the air by the forward movement of wings, and <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a> by spinning wing-shaped <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">rotors</a> sometimes called "rotary wings." A wing is a flat, horizontal surface, usually shaped in cross-section as an <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">aerofoil</a>. To fly, air must flow over the wing and generate <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>. A <i>flexible wing</i> is a wing made of fabric or thin sheet material, often stretched over a rigid frame. A <i><a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kite</a></i> is tethered to the ground and relies on the speed of the wind over its wings, which may be flexible or rigid, fixed, or rotary.
</p><p>With powered lift, the aircraft directs its engine thrust <a href="/wiki/Vertical_and_horizontal" title="Vertical and horizontal">vertically</a> downward. <a href="/wiki/V/STOL" title="V/STOL">V/STOL</a> aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/Harrier_Jump_Jet" title="Harrier Jump Jet">Harrier Jump Jet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II#F-35B" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">Lockheed Martin F-35B</a> take off and land vertically using powered lift and transfer to aerodynamic lift in steady flight.
</p><p>A pure <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> is not usually regarded as an aerodyne because it does not depend on the air for its lift (and can even fly into space); however, many aerodynamic lift vehicles have been powered or assisted by rocket motors. Rocket-powered missiles that obtain aerodynamic lift at very high speed due to airflow over their bodies are a marginal case.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed-wing">Fixed-wing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fixed-wing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">Fixed-wing aircraft</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="4096" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg/220px-Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg/330px-Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg/440px-Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>, the world's largest passenger <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a></div></div></div>
<p>The forerunner of the fixed-wing aircraft is the <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kite</a>. Whereas a fixed-wing aircraft relies on its forward speed to create airflow over the wings, a kite is tethered to the ground and relies on the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> blowing over its wings to provide lift. Kites were the first kind of aircraft to fly and <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology_in_China" title="History of science and technology in China">were invented in China</a> around 500 BC. Much aerodynamic research was done with kites before test aircraft, <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind tunnels</a>, and computer modelling programs became available.
</p><p>The first heavier-than-air craft capable of controlled free-flight were <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a>. A glider designed by <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a> carried out the first true manned, controlled flight in 1853.
</p><p>The practical, powered, fixed-wing aircraft (the <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplane</a> or aeroplane) was invented by <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wilbur and Orville Wright</a>. Besides the method of <a href="#Propulsion">propulsion</a>, fixed-wing aircraft are in general characterized by their <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration" title="Wing configuration">wing configuration</a>. The most important wing characteristics are:
</p>
<ul><li>Number of wings — <a href="/wiki/Monoplane" title="Monoplane">monoplane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biplane" title="Biplane">biplane</a>, etc.</li>
<li>Wing support — Braced or cantilever, rigid, or flexible.</li>
<li>Wing planform — including <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a>, angle of <a href="/wiki/Swept_wing" title="Swept wing">sweep</a>, and any variations along the span (including the important class of <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wings</a>).</li>
<li>Location of the horizontal stabilizer, if any.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aeronautics)" title="Dihedral (aeronautics)">Dihedral angle</a> — positive, zero, or negative (anhedral).</li></ul>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration#Variable_geometry" title="Wing configuration">variable geometry</a> aircraft can change its wing configuration during flight.
</p><p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Flying_wing" title="Flying wing">flying wing</a></i> has no fuselage, though it may have small blisters or pods. The opposite of this is a <i><a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a></i>, which has no wings, though it may have small stabilizing and control surfaces.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ground-effect_vehicle" title="Ground-effect vehicle">Wing-in-ground-effect</a> vehicles are generally not considered aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They "fly" efficiently close to the surface of the ground or water, like conventional aircraft during takeoff. An example is the Russian ekranoplan nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea_Monster" title="Caspian Sea Monster">Caspian Sea Monster</a>". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_human-powered_aircraft" title="History of human-powered aircraft">Man-powered aircraft</a> also rely on <a href="/wiki/Ground_effect_(aerodynamics)" title="Ground effect (aerodynamics)">ground effect</a> to remain airborne with minimal pilot power, but this is only because they are so underpowered—in fact, the airframe is capable of flying higher.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotorcraft">Rotorcraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rotorcraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">Rotorcraft</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2015" data-file-width="3034" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg/220px-20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg/330px-20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg/440px-20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">autogyro</a></div></div></div>
<p>Rotorcraft, or rotary-wing aircraft, use a spinning <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">rotor</a> with aerofoil cross-section blades (a <i>rotary wing</i>) to provide lift. Types include <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">autogyros</a>, and various hybrids such as <a href="/wiki/Gyrodyne" title="Gyrodyne">gyrodynes</a> and compound rotorcraft.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">Helicopters</a></i> have a rotor turned by an engine-driven shaft. The rotor pushes air downward to create lift. By tilting the rotor forward, the downward flow is tilted backward, producing thrust for forward flight. Some helicopters have more than one rotor and a few have rotors turned by gas jets at the tips. Some have a <a href="/wiki/Tail_rotor" title="Tail rotor">tail rotor</a> to counteract the rotation of the main rotor, and to aid directional control.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">Autogyros</a></i> have unpowered rotors, with a separate power plant to provide thrust. The rotor is tilted backward. As the autogyro moves forward, air blows upward across the rotor, making it spin. This spinning increases the speed of airflow over the rotor, to provide lift. <a href="/wiki/Rotor_kite" title="Rotor kite">Rotor kites</a> are unpowered autogyros, which are towed to give them forward speed or tethered to a static anchor in high-wind for kited flight.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Cyclogyro" title="Cyclogyro">Cyclogyros</a></i> rotate their wings about a horizontal axis.
</p><p><i>Compound rotorcraft</i> have wings that provide some or all of the lift in forward flight. They are nowadays classified as <i><a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">powered lift</a></i> types and not as rotorcraft. <i><a href="/wiki/Tiltrotor" title="Tiltrotor">Tiltrotor</a></i> aircraft (such as the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Boeing_V-22_Osprey" title="Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey">Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tiltwing" title="Tiltwing">tiltwing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tail-sitter" title="Tail-sitter">tail-sitter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coleopter" title="Coleopter">coleopter</a> aircraft have their rotors/<a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aeronautics)" title="Propeller (aeronautics)">propellers</a> horizontal for vertical flight and vertical for forward flight.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods_of_lift">Other methods of lift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other methods of lift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2589" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg/220px-X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg/330px-X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg/440px-X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>X-24B lifting body</div></div></div>
<ul><li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a></i> is an aircraft body shaped to produce lift. If there are any wings, they are too small to provide significant lift and are used only for stability and control. Lifting bodies are not efficient: they suffer from high drag, and must also travel at high speed to generate enough lift to fly. Many of the research prototypes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta_X-24" title="Martin Marietta X-24">Martin Marietta X-24</a>, which led up to the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, were lifting bodies, though the Space Shuttle is not, and some <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_speed" title="Supersonic speed">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a> obtain lift from the airflow over a tubular body.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">Powered lift</a></i> types rely on engine-derived lift for vertical takeoff and landing (<a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a>). Most types transition to fixed-wing lift for horizontal flight. Classes of powered lift types include <a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a> jet aircraft (such as the <a href="/wiki/Harrier_Jump_Jet" title="Harrier Jump Jet">Harrier Jump Jet</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Tiltrotor" title="Tiltrotor">tiltrotors</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Boeing_V-22_Osprey" title="Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey">Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey</a>, among others. A few experimental designs rely entirely on engine thrust to provide lift throughout the whole flight, including personal fan-lift hover platforms and jetpacks. <a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a> research designs include the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Thrust_Measuring_Rig" title="Rolls-Royce Thrust Measuring Rig">Rolls-Royce Thrust Measuring Rig</a>.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Flettner_airplane" title="Flettner airplane">Flettner airplane</a></i> uses a rotating cylinder in place of a fixed wing, obtaining lift from the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">Magnus effect</a>.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopter</a></i> obtains thrust by flapping its wings.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_speed_extremes">Size and speed extremes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Size and speed extremes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The smallest aircraft are toys/recreational items, and <a href="/wiki/AeroVironment_Nano_Hummingbird" title="AeroVironment Nano Hummingbird">nano aircraft</a>.
</p><p>The largest aircraft by dimensions and volume (as of 2016) is the 302 ft (92 m) long British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airlander_10" title="Airlander 10">Airlander 10</a>, a hybrid blimp, with helicopter and fixed-wing features, and reportedly capable of speeds up to 90 mph (140 km/h; 78 kn), and an airborne endurance of two weeks with a payload of up to 22,050 lb (10,000 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airlander_cbs_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-airlander_cbs-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crash_cnn_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-crash_cnn-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest aircraft by weight and largest regular fixed-wing aircraft ever built, as of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, is the <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-225_Mriya" title="Antonov An-225 Mriya">Antonov An-225 <i>Mriya</i></a>. That Ukrainian-built six-engine Russian transport of the 1980s is 84 m (276 ft) long, with an 88 m (289 ft) wingspan. It holds the world payload record, after transporting 428,834 lb (194,516 kg) of goods, and has recently flown 100 t (220,000 lb) loads commercially. With a maximum loaded weight of 550–700 t (1,210,000–1,540,000 lb), it is also the heaviest aircraft built to date. It can cruise at 500 mph (800 km/h; 430 kn).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biggest_fox_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-biggest_fox-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biggest_chron_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-biggest_chron-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worlds_largest_might_lose_smithsonian_2015_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-worlds_largest_might_lose_smithsonian_2015-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-largest_aero_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-largest_aero-21">[21]</a></sup> The aircraft was destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Ukrainian_War" title="Russo-Ukrainian War">Russo-Ukrainian War</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The largest military airplanes are the Ukrainian <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-124_Ruslan" title="Antonov An-124 Ruslan">Antonov An-124 <i>Ruslan</i></a> (world's second-largest airplane, also used as a civilian transport),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2nd_nasa_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-2nd_nasa-22">[22]</a></sup> and American <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-5_Galaxy" title="Lockheed C-5 Galaxy">Lockheed C-5 Galaxy</a> transport, weighing, loaded, over 380 t (840,000 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-largest_aero_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-largest_aero-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wide_nasa_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-wide_nasa-23">[23]</a></sup> The 8-engine, piston/propeller <a href="/wiki/Hughes_H-4_Hercules" title="Hughes H-4 Hercules">Hughes H-4 <i>Hercules</i></a> "Spruce Goose" — an American <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> wooden flying boat transport with a greater wingspan (94m/260ft) than any current aircraft and a tail height equal to the tallest (Airbus A380-800 at 24.1m/78ft) — flew only one short hop in the late 1940s and never flew out of <a href="/wiki/Ground_effect_(aerodynamics)" title="Ground effect (aerodynamics)">ground effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-largest_aero_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-largest_aero-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest civilian airplanes, apart from the above-noted An-225 and An-124, are the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_Beluga" title="Airbus Beluga">Airbus Beluga</a> cargo transport derivative of the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">Airbus A300</a> jet airliner, the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Dreamlifter" title="Boeing Dreamlifter">Boeing Dreamlifter</a> cargo transport derivative of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> jet airliner/transport (the 747-200B was, at its creation in the 1960s, the heaviest aircraft ever built, with a maximum weight of over 400 t (880,000 lb)),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wide_nasa_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-wide_nasa-23">[23]</a></sup> and the double-decker <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a> "super-jumbo" jet airliner (the world's largest passenger airliner).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-largest_aero_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-largest_aero-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airbus_times_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-airbus_times-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_large_aircraft" title="List of large aircraft">List of large aircraft</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speeds">Speeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Speeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fastest recorded powered aircraft flight and fastest recorded aircraft flight of an air-breathing powered aircraft was of the <a href="/wiki/NASA_X-43" title="NASA X-43">NASA X-43</a>A <i>Pegasus</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a>-powered, <a href="/wiki/Hypersonic_speed" title="Hypersonic speed">hypersonic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a> experimental research aircraft, at <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 9.6, exactly 3,292.8 m/s (11,854 km/h; 6,400.7 kn; 7,366 mph). The X-43A set that new mark, and broke its own world record of Mach 6.3, exactly 2,160.9 m/s (7,779 km/h; 4,200.5 kn; 4,834 mph), set in March 2004, on its third and final flight on 16 November 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-x43_nasa_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-x43_nasa-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fastest_guinness_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-fastest_guinness-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to the X-43A, the fastest recorded powered airplane flight (and still the record for the fastest manned, powered airplane / fastest manned, non-spacecraft aircraft) was of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">North American X-15A-2</a>, rocket-powered airplane at Mach 6.72, or 2,304.96 m/s (8,297.9 km/h; 4,480.48 kn; 5,156.0 mph), on 3 October 1967. On one flight it reached an altitude of 354,300 ft (108,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_aero_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-speed_aero-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manned_guinness_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-manned_guinness-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_magz_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-fast_magz-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The fastest known, production aircraft (other than rockets and missiles) currently or formerly operational (as of 2016) are:
</p>
<ul><li>The fastest fixed-wing aircraft, and fastest glider, is the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, a rocket-glider hybrid, which has re-entered the atmosphere as a fixed-wing glider at more than Mach 25, equal to 8,575 m/s (30,870 km/h; 16,668 kn; 19,180 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_aero_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-speed_aero-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-regimes_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-regimes-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fastest military airplane ever built: <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird</a>, a U.S. <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> jet fixed-wing aircraft, known to fly beyond Mach 3.3, equal to 1,131.9 m/s (4,075 km/h; 2,200.2 kn; 2,532 mph). On 28 July 1976, an SR-71 set the record for the fastest and highest-flying operational aircraft with an absolute speed record of 2,193 mph (3,529 km/h; 1,906 kn; 980 m/s) and an absolute altitude record of 85,068 ft (25,929 m). At its retirement in January 1990, it was the fastest air-breathing aircraft / fastest jet aircraft in the world, a record still standing as of August 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_aero_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-speed_aero-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lock_museum_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-lock_museum-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr71_gone_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sr71_gone-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ab_ga_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ab_ga-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr71_usaf_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-sr71_usaf-35">[35]</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Note: Some sources refer to the above-mentioned X-15 as the "fastest military airplane" because it was partly a project of the U.S. Navy and Air Force; however, the X-15 was not used in non-experimental actual military operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_magz_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-fast_magz-29">[29]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li>The fastest current military aircraft are the Soviet/Russian <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-25">Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-25</a> — capable of Mach 3.2, equal to 1,097.6 m/s (3,951 km/h; 2,133.6 kn; 2,455 mph), at the expense of engine damage, or Mach 2.83, equal to 970.69 m/s (3,494.5 km/h; 1,886.87 kn; 2,171.4 mph), normally — and the Russian <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan_MiG-31" title="Mikoyan MiG-31">Mikoyan MiG-31</a>E (also capable of Mach 2.83 normally). Both are fighter-interceptor jet airplanes, in active operations as of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9_business_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-9_business-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_bus_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-fast_bus-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_bloom_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-five_bloom-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fastest civilian airplane ever built, and fastest passenger airliner ever built: the briefly operated <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-144" title="Tupolev Tu-144">Tupolev Tu-144</a> supersonic jet airliner (Mach 2.35, 1,600 mph, 2,587 km/h), which was believed to cruise at about Mach 2.2. The Tu-144 (officially operated from 1968 to 1978, ending after two crashes of the small fleet) was outlived by its rival, the <i><a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a></i> (Mach 2.23), a French/British supersonic airliner, known to cruise at Mach 2.02 (1.450 mph, 2,333 kmh at cruising altitude), operating from 1976 until the small Concorde fleet was grounded permanently in 2003, following the crash of one in the early 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_aero_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-speed_aero-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_magz_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-fast_magz-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fastest_airl_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-fastest_airl-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_guin_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-fast_guin-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fastest civilian airplane currently flying: the <a href="/wiki/Cessna_Citation_X" title="Cessna Citation X">Cessna Citation X</a>, an American business jet, capable of Mach 0.935, or 320.705 m/s (1,154.54 km/h; 623.401 kn; 717.40 mph). Its rival, the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulfstream_G650" title="Gulfstream G650">Gulfstream G650</a> business jet, can reach Mach 0.925, or 317.275 m/s (1,142.19 km/h; 616.733 kn; 709.72 mph)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_aero_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-speed_aero-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_magz_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-fast_magz-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cessna_rolls_out_2016_04_15_wbj_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-cessna_rolls_out_2016_04_15_wbj-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fastest airliner currently flying is the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>, quoted as being capable of cruising over Mach 0.885, 303.555 m/s (1,092.80 km/h; 590.064 kn; 679.03 mph). Previously, the fastest were the troubled, short-lived Russian (Soviet Union) <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-144" title="Tupolev Tu-144">Tupolev Tu-144</a> SST (Mach 2.35; equal to 806.05 m/s (2,901.8 km/h; 1,566.84 kn; 1,803.1 mph)) and the French/British <i>Concorde</i>, with a maximum speed of Mach 2.23 or 686 m/s (2,470 km/h; 1,333 kn; 1,530 mph) and a normal cruising speed of Mach 2 or 320.705 m/s (1,154.54 km/h; 623.401 kn; 717.40 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_aero_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-speed_aero-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fastest_airl_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-fastest_airl-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fast_guin_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-fast_guin-40">[40]</a></sup> Before them, the <a href="/wiki/Convair_990_Coronado" title="Convair 990 Coronado">Convair 990 Coronado</a> jet airliner of the 1960s flew at over 600 mph (970 km/h; 520 kn; 270 m/s).</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propulsion">Propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unpowered_aircraft">Unpowered aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unpowered aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unpowered_aircraft" title="Unpowered aircraft">Unpowered aircraft</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="831" data-file-width="1108" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG/220px-PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG/330px-PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG/440px-PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PH-1274_Rolladen-Schneider_LS-4b.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sailplane (<a href="/wiki/Rolladen-Schneider_LS4" title="Rolladen-Schneider LS4">Rolladen-Schneider LS4</a>)</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">Gliders</a> are heavier-than-air aircraft that do not employ propulsion once airborne. Take-off may be by launching forward and downward from a high location, or by pulling into the air on a tow-line, either by a ground-based winch or vehicle, or by a powered "tug" aircraft. For a glider to maintain its forward air speed and lift, it must descend in relation to the air (but not necessarily in relation to the ground). Many gliders can "soar", <i>i.e.</i>, gain height from updrafts such as thermal currents. The first practical, controllable example was designed and built by the British scientist and pioneer <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a>, whom many recognise as the first aeronautical engineer. Common examples of gliders are <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">sailplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hang_gliding" title="Hang gliding">hang gliders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paragliding" title="Paragliding">paragliders</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">Balloons</a> drift with the wind, though normally the pilot can control the altitude, either by heating the air or by releasing ballast, giving some directional control (since the wind direction changes with altitude). A wing-shaped hybrid balloon can glide directionally when rising or falling; but a spherically shaped balloon does not have such directional control.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">Kites</a> are aircraft<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> that are tethered to the ground or other object (fixed or mobile) that maintains tension in the tether or <a href="/wiki/Kite_line" title="Kite line">kite line</a>; they rely on virtual or real wind blowing over and under them to generate lift and drag. <a href="/wiki/Kytoon" title="Kytoon">Kytoons</a> are balloon-kite hybrids that are shaped and tethered to obtain kiting deflections, and can be lighter-than-air, neutrally buoyant, or heavier-than-air.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powered_aircraft">Powered aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Powered aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft" title="Powered aircraft">Powered aircraft</a></div>
<p>Powered aircraft have one or more onboard sources of mechanical power, typically <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">aircraft engines</a> although rubber and manpower have also been used. Most aircraft engines are either lightweight <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">reciprocating engines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>. Engine fuel is stored in tanks, usually in the wings but larger aircraft also have additional <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">fuel tanks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Propeller_aircraft">Propeller aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Propeller aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft#Propeller_aircraft" title="Powered aircraft">Powered aircraft § Propeller aircraft</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="768" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg/220px-WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg/330px-WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg/440px-WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a>-engined <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada_DHC-6_Twin_Otter" title="De Havilland Canada DHC-6 Twin Otter">DeHavilland Twin Otter</a> adapted as a <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">floatplane</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft#Propeller_aircraft" title="Powered aircraft">Propeller aircraft</a> use one or more <a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aeronautics)" title="Propeller (aeronautics)">propellers</a> (airscrews) to create thrust in a forward direction. The propeller is usually mounted in front of the power source in <i><a href="/wiki/Tractor_configuration" title="Tractor configuration">tractor configuration</a></i> but can be mounted behind in <i><a href="/wiki/Pusher_configuration" title="Pusher configuration">pusher configuration</a></i>. Variations of propeller layout include <i><a href="/wiki/Contra-rotating_propellers" title="Contra-rotating propellers">contra-rotating propellers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ducted_fan" title="Ducted fan">ducted fans</a></i>.
</p><p>Many kinds of power plant have been used to drive propellers. Early airships used man power or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_aircraft" title="Steam aircraft">steam engines</a>. The more practical <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion piston engine</a> was used for virtually all fixed-wing aircraft until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and is still used in many smaller aircraft. Some types use turbine engines to drive a propeller in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a> or <a href="/wiki/Propfan" title="Propfan">propfan</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_human-powered_aircraft" title="History of human-powered aircraft">Human-powered flight</a> has been achieved, but has not become a practical means of transport. Unmanned aircraft and models have also used power sources such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_aircraft" title="Electric aircraft">electric motors</a> and rubber bands.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_aircraft">Jet aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Jet aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">Jet aircraft</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1822" data-file-width="2736" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg/220px-Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg/330px-Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg/440px-Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">Lockheed Martin F-22A Raptor</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">Jet aircraft</a> use <a href="/wiki/Airbreathing_jet_engine" title="Airbreathing jet engine">airbreathing jet engines</a>, which take in air, burn fuel with it in a <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a>, and accelerate the exhaust rearwards to provide thrust.
</p><p>Different jet engine configurations include the <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a>, sometimes with the addition of an <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a>. Those with no rotating turbomachinery include the <a href="/wiki/Pulsejet" title="Pulsejet">pulsejet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a>. These mechanically simple engines produce no thrust when stationary, so the aircraft must be launched to flying speed using a catapult, like the <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V-1 flying bomb</a>, or a rocket, for example. Other engine types include the <a href="/wiki/Motorjet" title="Motorjet">motorjet</a> and the dual-cycle <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J58" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J58">Pratt &amp; Whitney J58</a>.
</p><p>Compared to engines using propellers, jet engines can provide much higher thrust, higher speeds and, above about 40,000 ft (12,000 m), greater efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist8_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-hist8-44">[44]</a></sup> They are also much more fuel-efficient than <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>. As a consequence nearly all large, high-speed or high-altitude aircraft use jet engines.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotorcraft_2">Rotorcraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rotorcraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">Rotorcraft</a></div>
<p>Some rotorcraft, such as <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, have a powered rotary wing or <i><a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">rotor</a></i>, where the rotor disc can be angled slightly forward so that a proportion of its lift is directed forwards. The rotor may, like a propeller, be powered by a variety of methods such as a piston engine or turbine. Experiments have also used <a href="/wiki/Tip_jet" title="Tip jet">jet nozzles at the rotor blade tips</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_powered_aircraft">Other types of powered aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other types of powered aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft" title="Rocket-powered aircraft">Rocket-powered aircraft</a></i> have occasionally been experimented with, and the <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Me_163_Komet" title="Messerschmitt Me 163 Komet">Messerschmitt Me 163 <i>Komet</i></a> fighter even saw action in the Second World War. Since then, they have been restricted to research aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">North American X-15</a>, which traveled up into space where air-breathing engines cannot work (rockets carry their own oxidant). Rockets have more often been used as a supplement to the main power plant, typically for the <a href="/wiki/JATO" title="JATO">rocket-assisted take off</a> of heavily loaded aircraft, but also to provide high-speed dash capability in some hybrid designs such as the <a href="/wiki/Saunders-Roe_SR.53" title="Saunders-Roe SR.53">Saunders-Roe SR.53</a>.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopter</a></i> obtains thrust by flapping its wings. It has found practical use in a <a href="/wiki/Ornithopter#Applications_for_unmanned_ornithopters" title="Ornithopter">model hawk</a> used to freeze prey animals into stillness so that they can be captured, and in toy birds.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aircraft are <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_design_process" title="Aircraft design process">designed</a> according to many factors such as customer and manufacturer demand, <a href="/wiki/Aviation_safety" title="Aviation safety">safety</a> protocols and physical and economic constraints. For many types of aircraft the design process is regulated by national airworthiness authorities.
</p><p>The key parts of an aircraft are generally divided into three categories:
</p>
<ul><li>The <i>structure</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Airframe" title="Airframe">airframe</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-far_amt_2012_faa_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-far_amt_2012_faa-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_dictionary_1980_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-janes_dictionary_1980-48">[48]</a></sup>) comprises the main load-bearing elements and associated equipment, as well as flight controls.</li>
<li>The <i>propulsion system</i> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powerplant" title="Powerplant">powerplant</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phak_glossary_faa_gov_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-phak_glossary_faa_gov-49">[49]</a></sup>) (if it is powered) comprises the power source and associated equipment, as described above.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a></i> comprise the electrical and electronic control, navigation and communication systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_dictionary_1980_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-janes_dictionary_1980-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary_of_aviation_1974_fell_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary_of_aviation_1974_fell-50">[50]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The approach to structural design varies widely between different types of aircraft. Some, such as paragliders, comprise only flexible materials that act in tension and rely on aerodynamic pressure to hold their shape. A <a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">balloon</a> similarly relies on internal gas pressure, but may have a rigid basket or gondola slung below it to carry its payload. Early aircraft, including <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>, often employed flexible <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_dope" title="Aircraft dope">doped</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_fabric_covering" title="Aircraft fabric covering">aircraft fabric covering</a> to give a reasonably smooth aeroshell stretched over a rigid frame. Later aircraft employed semi-<a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> techniques, where the skin of the aircraft is stiff enough to share much of the flight loads. In a true monocoque design there is no internal structure left. With the recent emphasis on sustainability hemp has picked up some attention, having a way smaller carbon foot print and 10 times stronger than steel, hemp could become the standard of manufacturing in the future. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The key structural parts of an aircraft depend on what type it is.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerostats">Aerostats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aerostats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostat</a></div>
<p>Lighter-than-air types are characterised by one or more gasbags, typically with a supporting structure of flexible cables or a rigid framework called its hull. Other elements such as engines or a gondola may also be attached to the supporting structure.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynes">Aerodynes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Aerodynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="487" data-file-width="663" decoding="async" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg/220px-Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg/330px-Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg/440px-Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airframe diagram for an <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">AgustaWestland AW101</a> <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a></div></div></div>
<p>Heavier-than-air types are characterised by one or more wings and a central <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>. The fuselage typically also carries a tail or <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a> for stability and control, and an undercarriage for takeoff and landing. Engines may be located on the fuselage or wings. On a <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> the wings are rigidly attached to the fuselage, while on a <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a> the wings are attached to a rotating vertical shaft. Smaller designs sometimes use flexible materials for part or all of the structure, held in place either by a rigid frame or by air pressure. The fixed parts of the structure comprise the <a href="/wiki/Airframe" title="Airframe">airframe</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power">Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Propulsion" title="Propulsion">Propulsion</a></div>
<p>The source of motive power for an aircraft is normally called the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powerplant" title="Powerplant">powerplant</a>,</i> and includes <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor" title="Motor">motor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propellor" title="Propellor">propellor</a> or <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rotor" title="Rotor">rotor</a>, (if any), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_nozzle" title="Jet nozzle">jet nozzles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrust_reverser" title="Thrust reverser">thrust reversers</a> (if any), and accessories essential to the functioning of the engine or motor (e.g.: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starter_motor" title="Starter motor">starter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignition_system" title="Ignition system">ignition system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intake" title="Intake">intake system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_system" title="Exhaust system">exhaust system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_fuel_system" title="Aircraft fuel system">fuel system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lubrication" title="Lubrication">lubrication</a> system, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engine_cooling_system" title="Engine cooling system">engine cooling system</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Engine_controls&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Engine controls (page does not exist)">engine controls</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phak_glossary_faa_gov_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-phak_glossary_faa_gov-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Powered aircraft are typically powered by <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piston_engine" title="Piston engine">piston</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internal_combustion_engine_grc_nasa_gov_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-internal_combustion_engine_grc_nasa_gov-52">[52]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_turbine_engine" title="Gas turbine engine">turbine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engines_grc_nasa_gov_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-engines_grc_nasa_gov-53">[53]</a></sup>) burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> -- typically <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> (<a href="/wiki/Avgas" title="Avgas">avgas</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>. A very few are powered by <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> propulsion, or by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_motors" title="Electric motors">electric motors</a>, or by internal combustion engines of other types, or using other fuels. A very few have been powered, for short flights, by <a href="/wiki/Human-powered_aircraft" title="Human-powered aircraft">human muscle energy</a> (e.g.: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gossamer_Condor" title="Gossamer Condor">Gossamer Condor</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natl_air_and_space_museum_bryan_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-natl_air_and_space_museum_bryan-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_encyclopedia_of_aviation_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-janes_encyclopedia_of_aviation-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-electrified_aircraft_nasa_gov_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-electrified_aircraft_nasa_gov-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avionics">Avionics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Avionics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">Avionics</a></div>
<p>The avionics comprise any <i>electronic</i> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_flight_control_system" title="Aircraft flight control system">aircraft flight control systems</a> and related equipment, including electronic <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a> instrumentation, navigation, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, monitoring, and <a href="/wiki/Communications_system" title="Communications system">communications systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary_45-4"><a href="#cite_note-websters_3rd_new_intl_dictionary-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed_46-4"><a href="#cite_note-asa_dictionary_3rd_ed-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_dictionary_1980_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-janes_dictionary_1980-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary_of_aviation_1974_fell_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary_of_aviation_1974_fell-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_characteristics">Flight characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Flight characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_envelope">Flight envelope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Flight envelope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flight_envelope" title="Flight envelope">Flight envelope</a></div>
<p>The flight envelope of an aircraft refers to its approved design capabilities in terms of <a href="/wiki/Airspeed" title="Airspeed">airspeed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Load_factor_(aeronautics)" title="Load factor (aeronautics)">load factor</a> and altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The term can also refer to other assessments of aircraft performance such as maneuverability. When an aircraft is abused, for instance by diving it at too-high a speed, it is said to be flown <i>outside the envelope</i>, something considered foolhardy since it has been taken beyond the design limits which have been established by the manufacturer. Going beyond the envelope may have a known outcome such as <a href="/wiki/Aeroelasticity#Flutter" title="Aeroelasticity">flutter</a> or entry to a non-recoverable spin (possible reasons for the boundary).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1196" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg/220px-Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg/330px-Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg/440px-Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777#777-200LR" title="Boeing 777">Boeing 777-200LR</a> is one of the longest-range airliners, capable of flights of more than halfway around the world.</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range (aeronautics)</a></div>
<p>The range is the distance an aircraft can fly between <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a>, as limited by the time it can remain airborne.
</p><p>For a powered aircraft the time limit is determined by the fuel load and rate of consumption.
</p><p>For an unpowered aircraft, the maximum flight time is limited by factors such as weather conditions and pilot endurance. Many aircraft types are restricted to daylight hours, while balloons are limited by their supply of lifting gas. The range can be seen as the average ground speed multiplied by the maximum time in the air.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A350#A350-900ULR" title="Airbus A350">Airbus A350-900ULR</a> is now the longest range airliner.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_dynamics">Flight dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Flight dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(fixed-wing_aircraft)" title="Flight dynamics (fixed-wing aircraft)">Flight dynamics (fixed-wing aircraft)</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flight_dynamics_with_text.png"><img alt="Flight dynamics with text.png" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/200px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/300px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/400px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div>
<p>Flight dynamics is the science of air vehicle orientation and control in three dimensions. The three critical flight dynamics parameters are the <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_rotation" title="Angle of rotation">angles of rotation</a> around <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">three axes</a> which pass through the vehicle's <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass#Center_of_gravity" title="Center of mass">center of gravity</a>, known as <i><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes#Pitch" title="Aircraft principal axes">pitch</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes#Roll" title="Aircraft principal axes">roll</a>,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes#Yaw" title="Aircraft principal axes">yaw</a></i>.
</p>
<ul><li>Roll is a rotation about the longitudinal axis (equivalent to the rolling or <a href="/wiki/Sailing#Heeling" title="Sailing">heeling</a> of a ship) giving an up-down movement of the wing tips measured by the roll or bank angle.</li>
<li>Pitch is a rotation about the sideways horizontal axis giving an up-down movement of the aircraft nose measured by the <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a>.</li>
<li>Yaw is a rotation about the vertical axis giving a side-to-side movement of the nose known as sideslip.</li></ul>
<p>Flight dynamics is concerned with the stability and control of an aircraft's rotation about each of these axes.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">Stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aircraft_tail.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Aircraft_tail.JPG/220px-Aircraft_tail.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Aircraft_tail.JPG/330px-Aircraft_tail.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Aircraft_tail.JPG/440px-Aircraft_tail.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aircraft_tail.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747-200</a></div></div></div>
<p>An aircraft that is unstable tends to diverge from its intended flight path and so is difficult to fly. A very stable aircraft tends to stay on its flight path and is difficult to maneuver. Therefore, it is important for any design to achieve the desired degree of stability. Since the widespread use of digital computers, it is increasingly common for designs to be inherently unstable and rely on computerised control systems to provide artificial stability.
</p><p>A fixed wing is typically unstable in pitch, roll, and yaw. Pitch and yaw stabilities of conventional fixed wing designs require <a href="/wiki/Stabilizer_(aeronautics)" title="Stabilizer (aeronautics)">horizontal and vertical stabilisers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crane_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crane-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GroundUp_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-GroundUp-60">[60]</a></sup> which act similarly to the feathers on an arrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> These stabilizing surfaces allow equilibrium of aerodynamic forces and to stabilise the <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">flight dynamics</a> of pitch and yaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crane_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crane-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GroundUp_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-GroundUp-60">[60]</a></sup> They are usually mounted on the tail section (<a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a>), although in the <a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">canard</a> layout, the main aft wing replaces the canard foreplane as pitch stabilizer. <a href="/wiki/Tandem_wing" title="Tandem wing">Tandem wing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tailless_aircraft" title="Tailless aircraft">tailless aircraft</a> rely on the same general rule to achieve stability, the aft surface being the stabilising one.
</p><p>A rotary wing is typically unstable in yaw, requiring a vertical stabiliser.
</p><p>A balloon is typically very stable in pitch and roll due to the way the payload is slung underneath the center of lift.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Flight_control_surfaces" title="Flight control surfaces">Flight control surfaces</a> enable the pilot to control an aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(fixed-wing_aircraft)" title="Flight dynamics (fixed-wing aircraft)">flight attitude</a> and are usually part of the wing or mounted on, or integral with, the associated stabilizing surface. Their development was a critical advance in the history of aircraft, which had until that point been uncontrollable in flight.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">Aerospace engineers</a> develop <a href="/wiki/Control_system" title="Control system">control systems</a> for a vehicle's orientation (attitude) about its <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>. The control systems include actuators, which exert forces in various directions, and generate rotational forces or <a href="/wiki/Moment_(physics)" title="Moment (physics)">moments</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_center" title="Aerodynamic center">aerodynamic center</a> of the aircraft, and thus rotate the aircraft in pitch, roll, or yaw. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Pitching_moment" title="Pitching moment">pitching moment</a> is a vertical force applied at a distance forward or aft from the aerodynamic center of the aircraft, causing the aircraft to pitch up or down. Control systems are also sometimes used to increase or decrease drag, for example to slow the aircraft to a safe speed for landing.
</p><p>The two main aerodynamic forces acting on any aircraft are lift supporting it in the air and <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> opposing its motion. Control surfaces or other techniques may also be used to affect these forces directly, without inducing any rotation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_of_aircraft_use">Impacts of aircraft use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Impacts of aircraft use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">Environmental impact of aviation</a></div>
<p>Aircraft permit long distance, high speed <a href="/wiki/Air_travel" title="Air travel">travel</a> and may be a more <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_in_transport#Aircraft" title="Energy efficiency in transport">fuel efficient</a> mode of transportation in some circumstances. Aircraft have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">environmental and climate impacts</a> beyond fuel efficiency considerations, however. They are also relatively <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_noise_pollution" title="Aircraft noise pollution">noisy</a> compared to other forms of travel and high altitude aircraft generate <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a>, which experimental evidence suggests may <a href="/wiki/Global_dimming" title="Global dimming">alter weather patterns</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_for_aircraft">Uses for aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Uses for aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aircraft are produced in several different types optimized for various uses; <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">military aircraft</a>, which includes not just combat types but many types of supporting aircraft, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil aircraft</a>, which include all non-military types, experimental and model.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1181" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg/220px-Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg/330px-Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg/440px-Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">Boeing B-17E</a> in flight</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">Military aircraft</a></div>
<p>A military aircraft is any aircraft that is operated by a legal or insurrectionary armed service of any type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunston_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunston-62">[62]</a></sup> Military aircraft can be either combat or non-combat:
</p>
<ul><li>Combat aircraft are aircraft designed to destroy enemy equipment using its own armament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunston_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-gunston-62">[62]</a></sup> Combat aircraft divide broadly into <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bombers</a>, with several in-between types, such as <a href="/wiki/Fighter-bomber" title="Fighter-bomber">fighter-bombers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attack_aircraft" title="Attack aircraft">attack aircraft</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Attack_helicopter" title="Attack helicopter">attack helicopters</a>.</li>
<li>Non-combat aircraft are not designed for combat as their primary function, but may carry weapons for self-defense. Non-combat roles include search and rescue, reconnaissance, observation, transport, training, and <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a>. These aircraft are often variants of civil aircraft.</li></ul>
<p>Most military aircraft are powered heavier-than-air types. Other types, such as gliders and balloons, have also been used as military aircraft; for example, balloons were used for observation during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">military gliders</a> were used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> to land troops.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil">Civil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Civil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2215" data-file-width="3322" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg/220px-Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg/330px-Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg/440px-Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW109" title="AgustaWestland AW109">Agusta A109</a> helicopter of the <a href="/wiki/Rega_(air_rescue)" title="Rega (air rescue)">Swiss air rescue service</a></div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">Civil aviation</a></div>
<p>Civil aircraft divide into <i>commercial</i> and <i>general</i> types, however there are some overlaps.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Commercial_aviation" title="Commercial aviation">Commercial aircraft</a> include types designed for scheduled and charter airline flights, carrying passengers, <a href="/wiki/Airmail" title="Airmail">mail</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a>. The larger passenger-carrying types are the airliners, the largest of which are <a href="/wiki/Wide-body_aircraft" title="Wide-body aircraft">wide-body aircraft</a>. Some of the smaller types are also used in <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a>, and some of the larger types are used as <a href="/wiki/Air_transports_of_heads_of_state_and_government" title="Air transports of heads of state and government">VIP aircraft</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">General aviation</a> is a catch-all covering other kinds of <a href="/wiki/Private_aviation" title="Private aviation">private</a> (where the pilot is not paid for time or expenses) and commercial use, and involving a wide range of aircraft types such as <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jets (bizjets)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trainer_aircraft" title="Trainer aircraft">trainers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homebuilt_aircraft" title="Homebuilt aircraft">homebuilt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warbird" title="Warbird">warbirds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloons</a> to name a few. The vast majority of aircraft today are general aviation types.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental">Experimental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Experimental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Experimental_aircraft" title="Experimental aircraft">Experimental aircraft</a></div>
<p>An experimental aircraft is one that has not been fully proven in flight, or that carries a <a href="/wiki/Airworthiness_certificate#Special_Airworthiness_Certificate" title="Airworthiness certificate">Special Airworthiness Certificate</a>, called an Experimental Certificate in United States parlance. This often implies that the aircraft is testing new aerospace technologies, though the term also refers to amateur-built and kit-built aircraft, many of which are based on proven designs.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model">Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Model_aircraft" title="Model aircraft">Model aircraft</a></div>
<p>A model aircraft is a small unmanned type made to fly for fun, for static display, for aerodynamic research or for other purposes. A <a href="/wiki/Scale_model" title="Scale model">scale model</a> is a replica of some larger design.
</p>
