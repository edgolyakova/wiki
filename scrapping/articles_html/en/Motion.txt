<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Motion</span></h1>

<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Change in position of an object</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Motion_(disambiguation)" title="Motion (disambiguation)">Motion (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1533" data-file-width="2300" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg/300px-Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg/450px-Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg/600px-Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Motor_cycle_stunt2_amk.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A motorcyclist doing a <a href="/wiki/Wheelie" title="Wheelie">wheelie</a>, representing motion</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>motion</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> in which an object changes its <a href="/wiki/Position_(geometry)" title="Position (geometry)">position</a> with respect to time. Motion is mathematically described in terms of <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(geometry)" title="Displacement (geometry)">displacement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> to an observer and measuring the change in position of the body relative to that frame with change in time. The branch of physics describing the motion of objects without reference to its cause is called <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">kinematics</a>, while the branch studying forces and their effect on motion is called <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a>.
</p><p>If an object is not changing relative to a given frame of reference, the object is said to be <i>at rest</i>, <i>motionless</i>, <i>immobile</i>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stationary" title="wikt:stationary">stationary</a></i>, or to have a constant or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-invariant" title="Time-invariant">time-invariant</a> position with reference to its surroundings. Modern physics holds that, as there is no absolute frame of reference, Newton's concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Absolute_space_and_time" title="Absolute space and time">absolute motion</a></i> cannot be determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> As such, everything in the universe can be considered to be in motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyson-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 20–21">: 20–21 </span></sup>
</p><p>Motion applies to various physical systems: objects, bodies, matter particles, matter fields, <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, radiation fields, radiation particles, <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">space-time</a>. One can also speak on the motion of images, shapes, and boundaries. In general, the term motion signifies a continuous change in the positions or configuration of a physical system in space. For example, one can talk about the motion of a wave or the motion of a <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantum</a> particle, where the configuration consists of probabilities of the wave or particle occupying specific positions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laws_of_motion">Laws of motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Laws of motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In physics, the motion of <span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/massive#:~:text=(physics%2C%20of%20a%20particle)%20Possessing%20mass.">massive</a></span> bodies is described through two related sets of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_law" title="Scientific law">laws</a> of mechanics. <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">Classical mechanics</a> for super atomic (larger than an atom) objects (such as <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanic" title="Quantum mechanic">quantum mechanics</a> for <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">sub-atomic</a> objects (such as <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a>). <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_mechanics">Classical mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">Kinematics</a></div>
<p>Classical mechanics is used for describing the motion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> objects moving at speeds significantly slower than the speed of light, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projectiles" title="Projectiles">projectiles</a> to parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinery" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_objects" title="Astronomical objects">astronomical objects</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>. It produces very accurate results within these domains and is one of the oldest and largest scientific descriptions in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>.
</p><p>Classical mechanics is fundamentally based on <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's laws of motion</a>. These laws describe the relationship between the forces acting on a body and the motion of that body. They were first compiled by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Sir Isaac Newton</a> in his work <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i>, which was first published on July 5, 1687. Newton's three laws are:
</p>
<ol><li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">body</a> at rest will remain at rest, and a body in motion will remain in motion unless it is acted upon by an external force. (This is known as the law of <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>.)</li>
<li>Force (<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle {\vec {F}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mover>

<mo stretchy="false">→<!-- → --></mo>
</mover>
</mrow>
</mrow>

</mrow>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_mechanics">Quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">Quantum mechanics</a> is a set of principles describing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_systems" title="Physical systems">physical reality</a> at the atomic level of matter (<a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">subatomic particles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, and even smaller <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>). These descriptions include the simultaneous wave-like and particle-like behavior of both <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> energy as described in the <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave–particle duality">wave–particle duality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In classical mechanics, accurate <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">predictions</a> of the state of objects can be calculated, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_location" title="Absolute location">location</a> and <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>. In quantum mechanics, due to the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">Heisenberg uncertainty principle</a>, the complete state of a subatomic particle, such as its location and velocity, cannot be simultaneously determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to describing the motion of atomic level phenomena, quantum mechanics is useful in understanding some large-scale phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Superfluidity" title="Superfluidity">superfluidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">biological systems</a>, including the function of <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">smell receptors</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">structures of protein</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orders_of_magnitude">Orders of magnitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orders of magnitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Humans, like all known things in the universe, are in constant motion;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyson_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tyson-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 8–9">: 8–9 </span></sup> however, aside from obvious movements of the various external <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">body</a> parts and <a href="/wiki/Animal_locomotion" title="Animal locomotion">locomotion</a>, humans are in motion in a variety of ways which are more difficult to <a href="/wiki/Motion_perception" title="Motion perception">perceive</a>.  Many of these "imperceptible motions" are only perceivable with the help of special tools and careful observation. The larger scales of imperceptible motions are difficult for humans to perceive for two reasons: <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's laws of motion</a> (particularly the third) which prevents the feeling of motion on a mass to which the observer is connected, and the lack of an obvious <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> which would allow individuals to easily see that they are moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The smaller scales of these motions are too small to be detected conventionally with human <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe">Universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">Spacetime</a> (the fabric of the universe) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expanding</a>, meaning everything in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is stretching, like a <a href="/wiki/Rubber_band" title="Rubber band">rubber band</a>. This motion is the most obscure as it is not physical motion, but rather a change in the very nature of the universe. The primary source of verification of this expansion was provided by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> who demonstrated that all galaxies and distant astronomical objects were moving away from Earth, known as <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>, predicted by a universal expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galaxy">Galaxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Galaxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">Milky Way Galaxy</a> is moving through <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> and many astronomers believe the velocity of this motion to be approximately 600 kilometres per second (1,340,000 mph) relative to the observed locations of other nearby galaxies. Another reference frame is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">Cosmic microwave background</a>. This frame of reference indicates that the Milky Way is moving at around 582 kilometres per second (1,300,000 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dipole_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-dipole-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (August 2016)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sun_and_Solar_System">Sun and Solar System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sun and Solar System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_motion" title="Planetary motion">Planetary motion</a></div>
<p>The Milky Way is <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotating</a> around its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">dense</a> <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">Galactic Center</a>, thus the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is moving in a circle within the <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>. Away from the central bulge, or outer rim, the typical stellar <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> is between 210 and 240 kilometres per second (470,000 and 540,000 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fn4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-fn4-10">[10]</a></sup> All planets and their moons move with the Sun. Thus, the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is in motion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Earth is <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotating</a> or spinning around its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">axis</a>. This is evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>, at the equator the earth has an eastward velocity of 0.4651 kilometres per second (1,040 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_goodard_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_goodard-11">[11]</a></sup> The Earth is also <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbiting</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_revolution" title="Orbital revolution">orbital revolution</a>. A complete orbit around the sun takes one <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>, or about 365 days; it averages a speed of about 30 kilometres per second (67,000 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth_fact_sheet_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-earth_fact_sheet-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continents">Continents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Continents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_tectonic" title="Plate tectonic">Plate tectonics</a> tells us that the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> are drifting on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_current" title="Convection current">convection currents</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, causing them to move across the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> at the slow speed of approximately 2.54 centimetres (1 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, the velocities of plates range widely. The fastest-moving plates are the oceanic plates, with the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Plate" title="Cocos Plate">Cocos Plate</a> advancing at a rate of 75 millimetres (3.0 in) per year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> moving 52–69 millimetres (2.0–2.7 in) per year. At the other extreme, the slowest-moving plate is the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, progressing at a typical rate of about 21 millimetres (0.83 in) per year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_body">Internal body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Internal body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The human <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> is constantly contracting to move <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> throughout the body. Through larger veins and arteries in the body, blood has been found to travel at approximately 0.33 m/s. Though considerable variation exists, and peak flows in the <a href="/wiki/Venae_cavae" title="Venae cavae">venae cavae</a> have been found between 0.1 and 0.45 metres per second (0.33 and 1.48 ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscles</a> of hollow internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscera" title="Viscera">organs</a> are moving. The most familiar would be the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a> which is where digested <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> is forced throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_tract" title="Digestive tract">digestive tract</a>. Though different foods travel through the body at different rates, an average speed through the human <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> is 3.48 kilometres per hour (2.16 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The human <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a> is also constantly causing movements of excess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluids" title="Fluids">fluids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a>, and immune system related products around the body. The lymph fluid has been found to move through a lymph capillary of the <a href="/wiki/Human_skin" title="Human skin">skin</a> at approximately 0.0000097 m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cells">Cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> have many structures and organelles which move throughout them. <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_streaming" title="Cytoplasmic streaming">Cytoplasmic streaming</a> is a way in which cells move molecular substances throughout the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_proteins" title="Motor proteins">motor proteins</a> work as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_motors" title="Molecular motors">molecular motors</a> within a cell and move along the surface of various cellular substrates such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microtubules" title="Microtubules">microtubules</a>, and motor proteins are typically powered by the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP), and convert chemical energy into mechanical work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesicle_(biology)" title="Vesicle (biology)">Vesicles</a> propelled by motor proteins have been found to have a velocity of approximately 0.00000152 m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particles">Particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics" title="Laws of thermodynamics">laws of thermodynamics</a>, all <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">particles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> are in constant random motion as long as the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> is above <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>. Thus the <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> which make up the human body are vibrating, colliding, and moving. This motion can be detected as temperature; higher temperatures, which represent greater <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> in the particles, feel warm to humans who sense the thermal energy transferring from the object being touched to their nerves. Similarly, when lower temperature objects are touched, the senses perceive the transfer of heat away from the body as a feeling of cold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subatomic_particles">Subatomic particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Subatomic particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within the standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital_model" title="Atomic orbital model">atomic orbital model</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> exist in a region around the nucleus of each atom.  This region is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_cloud" title="Electron cloud">electron cloud</a>.  According to <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">Bohr's model</a> of the atom, electrons have a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_velocity" title="Electron velocity">velocity</a>, and the larger the nucleus they are orbiting the faster they would need to move.  If electrons were to move about the electron cloud in strict paths the same way planets orbit the sun, then electrons would be required to do so at speeds which would far exceed the speed of light.  However, there is no reason that one must confine oneself to this strict conceptualization (that electrons move in paths the same way macroscopic objects do), rather one can conceptualize electrons to be 'particles' that capriciously exist within the bounds of the electron cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Inside the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> are also probably moving around due to the electrical repulsion of the protons and the presence of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> of both particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Light">Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">Speed of light</a></div>
<p>Light moves at a speed of 299,792,458 m/s, or 299,792.458 kilometres per second (186,282.397 mi/s), in a vacuum. The speed of light in vacuum (or <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle c}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>c</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle c}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="c" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.007ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span>) is also the speed of all <a href="/wiki/Massless_particle" title="Massless particle">massless particles</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">fields</a> in a vacuum, and it is the upper limit on the speed at which energy, matter, information or <a href="/wiki/Causality_(physics)" title="Causality (physics)">causation</a> can travel. The speed of light in vacuum is thus the upper limit for speed for all physical systems.
</p><p>In addition, the speed of light is an <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(physics)" title="Invariant (physics)">invariant</a> quantity: it has the same value, irrespective of the position or speed of the observer. This property makes the speed of light <i>c</i> a natural measurement unit for speed and a <a href="/wiki/Physical_constant" title="Physical constant">fundamental constant</a> of nature.
</p><p>In 2011, the speed of light was redefined alongside all seven SI base units using what it calls "the explicit-constant formulation", where each "unit is defined indirectly by specifying explicitly an exact value for a well-recognized fundamental constant", as was done for the speed of light. A new, but completely equivalent, wording of the metre's definition was proposed: "The metre, symbol m, is the unit of length; its magnitude is set by fixing the numerical value of the speed of light in vacuum to be equal to exactly <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7008299792458000000♠"></span>299<span style="margin-left:.25em;">792</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">458</span></span> when it is expressed in the SI unit <span class="nowrap">m s<sup>−1</sup></span>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  This implicit change to the speed of light was one of the changes that was incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/2019_redefinition_of_the_SI_base_units" title="2019 redefinition of the SI base units">2019 redefinition of the SI base units</a>, also termed the <i>New SI</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superluminal_motion">Superluminal motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Superluminal motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some motion appears to an observer to exceed the speed of light. Bursts of energy moving out along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_jet" title="Relativistic jet">relativistic jets</a> emitted from these objects can have a <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a> that appears greater than the speed of light. All of these sources are thought to contain a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>, responsible for the ejection of mass at high velocities. <a href="/wiki/Light_echo" title="Light echo">Light echoes</a> can also produce apparent superluminal motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This occurs owing to how motion is often calculated at long distances; oftentimes calculations fail to account for the fact that the speed of light is finite. When measuring the movement of distant objects across the sky, there is a large time delay between what has been observed and what has occurred, due to the large distance the light from the distant object has to travel to reach us. The error in the above naive calculation comes from the fact that when an object has a component of velocity directed towards the Earth, as the object moves closer to the Earth that time delay becomes smaller. This means that the apparent speed as calculated above is <i>greater</i> than the actual speed. Correspondingly, if the object is moving away from the Earth, the above calculation underestimates the actual speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_motion">Types of motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Types of motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion" title="Simple harmonic motion">Simple harmonic motion</a> – motion in which the body oscillates in such a way that the restoring force acting on it is directly proportional to the body's displacement. Mathematically Force is directly proportional to the negative of displacement. Negative sign signifies the restoring nature of the force. (e.g., that of a <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Linear_motion" title="Linear motion">Linear motion</a> – motion which follows a straight <a href="/wiki/Line_(geometry)" title="Line (geometry)">linear</a> path, and whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Displacement_(vector)" title="Displacement (vector)">displacement</a> is exactly the same as its <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectory</a>. [Also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectilinear_motion" title="Rectilinear motion">rectilinear motion</a>]</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_motion" title="Reciprocating motion">Reciprocal motion</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a> (i.e. the random movement of particles)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Circular_motion" title="Circular motion">Circular motion</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_motion" title="Rotational motion">Rotatory motion</a> – a motion about a fixed point. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ferris_wheel" title="Ferris wheel">Ferris wheel</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Curvilinear_motion" title="Curvilinear motion">Curvilinear motion</a> – It is defined as the motion along a curved path that may be planar or in three dimensions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rolling" title="Rolling">Rolling</a> motion – (as of the wheel of a bicycle)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscillatory" title="Oscillatory">Oscillatory</a> – (swinging from side to side)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">Vibratory motion</a></li>
<li>Combination (or simultaneous) motions – Combination of two or more above listed motions</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Projectile_motion" title="Projectile motion">Projectile motion</a> –  uniform horizontal motion + vertical accelerated motion</li></ul>
