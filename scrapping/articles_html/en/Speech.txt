<p><b>Speech</b> is human vocal <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a> using <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>. Each language uses <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">phonetic</a> combinations of <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonant</a> sounds that form the sound of its words (that is, all English words sound different from all French words, even if they are the same word, e.g., "role" or "hotel"), and using those words in their semantic character as  words in the <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> of a language according to the <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactic</a> constraints that <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">govern</a> lexical words' function in a sentence. In speaking, speakers perform many different intentional <a href="/wiki/Speech_act" title="Speech act">speech acts</a>, e.g., informing, declaring, asking, persuading, directing, and can use <a href="/wiki/Elocution" title="Elocution">enunciation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">intonation</a>, degrees of <a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">loudness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, and other non-representational or <a href="/wiki/Paralanguage" title="Paralanguage">paralinguistic</a> aspects of vocalization to convey meaning.  In their speech, speakers also unintentionally communicate many aspects of their social position such as sex, age, place of origin (through <a href="/wiki/Accent_(sociolinguistics)" title="Accent (sociolinguistics)">accent</a>), physical states (alertness and sleepiness, vigor or weakness, health or illness), psychological states (emotions or moods), physico-psychological states (sobriety or <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication" title="Alcohol intoxication">drunkenness</a>, normal consciousness and <a href="/wiki/Trance" title="Trance">trance</a> states), education or experience, and the like.
</p><p>Although people ordinarily use speech in dealing with other persons (or animals), when people <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">swear</a> they do not always mean to communicate anything to anyone, and sometimes in expressing urgent emotions or desires they use speech as a quasi-magical cause, as when they encourage a player in a game to do or warn them not to do something.  There are also many situations in which people engage in solitary speech. People <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talking_to_oneself" title="Talking to oneself">talk to themselves</a> sometimes in acts that are a development of what some <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologists</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lev_Vygotsky" title="Lev Vygotsky">Lev Vygotsky</a>) have maintained is the use of silent speech in an <a href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_(psychology)" title="Stream of consciousness (psychology)">interior monologue</a> to vivify and organize <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a>, sometimes in the momentary adoption of a dual persona as self addressing self as though addressing another person.  Solo speech can be used <a href="/wiki/Memorization" title="Memorization">to memorize</a> or to test one's memorization of things, and in <a href="/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">prayer</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a> (e.g., the use of a <a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">mantra</a>).
</p><p>Researchers study many different aspects of speech:  speech production and <a href="/wiki/Speech_perception" title="Speech perception">speech perception</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sounds</a> used in a language, <a href="/wiki/Speech_repetition" title="Speech repetition">speech repetition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speech_error" title="Speech error">speech errors</a>, the ability to map heard spoken words onto the vocalizations needed to recreate them, which plays a key role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children" title="Children">children</a>'s enlargement of their <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a>, and what different areas of the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Broca%27s_area" title="Broca's area">Broca's area</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wernicke%27s_area" title="Wernicke's area">Wernicke's area</a>, underlie speech. Speech is the subject of study for  <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication_studies" title="Communication studies">communication studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_pathology" title="Speech pathology">speech pathology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otolaryngology" title="Otolaryngology">otolaryngology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustics</a>. 
Speech compares with <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">written language</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which may differ in its vocabulary, syntax, and phonetics from the spoken language, a situation called <a href="/wiki/Diglossia" title="Diglossia">diglossia</a>.
</p><p>The evolutionary <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_language" title="Origin of language">origins of speech</a> are unknown and subject to much debate and <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">speculation</a>. While <a href="/wiki/Animal_language" title="Animal language">animals also communicate</a> using vocalizations, and trained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apes" title="Apes">apes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Washoe_(chimpanzee)" title="Washoe (chimpanzee)">Washoe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kanzi" title="Kanzi">Kanzi</a> can use simple <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">sign language</a>, no animals' vocalizations are articulated phonemically and syntactically, and do not constitute speech.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_speech" title="Origin of speech">Origin of speech</a></div>
<p>Although related to the more general problem of the <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_language" title="Origin of language">origin of language</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> of distinctively human speech capacities has become a distinct and in many ways separate area of scientific research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hockett_1960_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hockett_1960-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corballis_2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corballis_2002-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lieberman_1984_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lieberman_1984-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lieberman,_P_2000_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lieberman,_P_2000-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abry_Boë_1998_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abry_Boë_1998-6">[6]</a></sup> The topic is a separate one because language is not necessarily spoken: it can equally be <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">written</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">signed</a>. Speech is in this sense optional, although it is the default modality for language.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Places_of_articulation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Places_of_articulation.svg/220px-Places_of_articulation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Places_of_articulation.svg/330px-Places_of_articulation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Places_of_articulation.svg/440px-Places_of_articulation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Places_of_articulation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Places of articulation (passive and active):<br/>1. Exo-labial, 2. Endo-labial, 3. Dental, 4. Alveolar, 5. Post-alveolar, 6. Pre-palatal, 7. Palatal, 8. Velar, 9. Uvular, 10. Pharyngeal, 11. Glottal, 12. Epiglottal, 13. Radical, 14. Postero-dorsal, 15. Antero-dorsal, 16. Laminal, 17. Apical, 18. Sub-apical</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">Monkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">apes</a> and humans, like many other animals, have evolved specialised mechanisms for producing <i>sound</i> for purposes of social communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On the other hand, no monkey or ape uses its <i>tongue</i> for such purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riede_2005_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riede_2005-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riede_Bronson_2006_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riede_Bronson_2006-9">[9]</a></sup> The human species' unprecedented use of the tongue, lips and other moveable parts seems to place speech in a quite separate category, making its evolutionary emergence an intriguing theoretical challenge in the eyes of many scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitch_2000_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fitch_2000-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Determining the timeline of human speech evolution is made additionally challenging by the lack of data in the fossil record. The human <a href="/wiki/Vocal_tract" title="Vocal tract">vocal tract</a> does not fossilize, and indirect evidence of vocal tract changes in hominid fossils has proven inconclusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitch_2000_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fitch_2000-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Speech_production" title="Speech production">Speech production</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistics</a></div>
<p>Speech production is an unconscious multi-step process by which thoughts are generated into spoken utterances. Production involves the unconscious mind selecting appropriate words and the appropriate form of those words from the lexicon and morphology, and the organization of those words through the syntax. Then, the phonetic properties of the words are retrieved and the sentence is articulated through the articulations associated with those phonetic properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Articulatory_phonetics" title="Articulatory phonetics">articulatory phonetics</a> is the study of how the tongue, lips, jaw, vocal cords, and other speech organs are used to make sounds. Speech sounds are categorized by <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">manner of articulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a>. Place of articulation refers to where in the neck or mouth the airstream is constricted. Manner of articulation refers to the manner in which the speech organs interact, such as how closely the air is restricted, what form of airstream is used (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Pulmonic_consonant" title="Pulmonic consonant">pulmonic</a>, implosive, ejectives, and clicks), whether or not the vocal cords are vibrating, and whether the nasal cavity is opened to the airstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The concept is primarily used for the production of <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonants</a>, but can be used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vowels" title="Vowels">vowels</a> in qualities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voicing_(phonetics)" title="Voicing (phonetics)">voicing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nasalization" title="Nasalization">nasalization</a>. For any place of articulation, there may be several manners of articulation, and therefore several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homorganic" title="Homorganic">homorganic</a> consonants.
</p><p>Normal human speech is pulmonic, produced with pressure from the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>, which creates <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Glottis" title="Glottis">glottis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">larynx</a>, which is then modified by the vocal tract and mouth into different vowels and consonants. However humans can pronounce words without the use of the lungs and glottis in <a href="/wiki/Alaryngeal_speech" title="Alaryngeal speech">alaryngeal speech</a>, of which there are three types: <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_speech" title="Esophageal speech">esophageal speech</a>, pharyngeal speech and buccal speech (better known as <a href="/wiki/Donald_Duck_talk" title="Donald Duck talk">Donald Duck talk</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Errors">Errors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Errors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speech_error" title="Speech error">Speech error</a></div>
<p>Speech production is a complex activity, and as a consequence errors are common, especially in children. Speech errors come in many forms and are used to provide evidence to support hypotheses about the nature of speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As a result, speech errors are often used in the construction of models for language production and <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">child language acquisition</a>. For example, the fact that children often make the error of over-regularizing the -ed past tense suffix in English (e.g. saying 'singed' instead of 'sang') shows that the regular forms are acquired earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Speech errors associated with certain kinds of aphasia have been used to map certain components of speech onto the brain and see the relation between different aspects of production; for example, the difficulty of <a href="/wiki/Expressive_aphasia" title="Expressive aphasia">expressive aphasia</a> patients in producing regular past-tense verbs, but not irregulars like 'sing-sang' has been used to demonstrate that regular inflected forms of a word are not individually stored in the lexicon, but produced from affixation to the base form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perception">Perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speech_perception" title="Speech perception">Speech perception</a></div>
<p>Speech perception refers to the processes by which humans can interpret and understand the sounds used in language. The study of speech perception is closely linked to the fields of <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">phonetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> in linguistics and cognitive psychology and perception in psychology. Research in speech perception seeks to understand how listeners recognize speech sounds and use this information to understand <a href="/wiki/Spoken_language" title="Spoken language">spoken language</a>. Research into speech perception also has applications in building <a href="/wiki/Speech_recognition" title="Speech recognition">computer systems that can recognize speech</a>, as well as improving speech recognition for hearing- and language-impaired listeners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Speech perception is <a href="/wiki/Categorical_perception" title="Categorical perception">categorical</a>, in that people put the sounds they hear into categories rather than perceiving them as a spectrum. People are more likely to be able to hear differences in sounds across categorical boundaries than within them. A good example of this is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice-onset_time" title="Voice-onset time">voice onset time</a> (VOT), one aspect of the phonetic production of consonant sounds. For example, Hebrew speakers, who distinguish voiced /b/ from voiceless /p/, will more easily detect a change in VOT from -10 ( perceived as /b/ ) to 0 ( perceived as /p/ ) than a change in VOT from +10 to +20, or -10 to -20, despite this being an equally large change on the VOT spectrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Language_development" title="Language development">Language development</a></div>
<p>Most human children develop proto-speech babbling behaviors when they are four to six months old. Most will begin saying their first words at some point during the first year of life. Typical children progress through two or three word phrases before they are three to short sentences by four years of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repetition">Repetition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Repetition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speech_repetition" title="Speech repetition">Speech repetition</a></div>
<p>In speech repetition, speech being heard is quickly turned from sensory input into motor instructions needed for its immediate or delayed vocal imitation (in <a href="/wiki/Baddeley%27s_model_of_working_memory#Phonological_loop" title="Baddeley's model of working memory">phonological memory</a>). This type of mapping plays a key role in enabling children to expand their spoken vocabulary. Masur (1995) found that how often children repeat novel words versus those they already have in their lexicon is related to the size of their lexicon later on, with young children who repeat more novel words having a larger lexicon later in development. Speech repetition could help facilitate the acquisition of this larger lexicon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>There are several organic and psychological factors that can affect speech. Among these are:
</p>
<ol><li>Diseases and disorders of the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Vocal_cords" title="Vocal cords">vocal cords</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a>, respiratory infections (bronchitis), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocal_fold_nodules" title="Vocal fold nodules">vocal fold nodules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancers</a> of the lungs and throat.</li>
<li>Diseases and disorders of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Alogia" title="Alogia">alogia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aphasia" title="Aphasia">aphasias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dysarthria" title="Dysarthria">dysarthria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dystonia" title="Dystonia">dystonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speech_processing" title="Speech processing">speech processing</a> disorders, where impaired <a href="/wiki/Motor_planning" title="Motor planning">motor planning</a>, nerve transmission, phonological processing or perception of the message (as opposed to the actual sound) leads to poor speech production.</li>
<li>Hearing problems, such as <a href="/wiki/Otitis_media" title="Otitis media">otitis media with effusion</a>, and listening problems, <a href="/wiki/Auditory_processing_disorder" title="Auditory processing disorder">auditory processing disorders</a>, can lead to phonological problems. In addition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysphasia" title="Dysphasia">dysphasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anomic_aphasia" title="Anomic aphasia">anomia</a> and auditory processing disorder impede the quality of auditory perception, and therefore, expression.  Those who are <a href="/wiki/Deafness" title="Deafness">deaf</a> or hard of hearing may be considered to fall into this category.</li>
<li>Articulatory problems, such as slurred speech, <a href="/wiki/Stuttering" title="Stuttering">stuttering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisp_(speech)" title="Lisp (speech)">lisping</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleft_palate" title="Cleft palate">cleft palate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ataxia" title="Ataxia">ataxia</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">nerve</a> damage leading to problems in <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">articulation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tourette_syndrome" title="Tourette syndrome">Tourette syndrome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tic" title="Tic">tics</a> can also affect speech. Various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital</a> and acquired <a href="/wiki/Tongue_disease" title="Tongue disease">tongue diseases</a> can affect speech as can <a href="/wiki/Motor_neuron_disease" title="Motor neuron disease">motor neuron disease</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychiatric" title="Psychiatric">Psychiatric</a> disorders have been shown to change speech acoustic features, where for instance, <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental frequency</a> of voice (perceived as pitch) tends to be significantly lower in <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">major depressive disorder</a> than in healthy controls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Low_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Low-21">[21]</a></sup> Therefore, speech is being investigated as a potential biomarker for mental health disorders.</li></ol>
<p>Speech and language disorders can also result from stroke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> brain injury,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> hearing loss,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> developmental delay,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> a cleft palate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> cerebral palsy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> or emotional issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speech%E2%80%93language_pathology" title="Speech–language pathology">Speech–language pathology</a></div>
<p>Speech-related diseases, disorders, and conditions can be treated by a speech-language pathologist (SLP) or speech therapist. SLPs assess levels of speech needs, make diagnoses based on the assessments, and then treat the diagnoses or address the needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brain_physiology">Brain physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Brain physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_model">Classical model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Classical model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BrocasAreaSmall.png"><img alt="Diagram of the brain" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="595" data-file-width="851" decoding="async" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/BrocasAreaSmall.png/180px-BrocasAreaSmall.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/BrocasAreaSmall.png/270px-BrocasAreaSmall.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/BrocasAreaSmall.png/360px-BrocasAreaSmall.png 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BrocasAreaSmall.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Broca's and Wernicke's areas</div></div></div>
<p>The classical or <a href="/wiki/Wernicke%E2%80%93Geschwind_model" title="Wernicke–Geschwind model">Wernicke-Geschwind model</a> of the language system in the brain focuses on <a href="/wiki/Broca%27s_area" title="Broca's area">Broca's area</a> in the inferior <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wernicke%27s_area" title="Wernicke's area">Wernicke's area</a> in the posterior <a href="/wiki/Superior_temporal_gyrus" title="Superior temporal gyrus">superior temporal gyrus</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Lateralization_of_brain_function" title="Lateralization of brain function">dominant hemisphere</a> of the brain (typically the left hemisphere for language). In this model, a linguistic auditory signal is first sent from the <a href="/wiki/Auditory_cortex" title="Auditory cortex">auditory cortex</a> to Wernicke's area. The <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> is accessed in Wernicke's area, and these words are sent via the <a href="/wiki/Arcuate_fasciculus" title="Arcuate fasciculus">arcuate fasciculus</a> to Broca's area, where morphology, syntax, and instructions for articulation are generated. This is then sent from Broca's area to the <a href="/wiki/Motor_cortex" title="Motor cortex">motor cortex</a> for articulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Broca" title="Paul Broca">Paul Broca</a> identified an approximate region of the brain in 1861 which, when damaged in two of his patients, caused severe deficits in speech production, where his patients were unable to speak beyond a few monosyllabic words. This deficit, known as Broca's or <a href="/wiki/Expressive_aphasia" title="Expressive aphasia">expressive aphasia</a>, is characterized by difficulty in speech production where speech is slow and labored, function words are absent, and syntax is severely impaired, as in <a href="/wiki/Telegraphic_speech" title="Telegraphic speech">telegraphic speech</a>. In expressive aphasia, speech comprehension is generally less affected except in the comprehension of grammatically complex sentences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">[31]</a></sup> Wernicke's area is named after <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wernicke" title="Carl Wernicke">Carl Wernicke</a>, who in 1874 proposed a connection between damage to the posterior area of the left superior temporal gyrus and aphasia, as he noted that not all aphasic patients had had damage to the prefrontal cortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Damage to Wernicke's area produces Wernicke's or <a href="/wiki/Receptive_aphasia" title="Receptive aphasia">receptive aphasia</a>, which is characterized by relatively normal syntax and prosody but severe impairment in lexical access, resulting in poor comprehension and nonsensical or <a href="/wiki/Jargon_aphasia" title="Jargon aphasia">jargon speech</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_research">Modern research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modern models of the neurological systems behind linguistic comprehension and production recognize the importance of Broca's and Wernicke's areas, but are not limited to them nor solely to the left hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakai_2017_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nakai_2017-33">[33]</a></sup> Instead, multiple streams are involved in speech production and comprehension. Damage to the left <a href="/wiki/Lateral_sulcus" title="Lateral sulcus">lateral sulcus</a> has been connected with difficulty in processing and producing morphology and syntax, while lexical access and comprehension of irregular forms (e.g. eat-ate) remain unaffected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
Moreover, the circuits involved in human speech comprehension dynamically adapt with learning, for example, by becoming more efficient in terms of processing time when listening to familiar messages such as learned verses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CervantesConstantino_&amp;_Simon_2018_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-CervantesConstantino_&amp;_Simon_2018-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_communication">Animal communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Animal communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talking_animals" title="Talking animals">Talking animals</a></div>
<p>Some non-human animals can produce sounds or gestures resembling those of a human language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Several species or groups of animals have developed <a href="/wiki/Animal_communication" title="Animal communication">forms of communication</a> which superficially resemble verbal language, however, these usually are not considered a language because they lack one or more of the <a href="/wiki/Language#Definitions" title="Language">defining characteristics</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recursion#Recursion_in_language" title="Recursion">recursion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(linguistics)" title="Displacement (linguistics)">displacement</a>. Researchers have been successful in teaching some animals to make gestures similar to <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">sign language</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> although whether this should be considered a language has been disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
