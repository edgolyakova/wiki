<p><b>Physical chemistry</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microscopic_scale" title="Microscopic scale">microscopic</a> phenomena in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a> systems in terms of the principles, practices, and concepts of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a>, <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chemistry" title="Quantum chemistry">quantum chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analytical_dynamics" title="Analytical dynamics">analytical dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical equilibria</a>.
</p><p>Physical chemistry, in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_physics" title="Chemical physics">chemical physics</a>, is predominantly (but not always) a supra-molecular science, as the majority of the principles on which it was founded relate to the bulk rather than the molecular or atomic structure alone (for example, chemical equilibrium and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloids" title="Colloids">colloids</a>).
</p><p>Some of the relationships that physical chemistry strives to resolve include the effects of:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">Intermolecular forces</a> that act upon the physical properties of materials (<a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plasticity</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> in <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">Reaction kinetics</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">rate of a reaction</a>.</li>
<li>The identity of ions and the <a href="/wiki/Conductivity_(electrolytic)" title="Conductivity (electrolytic)">electrical conductivity</a> of materials.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Surface_science" title="Surface science">Surface science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membranes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Interaction of one body with another in terms of quantities of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> called <a href="/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics" title="Chemical thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>.</li>
<li>Transfer of heat between a chemical system and its surroundings during change of <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> taking place called <a href="/wiki/Thermochemistry" title="Thermochemistry">thermochemistry</a></li>
<li>Study of <a href="/wiki/Colligative_properties" title="Colligative properties">colligative properties</a> of number of species present in solution.</li>
<li>Number of phases, number of components and degree of freedom (or variance) can be correlated with one another with help of <a href="/wiki/Phase_rule" title="Phase rule">phase rule</a>.</li>
<li>Reactions of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">electrochemical cells</a>.</li>
<li>Behaviour of microscopic systems using <a href="/wiki/Introduction_to_quantum_mechanics" title="Introduction to quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and macroscopic systems using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_thermodynamics" title="Statistical thermodynamics">statistical thermodynamics</a>.</li></ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_concepts">Key concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Key concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The key concepts of physical chemistry are the ways in which pure <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> is applied to chemical problems.
</p><p>One of the key concepts in classical chemistry is that all <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical compounds</a> can be described as groups of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bonded</a> together and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a> can be described as the making and breaking of those bonds. Predicting the properties of chemical compounds from a description of atoms and how they bond is one of the major goals of physical chemistry. To describe the atoms and bonds precisely, it is necessary to know both where the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a> of the atoms are, and how electrons are distributed around them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkins249_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atkins249-2">[2]</a></sup><br/>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disciplines">Disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quantum_chemistry" title="Quantum chemistry">Quantum chemistry</a>, a subfield of physical chemistry especially concerned with the application of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> to chemical problems, provides tools to determine how strong and what shape bonds are,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkins249_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atkins249-2">[2]</a></sup> how nuclei move, and how light can be absorbed or emitted by a chemical compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">Spectroscopy</a> is the related sub-discipline of physical chemistry which is specifically concerned with the interaction of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> with matter.
</p><p>Another set of important questions in chemistry concerns what kind of reactions can happen spontaneously and which properties are possible for a given chemical mixture. This is studied in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics" title="Chemical thermodynamics">chemical thermodynamics</a>, which sets limits on quantities like how far a reaction can proceed, or how much <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> can be converted into work in an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>, and which provides links between properties like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion_coefficient" title="Thermal expansion coefficient">thermal expansion coefficient</a> and rate of change of <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> with <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It can frequently be used to assess whether a reactor or engine design is feasible, or to check the validity of experimental data. To a limited extent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasi-equilibrium" title="Quasi-equilibrium">quasi-equilibrium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics" title="Non-equilibrium thermodynamics">non-equilibrium thermodynamics</a> can describe irreversible changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hill1-5">[5]</a></sup> However, classical thermodynamics is mostly concerned with systems in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible changes</a> and not what actually does happen, or how fast, away from equilibrium.
</p><p>Which reactions do occur and how fast is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">chemical kinetics</a>, another branch of physical chemistry. A key idea in chemical kinetics is that for <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reactants</a> to react and form <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a>, most chemical species must go through <a href="/wiki/Transition_state" title="Transition state">transition states</a> which are higher in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_free_energy" title="Thermodynamic free energy">energy</a> than either the reactants or the products and serve as a barrier to reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In general, the higher the barrier, the slower the reaction. A second is that most chemical reactions occur as a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Elementary_reaction" title="Elementary reaction">elementary reactions</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> each with its own transition state. Key questions in kinetics include how the rate of reaction depends on temperature and on the concentrations of reactants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalysts" title="Catalysts">catalysts</a> in the reaction mixture, as well as how catalysts and reaction conditions can be engineered to optimize the reaction rate.
</p><p>The fact that how fast reactions occur can often be specified with just a few concentrations and a temperature, instead of needing to know all the positions and speeds of every molecule in a mixture, is a special case of another key concept in physical chemistry, which is that to the extent an engineer needs to know, everything going on in a mixture of very large numbers (perhaps of the order of the <a href="/wiki/Avogadro_constant" title="Avogadro constant">Avogadro constant</a>, 6 x 10<sup>23</sup>) of particles can often be described by just a few variables like pressure, temperature, and concentration. The precise reasons for this are described in <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandler_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chandler-8">[8]</a></sup> a specialty within physical chemistry which is also shared with physics. Statistical mechanics also provides ways to predict the properties we see in everyday life from molecular properties without relying on empirical correlations based on chemical similarities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hill1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_chemistry" title="History of chemistry">History of chemistry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2256" data-file-width="2988" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg/220px-Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg/330px-Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg/440px-Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lomonosov_Chymiae_Physicae_1752.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fragment of M. Lomonosov's manuscript 'Physical Chemistry' (1752)</div></div></div>
<p>The term "physical chemistry" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Lomonosov" title="Mikhail Lomonosov">Mikhail Lomonosov</a> in 1752, when he presented a lecture course entitled "A Course in True Physical Chemistry" (Russian: «Курс истинной физической химии») before the students of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petersburg_University" title="Petersburg University">Petersburg University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the preamble to these lectures he gives the definition: "Physical chemistry is the science that must explain under provisions of physical experiments the reason for what is happening in complex bodies through chemical operations".
</p><p>Modern physical chemistry originated in the 1860s to 1880s with work on <a href="/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics" title="Chemical thermodynamics">chemical thermodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> in solutions, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">chemical kinetics</a> and other subjects. One milestone was the publication in 1876 by <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs" title="Josiah Willard Gibbs">Josiah Willard Gibbs</a> of his paper, <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Equilibrium_of_Heterogeneous_Substances" title="On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances">On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances</a></i>.  This paper introduced several of the cornerstones of physical chemistry, such as <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_potential" title="Chemical potential">chemical potentials</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gibbs%27_phase_rule" title="Gibbs' phase rule">Gibbs' phase rule</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journal</a> specifically in the field of physical chemistry was the German journal, <i><a href="/wiki/Zeitschrift_f%C3%BCr_Physikalische_Chemie" title="Zeitschrift für Physikalische Chemie">Zeitschrift für Physikalische Chemie</a></i>, founded in 1887 by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Henricus_van_%27t_Hoff" title="Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff">Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff</a>. Together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Svante_August_Arrhenius" title="Svante August Arrhenius">Svante August Arrhenius</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  these were the leading figures in physical chemistry in the late 19th century and early 20th century. All three were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> between 1901 and 1909.
</p><p>Developments in the following decades include the application of <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a> to chemical systems and work on <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_chemistry" title="Surface chemistry">surface chemistry</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Irving_Langmuir" title="Irving Langmuir">Irving Langmuir</a> made many contributions. Another important step was the development of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> into <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chemistry" title="Quantum chemistry">quantum chemistry</a> from the 1930s, where <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a> was one of the leading names. Theoretical developments have gone hand in hand with developments in experimental methods, where the use of different forms of <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectroscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microwave_spectroscopy" title="Microwave spectroscopy">microwave spectroscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron_paramagnetic_resonance" title="Electron paramagnetic resonance">electron paramagnetic resonance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy">nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy</a>, is probably the most important 20th century development.
</p><p>Further development in physical chemistry may be attributed to discoveries in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chemistry" title="Nuclear chemistry">nuclear chemistry</a>, especially in isotope separation (before and during World War II), more recent discoveries in <a href="/wiki/Astrochemistry" title="Astrochemistry">astrochemistry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> as well as the development of calculation algorithms in the field of "additive physicochemical properties" (practically all physicochemical properties, such as boiling point, critical point, surface tension, vapor pressure, etc.—more than 20 in all—can be precisely calculated from chemical structure alone, even if the chemical molecule remains unsynthesized),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and herein lies the practical importance of contemporary physical chemistry.
</p><p>See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_contribution_method" title="Group contribution method">Group contribution method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lydersen_method" title="Lydersen method">Lydersen method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joback_method" title="Joback method">Joback method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benson_group_increment_theory" title="Benson group increment theory">Benson group increment theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_structure%E2%80%93activity_relationship" title="Quantitative structure–activity relationship">quantitative structure–activity relationship</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journals">Journals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Journals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main category: <a href="/wiki/Category:Physical_chemistry_journals" title="Category:Physical chemistry journals">Physical chemistry journals</a></div>
<p>Some journals that deal with physical chemistry include <i><a href="/wiki/Zeitschrift_f%C3%BCr_Physikalische_Chemie" title="Zeitschrift für Physikalische Chemie">Zeitschrift für Physikalische Chemie</a></i> (1887); <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_Physical_Chemistry_A" title="Journal of Physical Chemistry A">Journal of Physical Chemistry A</a></i> (from 1896 as <i>Journal of Physical Chemistry</i>, renamed in 1997); <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Chemistry_Chemical_Physics" title="Physical Chemistry Chemical Physics">Physical Chemistry Chemical Physics</a></i> (from 1999, formerly <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faraday_Transactions" title="Faraday Transactions">Faraday Transactions</a></i> with a history dating back to 1905); <i><a href="/wiki/Macromolecular_Chemistry_and_Physics" title="Macromolecular Chemistry and Physics">Macromolecular Chemistry and Physics</a></i> (1947); <i><a href="/wiki/Annual_Review_of_Physical_Chemistry" title="Annual Review of Physical Chemistry">Annual Review of Physical Chemistry</a></i> (1950); <i><a href="/wiki/Molecular_Physics_(journal)" title="Molecular Physics (journal)">Molecular Physics</a></i> (1957); <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Physical_Organic_Chemistry" title="Journal of Physical Organic Chemistry">Journal of Physical Organic Chemistry</a></i> (1988); <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_Physical_Chemistry_B" title="Journal of Physical Chemistry B">Journal of Physical Chemistry B</a></i> (1997); <i><a href="/wiki/ChemPhysChem" title="ChemPhysChem">ChemPhysChem</a></i> (2000); <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_Physical_Chemistry_C" title="Journal of Physical Chemistry C">Journal of Physical Chemistry C</a></i> (2007); and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_Physical_Chemistry_Letters" title="Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters">Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters</a></i> (from 2010, combined letters previously published in the separate journals)
</p><p>Historical journals that covered both chemistry and physics include <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_de_chimie_et_de_physique" title="Annales de chimie et de physique">Annales de chimie et de physique</a></i> (started in 1789, published under the name given here from 1815 to 1914).
</p>
