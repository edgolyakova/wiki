<p><b>Hydropower</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">ὕδωρ</span>, "water"), also known as <b>water power</b>, is the use of falling or fast-running <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> to <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">produce electricity</a> or to power machines. This is achieved by <a href="/wiki/Energy_transformation" title="Energy transformation">converting</a> the <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">gravitational potential</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of a water source to produce power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Hydropower is a method of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a> production.
</p><p>Since ancient times, hydropower from <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermills</a> has been used as a <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> source for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and the operation of mechanical devices, such as <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> mills, <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammers</a>, dock <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a>, domestic <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">lifts</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ore_mill" title="Ore mill">ore mills</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Trompe" title="Trompe">trompe</a>, which produces compressed air from falling water, is sometimes used to power other machinery at a distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hydropower is now used principally for <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric power generation</a>, and is also applied as one half of an energy storage system known as <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a>.
</p><p>Hydropower is an attractive alternative to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> as it does not directly produce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">atmospheric pollutants</a> and it provides a relatively consistent source of power. Nonetheless, it has economic, sociological, and environmental downsides and requires a sufficiently energetic source of water, such as a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> or elevated <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> International institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> view hydropower as a low-carbon means for <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP58132_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP58132-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Evidence suggests that the fundamentals of hydropower date to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek civilization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> Other evidence indicates that the waterwheel independently emerged in China around the same period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> Evidence of <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermills</a> date to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">ancient Near East</a> in the 4th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reynolds_1983_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds_1983-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 14">: 14 </span></sup> Moreover, evidence indicates the use of hydropower using irrigation machines to ancient civilizations such as <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> Studies suggest that the water wheel was the initial form of water power and it was driven by either humans or animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, water-powered mills were described by <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> by the first century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oleson2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oleson2-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbegal_mill" title="Barbegal mill">Barbegal mill</a>, located in modern-day France, had 16 water wheels processing up to 28 tons of grain per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup> Roman waterwheels were also used for sawing marble such as the <a href="/wiki/Hierapolis_sawmill" title="Hierapolis sawmill">Hierapolis sawmill</a> of the late 3rd century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Such sawmills had a waterwheel that drove two crank-and-connecting rods to power two saws. It also appears in two 6th century <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saw_mill" title="Saw mill">saw mills</a> excavated at <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerasa" title="Gerasa">Gerasa</a> respectively. The <a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">crank</a> and connecting rod mechanism of these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_watermills" title="List of Roman watermills">Roman watermills</a> converted the rotary motion of the waterwheel into the linear movement of the saw blades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Water-powered trip hammers and bellows in China, during the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (202 BC - 220 AD), were initially thought to be powered by <a href="/wiki/Water_scoop_(hydropower)" title="Water scoop (hydropower)">water scoops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reynolds_1983_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds_1983-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 26–30">: 26–30 </span></sup> However, some historians suggested that they were powered by waterwheels. This is since it was theorized that water scoops would not have had the motive force to operate their <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> bellows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Many texts describe the Hun waterwheel; some of the earliest ones are the <i><a href="/wiki/Jijiupian" title="Jijiupian">Jijiupian</a></i> dictionary of 40 BC, <a href="/wiki/Yang_Xiong_(author)" title="Yang Xiong (author)">Yang Xiong</a>'s text known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Fangyan_(book)" title="Fangyan (book)">Fangyan</a></i> of 15 BC, as well as <i>Xin Lun,</i> written by <a href="/wiki/Huan_Tan" title="Huan Tan">Huan Tan</a> about 20 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-12">[12]</a></sup> It was also during this time that the engineer <a href="/wiki/Du_Shi" title="Du Shi">Du Shi</a> (c. AD 31) applied the power of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterwheel" title="Waterwheel">waterwheels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> in forging cast iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another example of the early use of hydropower is seen in <a href="/wiki/Hushing" title="Hushing">hushing</a>. Hushing is the use of the power of a wave of water released from a tank in the extraction of metal ores.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The method was first used at the <a href="/wiki/Dolaucothi_Gold_Mines" title="Dolaucothi Gold Mines">Dolaucothi Gold Mines</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> from 75 AD onwards. This method was further developed in Spain in mines such as <a href="/wiki/Las_M%C3%A9dulas" title="Las Médulas">Las Médulas</a>. Hushing was also widely used in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Medieval</a> and later periods to extract <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> ores. It later evolved into <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_mining" title="Hydraulic mining">hydraulic mining</a> when used during the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a> in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_Empire" title="Islamic Empire">Islamic Empire</a> spanned a large region, mainly in Asia and Africa, along with other surrounding areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Agricultural_Revolution" title="Arab Agricultural Revolution">Arab Agricultural Revolution</a> (8th–13th centuries), hydropower was widely used and developed. Early uses of <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> emerged along with large hydraulic <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factory</a> complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-15">[15]</a></sup> A wide range of water-powered industrial mills were used in the region including <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling</a> mills, <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice_huller" title="Rice huller">hullers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ship_mill" title="Ship mill">ship mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stamp_mill" title="Stamp mill">stamp mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_refinery" title="Sugar refinery">sugar mills</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tide_mill" title="Tide mill">tide mills</a>. By the 11th century, every province throughout the Islamic Empire had these industrial mills in operation, from <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucas2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lucas2-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 10">: 10 </span></sup> Muslim engineers also used <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbines</a> while employing <a href="/wiki/Gear" title="Gear">gears</a> in watermills and water-raising machines. They also pioneered the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dams" title="Dams">dams</a> as a source of water power, used to provide additional power to watermills and water-raising machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hassan2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hassan2-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, in his book, <i>The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices</i>, the Muslim mechanical engineer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Jazari" title="Al-Jazari">Al-Jazari</a> (1136–1206) described designs for 50 devices. Many of these devices were water-powered, including clocks, a device to serve wine, and five devices to lift water from rivers or pools, where three of them are animal-powered and one can be powered by animal or water. Moreover, they included an endless belt with jugs attached, a cow-powered <a href="/wiki/Shadoof" title="Shadoof">shadoof</a> (a crane-like irrigation tool), and a reciprocating device with hinged valves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron_portrait.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="397" data-file-width="322" decoding="async" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron_portrait.jpg/220px-Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron_portrait.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f8/Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron_portrait.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron_portrait.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Benoît Fourneyron, the French engineer who developed the first hydropower turbine</div></div></div>
<p>In the 19th century, French engineer <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Fourneyron" title="Benoît Fourneyron">Benoît Fourneyron</a> developed the first hydropower turbine. This device was implemented in the commercial plant of <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a> in 1895 and it is still operating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> In the early 20th century, English engineer <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">William Armstrong</a> built and operated the first private electrical power station which was located in his house in <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">Cragside</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1753, the French engineer <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Forest_de_B%C3%A9lidor" title="Bernard Forest de Bélidor">Bernard Forest de Bélidor</a> published his book, <i>Architecture Hydraulique</i>, which described vertical-axis and horizontal-axis hydraulic machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doehis_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-doehis-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The growing demand for the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> would drive development as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watenc_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-watenc-20">[20]</a></sup> At the beginning of the Industrial Revolution in Britain, water was the main power source for new inventions such as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Arkwright" title="Richard Arkwright">Richard Arkwright</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Water_frame" title="Water frame">water frame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-21">[21]</a></sup> Although water power gave way to steam power in many of the larger mills and factories, it was still used during the 18th and 19th centuries for many smaller operations, such as driving the bellows in small <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Dyfi_Furnace" title="Dyfi Furnace">Dyfi Furnace</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, such as those built at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anthony_Falls" title="Saint Anthony Falls">Saint Anthony Falls</a>, which uses the 50-foot (15 m) drop in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Technological advances moved the open water wheel into an enclosed <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Water_engine" title="Water engine">water motor</a>. In 1848, the British-American engineer <a href="/wiki/James_B._Francis" title="James B. Francis">James B. Francis</a>, head engineer of Lowell's Locks and Canals company, improved on these designs to create a turbine with 90% efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He applied scientific principles and testing methods to the problem of turbine design. His mathematical and graphical calculation methods allowed the confident design of high-efficiency turbines to exactly match a site's specific flow conditions. The <a href="/wiki/Francis_turbine" title="Francis turbine">Francis reaction turbine</a> is still in use. In the 1870s, deriving from uses in the California mining industry, <a href="/wiki/Lester_Allan_Pelton" title="Lester Allan Pelton">Lester Allan Pelton</a> developed the high-efficiency <a href="/wiki/Pelton_wheel" title="Pelton wheel">Pelton wheel impulse turbine</a>, which used hydropower from the high head streams characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada" title="Sierra Nevada">Sierra Nevada</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_amount_of_available_power">Calculating the amount of available power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Calculating the amount of available power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A hydropower resource can be evaluated by its available <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a>. Power is a function of the <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">hydraulic head</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">volumetric flow rate</a>. The head is the energy per unit weight (or unit mass) of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The static head is proportional to the difference in height through which the water falls. Dynamic head is related to the velocity of moving water. Each unit of water can do an amount of work equal to its weight times the head.

<p>Operators of hydroelectric stations compare the total electrical energy produced with the theoretical potential energy of the water passing through the turbine to calculate efficiency. Procedures and definitions for calculation of efficiency are given in test codes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASME" title="ASME">ASME</a> PTC 18 and <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a> 60041. Field testing of turbines is used to validate the manufacturer's efficiency guarantee. Detailed calculation of the efficiency of a hydropower turbine accounts for the head lost due to flow friction in the power canal or penstock, rise in tailwater level due to flow, the location of the station and effect of varying gravity, the air temperature and barometric pressure, the density of the water at ambient temperature, and the relative altitudes of the forebay and tailbay. For precise calculations, errors due to rounding and the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Significant_digit" title="Significant digit">significant digits</a> of constants must be considered.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Some hydropower systems such as <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheels</a> can draw power from the flow of a body of water without necessarily changing its height. In this case, the available power is the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of the flowing water. Over-shot water wheels can efficiently capture both types of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> The flow in a stream can vary widely from season to season. The development of a hydropower site requires analysis of <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">flow records</a>, sometimes spanning decades, to assess the reliable annual energy supply. Dams and reservoirs provide a more dependable source of power by smoothing seasonal changes in water flow. However, reservoirs have a significant <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs" title="Environmental impact of reservoirs">environmental impact</a>, as does alteration of naturally occurring streamflow. Dam design must account for the worst-case, "probable maximum flood" that can be expected at the site; a <a href="/wiki/Spillway" title="Spillway">spillway</a> is often included to route flood flows around the dam. A computer <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_model" title="Hydrological model">model of the hydraulic basin</a> and rainfall and snowfall records are used to predict the maximum flood.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages_and_limitations">Disadvantages and limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disadvantages and limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some disadvantages of hydropower have been identified. People who live near a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydro_plant" title="Hydro plant">hydro plant</a> site are displaced during construction or when <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> banks become unstable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> Another potential disadvantage is cultural or <a href="/wiki/List_of_religious_sites" title="List of religious sites">religious sites</a> may block construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[note 2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">Dams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a> can have major negative impacts on river <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> such as preventing some animals traveling upstream, cooling and de-oxygenating of water released downstream, and loss of nutrients due to settling of particulates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> River sediment builds river deltas and dams prevent them from restoring what is lost from erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup>  Large and deep dam and reservoir plants cover large areas of land which causes <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> from underwater rotting vegetation. Furthermore, although at lower levels than other <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources, it was found that hydropower produces <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gas which is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> gas. This occurs when organic matters accumulate at the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> because of the <a href="/wiki/Deoxygenation" title="Deoxygenation">deoxygenation</a> of water which triggers <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-30">[28]</a></sup> Furthermore, studies found that the construction of dams and reservoirs can result in habitat loss for some aquatic species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dam_failure" title="Dam failure">Dam failures</a> can have catastrophic effects, including loss of life, property and pollution of land.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_power">Mechanical power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mechanical power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Watermills">Watermills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Watermills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="excerpt-block"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1066933788">.mw-parser-output .excerpt-hat .mw-editsection-like{font-style:normal}</style><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">Watermill</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Watermill&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" decoding="async" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg/280px-Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg/420px-Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg/560px-Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau_JPG02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Watermill of <a href="/wiki/Braine-le-Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Braine-le-Château">Braine-le-Château</a>, Belgium (12th century)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg/220px-WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg/330px-WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg/440px-WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WaterMill_Interior_MotionBlur.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Interior of the <a href="/wiki/Lyme_Regis" title="Lyme Regis">Lyme Regis</a> watermill, UK (14th century)</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermill</a> or water mill is a mill that uses hydropower. It is a structure that uses a <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">water wheel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbine</a> to drive a mechanical process such as <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">milling (grinding)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rolling" title="Rolling">rolling</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammering</a>. Such processes are needed in the production of many material goods, including <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">lumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>, and many <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> products. These watermills may comprise <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mills</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textile_mill" title="Textile mill">textile mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hammermill" title="Hammermill">hammermills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">trip hammering</a> mills, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_mill" title="Rolling mill">rolling mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wire_drawing" title="Wire drawing">wire drawing</a> mills.
</p><p>One major way to classify watermills is by wheel orientation (vertical or horizontal), one powered by a vertical waterwheel through a <a href="/wiki/Gear_train" title="Gear train">gear</a> mechanism, and the other equipped with a horizontal waterwheel without such a mechanism. The former type can be further divided, depending on where the water hits the wheel paddles, into undershot, overshot, breastshot and pitchback (backshot or reverse shot) waterwheel mills. Another way to classify water mills is by an essential trait about their location: <a href="/wiki/Tide_mill" title="Tide mill">tide mills</a> use the movement of the tide; <a href="/wiki/Ship_mill" title="Ship mill">ship mills</a> are water mills onboard (and constituting) a ship.
</p>
Watermills impact the river dynamics of the watercourses where they are installed. During the time watermills operate channels tend to <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentate</a>, particularly backwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watermill_MaasSchuttrumpf2019_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watermill_MaasSchuttrumpf2019-31">[29]</a></sup> Also in the backwater area, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">inundation events</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a> of adjacent <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a> increase. Over time however these effects are cancelled by river banks becoming higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watermill_MaasSchuttrumpf2019_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watermill_MaasSchuttrumpf2019-31">[29]</a></sup> Where mills have been removed, <a href="/wiki/River_incision" title="River incision">river incision</a> increases and channels deepen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watermill_MaasSchuttrumpf2019_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Watermill_MaasSchuttrumpf2019-31">[29]</a></sup></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressed_air">Compressed air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Compressed air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Trompe" title="Trompe">Trompe</a></div>
<p>A plentiful head of water can be made to generate <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a> directly without moving parts. In these designs, a falling column of water is deliberately mixed with air bubbles generated through turbulence or a <a href="/wiki/Venturi_effect" title="Venturi effect">venturi</a> pressure reducer at the high-level intake. This allows it to fall down a shaft into a subterranean, high-roofed chamber where the now-compressed air separates from the water and becomes trapped. The height of the falling water column maintains compression of the air in the top of the chamber, while an outlet, submerged below the water level in the chamber allows water to flow back to the surface at a lower level than the intake. A separate outlet in the roof of the chamber supplies the compressed air. A facility on this principle was built on the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_River_(Timiskaming_District)" title="Montreal River (Timiskaming District)">Montreal River</a> at Ragged Shutes near <a href="/wiki/Cobalt,_Ontario" title="Cobalt, Ontario">Cobalt, Ontario</a> in 1910 and supplied 5,000 horsepower to nearby mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity">Electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</a></div>
<p>Hydroelectricity is the biggest hydropower application. Hydroelectricity generates about 15% of global electricity and provides at least 50% of the total electricity supply for more than 35 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Hydroelectricity generation starts with converting either the <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> of water that is present due to the site's elevation or the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of moving water into electrical energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-30">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hydroelectric power plants vary in terms of the way they harvest energy. One type involves a dam and a <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a>. The water in the reservoir is available on demand to be used to generate electricity by passing through channels that connect the dam to the reservoir. The water spins a turbine, which is connected to the generator that produces electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-:7-30">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The other type is called a run-of-river plant. In this case, a barrage is built to control the flow of water, absent a <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a>. The run-of river power plant needs continuous water flow and therefore has less ability to provide power on demand. The kinetic energy of flowing water is the main source of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-:7-30">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both designs have limitations. For example, dam construction can result in discomfort to nearby residents. The dam and reservoirs occupy a relatively large amount of space that may be opposed by nearby communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> Moreover, reservoirs can potentially have major environmental consequences such as harming downstream habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-:7-30">[28]</a></sup> On the other hand, the limitation of the run-of-river project is the decreased efficiency of electricity generation because the process depends on the speed of the seasonal river flow. This means that the rainy season increases electricity generation compared to the dry season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The size of hydroelectric plants can vary from small plants called <a href="/wiki/Micro_hydro" title="Micro hydro">micro hydro</a>, to large plants supply that power to a whole country. As of 2019, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_power_stations" title="List of largest power stations">five largest power stations in the world</a> are conventional hydroelectric power stations with dams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hydroelectricity can also be used to store energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> between two reservoirs at different heights with <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage</a>. Water is pumped uphill into reservoirs during periods of low demand to be released for generation when demand is high or system generation is low.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>
Other forms of electricity generation with hydropower include <a href="/wiki/Tidal_stream_generator" title="Tidal stream generator">tidal stream generators</a> using energy from <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> generated from oceans, rivers, and human-made canal systems to generating electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_30-5"><a href="#cite_note-:7-30">[28]</a></sup></p><ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rain_power">Rain power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rain power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rain has been referred to as "one of the last unexploited energy sources in nature. When it rains, billions of litres of water can fall, which have enormous electric potential if used in the right way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IrishTimes_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-IrishTimes-37">[35]</a></sup> Research is being done into the different methods of generating power from rain, such as by using the energy in the impact of raindrops. This is in its very early stages with new and emerging technologies being tested, prototyped and created. Such power has been called rain power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian1-38">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gadget_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-gadget-39">[37]</a></sup> One method in which this has been attempted is by using hybrid solar panels called "all-weather solar panels" that can generate electricity from both the sun and the rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to zoologist and science and technology educator, Luis Villazon, "A 2008 French study estimated that you could use piezoelectric devices, which generate power when they move, to extract 12 milliwatts from a raindrop. Over a year, this would amount to less than 0.001kWh per square metre – enough to power a remote sensor." Villazon suggested a better application would be to collect the water from fallen rain and use it to drive a turbine, with an estimated energy generation of 3 kWh of energy per year for a 185 m<sup>2</sup> roof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceFocus_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceFocus-41">[39]</a></sup> A microturbine-based system created by three students from the Technological University of Mexico has been used to generate electricity. The Pluvia system "uses the stream of rainwater runoff from houses' rooftop rain gutters to spin a microturbine in a cylindrical housing. Electricity generated by that turbine is used to charge 12-volt batteries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term rain power has also been applied to hydropower systems which include the process of capturing the rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IrishTimes_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-IrishTimes-37">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceFocus_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceFocus-41">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
