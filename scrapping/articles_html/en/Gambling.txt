<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Wagering of money on a game of chance or event with an uncertain outcome</div>

<p><b>Gambling</b> (also known as <b>betting</b> or <b>gaming</b>) is the wagering of something of <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> ("the stakes") on an <a href="/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)" title="Event (probability theory)">event</a> with an uncertain outcome with the intent of winning something else of value. Gambling thus requires three elements to be present: <a href="/wiki/Consideration" title="Consideration">consideration</a> (an amount wagered), <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> (chance), and a <a href="/wiki/Prize" title="Prize">prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The outcome of the wager is often immediate, such as a single roll of <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a>, a spin of a <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a> wheel, or a horse crossing the finish line, but longer time frames are also common, allowing wagers on the outcome of a future sports contest or even an entire sports season.
</p><p>The term "gaming"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in this context typically refers to instances in which the activity has been specifically permitted by <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>. The two words are not mutually exclusive; <i>i.e.</i>, a "gaming" company offers (legal) "gambling" activities to the public<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and may be regulated by one of many <a href="/wiki/Gaming_control_board" title="Gaming control board">gaming control boards</a>, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Gaming_Control_Board" title="Nevada Gaming Control Board">Nevada Gaming Control Board</a>. However, this distinction is not universally observed in the English-speaking world. For instance, in the United Kingdom, the regulator of gambling activities is called the <a href="/wiki/Gambling_Commission" title="Gambling Commission">Gambling Commission</a> (not the Gaming Commission).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The word <i>gaming</i> is used more frequently since the rise of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_games" title="Computer games">computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> to describe activities that do not necessarily involve wagering, especially <a href="/wiki/Online_game" title="Online game">online gaming</a>, with the new usage still not having displaced the old usage as the primary definition in common dictionaries.  "Gaming" has also been used to circumvent laws against "gambling". The media and others have used one term or the other to frame conversations around the subjects, resulting in a shift of perceptions among their audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gambling is also a major international commercial activity, with the legal gambling market totaling an estimated $335 billion in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In other forms, gambling can be conducted with materials that have a value, but are not real money. For example, players of <a href="/wiki/Marble_(toy)#Marbles_games" title="Marble (toy)">marbles games</a> might wager marbles, and likewise games of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pogs" title="Pogs">Pogs</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Magic:_The_Gathering#Gambling" title="Magic: The Gathering">Magic: The Gathering</a></i> can be played with the collectible game pieces (respectively, small discs and trading cards) as stakes, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Metagaming" title="Metagaming">meta-game</a> regarding the value of a player's collection of pieces.
</p>

<p>Gambling dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> period, before written history. In <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> the earliest six-sided <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a> date to about 3000 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BCE</a>. However, they were based on <a href="/wiki/Astragalomancy" title="Astragalomancy">astragali</a> dating back thousands of years earlier. In China, gambling houses were widespread in the first millennium BCE, and betting on fighting animals was common. Lotto games and dominoes (precursors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pai_Gow" title="Pai Gow">Pai Gow</a>) appeared in China as early as the 10th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Playing cards appeared in the 9th century CE in China. Records trace gambling in Japan back at least as far as the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Poker" title="Poker">Poker</a>, the most popular U.S. card game associated with gambling, derives from the Persian game <a href="/wiki/As-Nas" title="As-Nas">As-Nas</a>, dating back to the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first known casino, the <a href="/wiki/Ridotto" title="Ridotto">Ridotto</a>, started operating in 1638 in Venice, Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Gambling_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Gambling in the United Kingdom">Gambling in the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_gambling_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of gambling in the United Kingdom">History of gambling in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Gambling has been a main recreational activity in Great Britain for centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Horseracing has been a favorite theme for over three centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It has been heavily regulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Historically much of the opposition comes from evangelical Protestants, and from social reformers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gambling_in_the_United_States" title="Gambling in the United States">Gambling in the United States</a></div>
<p>Gambling has been a popular activity in the United States for centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It has also been suppressed by law in many areas for almost as long. By the early 20th century, gambling was almost uniformly outlawed throughout the U.S. and thus became a largely illegal activity, helping to spur the growth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_mafia" title="American mafia">mafia</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_organizations" title="Criminal organizations">criminal organizations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The late 20th century saw a softening in attitudes towards gambling and a relaxation of laws against it.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools,_1494.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="562" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools%2C_1494.jpg/220px-Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools%2C_1494.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools%2C_1494.jpg/330px-Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools%2C_1494.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools%2C_1494.jpg/440px-Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools%2C_1494.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gamblers_in_the_Ship_of_Fools,_1494.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gamblers in the <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_Fools_(satire)" title="Ship of Fools (satire)">Ship of Fools</a>, 1494</div></div></div>

<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambling_law" title="Gambling law">Gambling law</a></div>
<p>Many jurisdictions, local as well as national, either ban gambling or heavily control it by licensing the vendors. Such regulation generally leads to <a href="/wiki/Tourism#Special_forms_of_tourism" title="Tourism">gambling tourism</a> and illegal gambling in the areas where it is not allowed. The involvement of governments, through regulation and taxation, has led to a close connection between many governments and gaming organizations, where legal gambling provides significant government revenue, such as in <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau, China</a>.
</p><p>There is generally legislation requiring that gaming devices be <a href="/wiki/Statistical_randomness" title="Statistical randomness">statistically random</a>, to prevent manufacturers from making some high-payoff results impossible. Since these high payoffs have very low <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>, a house bias can quite easily be missed unless the devices are checked carefully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most jurisdictions that allow gambling require participants to be above a certain age. In some jurisdictions, the <a href="/wiki/Gambling_age" title="Gambling age">gambling age</a> differs depending on the type of gambling. For example, in many American states one must be over 21 to enter a casino, but may buy a lottery ticket after turning 18.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insurance">Insurance</span></h3>
<p>Because contracts of <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> have many features in common with wagers, insurance contracts are often distinguished in law as agreements in which either party has an interest in the "bet-upon" outcome <i>beyond</i> the specific financial terms. e.g.: a "bet" with an insurer on whether one's house will burn down is not gambling, but rather <i>insurance</i> – as the homeowner has an obvious interest in the continued existence of his/her home <i>independent of</i> the purely financial aspects of the "bet" (i.e. the insurance policy). Nonetheless, both insurance and gambling contracts are typically considered <a href="/wiki/Aleatory_contract" title="Aleatory contract">aleatory contracts</a> under most legal systems, though they are subject to different types of regulation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asset_recovery">Asset recovery</span></h3>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Law" title="English Law">English Law</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_unjust_enrichment" title="English unjust enrichment">English unjust enrichment</a>), a gambling contract may not give a casino <a href="/wiki/Bona_fide_purchaser" title="Bona fide purchaser"><i>bona fide</i> purchaser</a> status, permitting the recovery of stolen funds in some situations. In <i><a href="/wiki/Lipkin_Gorman_v_Karpnale_Ltd" title="Lipkin Gorman v Karpnale Ltd">Lipkin Gorman v Karpnale Ltd</a></i>, where a solicitor used stolen funds to gamble at a casino, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> overruled the High Court's previous verdict, adjudicating that the casino return the stolen funds less those subject to any <a href="/wiki/Change_of_position" title="Change of position">change of position</a> defence. U.S. Law precedents are somewhat similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a> on recovery of gambling losses where the loser had stolen the funds see "Rights of owner of stolen money as against one who won it in gambling transaction from thief".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>An interesting question is what happens when the person trying to make recovery is the gambler's spouse, and the money or property lost was either the spouse's, or was <a href="/wiki/Community_property" title="Community property">community property</a>. This was a minor plot point in a <a href="/wiki/Perry_Mason_bibliography" title="Perry Mason bibliography">Perry Mason</a> novel, <i>The Case of the Singing Skirt</i>, and it cites an actual case <i>Novo v. Hotel Del Rio</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span></h3>
<p>Ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> poems like the <a href="/wiki/Gambler%27s_Lament" title="Gambler's Lament">Gambler's Lament</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i> testify to the popularity of gambling among ancient Indians.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2019)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the text <i><a href="/wiki/Arthashastra" title="Arthashastra">Arthashastra</a></i> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 4th century</span> <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BCE</a>) recommends taxation and control of gambling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span></h3>
<p>Ancient Jewish authorities frowned on gambling, even disqualifying professional gamblers from testifying in court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholicism">Catholicism</span></h4>
<p>The Catholic Church holds the position that there is no moral impediment to gambling, so long as it is fair, all bettors have a reasonable chance of winning, there is no <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a> involved, and the parties involved do not have actual knowledge of the outcome of the bet (unless they have disclosed this knowledge),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kucharek_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kucharek-25">[25]</a></sup> and as long as the following conditions are met: the gambler can afford to lose the bet, and stops when the limit is reached, and the motivation is entertainment and not personal gain leading to the "love of money"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> or making a living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In general, Catholic bishops have opposed <a href="/wiki/Casino" title="Casino">casino</a> gambling on the grounds that it too often tempts people into problem gambling or addiction, and has particularly negative effects on poor people; they sometimes also cite secondary effects such as increases in loan sharking, prostitution, corruption, and general public immorality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Some parish pastors have also opposed casinos for the additional reason that they would take customers away from church <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bingo_(U.S.)" title="Bingo (U.S.)">bingo</a> and annual festivals where games such as <a href="/wiki/Blackjack" title="Blackjack">blackjack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craps" title="Craps">craps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poker" title="Poker">poker</a> are used for fundraising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Aquinas" title="St. Thomas Aquinas">St. Thomas Aquinas</a> wrote that gambling should be especially forbidden where the losing bettor is underage or otherwise not able to consent to the transaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Gambling has often been seen as having <a href="/wiki/Social_cost" title="Social cost">social consequences</a>, as satirized by <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Balzac</a>. For these social and religious reasons, most legal jurisdictions limit gambling, as advocated by <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Pascal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestantism">Protestantism</span></h4>
<p>Gambling views among Protestants vary, with some either discouraging or forbidding their members from participation in gambling. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodists" title="Methodists">Methodists</a>, in accordance with the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Outward_holiness" title="Outward holiness">outward holiness</a>, oppose gambling which they believe is a sin that feeds on greed. Other denominations that discourage gambling are the <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Free_Methodist_Church" title="Free Methodist Church">Free Methodist Church</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Wesleyan_Church" title="Evangelical Wesleyan Church">Evangelical Wesleyan Church</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWC2015_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-EWC2015-36">[36]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Nazarene" title="Church of the Nazarene">Church of the Nazarene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other Protestants that oppose gambling include Mennonites, Schwarzenau Brethren,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBC2021_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-DBC2021-39">[39]</a></sup> Quakers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Reformed_Church_in_North_America" title="Christian Reformed Church in North America">Christian Reformed Church in North America</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Lutheran_Confession" title="Church of the Lutheran Confession">Church of the Lutheran Confession</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Baptist_Convention" title="Southern Baptist Convention">Southern Baptist Convention</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Assemblies_of_God" title="Assemblies of God">Assemblies of God</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Christian_denominations">Other Christian denominations</span></h4>
<p>Other churches that oppose gambling include the <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Iglesia_ni_Cristo" title="Iglesia ni Cristo">Iglesia ni Cristo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Members_Church_of_God_International" title="Members Church of God International">Members Church of God International</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span></h3>
<p>There is a consensus among the <i>‘<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulema" title="Ulema">Ulema</a>’</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">عُـلـمـاء</span>, Scholars (of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>)) that <a href="/wiki/Maisir" title="Maisir">gambling</a> is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haraam" title="Haraam">haraam</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">حَـرام</span>, sinful or forbidden). In assertions made during its prohibition, Muslim jurists describe gambling as being both un-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%E2%80%99an" title="Qur’an">Qur’anic</a>, and as being generally harmful to the Muslim <a href="/wiki/Ummah" title="Ummah">Ummah</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">أُمَّـة</span>, Community). The Arabic terminology for gambling is <i><a href="/wiki/Maisir" title="Maisir">Maisir</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>They ask you about intoxicants and gambling. Say: 'In them both lies grave sin, though some benefit, to mankind. But their sin is more grave than their benefit.' </p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Al-Baqara" title="Al-Baqara">Surah Al-Baqara</a> <a class="external text" href="https://quran.com/2?startingVerse=219" rel="nofollow">2:219</a></cite></div></blockquote>
<p>In parts of the world that implement full Shari‘ah, such as <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a>, punishments for Muslim gamblers can range up to 12 lashes or a one-year prison term and a fine for those who provide a venue for such practises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Some Islamic nations prohibit gambling; most other countries <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaming_regulation" title="Gaming regulation">regulate it</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD_Faith"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bahá'í_Faith">Bahá'í Faith</span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Kit%C3%A1b-i-Aqdas" title="Kitáb-i-Aqdas">Most Holy Book</a>, paragraph 155, gambling is forbidden.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-casino_games">Non-casino games</span></h3>
<p>Gambling games that take place outside of casinos include bingo (as played in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bingo_(US)" title="Bingo (US)">US</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bingo_(UK)" title="Bingo (UK)">UK</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dead_pool" title="Dead pool">dead pool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lottery" title="Lottery">lotteries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pull-tab" title="Pull-tab">pull-tab</a> games and <a href="/wiki/Scratchcard" title="Scratchcard">scratchcards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mahjong" title="Mahjong">Mahjong</a>.
</p><p>Other non-casino gambling games include:
</p>
<ul><li>Non-casino <a href="/wiki/Card_game" title="Card game">card games</a>, including historical games like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basset_(card_game)" title="Basset (card game)">Basset</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecart%C3%A9" title="Ecarté">Ecarté</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lansquenet" title="Lansquenet">Lansquenet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Put_option" title="Put option">Put</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed-odds_betting">Fixed-odds betting</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fixed-odds_betting" title="Fixed-odds betting">Fixed-odds betting</a></div>
<p>Fixed-odds betting and <a href="/wiki/Parimutuel_betting" title="Parimutuel betting">Parimutuel betting</a> frequently occur at many types of sporting events, and political elections. In addition many <a href="/wiki/Bookmaker" title="Bookmaker">bookmakers</a> offer fixed odds on a number of non-sports related outcomes, for example the direction and extent of movement of various <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">financial indices</a>, the winner of <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> competitions such as <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(franchise)" title="Big Brother (franchise)">Big Brother</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">election</a> results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Interactive <a href="/wiki/Prediction_market" title="Prediction market">prediction markets</a> also offer trading on these outcomes, with "shares" of results trading on an open market.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parimutuel_betting">Parimutuel betting</span></h4>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parimutuel_betting" title="Parimutuel betting">Parimutuel betting</a></div>
<p>One of the most widespread forms of gambling involves betting on <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse</a> or <a href="/wiki/Greyhound_racing" title="Greyhound racing">greyhound racing</a>. Wagering may take place through <a href="/wiki/Parimutuel_betting" title="Parimutuel betting">parimutuel</a> pools, or <a href="/wiki/Bookmaker" title="Bookmaker">bookmakers</a> may take bets personally. Parimutuel wagers pay off at prices determined by support in the wagering pools, while bookmakers pay off either at the odds offered at the time of accepting the bet; or at the median odds offered by track bookmakers at the time the race started.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arbitrage_betting">Arbitrage betting</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage_betting" title="Arbitrage betting">Arbitrage betting</a></div>
<p>Arbitrage betting is a theoretically risk-free betting system in which every outcome of an event is bet upon so that a known profit will be made by the bettor upon completion of the event regardless of the outcome. Arbitrage betting is a combination of the ancient art of <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">arbitrage</a> trading and gambling, which has been made possible by the large numbers of bookmakers in the marketplace, creating occasional opportunities for arbitrage.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_betting">Other types of betting</span></h3>
<p>One can also bet with another person that a statement is true or false, or that a specified event will happen (a "back bet") or will not happen (a "lay bet") within a specified time. This occurs in particular when two people have opposing but strongly held views on truth or events. Not only do the parties hope to gain from the bet, they place the bet also to demonstrate their certainty about the issue. Some means of determining the issue at stake must exist. Sometimes the amount bet remains nominal, demonstrating the outcome as one of principle rather than of financial importance.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bet_exchange" title="Bet exchange">Betting exchanges</a> allow consumers to both back and lay at odds of their choice. Similar in some ways to a stock exchange, a bettor may want to back a horse (hoping it will win) or lay a horse (hoping it will lose, effectively acting as bookmaker).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spread_betting" title="Spread betting">Spread betting</a> allows gamblers to wagering on the outcome of an event where the pay-off is based on the accuracy of the wager, rather than a simple "win or lose" outcome.  For example, a wager can be based on the when a point is scored in the game in minutes and each minute away from the prediction increases or reduces the payout.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Staking_systems">Staking systems</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Betting_strategy" title="Betting strategy">Betting strategy</a></div>
<p>Many betting systems have been created in an attempt to "beat the house" but no system can make a mathematically unprofitable bet in terms of <a href="/wiki/Expected_value" title="Expected value">expected value</a> profitable over time. Widely used systems include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Card_counting" title="Card counting">Card counting</a> – Many systems exist for <a href="/wiki/Blackjack" title="Blackjack">blackjack</a> to keep track of the ratio of ten values to all others; when this ratio is high the player has an advantage and should increase the amount of their bets. Keeping track of cards dealt confers an advantage in other games as well.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Due-column_betting" title="Due-column betting">Due-column betting</a> – A variation on fixed profits betting in which the bettor sets a target profit and then calculates a bet size that will make this profit, adding any losses to the target.</li>
<li>Fixed profits – the stakes vary based on the odds to ensure the same profit from each winning selection.</li>
<li>Fixed stakes – a traditional system of staking the same amount on each selection.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kelly_criterion" title="Kelly criterion">Kelly</a> – the optimum level to bet to maximize your future median bank level.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Martingale_(betting_system)" title="Martingale (betting system)">Martingale</a> – A system based on staking enough each time to recover losses from previous bet(s) until one wins.</li></ul>

<p>Many risk-return choices are sometimes referred to colloquially as "gambling."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Whether this terminology is acceptable is a matter of debate:
</p>
<ul><li>Emotional or physical risk-taking, where the risk-return ratio is not quantifiable (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skydiving" title="Skydiving">skydiving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_campaign" title="Political campaign">campaigning</a> for political office, asking someone for a date, etc.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">Insurance</a> is a method of shifting risk from one party to another. Insurers use actuarial methods to calculate appropriate premiums, which is similar to calculating gambling odds. Insurers set their premiums to obtain a long term positive expected return in the same manner that professional gamblers select which bets to make. While insurance is sometimes distinguished from gambling by the requirement of an <a href="/wiki/Insurable_interest" title="Insurable interest">insurable interest</a>, the equivalent in gambling is simply betting against one's own best interests (e.g., a sports coach betting against his own team to mitigate the financial repercussions of a losing season).</li>
<li>Situations where the possible return is of secondary importance to the wager/purchase (e.g. entering a <a href="/wiki/Raffle" title="Raffle">raffle</a> in support of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charitable_gambling" title="Charitable gambling">charitable</a> cause)</li></ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Investments" title="Investments">Investments</a> are also usually not considered gambling, although some investments can involve significant risk. Examples of investments include <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a>. Starting a business can also be considered a form of investment. Investments are generally not considered gambling when they meet the following criteria:
</p>
<ul><li>Economic utility</li>
<li>Positive expected returns (at least in the long term)</li>
<li>Underlying value independent of the risk being undertaken</li></ul>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">speculative</a> investment activities are particularly risky, but are sometimes perceived to be different from gambling:
</p>
<ul><li>Foreign currency exchange (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forex" title="Forex">forex</a>) transactions</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prediction_markets" title="Prediction markets">Prediction markets</a></li>
<li>Securities <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivatives</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Option_(finance)" title="Option (finance)">options</a> or <a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">futures</a>, where the value of the derivative is dependent on the value of the underlying asset at a specific point in time (typically the derivative's associated expiration date)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_consequences">Negative consequences</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Problem_gambling" title="Problem gambling">Problem gambling</a></div>
<p>Studies show that though many people participate in gambling as a form of <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a> or to earn an income, gambling, like any behavior involving variation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_chemistry" title="Brain chemistry">brain chemistry</a>, can become a <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_addiction" title="Behavioral addiction">behavioral addiction</a>. Behavioral addiction can occur with all the negative consequences in a person's life minus the physical issues faced by people who compulsively engage in drug and alcohol abuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Problem gambling has multiple symptoms. Gamblers often gamble to try to win back money they have lost, and some gamble to relieve feelings of helplessness and anxiety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Advertising_Standards_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Advertising Standards Authority (United Kingdom)">Advertising Standards Authority</a> has censured several betting firms for advertisements disguised as news articles suggesting falsely that a person had cleared debts and paid for medical expenses by gambling online . The firms face possible fines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2020 study of 32 countries found that the greater the amount of gambling activity in a given country, the more volatile that country's stock market prices are.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_biases">Psychological biases</span></h2>
<p>Gamblers may exhibit a number of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_bias" title="Cognitive bias">cognitive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motivated_reasoning" title="Motivated reasoning">motivational</a> biases that distort the perceived odds of events and that influence their preferences for gambles.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Preference for likely outcomes</i>. When gambles are selected through a choice process – when people indicate which gamble they prefer from a set of gambles (e.g., win/lose, over/under) – people tend to prefer to bet on the outcome that is more likely to occur. Bettors tend to prefer to bet on favorites in athletic competitions, and sometimes will accept even bets on favorites when offered more favorable bets on the less likely outcome (e.g., an underdog team).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Optimism_bias" title="Optimism bias">Optimism</a>/Desirability Bias<b>.</b></i> Gamblers also exhibit optimism, overestimating the likelihood that desired events will occur. Fans of NFL underdog teams, for example, will prefer to bet on their teams at even odds than to bet on the favorite, whether the bet is $5 or $50.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Reluctance to bet against (hedge) desired outcomes.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-61">[61]</a></sup> People are reluctant to bet against desired outcomes that are relevant to their identity. Gamblers exhibit reluctance to bet against the success of their preferred U.S. presidential candidates and Major League Baseball, National Football League, National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) basketball, and NCAA hockey teams. More than 45% of NCAA fans in Studies 5 and 6, for instance, turned down a "free" real $5 bet against their team. From a psychological perspective, such a "<a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)" title="Hedge (finance)">hedge</a>" creates an interdependence dilemma – a motivational conflict between a short-term monetary gain and the long-term benefits accrued from feelings of <a href="/wiki/Social_identity_theory" title="Social identity theory">identification</a> with and loyalty to a position, person, or group whom the bettor desires to succeed.  In economic terms, this conflicted decision can be modeled as a trade-off between the outcome utility gained by hedging (e.g., money) and the <a href="/wiki/Signalling_(economics)" title="Signalling (economics)">diagnostic costs</a> it incurs (e.g., disloyalty). People make <a href="/wiki/Self-perception_theory" title="Self-perception theory">inferences</a> about their beliefs and identity from their behavior. If a person is uncertain about an aspect of their identity, such as the extent to which they values a candidate or team, hedging may signal to them that they are not as committed to that candidate or team as they originally believed. If the diagnostic cost of this self-signal and the resulting identity change are substantial, it may outweigh the outcome utility of hedging, and they may reject even very generous hedges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Ratio bias.</i> Gamblers will prefer gambles with worse odds that are drawn from a large sample (e.g., drawing one red ball from an urn containing 89 red balls and 11 blue balls) to better odds that are drawn from a small sample (drawing one red ball from an urn containing 9 red balls and one blue ball).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Gambler%27s_fallacy" title="Gambler's fallacy">Gambler's fallacy</a>/positive recency bias.</i></li></ul>
