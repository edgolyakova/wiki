<h1 class="firstHeading mw-first-heading" id="firstHeading"><span class="mw-page-title-main">Earth science</span></h1>
<div class="vector-body" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="contentSub2"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Fields of natural science related to Earth</div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DirkvdM_rocks.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="852" data-file-width="1136" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/DirkvdM_rocks.jpg/220px-DirkvdM_rocks.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/DirkvdM_rocks.jpg/330px-DirkvdM_rocks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/DirkvdM_rocks.jpg/440px-DirkvdM_rocks.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DirkvdM_rocks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The rocky side of a mountain creek in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Earth science</b> or <b>geoscience</b> includes all fields of <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. This is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> dealing with the physical, chemical, and biological <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complex</a> constitutions and <a href="/wiki/Synergy" title="Synergy">synergistic</a> linkages of Earth's four spheres, namely <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a>. Earth science can be considered to be a branch of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>, but with a much older history. Earth science encompasses four main branches of study, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, each of which is further broken down into more specialized fields.
</p><p>There are both <a href="/wiki/Reductionism" title="Reductionism">reductionist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> approaches to Earth sciences. It is also the study of Earth and its neighbors in space. Some Earth scientists use their knowledge of the planet to locate and develop energy and mineral resources. Others study the impact of human activity on Earth's environment, and design methods to protect the planet. Some use their knowledge about Earth processes such as <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a> to plan communities that will not expose people to these dangerous events.
</p><p>Earth sciences can include the study of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, and the large-scale structure of Earth's interior, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Typically, Earth scientists use tools from <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">chronology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> to build a quantitative understanding of how Earth works and evolves. For example, meteorologists study the weather and watch for dangerous storms. Hydrologists examine water and warn of floods. Seismologists study earthquakes and try to understand where they will strike. Geologists study rocks and help to locate useful minerals. Earth scientists often work in the field—perhaps climbing mountains, exploring the seabed, crawling through caves, or wading in swamps. They measure and collect samples (such as rocks or river water), then record their findings on charts and maps.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_(50754).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2949" data-file-width="3436" decoding="async" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_%2850754%29.jpg/220px-Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_%2850754%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_%2850754%29.jpg/330px-Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_%2850754%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_%2850754%29.jpg/440px-Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_%2850754%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Layers_of_sedimentary_rock_in_Makhtesh_Ramon_(50754).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Layers of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> in <a href="/wiki/Makhtesh_Ramon" title="Makhtesh Ramon">Makhtesh Ramon</a></div></div></div>
<p>Geology is the study of the lithosphere, or Earth's surface, including the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>. It includes the physical characteristics and processes that occur in the lithosphere as well as how they are affected by <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>. It incorporates aspects of chemistry, physics, and biology as elements of geology interact. <a href="/wiki/Historical_geology" title="Historical geology">Historical geology</a> is the application of geology to interpret Earth's history and how it changes over time. <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">Geochemistry</a> studies the chemical components and processes of the Earth. <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysics</a> studies the physical properties of the lithosphere. <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">Paleontology</a> studies fossilized biological material in the lithosphere. <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">Planetary geology</a> studies geology as it pertains to extraterrestrial bodies. <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</a> studies the origin of landscapes. <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">Structural geology</a> studies the deformation of rocks to produce mountains and lowlands. Resource geology studies how energy resources can be obtained from minerals. <a href="/wiki/Environmental_geology" title="Environmental geology">Environmental geology</a> studies how pollution and contaminates affect soil and rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmithPun200614–16_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmithPun200614–16-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">Mineralogy</a> is the study of minerals. It includes the study of mineral formation, <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a>, hazards associated with minerals, and the physical and chemical properties of minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldar2020109_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldar2020109-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">Petrology</a> is the study of rocks, including the formation and composition of rocks. <a href="/wiki/Petrography" title="Petrography">Petrography</a> is a branch of petrology that studies the typology and classification of rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaldar2020145_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaldar2020145-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> ranges, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological phenomena</a> that can be explained in terms of physical and chemical processes in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Beneath the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> lies the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> which is heated by the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_elements" title="Heavy elements">heavy elements</a>. The mantle is not quite solid and consists of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> which is in a state of semi-perpetual <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convection" title="wikt:convection">convection</a>. This convection process causes the lithospheric plates to move, albeit slowly. The resulting process is known as <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldroyd_tectonics_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_tectonics-9">[9]</a></sup> Areas of the crust where new crust is created are called <i>divergent boundaries</i>, those where it is brought back into the Earth are <i>convergent boundaries</i> and those where plates slide past each other, but no new lithospheric material is created or destroyed, are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform</a> (or conservative) boundaries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldroyd_tectonics_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_tectonics-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</a> result from the movement of the lithospheric plates, and they often occur near convergent boundaries where parts of the crust are forced into the earth as part of subduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_subduction_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith_subduction-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plate tectonics might be thought of as the process by which the Earth is resurfaced. As the result of <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a>, new crust and lithosphere is created by the flow of magma from the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> to the near surface, through fissures, where it cools and solidifies. Through  <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a>, oceanic crust and lithosphere returns to the convecting mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldroyd_tectonics_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_tectonics-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanoes" title="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</a> result primarily from the melting of subducted crust material. Crust material that is forced into the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> melts, and some portion of the melted material becomes light enough to rise to the surface—giving birth to volcanoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_subduction_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith_subduction-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_science">Atmospheric science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Atmospheric science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Off_topic plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>may contain material <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Guide_to_writing_better_articles#Stay_on_topic" title="Wikipedia:Guide to writing better articles">unrelated or insufficiently related to the topic of the article</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> or discuss this issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Earth_science" title="Talk:Earth science">talk page</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2022</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_science" title="Atmospheric science">Atmospheric science</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/220px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/330px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/440px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> shields the surface of Earth from the charged particles of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<br/>(<i>image not to scale.</i>)</div></div></div>
<p>Atmospheric science initially developed in the late-19th century as a means to forecast the weather through <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, the study of weather. <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">Atmospheric chemistry</a> was developed in the 20th century to measure <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and expanded in the 1970s in response to <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>. <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">Climatology</a> studies the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> are the five layers which make up Earth's atmosphere. 75% of the gases in the atmosphere are located within the troposphere, the lowest layer. In all, the atmosphere is made up of about 78.0% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 20.9% <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and 0.92% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, and small amounts of other gases including CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_atmosphere_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams_atmosphere-14">[14]</a></sup> Water vapor and CO<sub>2</sub> allow the Earth's atmosphere to catch and hold the Sun's energy through the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This allows Earth's surface to be warm enough to have liquid water and support life. In addition to storing heat, the atmosphere also protects living organisms by shielding the Earth's surface from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>—which are often incorrectly thought to be deflected by the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The magnetic field—created by the internal motions of the core—produces the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> which protects Earth's atmosphere from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> As the Earth is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_the_earth" title="Age of the earth">4.5 billion</a> years old,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age_earth_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-age_earth-18">[18]</a></sup> it would have lost its atmosphere by now if there were no protective magnetosphere.
</p>
<h2><span id="Earth.27s_magnetic_field"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_magnetic_field">Earth's magnetic field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earth's magnetic field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="excerpt-block"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1066933788">.mw-parser-output .excerpt-hat .mw-editsection-like{font-style:normal}</style><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable dablink excerpt-hat selfref" role="note">This section is an excerpt from <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>.<span class="mw-editsection-like plainlinks"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_magnetic_field&amp;action=edit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><div class="excerpt">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="787" data-file-width="717" decoding="async" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif/220px-Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif/330px-Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif/440px-Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geodynamo_Between_Reversals.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Computer simulation of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s field in a period of normal polarity between reversals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth's_magnetic_field_selfconsistent_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Earth's_magnetic_field_selfconsistent-19">[19]</a></sup> The lines represent magnetic field lines, blue when the field points towards the center and yellow when away. The rotation axis of Earth is centered and vertical. The dense clusters of lines are within Earth's core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth's_magnetic_field_Glatzmaier_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Earth's_magnetic_field_Glatzmaier-20">[20]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>, also known as the geomagnetic field, is the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> that extends from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structure_of_Earth" title="Structure of Earth">Earth's interior</a> out into space, where it interacts with the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, a stream of <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particles</a> emanating from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The magnetic field is generated by <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric currents</a> due to the motion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_current" title="Convection current">convection currents</a> of a mixture of molten iron and nickel in <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core" title="Earth's outer core">Earth's outer core</a>: these convection currents are caused by heat escaping from the core, a natural process called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodynamo" title="Geodynamo">geodynamo</a>. The magnitude of Earth's magnetic field  at its surface ranges from 25 to 65 μT (0.25 to 0.65 G).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth's_magnetic_field_GeomagneticReferenceField_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Earth's_magnetic_field_GeomagneticReferenceField-21">[21]</a></sup> As an approximation, it is represented by a field of a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole" title="Magnetic dipole">magnetic dipole</a> currently tilted at an angle of about 11° with respect to <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">Earth's rotational axis</a>, as if there were an enormous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_magnet" title="Bar magnet">bar magnet</a> placed at that angle through the center of Earth. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_geomagnetic_pole" title="North geomagnetic pole">North geomagnetic pole</a> actually represents the South pole of Earth's magnetic field, and conversely the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_geomagnetic_pole" title="South geomagnetic pole">South geomagnetic pole</a> corresponds to the north pole of Earth's magnetic field (because opposite magnetic poles attract and the north end of a magnet, like a compass needle, points toward Earth's South magnetic field, i.e., the North geomagnetic pole near the Geographic North Pole). As of 2015, the North geomagnetic pole was located on <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, Canada.
</p><p>While the North and South <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">magnetic poles</a> are usually located near the geographic poles, they slowly and continuously move over geological time scales, but sufficiently slowly for ordinary <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compasses</a> to remain useful for navigation. However, at irregular intervals averaging several hundred thousand years, <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">Earth's field reverses</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Magnetic_Pole" title="North Magnetic Pole">North</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Magnetic_Pole" title="South Magnetic Pole">South Magnetic Poles</a> respectively, abruptly switch places. These reversals of the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_pole" title="Geomagnetic pole">geomagnetic poles</a> leave a record in rocks that are of value to <a href="/wiki/Paleomagnetism" title="Paleomagnetism">paleomagnetists</a> in calculating geomagnetic fields in the past. Such information in turn is helpful in studying the motions of continents and ocean floors in the process of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>.
</p>
The <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> is the region above the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> that is defined by the extent of Earth's magnetic field in space. It extends several tens of thousands of kilometres into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>, protecting Earth from the charged particles of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> that would otherwise strip away the upper atmosphere, including the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> that protects Earth from <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet#Harmful_effects" title="Ultraviolet">the harmful ultraviolet radiation</a>.</div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrology">Hydrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hydrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">Hydrology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Watercyclesummary.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="498" data-file-width="729" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Watercyclesummary.jpg/220px-Watercyclesummary.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Watercyclesummary.jpg/330px-Watercyclesummary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Watercyclesummary.jpg/440px-Watercyclesummary.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Watercyclesummary.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Movement of water through the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hydrology is the study of the hydrosphere and the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">movement of water on Earth</a>. It emphasizes the study of how humans use and interact with freshwater supplies. Study of water's movement is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> and other branches of Earth science. Applied hydrology involves engineering to maintain aquatic environments and distribute water supplies. Subdisciplines of hydrology include <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogeology" title="Hydrogeology">hydrogeology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecohydrology" title="Ecohydrology">ecohydrology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a>. Oceanography is the study of oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Hydrogeology is the study of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>. It includes the mapping of groundwater supplies and the analysis of groundwater contaminants. Applied hydrogeology seeks to prevent contamination of groundwater and <a href="/wiki/Mineral_spring" title="Mineral spring">mineral springs</a> and make it available as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>. The earliest exploitation of groundwater resources dates back to 3000 BC, and hydrogeology as a science was developed by hydrologists beginning in the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Ecohydrology is the study of ecological systems in the hydrosphere. It can be divided into the physical study of aquatic ecosystems and the biological study of aquatic organisms. Ecohydrology includes the effects that organisms and aquatic ecosystems have on one another as well as how these ecoystems are affected by humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Glaciology is the study of the cryosphere, including <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and coverage of the Earth by ice and snow. Concerns of glaciology include access to glacial freshwater, mitigation of glacial hazards, obtaining resources that exist beneath frozen land, and addressing the effects of climate change on the cryosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a></div>
<p>Ecology is the study of the biosphere. This includes the study of nature and of how living things interact with the Earth and one another. It considers how living things use resources such as oxygen, water, and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> from the Earth to sustain themselves. It also considers how humans and other living creatures cause changes to nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_geography">Physical geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Physical geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">Physical geography</a></div>
<p>Physical geography is the study of <a href="/wiki/Earth_system_science" title="Earth system science">Earth's systems</a> and how they interact with one another as part of a single self-contained system. It incorporates astronomy, mathematical geography, meteorology, climatology, geology, geomorphology, biology, biogeography, pedology, and soils geography. Physical geography is distinct from <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">human geography</a>, which studies the human populations on Earth, though it does include human effects on the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p><a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">Methodologies</a> vary depending on the nature of the subjects being studied. Studies typically fall into one of three categories: observational, experimental, or theoretical. Earth scientists often conduct sophisticated computer analysis or visit an interesting location to study earth phenomena (e.g. Antarctica or <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hot spot</a> island chains).
</p><p>A foundational idea in Earth science is the notion of <a href="/wiki/Uniformitarianism" title="Uniformitarianism">uniformitarianism</a>, which states that "ancient geologic features are interpreted by understanding active processes that are readily observed." In other words, any geologic processes at work in the present have operated in the same ways throughout geologic time. This enables those who study Earth's history to apply knowledge of how Earth processes operate in the present to gain insight into how the planet has evolved and changed throughout long history.
</p>

<p>Earth science generally recognizes four spheres, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheeling_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-wheeling-28">[28]</a></sup> these correspond to <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>. Also included by some are the <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>) as a distinct portion of the hydrosphere and the <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>) as an active and intermixed sphere.
The following <a href="/wiki/Branches_of_science" title="Branches of science">fields of science</a> are generally categorized within the Earth sciences:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a> describes the <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocky</a> parts of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>) and its historic development. Major subdisciplines are <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">petrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structural geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_geology" title="Engineering geology">engineering geology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sedimentology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_5_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith_5-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">Physical geography</a> focuses on <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> as an Earth science. Physical geography is the study of Earth's seasons, climate, atmosphere, soil, streams, landforms, and oceans. Physical geography can be divided into several branches or related fields, as follows: <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_geography" title="Environmental geography">environmental geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palaeogeography" title="Palaeogeography">palaeogeography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coastal_geography" title="Coastal geography">coastal geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a> investigate the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">shape of the Earth</a>, its reaction to forces and its <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_field" title="Gravity field">gravity fields</a>. Geophysicists explore the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">Earth's core</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismic</a> activity of the lithosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_5_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith_5-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Geophysics is commonly used to supplement the work of <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a> in developing a comprehensive understanding of crustal geology, particularly in mineral and petroleum exploration. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismologist" title="Seismologist">Seismologists</a> use <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_survey" title="Geophysical survey">geophysics</a> to understand plate tectonic shifting, as well as predict seismic activity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">Geochemistry</a> is defined as the study of the processes that control the abundance, composition, and distribution of chemical compounds and isotopes in geologic environments. Geochemists use the tools and principles of chemistry to study the composition, structure, processes, and other physical aspects of the Earth. Major subdisciplines are <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_geochemistry" title="Aqueous geochemistry">aqueous geochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmochemistry" title="Cosmochemistry">cosmochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isotope_geochemistry" title="Isotope geochemistry">isotope geochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemistry" title="Biogeochemistry">biogeochemistry</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_science" title="Soil science">Soil science</a> covers the outermost layer of the Earth's crust that is subject to soil formation processes (or <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Major subdivisions in this field of study include <a href="/wiki/Edaphology" title="Edaphology">edaphology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">pedology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a> covers the interactions between organisms and their environment. This field of study differentiates the study of Earth from the study of other planets in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, Earth being its only planet teeming with life.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">Hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnology</a> are studies which focus on the movement, distribution, and quality of the water and involves all the components of the hydrologic cycle on the Earth and its atmosphere (or <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>). "Sub-disciplines of hydrology include hydrometeorology, surface water hydrology, hydrogeology, watershed science, forest hydrology, and water chemistry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">Glaciology</a> covers the <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">icy</a> parts of the Earth (or <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a>).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">Atmospheric sciences</a> cover the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> parts of the Earth (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>) between the surface and the <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> (about 1000 km). Major subdisciplines include meteorology, climatology, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_physics" title="Atmospheric physics">atmospheric physics</a>.</li></ul>
