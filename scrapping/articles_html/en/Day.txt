<p>Generally, a <b>day</b> is roughly the time of one <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">rotation of the Earth</a> (about 24 <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hours</a>) or one rotation of other large <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical objects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In everyday life, the word "day" often refers to a <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">solar day</a>, which is the length between two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_noon" title="Solar noon">solar noons</a> or times the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> reaches the highest point. The word "day" may also refer to <i><a href="/wiki/Daytime" title="Daytime">daytime</a></i>, a time period when the location receives <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">direct and indirect sunlight</a>. On Earth, as a location passes through its day, it experiences <a href="/wiki/Morning" title="Morning">morning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">noon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afternoon" title="Afternoon">afternoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evening" title="Evening">evening</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>. The effect of a day is vital to many <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> processes, which is called the <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a>.
</p><p>Days can be organized into <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendars</a> as <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">dates</a>, usually into <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">months</a> and <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">years</a>. Most calendars' arrangement of dates use either or both the Sun's (<a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">solar calendar</a>) or the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>'s (<a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendar</a>) relative position in the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a>. The start of a day is commonly accepted as roughly the time of the middle of the night or <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">midnight</a>, written as 0:00 or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12_am" title="12 am">12 am</a> in <a href="/wiki/12-hour_clock" title="12-hour clock">12-</a> or <a href="/wiki/24-hour_clock" title="24-hour clock">24-hour clock</a>. Because the time of midnight varies between locations, <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">time zones</a> are set up to facilitate the use of a uniform <a href="/wiki/Standard_time" title="Standard time">standard time</a>.
</p><p>In specific applications, the definition of a day are slightly modified, such as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> day (exactly 86,400 <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">seconds</a>) used for <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time_metrology" title="Time metrology">standards keeping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_mean_time" title="Local mean time">local mean time</a> accounting of the <a href="/wiki/Day_length_fluctuations" title="Day length fluctuations">Earth's natural fluctuation of a solar day</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_day" title="Stellar day">stellar day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_time" title="Sidereal time">sidereal day</a> (using the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>) used for <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. In some countries, a day may be set starting earlier or later via the <a href="/wiki/Daylight_saving_time" title="Daylight saving time">daylight saving time</a>, making it possible to have a 23- or 25-hour civil day.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>dæg</i>, with its cognates such as <i>dagur</i> in <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, <i>Tag</i> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, and <i>dag</i> in <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> – all stemming from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> root <i>*dagaz</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> As of October 17, 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <i>day</i> is the 205th most common word in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US English</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_Words_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-English_Words-3">[3]</a></sup> and the 210th most common in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK English</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_Words_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-English_Words-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apparent_and_mean_solar_day">Apparent and mean solar day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apparent and mean solar day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif/220px-EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif/330px-EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif/440px-EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EpicEarth-Globespin-tilt-23.4.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth's rotation imaged by <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory" title="Deep Space Climate Observatory">Deep Space Climate Observatory</a>, showing axis tilt</div></div></div>
<p>Several definitions of this universal human concept are used according to context, need and convenience. Besides the day of 24 hours (86,400 seconds), the word <i>day</i> is used for several different spans of time based on the rotation of the Earth around its axis. An important one is the solar day, defined as the time it takes for the Sun to return to its culmination point (its highest point in the sky). Because celestial orbits are not perfectly circular, and thus objects travel at different speeds at various positions in their orbit, a solar day is not the same length of time throughout the orbital year. Because the Earth moves along an <a href="/wiki/Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes#Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes_of_the_planets" title="Semi-major and semi-minor axes">eccentric orbit</a> around the Sun while the Earth spins on an inclined axis, this period can be up to 7.9 seconds more than (or less than) 24 hours. In recent decades, the average length of a solar day on Earth has been about 86,400.002 seconds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (24.000 000 6 hours) and there are currently about 365.2421875 solar days in one mean <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical year</a>.
</p><p>Ancient custom has a new day start at either the rising or setting of the Sun on the local horizon (Italian reckoning, for example, being 24 hours from sunset, oldstyle).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The exact moment of, and the interval between, two sunrises or sunsets depends on the geographical position (<a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and latitude, as well as altitude), and the time of <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a> (as indicated by ancient hemispherical <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundials</a>).
</p><p>A more constant day can be defined by the Sun passing through the local <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a>, which happens at local <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">noon</a> (upper <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">culmination</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">midnight</a> (lower culmination). The exact moment is dependent on the geographical longitude, and to a lesser extent on the time of the year. The length of such a day is nearly constant (24 hours ± 30 seconds). This is the time as indicated by modern sundials.
</p><p>A further improvement defines a fictitious mean Sun that moves with constant speed along the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a>; the speed is the same as the average speed of the real Sun, but this removes the variation over a year as the Earth moves along its orbit around the Sun (due to both its velocity and its axial tilt).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_day">Civil day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Civil day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For civil purposes, a common clock time is typically defined for an entire region based on the local mean solar time at a central meridian. Such <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone"><i>time zones</i></a> began to be adopted about the middle of the 19th century when <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railroads</a> with regularly occurring schedules came into use, with most major countries having adopted them by 1929. As of 2015, throughout the world, 40 such zones are now in use: the central zone, from which all others are defined as offsets, is known as <a href="/wiki/UTC%C2%B100:00" title="UTC±00:00">UTC±00</a>, which uses <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)</a>.
</p><p>The most common convention starts the civil day at <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">midnight</a>: this is near the time of the <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">lower culmination</a> of the Sun on the central meridian of the time zone. Such a day may be referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Calendar_day" title="Calendar day">calendar day</a>.
</p><p>A day is commonly divided into 24 hours of 60 minutes, with each minute composed of 60 seconds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sidereal_day">Sidereal day<span class="anchor" id="Extraterrestrial_bodies"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sidereal day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation#Stellar_day" title="Earth's rotation">Earth's rotation § Stellar day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_time" title="Sidereal time">Sidereal time</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">Rotation period</a></div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_time" title="Sidereal time">sidereal day</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_day" title="Stellar day">stellar day</a> is the span of time it takes for the Earth to make one entire <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with respect to the celestial background or a distant star (assumed to be fixed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> Measuring a day as such is used in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> A sidereal day is about 4 minutes less than a solar day of 24 hours (23 hours 56 minutes and 4.09 seconds), or 0.99726968 of a solar day of 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen296_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen296-8">[8]</a></sup> There are about 366.2422 stellar days in one mean tropical year (one stellar day more than the number of solar days).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_International_System_of_Units">In the International System of Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In the International System of Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI), a day <a href="/wiki/Non-SI_units_mentioned_in_the_SI" title="Non-SI units mentioned in the SI">not an official unit, but is accepted for use with SI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Non-SI_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Non-SI-14">[14]</a></sup> A day, with symbol d, is defined using SI units as 86,400 seconds; the second is the base unit of time in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">SI units</a>. In 1967–68, during the 13th CGPM (Resolution 1),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> (BIPM) redefined a second as "... the duration of 9,192,631,770 periods of the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radiation</a> corresponding to the transition between two <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">hyperfine</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">levels</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Stationary_state" title="Stationary state">ground state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> 133 <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This makes the SI-based day last exactly 794,243,384,928,000 of those periods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_decimal_and_metric_time">In decimal and metric time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In decimal and metric time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Decimal_time" title="Decimal time">decimal time</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metric_time" title="Metric time">Metric time</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2533" data-file-width="2533" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg/220px-Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg/330px-Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg/440px-Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Decimal_Clock_face_by_Pierre_Daniel_Destigny_1798-1805.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Decimal clock face, made in around the start of the 19th century</div></div></div>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Decimal_time" title="Decimal time">decimal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metric_time" title="Metric time">metric time</a> proposals have been made, but do not redefine the day, and use the day or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">sidereal day</a> as a base unit. Metric time uses metric prefixes to keep time. It uses the day as the base unit, and smaller units being fractions of a day: a metric hour (<i>deci</i>) is <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020198016">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">1</span>⁄<span class="den">10</span></span> of a day; a metric minute (<i>milli</i>) is <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1020198016" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">1</span>⁄<span class="den">1000</span></span> of a day; etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Similarly, in decimal time, the length of a day is static to normal time. A day is also split into 10 hours, and 10 days comprise a <i>décade –</i> the equivalent to a week. 3 <i>décades</i> make a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 35">: 35 </span></sup> Various decimal time proposals which do not redefine the day: Henri de Sarrauton's proposal kept days, and subdivided hours into 100 minutes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 42">: 42 </span></sup> in Mendizábal y Tamborel's proposal, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">sidereal day</a> was the basic unit, with subdivisions made upon it;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Pages: 42–43">: 42–43 </span></sup> and Rey-Pailhade's proposal divided the day 100 <i>cés.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-:5-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 42">: 42 </span></sup></i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_definitions">Other definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word refers to various similarly defined ideas, such as:
</p>
<dl><dt>Full day</dt></dl>
<ul><li>24 <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hours</a> (exactly) (a <a href="/wiki/Nychthemeron" title="Nychthemeron">nychthemeron</a>)</li>
<li>A day counting approximation, for example "See you in three days." or "the following day"</li>
<li>The full day covering both the dark and light periods, beginning from the start of the dark period or from a point near the middle of the dark period</li>
<li>A full dark and light period, sometimes called a <i><a href="/wiki/Nychthemeron" title="Nychthemeron">nychthemeron</a></i> in English, from the Greek for <i><a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>-day</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> or more colloquially the term <span class="nowrap"><i>24 hours</i></span>. In other languages, <span class="nowrap"><i>24 hours</i></span> is also often used. Other languages also have a separate word for a full day.</li>
<li>Part of a date: the day of the year (<i>doy</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_date" title="Ordinal date">ordinal dates</a>, day of the month (<i>dom</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">calendar dates</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weekday" title="Weekday">day of the week</a> (<i>dow</i>) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Week_date" title="Week date">week dates</a>.</li>
<li>Time regularly spend at paid work on a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work_day" title="Work day">work day</a>, cf. <a href="/wiki/Man-hour" title="Man-hour">man-day</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workweek" title="Workweek">workweek</a>.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Daytime</dt></dl>
<ul><li>The period of light when the Sun is above the local <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> (that is, the time period from <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>)</li>
<li>The time period from 06:00–18:00 (6:00 <a href="/wiki/12-hour_clock" title="12-hour clock">am</a> – 6:00 pm) or 21:00 (9:00 pm) or another fixed clock period overlapping or offset from other time periods such as "<a href="/wiki/Morning" title="Morning">morning</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Evening" title="Evening">evening</a>", or "<a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>".</li>
<li>The time period from first-light "<a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a>" to last-light "<a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a>".</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Other</dt></dl>
<ul><li>A specific period of the day, which may vary by context, such as "the school day" or "the work day".</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_in_length">Variations in length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variations in length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Leap_second" title="Leap second">Leap second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">Tidal acceleration</a></div>
<p>Mainly due to <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">tidal deceleration</a> – the Moon's <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational pull</a> slowing down the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">Earth's rotation</a> – the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">rotational period</a> is slowing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup> Because of the way the second is defined, the mean length of a solar day is now about 86,400.002 seconds, and is increasing by about 2 <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">milliseconds</a> per century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the rotation rate of the Earth is slowing, the length of a <abbr title="International System of Units">SI</abbr> second fell out of sync with a second derived from the rotational period of the earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup> This arose the need for <a href="/wiki/Leap_second" title="Leap second">leap seconds</a>, which insert extra seconds into <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup> Although typically 86,400 <abbr title="International System of Units">SI</abbr> seconds in duration, a civil day can be either 86,401 or 86,399 SI seconds long on such a day. Other than the two-millisecond variation from tidal deceleration, <a href="/wiki/Day_length_fluctuations" title="Day length fluctuations">other factors minutely affect the day's length</a>, which creates irregularity in the placement of leap seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Leap seconds are announced in advance by the <a href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service" title="International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service">International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS)</a>, which measures the Earth's rotation and determines whether a leap second is necessary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_day_lengths">Geological day lengths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Geological day lengths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Discovered by paleontologist <a href="/wiki/John_W._Wells" title="John W. Wells">John W. Wells</a>, the day lengths of geological periods have been estimated by measuring sedimentation rings in coral <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup> due to some biological systems being affected by the tide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup> The length of a day at the Earth's formation is estimated at 6 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup> Arbab I. Arbab plotted day lengths over time and found a curved line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup> Arbab attributed this to the change of water volume present affecting Earth's rotation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries">Boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:210px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="237" data-file-width="245" decoding="async" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg/208px-Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg 1.5x" width="208"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun_and_Moon_Nuremberg_chronicle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, Hartmann Schedel's <i><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Chronicle" title="Nuremberg Chronicle">Nuremberg Chronicle</a></i>, 1493</div></div></div>
<p>For most <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> animals, the day naturally begins at dawn and ends at sunset. Humans, with their cultural norms and scientific knowledge, have employed several different conceptions of the day's boundaries. In the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a> 1:5 defines a day in terms of "evening" and "morning" before recounting the creation of a sun to illuminate it: "And God called the light Day, and the darkness he called Night. And the evening and the morning were the first day." Common convention among the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Romans</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_China" title="Ancient China">ancient Chinese</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and in modern times is for the civil day to begin at midnight, i.e. 00:00, and to last a full 24 hours until 24:00 (i.e. 00:00 of the next day).
In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> the day was reckoned from sunrise to sunrise. The <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> day begins at either sunset or nightfall (when three second-<a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">magnitude</a> stars appear).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Medieval</a> Europe also followed this tradition, known as <a href="/wiki/Florentine_calendar" title="Florentine calendar">Florentine</a> reckoning: in this system, a reference like "two hours into the day" meant <i>two hours after sunset</i> and thus times during the evening need to be shifted back one calendar day in modern reckoning.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Days such as <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>, and the Eve of <a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_Rome" title="Agnes of Rome">Saint Agnes</a> are remnants of the older pattern when <a href="/wiki/Holiday#Religious_holiday" title="Holiday">holidays</a> began during the prior evening. Prior to 1926, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> had two time systems: Turkish (counting the hours from sunset) and French (counting the hours from midnight).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts">Parts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Parts_of_a_day" title="Category:Parts of a day">Category:Parts of a day</a></div>
<p>Humans have divided the day in rough periods, which can have cultural implications, and other effects on humans' biological processes. The parts of the day do not have set times; they can vary by lifestyle or hours of daylight in a given place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daytime">Daytime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Daytime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Daytime" title="Daytime">Daytime</a></div>
<p>A day, in the sense of <a href="/wiki/Daytime" title="Daytime">daytime</a> that is distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Night#Duration_and_geographical" title="Night">night time</a>, is commonly defined as the period during which <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> directly reaches the ground, assuming that there are no local obstacles. The length of daytime averages slightly more than half of the 24-hour day. Two effects make daytime on average longer than nights. The Sun is not a point, but has an apparent size of about 32 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">minutes of arc</a>. Additionally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracts</a> sunlight in such a way that some of it reaches the ground even when the Sun is below the horizon by about 34 minutes of arc. So the first light reaches the ground when the centre of the Sun is still below the horizon by about 50 minutes of arc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Thus, daytime is on average around 7 minutes longer than 12 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Daytime is further distinguished into <a href="/wiki/Morning" title="Morning">morning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afternoon" title="Afternoon">afternoon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Evening" title="Evening">evening</a>. Morning occurs between <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">noon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Afternoon occurs between noon and <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> This period of time sees human's highest <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">body temperature</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> an increase of <a href="/wiki/Traffic_collision" title="Traffic collision">traffic collisions</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and a decrease of <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Evening occurs between the end of afternoon and before sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twilight">Twilight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Twilight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">Twilight</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2981" data-file-width="4472" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg/220px-Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg/330px-Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg/440px-Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kaohsiung_harbor_sunrise_skyline.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Twilight in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Kaohsiung" title="Port of Kaohsiung">Port of Kaohsiung</a>, Taiwan.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">Twilight</a> is the period before <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> and after <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> in which there is natural light but no direct sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNO_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNO-35">[35]</a></sup> Twilight can be subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a>, or into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_twilight" title="Civil twilight">civil twilight</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_twilight" title="Nautical twilight">nautical twilight</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_twilight" title="Astronomical twilight">astronomical twilight</a>. Civil twilight begins when the sun is 6 degrees below the horizon; nautical begins at 12 degrees; and astronomical begins at 18 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Night">Night</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Night">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">Night</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">Night</a> is the period in which the sky is <a href="/wiki/Darkness" title="Darkness">dark</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> or the period between <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a> where no light is visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Due to the darkness of night, it affects the <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">artificial light</a> during night can disrupt circadian rhythms and sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
