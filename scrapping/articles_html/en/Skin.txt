<p><b>Skin</b> is the layer of usually soft, flexible outer tissue covering the body of a <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> animal, with three main functions: protection, regulation, and sensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other <a href="/wiki/Cuticle" title="Cuticle">animal coverings</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_exoskeleton" title="Arthropod exoskeleton">arthropod exoskeleton</a>, have different <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">developmental origin</a>, structure and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a>. The adjective <b>cutaneous</b> means "of the skin" (from Latin <i>cutis</i> 'skin'). In <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, the skin is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Integumentary_system" title="Integumentary system">integumentary system</a> made up of multiple layers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectodermal" title="Ectodermal">ectodermal</a> <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a> and guards the underlying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ligament" title="Ligament">ligaments</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_organs" title="Internal organs">internal organs</a>. Skin of a different nature exists in <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Skin (including cutaneous and subcutaneous tissues) plays crucial roles in formation, structure, and function of extraskeletal apparatus such as horns of bovids (e.g., cattle) and rhinos, cervids' antlers, giraffids' ossicones, armadillos' osteoderm, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Os_penis" title="Os penis">os penis</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Os_clitoris" title="Os clitoris">os clitoris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>All mammals have some hair on their skin, even <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammals</a> like <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porpoise" title="Porpoise">porpoises</a> that appear to be hairless.
The skin interfaces with the environment and is the first line of defense from external factors. For example, the skin plays a key role in protecting the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">body</a> against <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proksch_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proksch-4">[4]</a></sup> and excessive water loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madison_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madison-5">[5]</a></sup> Its other functions are <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">temperature regulation</a>, sensation, and the production of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a> folates.  Severely damaged skin may heal by forming <a href="/wiki/Scar" title="Scar">scar tissue</a>. This is sometimes discoloured and depigmented. The thickness of skin also varies from location to location on an organism. In humans, for example, the skin located under the eyes and around the eyelids is the thinnest skin on the body at 0.5 mm thick and is one of the first areas to show signs of aging such as "crows feet" and wrinkles. The skin on the palms and the soles of the feet is the thickest skin on the body as 4 mm thick. The speed and quality of wound healing in skin is promoted by the reception of <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">Fur</a> is dense hair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Primarily, fur augments the insulation the skin provides but can also serve as a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sex_characteristic" title="Secondary sex characteristic">secondary sexual characteristic</a> or as <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>. On some animals, the skin is very hard and thick and can be processed to create <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>. <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">Reptiles</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> have hard protective scales on their skin for protection, and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> have hard <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>, all made of tough <a href="/wiki/Beta-keratin" title="Beta-keratin">beta-keratins</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">Amphibian</a> skin is not a strong barrier, especially regarding the passage of chemicals via skin, and is often subject to <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a> and diffusive forces. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frog</a> sitting in an <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> solution would be sedated quickly as the chemical diffuses through its skin. <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">Amphibian</a> skin plays key roles in everyday survival and their ability to exploit a wide range of habitats and ecological conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarke_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word skin originally only referred to dressed and tanned animal hide and the usual word for human skin was hide.
Skin is a borrowing from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> skinn "animal hide, fur", ultimately from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">Proto-Indo-European</a> root *sek-, meaning "to cut" (probably a reference to the fact that in those times animal hide was commonly cut off to be used as garment).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

</p>

<p>Mammalian skin is composed of two primary layers:
</p>
<ul><li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Epidermis" title="Epidermis">epidermis</a></i>, which provides waterproofing and serves as a barrier to infection.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a></i>, which serves as a location for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appendages" title="Appendages">appendages</a> of skin.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidermis">Epidermis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Epidermis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Epidermis" title="Epidermis">Epidermis</a></div>
<p>The epidermis is composed of the outermost layers of the skin. It forms a protective barrier over the body's surface, responsible for keeping water in the body and preventing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a> from entering, and is a stratified squamous <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooks_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rooks-12">[12]</a></sup> composed of <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">proliferating</a> basal and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_differentiation" title="Cell differentiation">differentiated</a> suprabasal <a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">keratinocytes</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">Keratinocytes</a> are the major <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, constituting 95% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooks_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rooks-12">[12]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Merkel_cell" title="Merkel cell">Merkel cells</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Melanocyte" title="Melanocyte">melanocytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Langerhans_cell" title="Langerhans cell">Langerhans cells</a> are also present. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> can be further subdivided into the following <i>strata</i> or layers (beginning with the outermost layer):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ageingskin_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ageingskin-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Stratum_corneum" title="Stratum corneum">Stratum corneum</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stratum_lucidum" title="Stratum lucidum">Stratum lucidum</a> (only in <a href="/wiki/Hand#Human_anatomy" title="Hand">palms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sole_(foot)" title="Sole (foot)">soles</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stratum_granulosum" title="Stratum granulosum">Stratum granulosum</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stratum_spinosum" title="Stratum spinosum">Stratum spinosum</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stratum_basale" title="Stratum basale">Stratum basale</a> (also called the <i>stratum germinativum</i>)</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">Keratinocytes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Stratum_basale" title="Stratum basale">stratum basale</a> proliferate through <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> and the daughter <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> move up the strata changing shape and composition as they undergo multiple stages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_differentiation" title="Cell differentiation">cell differentiation</a> to eventually become anucleated. During that process, <a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">keratinocytes</a> will become highly organized, forming <a href="/wiki/Cell_junction" title="Cell junction">cellular junctions</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desmosomes" title="Desmosomes">desmosomes</a>) between each other and secreting <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a> which contribute to the formation of an <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a> and provide mechanical <a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">strength</a> to the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pmid_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pmid-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">Keratinocytes</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Stratum_corneum" title="Stratum corneum">stratum corneum</a> are eventually shed from the surface (<a href="/wiki/Desquamation" title="Desquamation">desquamation</a>).
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> contains no <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> in the deepest layers are nourished by diffusion from <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a> extending to the upper layers of the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basement_membrane">Basement membrane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Basement membrane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Basement_membrane" title="Basement membrane">Basement membrane</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Epidermis" title="Epidermis">epidermis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> are separated by a thin sheet of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibers" title="Fibers">fibers</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Basement_membrane" title="Basement membrane">basement membrane</a>, which is made through the action of both <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a>.
The basement membrane controls the traffic of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> between the dermis and epidermis but also serves, through the binding of a variety of <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Growth_factors" title="Growth factors">growth factors</a>, as a reservoir for their controlled release during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiological" title="Physiological">physiological</a> remodeling or repair processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dermis">Dermis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dermis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">Dermis</a></div>
<p>The <b>dermis</b> is the layer of skin beneath the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> that consists of <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> and cushions the body from <a href="/wiki/Stress_(biology)" title="Stress (biology)">stress</a> and strain. The <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> provides tensile <a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">strength</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elasticity</a> to the skin through an <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a> composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collagen_fibrils" title="Collagen fibrils">collagen fibrils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfibrils" title="Microfibrils">microfibrils</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_fibers" title="Elastic fibers">elastic fibers</a>, embedded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyaluronan" title="Hyaluronan">hyaluronan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proteoglycan" title="Proteoglycan">proteoglycans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pmid_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pmid-14">[14]</a></sup> Skin proteoglycans  are varied and have very specific locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyaluronan" title="Hyaluronan">hyaluronan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Versican" title="Versican">versican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decorin" title="Decorin">decorin</a> are present throughout the dermis and <a href="/wiki/Epidermis" title="Epidermis">epidermis</a> <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a>, whereas <a href="/wiki/Biglycan" title="Biglycan">biglycan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perlecan" title="Perlecan">perlecan</a> are only found in the epidermis.
</p><p>It harbors many <a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">mechanoreceptors</a> (nerve endings) that provide the sense of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch" title="Touch">touch</a> and heat through <a href="/wiki/Nociceptor" title="Nociceptor">nociceptors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermoreceptor" title="Thermoreceptor">thermoreceptors</a>. It also contains the <a href="/wiki/Hair_follicle" title="Hair follicle">hair follicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweat_gland" title="Sweat gland">sweat glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sebaceous_gland" title="Sebaceous gland">sebaceous glands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocrine_glands" title="Apocrine glands">apocrine glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_vessel" title="Lymphatic vessel">lymphatic vessels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> provide nourishment and waste removal from its own <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> as well as for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a>.
</p><p>Dermis and subcutaneous tissues are thought to contain germinative cells involved in formation of horns, osteoderm, and other extra-skeletal apparatus in mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> is tightly connected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Basement_membrane" title="Basement membrane">basement membrane</a> and is structurally divided into two areas: a superficial area adjacent to the epidermis, called the <i>papillary region</i>, and a deep thicker area known as the <i>reticular region</i>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Papillary_region">Papillary region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Papillary region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The papillary region is composed of loose ballsack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Areolar_connective_tissue" title="Areolar connective tissue">areolar connective tissue</a>. This is named for its fingerlike projections called <i>papillae</i> that extend toward the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a>. The papillae provide the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> with a "bumpy" surface that interdigitates with the epidermis, strengthening the connection between the two layers of skin.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reticular_region">Reticular region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reticular region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The reticular region lies deep in the papillary region and is usually much thicker. It is composed of dense irregular <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> and receives its name from the dense concentration of <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagenous</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_fibers" title="Elastic fibers">elastic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reticular_fiber" title="Reticular fiber">reticular</a> fibers that weave throughout it. These <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> fibers give the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> its properties of <a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">strength</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extensibility" title="Extensibility">extensibility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elasticity</a>.
Also located within the reticular region are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roots_of_the_hair" title="Roots of the hair">roots of the hair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweat_gland" title="Sweat gland">sweat glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sebaceous_gland" title="Sebaceous gland">sebaceous glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cutaneous_receptor" title="Cutaneous receptor">receptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nail_(anatomy)" title="Nail (anatomy)">nails</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subcutaneous_tissue">Subcutaneous tissue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subcutaneous tissue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue" title="Subcutaneous tissue">Subcutaneous tissue</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue" title="Subcutaneous tissue">subcutaneous tissue</a> (also hypodermis)  is not part of the skin, and lies below the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a>. Its purpose is to attach the skin to underlying <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> as well as supplying it with <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">nerves</a>. It consists of loose <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elastin" title="Elastin">elastin</a>. The main <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> types are <a href="/wiki/Fibroblast" title="Fibroblast">fibroblasts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macrophage" title="Macrophage">macrophages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adipocyte" title="Adipocyte">adipocytes</a> (the subcutaneous tissue contains 50% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_fat" title="Body fat">body fat</a>). <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">Fat</a> serves as padding and insulation for the body.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">Microorganisms</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus">Staphylococcus</a> epidermidis</i> colonize the skin surface. The density of <a href="/wiki/Skin_flora" title="Skin flora">skin flora</a> depends on region of the skin. The disinfected skin surface gets recolonized from <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> residing in the deeper areas of the <a href="/wiki/Hair_follicle" title="Hair follicle">hair follicle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gut</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urogenital" title="Urogenital">urogenital</a> openings.
</p>

<p>The epidermis of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and of most <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> consists entirely of live <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, with only minimal quantities of <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a> in the cells of the superficial layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It is generally permeable, and in the case of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a>, may actually be a major respiratory organ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fish</a> typically contains relatively little of the <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Tetrapod" title="Tetrapod">tetrapods</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Instead, in most species, it is largely replaced by solid, protective bony <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Apart from some particularly large dermal bones that form parts of the <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a>, these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a> are lost in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrapods" title="Tetrapods">tetrapods</a>, although many <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> do have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a> of a different kind, as do <a href="/wiki/Pangolin" title="Pangolin">pangolins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous_fish" title="Cartilaginous fish">Cartilaginous fish</a> have numerous tooth-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal_denticle" title="Dermal denticle">denticles</a> embedded in their skin, in place of true <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sweat_gland" title="Sweat gland">Sweat glands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sebaceous_gland" title="Sebaceous gland">sebaceous glands</a> are both unique to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, but other types of skin gland are found in other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a> typically have a numerous individual <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a>-secreting skin <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> that aid in insulation and protection, but may also have <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poison</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glands" title="Glands">glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photophore" title="Photophore">photophores</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> that produce a more watery, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serous" title="Serous">serous</a> fluid. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> are gathered together to form sac-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glands" title="Glands">glands</a>. Most living <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a> also possess <i>granular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glands" title="Glands">glands</a></i> in the skin, that secrete irritating or toxic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VB_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-VB-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Melanin" title="Melanin">melanin</a> is found in the skin of many species, in the <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> is often relatively colorless. Instead, the color of the skin is largely due to <a href="/wiki/Chromatophore" title="Chromatophore">chromatophores</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a>, which, in addition to melanin, may contain <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigments" title="Pigments">pigments</a>. Many species, such as <a href="/wiki/Chameleon" title="Chameleon">chameleons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flounder" title="Flounder">flounders</a> may be able to change the color of their skin by adjusting the relative size of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromatophores" title="Chromatophores">chromatophores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VB_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-VB-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amphibians">Amphibians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Amphibians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Amphibian#Skin" title="Amphibian">Amphibian § Skin</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Amphibians possess two types of <a href="/wiki/Gland" title="Gland">glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mucous_gland" title="Mucous gland">mucous</a> and granular (serous). Both of these glands are part of the <a href="/wiki/Integument" title="Integument">integument</a> and thus considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cutaneous" title="Cutaneous">cutaneous</a>. Mucous and granular glands are both divided into three different sections which all connect to structure the gland as a whole. The three individual parts of the gland are the duct, the intercalary region, and lastly the alveolar gland (sac). Structurally, the duct is derived via <a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">keratinocytes</a> and passes through to the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Epidermis" title="Epidermis">epidermal</a> or outer skin layer thus allowing external secretions of the body. The gland alveolus is a sac shaped structure that is found on the bottom or base region of the granular gland. The cells in this sac specialize in secretion. Between the alveolar gland and the duct is the intercalary system which can be summed up as a transitional region connecting the duct to the grand alveolar beneath the epidermal skin layer. In general, granular glands are larger in size than the mucous glands, however mucous glands hold a much greater majority in overall number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Granular_glands">Granular glands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Granular glands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Granular glands can be identified as <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venomous</a> and often differ in the type of toxin as well as the concentrations of secretions across various orders and species within the amphibians. They are located in clusters differing in concentration depending on <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibian</a> taxa. The toxins can be fatal to most vertebrates or have no effect against others. These glands are alveolar meaning they structurally have little sacs in which venom is produced and held before it is secreted upon defensive behaviors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Structurally, the ducts of the granular gland initially maintain a cylindrical shape. However, when the ducts become mature and full of  fluid, the base of the ducts become swollen due to the pressure from the inside. This causes the epidermal layer to form a pit like opening on the surface of the duct in which the inner fluid will be secreted in an upwards fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The intercalary region of granular glands is more developed and mature in comparison with mucous glands. This region resides as a ring of cells surrounding the basal portion of the duct which are argued to have an <a href="/wiki/Ectoderm" title="Ectoderm">ectodermal</a> muscular nature due to their influence over the lumen (space inside the tube) of the duct with dilation and constriction functions during secretions. The cells are found radially around the duct and provide a distinct attachment site for muscle fibers around the gland's body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The gland alveolus is a sac that is divided into three specific regions/layers. The outer layer or tunica fibrosa is composed of densely packed connective-tissue which connects with fibers from the spongy intermediate layer where elastic fibers, as well as nerves, reside. The nerves send signals to the muscles as well as the epithelial layers. Lastly, the epithelium or tunica propria encloses the gland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mucous_glands">Mucous glands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mucous glands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mucous_gland" title="Mucous gland">Mucous glands</a> are non-venomous and offer a different functionality for amphibians than granular. Mucous glands cover the entire surface area of the amphibian body and specialize in keeping the body lubricated. There are many other functions of the mucous glands such as controlling the pH, thermoregulation, adhesive properties to the environment, anti-predator behaviors (slimy to the grasp), chemical communication, even anti-bacterial/viral properties for protection against pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ducts of the mucous gland appear as cylindrical vertical tubes that break through the epidermal layer to the surface of the skin. The cells lining the inside of the ducts are oriented with their longitudinal axis forming 90-degree angles surrounding the duct in a helical fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Intercalary cells react identically to those of granular glands but on a smaller scale. Among the amphibians, there are taxa which contain a modified intercalary region (depending on the function of the glands), yet the majority share the same structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The alveolar or mucous glands are much more simple and only consist of an epithelium layer as well as connective tissue which forms a cover over the gland. This gland lacks a tunica propria and appears to have delicate and intricate fibers which pass over the gland's muscle and epithelial layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds_and_reptiles">Birds and reptiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Birds and reptiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> is closer to that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, with a layer of dead keratin-filled <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> at the surface, to help reduce water loss. A similar pattern is also seen in some of the more terrestrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Toad" title="Toad">toads</a>. However, in all of these animals there is no clear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_differentiation" title="Cell differentiation">differentiation</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> into distinct layers, as occurs in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, with the change in <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> type being relatively gradual. The mammalian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> always possesses at least a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratum_germinativum" title="Stratum germinativum">stratum germinativum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stratum_corneum" title="Stratum corneum">stratum corneum</a>, but the other intermediate layers found in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> are not always distinguishable.
<a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">Hair</a> is a distinctive feature of mammalian skin, while <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> are (at least among living species) similarly unique to <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VB_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-VB-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">Birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> have relatively few skin <a href="/wiki/Gland" title="Gland">glands</a>, although there may be a few structures for specific purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromone</a>-secreting <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> in some <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">uropygial gland</a> of most birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VB_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-VB-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-One_source plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-one_source" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><img alt="Question book-new.svg" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>relies largely or entirely upon a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">single source</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Skin##" title="Talk:Skin">talk page</a>. Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by introducing <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">citations</a> to additional sources.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2021</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Cutaneous structures arise from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> and include a variety of features such as hair, feathers, claws and nails. During embryogenesis, the epidermis splits into two layers: the periderm (which is lost) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratum_germinativum" title="Stratum germinativum">basal layer</a>.  The basal layer is a <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cell</a> layer and through asymmetrical divisions, becomes the source of skin cells throughout life. It is maintained as a stem cell layer through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autocrine" title="Autocrine">autocrine</a> signal, <a href="/wiki/TGF_alpha" title="TGF alpha">TGF alpha</a>, and through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paracrine" title="Paracrine">paracrine</a> signaling from FGF7 (<a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte_growth_factor" title="Keratinocyte growth factor">keratinocyte growth factor</a>) produced by the dermis below the basal cells. In mice, over-expression of these factors leads to an overproduction of <a href="/wiki/Stratum_granulosum" title="Stratum granulosum">granular cells</a> and thick skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Hair and feathers are formed in a regular pattern and it is believed to be the result of a reaction-diffusion system. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reaction-diffusion_system" title="Reaction-diffusion system">reaction-diffusion system</a> combines an activator, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_hedgehog" title="Sonic hedgehog">Sonic hedgehog</a>, with an inhibitor, BMP4 or BMP2, to form clusters of cells in a regular pattern. Sonic hedgehog-expressing epidermal cells induce the condensation of cells in the <a href="/wiki/Mesoderm" title="Mesoderm">mesoderm</a>. The clusters of mesodermal cells signal back to the epidermis to form the appropriate structure for that position. BMP signals from the epidermis inhibit the formation of placodes in nearby ectoderm.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>It is believed that the mesoderm defines the pattern. The epidermis instructs the mesodermal cells to condense and then the mesoderm instructs the epidermis of what structure to make through a series of reciprocal inductions. Transplantation experiments involving frog and newt epidermis indicated that the mesodermal signals are conserved between species but the epidermal response is species-specific meaning that the mesoderm instructs the epidermis of its position and the epidermis uses this information to make a specific structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Skin performs the following functions:
</p>
<ol><li>Protection: an anatomical barrier from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a> and damage between the internal and external  <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> in bodily defense. (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skin_absorption" title="Skin absorption">Skin absorption</a>.) <a href="/wiki/Langerhans_cell" title="Langerhans cell">Langerhans cells</a> in the skin are part of the <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_immune_system" title="Adaptive immune system">adaptive immune system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proksch_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Proksch-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madison_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Madison-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sensation" title="wikt:sensation">Sensation</a>: contains a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nerve_endings" title="Nerve endings">nerve endings</a> that jump to <a href="/wiki/Thermoreceptor" title="Thermoreceptor">heat and cold</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Touch" title="Touch">touch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a> <a href="/wiki/Injury" title="Injury">injury</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">somatosensory system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haptic_perception" title="Haptic perception">haptic perception</a>).</li>
<li>Thermoregulation: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccrine" title="Eccrine">Eccrine</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweat" title="Sweat">sweat</a>) glands and dilated blood vessels (increased superficial <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a>) aid heat loss, while constricted <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">vessels</a> greatly reduce cutaneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_flow" title="Blood flow">blood flow</a> and conserve heat. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erector_pili_muscle" title="Erector pili muscle">Erector pili muscles</a> in mammals adjust the angle of hair shafts to change the degree of insulation provided by hair or <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a>.</li>
<li>Control of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>: the skin provides a relatively dry and semi-impermeable barrier to reduce fluid loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madison_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Madison-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Storage and <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">synthesis</a>: acts as a storage center for <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> and water</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skin_absorption" title="Skin absorption">Absorption through the skin</a>: <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can diffuse into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a> in small amounts; some animals use their skin as their sole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiration_organ" title="Respiration organ">respiration organ</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> comprising the outermost 0.25–0.40 mm of the skin are "almost exclusively supplied by external oxygen", although the "contribution to total <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiration</a> is negligible")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Topical_medication" title="Topical medication">medications are absorbed through the skin</a>.</li>
<li>Water resistance: The skin acts as a water resistant barrier so essential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a> aren't washed out of the body. The nutrients and oils that help hydrate the skin are covered by the most outer skin layer, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a>. This is helped in part by the sebaceous glands that release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebum" title="Sebum">sebum</a>, an oily liquid. Water itself will not cause the elimination of oils on the skin, because the oils residing in our dermis flow and would be affected by water without the epidermis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">Camouflage</a>, whether the skin is naked or covered in fur, scales, or feathers, skin structures provide protective coloration and patterns that help to conceal animals from predators or prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanics">Mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Soft_tissue" title="Soft tissue">Soft tissue</a></div>
<p>Skin is a soft tissue and exhibits key mechanical behaviors of these tissues.  The most pronounced feature is the J-curve stress strain response, in which a region of large strain and minimal stress exists and corresponds to the microstructural straightening and reorientation of collagen fibrils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In some cases the intact skin is prestreched, like wetsuits around the diver's body, and in other cases the intact skin is under compression. Small circular holes punched on the skin may widen or close into ellipses, or shrink and remain circular, depending on preexisting stresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aging">Aging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tissue <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a> generally declines with age, in part because <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem</a>/progenitor cells fail to self-renew or <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">differentiate</a>.  In the skin of mice, <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrial</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_stress" title="Oxidative stress">oxidative stress</a> can promote <a href="/wiki/Cellular_senescence" title="Cellular senescence">cellular senescence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ageing" title="Ageing">aging</a> phenotypes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="removed citation to predatory publisher content (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Ordinarily mitochondrial superoxide dismutase (<a href="/wiki/SOD2" title="SOD2">SOD2</a>) protects against oxidative stress.   Using a mouse model of genetic SOD2 deficiency, it was shown that failure to express this important antioxidant enzyme in epidermal cells caused cellular senescence, nuclear <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring)" title="DNA damage (naturally occurring)">DNA damage</a>, and irreversible arrest of proliferation of a fraction of <a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">keratinocytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid26240345_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid26240345-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Skin aging is caused in part by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TGF-%CE%B2" title="TGF-β">TGF-β</a>, which reduces the subcutaneous fat that gives skin a pleasant appearance and texture.   <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TGF-%CE%B2" title="TGF-β">TGF-β</a> does this by blocking the conversion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal_fibroblasts" title="Dermal fibroblasts">dermal fibroblasts</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fat_cells" title="Fat cells">fat cells</a>; with fewer fat cells underneath to provide support, the skin becomes saggy and wrinkled.  Subcutaneous fat also produces <a href="/wiki/Cathelicidin" title="Cathelicidin">cathelicidin</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a> that fights bacterial infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Common changes in the skin as a result of aging range from <a href="/wiki/Wrinkle" title="Wrinkle">wrinkles</a>, discoloration, and skin laxity but can manifest in more severe forms such as skin malignancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-39">[39]</a></sup> Some research suggests that skin dryness can also arise from a decay in skin barrier function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-38">[38]</a></sup> Moreover, these factors can be accelerated and exacerbated by sun exposure in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Photoaging" title="Photoaging">photoaging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skin&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2021</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The term "skin" may also refer to the covering of a small animal, such as a <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat</a> (<a href="/wiki/Goatskin_(material)" title="Goatskin (material)">goatskin</a>), <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pig</a>, snake (<a href="/wiki/Snakeskin" title="Snakeskin">snakeskin</a>) etc. or the young of a large animal.
</p><p>The term <a href="/wiki/Hide_(skin)" title="Hide (skin)">hides</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rawhide_(material)" title="Rawhide (material)">rawhide</a> refers to the covering of a large adult animal such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">buffalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a> etc.
</p><p>Skins and <a href="/wiki/Hide_(skin)" title="Hide (skin)">hides</a> from the different animals are used for <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">clothing</a>, bags and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_products" title="Consumer products">consumer products</a>, usually in the form of <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, but also as <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">furs</a>.
</p><p>Skin from <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> was used to make <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscripts</a>.
</p><p>Skin can also be cooked to make <a href="/wiki/Pork_rind" title="Pork rind">pork rind</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pork_rind" title="Pork rind">crackling</a>.
</p>
